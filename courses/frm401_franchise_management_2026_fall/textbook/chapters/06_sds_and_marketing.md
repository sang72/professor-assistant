## Scale of Franchise Marketing

**Top marketing spenders (2002):**
- Ford, GM, DaimlerChrysler (their franchised dealers): Combined advertising budgets = $7.7 billion
- Yum Brands (Pizza Hut, KFC, Taco Bell): ~$1 billion

**Franchise marketing totals (estimated, 2002):**
- Total marketing expenditures by franchised organizations: $60 billion
  - Creative and production: ~$8 billion
  - National advertising: $20+ billion
  - Regional efforts: ~$22 billion
  - Local activities: ~$6 billion
- Over 600,000 franchised outlets in the U.S.
- Revenues estimated at more than $1 trillion

## Why is Marketing Important in a Franchise?

### 1. Marketing States the System's Promise to the Customer

Process:
1. Understand demographics, geographics, and psychographics of the PTA
2. Make a promise to customers that their needs will be met CONSISTENTLY across all points of contact
3. When the point-of-sale validates that promise through your SDS → a BRAND is built

**Larger the franchise system = more marketing dollars generated**

Eventually, only a small percentage of the budget is needed to produce first-class marketing material; the rest goes to buying advertising time and space.

### 2. Marketing Sells Franchises and Raises Capital

**From a debt perspective**: A successful franchise brand carries pre-existing recognition that promotes comfort in creditors. Banks like brand names!

Example: U.S. Small Business Administration has a special lending program for franchises.

**From an equity perspective (IPO)**: During the weeks leading up to an IPO, franchised offerings often use significant marketing expenditures to further leverage existing brand awareness. This has been credited with increased multiples on the stock and greater growth capacity.

### 3. Marketing Leverages Brand Awareness as the System Grows

Layered approach (local → regional → national) allows a system to grow into its marketing capabilities over time.

**The "virtuous cycle"**: 
Building more stores → more marketing dollars → attracts more customers → more revenue → continued ability to open more stores

## What Are the Forms of Marketing in Franchising?

### 1. Franchisor Marketing
**Responsibilities:**
- Centralized marketing and materials production
- Development of national advertising campaigns
- Purchase of media on a national level
- Advice and approval of local/regional advertising done by franchisee

### 2. Franchisee Marketing
**Responsibilities:**
- Regional and local advertising
- Contractual obligation to spend marketing dollars in local market on promotions and public relations

**Regional advertising cooperatives:**
- Franchisees pool contractually obligated portions of their marketing budget
- Franchisor has a seat on the regional board of cooperative directors (governance role)
- Example: New York regional cooperative covers NYC, Long Island, southern Connecticut, northern New Jersey
  - Even a franchise in southern Connecticut can advertise on "Imus in the Morning" or New York Yankees broadcasts

**Important note**: Although national advertising execution is a FRANCHISOR responsibility, it is FUNDED through a contractually obligated contribution of ALL franchisees to a franchisor-administered fund.

### 3. Supplier Cooperative Advertising

Most common in branded supplies (Coca-Cola, Pennzoil).

**Example structure (Mail Boxes Etc. + Weyerhaeuser):**
- Weyerhaeuser might contribute $1M+ to the national advertising fund
- Agreement: Weyerhaeuser contributes up to 20% for any ad that contains the Weyerhaeuser brand
- Full-page ad in New York Times costing $100,000 → Weyerhaeuser pays $20,000 if 20% of the ad represents Weyerhaeuser acceptably
- 30-second TV ad → Weyerhaeuser product must appear for ~6 seconds

**Risks:**
- May have to give up flexibility in advertising decisions
- Supplier may want to review and potentially veto the ad before it goes public

**The Downside: The Ultimate Brand Marriage — Ford and Firestone (TIP 6-1)**

94-year relationship between Ford Motor Company and Firestone Tire & Rubber Company:
- William Ford Jr. = son of heiress Martha Firestone
- Ford automobiles always equipped with Firestone tires
- August 2000: Recall of 6.5 million Firestone Wilderness tires on Ford Explorer
- Blame passed back and forth for months
- Federal investigation: Blame rested with Firestone (now Bridgestone American subsidiary)
- Both stock prices fell; CEO of each company was removed
- Firestone stock has recovered only slightly
- Lesson: When supplier is involved in a crisis, customers may identify BOTH brands with the problem

### 4. Joint Advertising

Example: Dunkin' Donuts and Baskin Robbins (both owned by Allied Domecq) promote each other's products within their respective franchise systems.

Allied Domecq later added Togo's Eatery → seeking both joint marketing and operational synergy from combining brands.

### 5. Cross-Promotion

Local promotion between your franchise and another local firm.

Examples:
- Dinner at Pizza Hut → tickets to professional sports franchise
- Back of supermarket receipt → coupon for free Taco Supreme at Taco Bell
- Curious George toy at McDonald's (licensing)
- Match Box cars at Midas Muffler

Creative marketing is a hallmark of cross-promotion.

## Local Marketing

Most often manifests in the form of promotional activities requiring deep local market knowledge.

**Example**: Subaru dealership franchisee must:
- Contribute % of sales to regional and national Subaru marketing funds
- AND satisfy local level marketing expenditure requirements
- Local approach: Sponsoring local youth sports teams (promotional activity > advertising)
- Uses franchisor's detailed target customer information combined with own local knowledge

## Regional Advertising

**Key achievement**: When a franchise gets big enough to pool advertising funds for regional media purchases.

**How it works:**
- All franchisees within a defined geographic region are OBLIGATED to participate
- Contribute as much as 4-5% of revenue to advertising fund
- Television reach has traditionally defined the region

**Krispy Kreme example** (extreme of focused regional strategy):
- Formed 1937, began franchising 1970s, didn't reach New York until 1996
- Filled in southeast first and dominated the market
- Focus on radio and television as principle measure of marketing efficiency

**Media buying progression:**
1. Local newspaper and radio
2. Cable television (regional)
3. Network television (national)

**Why cable is complex for national buys**: Cable TV industry is fragmented → national purchase would require buying spots from several cable operators → diminishes potential economies.

**Governance of regional cooperatives:**
- Multiple outlet owners often dominate decision-making (contribute most money)
- Single-unit owners gain disproportionate benefit (contribute least, receive same marketing benefits)
- Franchisor often participates through seat on the board — ensures consistency between regional and national advertising strategies

**Dunkin' Donuts "4-walls marketing"**: Dominates fast-food advertising in New England with formal regional program.
- Regional co-ops (Boston, Hartford, Worcester) focus on local events like:
  - Sponsorship of "slam dunk" exhibit in Basketball Hall of Fame
  - Dunkin' Donuts Night at American Hockey League's Hartford Wolf Pack

## National Advertising

**The "holy grail" of marketing economies.**

**The national advertising fund:**
- As outlets grow, franchisee contributions exceed amount needed to produce marketing materials
- The balance = dollars for national advertising
- Franchisees usually want formal voice in how national money is spent

**National advertising council:**
- Originally derived from 1970s IFA study
- Recommended: Franchisors preempt informal franchisee associations by forming formal governance structure
- Council roles: Manage relationship with advertising/marketing agency (including hiring and firing), administer funds, determine pricing and timing issues around promotion, introduce new products

**National advertising fund governance:**
- Often held in separate ESCROW account
- Protected with third-party certified audit
- Reviewed by advertising council
- **Warning**: Beware of franchises that don't audit their national advertising fund

**Critical question**: The national fund is used principally for solicitation for the sale of franchises vs. advertising for customers? This must be disclosed to franchisees.

## Production of Marketing Materials

**Process:**
1. Marketing department engages advertising and public relations agency
2. Working with representative group of franchisees, develop marketing THEME
3. Theme manifests in production materials
4. You (franchisee) pay through contributions to national advertising fund

**Production elements:**
- Print: newspaper layouts, magazine layouts, direct mail materials
- Electronic: radio and television material
- Web: Classified under print media umbrella

**Evaluation tip for prospective franchisees**: Look at and listen to the advertising. Ask questions of the franchisor about the links between concept development and each form of media and its execution.

## An Overview of Marketing and the License Agreement

**When the franchise relationship becomes litigious, marketing always seems to have some role in the breach.**

The license agreement should explicitly outline:
- ALL fees and expenditures mandated by the franchisor
- Any payments beyond the advertising fee (e.g., printing collateral material)
- Market penetration required to form a regional cooperative
- Governance of the national advertising mechanisms
- The proper checks and balances ensuring proper allocation of funds

## Potential Marketing Problems

### 1. Distribution of Benefits

Problem: There will not be an even distribution of benefits of advertising.

Example: If Alpha Graphics runs a Super Bowl ad when the New England Patriots are playing:
- New England-based franchisees receive MORE benefit than franchisees in Texas or Michigan
- Some advertising councils attempt to balance future campaigns based on past disparities
- Practically: Very difficult to execute

**Key rule**: Don't get in the way of good media purchases because of unequal distribution of benefits.

### 2. Marketing and Pricing Issues

**Promotional campaign problems**: Franchisor wants to boost revenues without regard for short-term effect on franchisee profitability.

**Classic example: Midas Muffler 2-for-1 special:**
- Franchisees must bear the extra cost of such a promotion
- "At participating stores" language included, but difficult for franchisees not to participate when customers come in expecting the deal
- Franchisor happy: More mufflers sold = enhanced royalty revenue
- Franchisee unhappy: Operating margin reduced

**Antitrust issues**: If Midas also manufactures the muffler and exhaust components, careful language must be written into the license agreement regarding pricing, limitations, and franchisee purchasing options.

---

## Marketing and the Free Riding Risk

**Local marketing expenditures are fertile ground for free-riding abuse.**

Why it's tempting: A little less money spent by any individual franchisee will not likely affect the brand or revenue of the entire system.

Example: A franchisee could take his or her family out to dinner and charge it to the local marketing account. The franchisor would have no way of knowing.

**Systemwide impact**: If each McDonald's franchise misallocated only $100/month of required local advertising → over $30 MILLION annually in reduced advertising systemwide.

## Conclusion

Ralph Waldo Emerson said: "If a man can write a better book, preach a better sermon, or make a better mouse-trap than his neighbor, though he build his house in the woods, the world will make a beaten path to his door."

**The authors' rebuttal**: If you build a better mousetrap, you had better make sure the world understands why it is better AND WHERE THEY CAN GET IT.

**Core marketing promise**: "We have what you need, and you can count on us to fill that need no matter which of our locations you go to."

**Simple messages that became cultural**: 
- "We'll change your oil in ten minutes"
- "It's time to make the donuts"
- "Billions and billions served"
- "We'll do it your way"
- "Just do it!"

**Why franchising marketing has grown for 40 years**: It integrates the value proposition for both customer and operator.
- More stores = more marketing dollars
- More marketing = more customers
- More revenue = ability to open more stores

**Required governance**: Well-defined governance with open and frequent communication among all franchise players is a necessity as systems grow.
