## SECTION 2: FULL LECTURE SCRIPT

Welcome back. Last session, we defined franchising precisely and explored what makes a franchisee entrepreneurial. Today, we compare franchising to alternative business models. When should a company franchise versus grow through corporate ownership?

[ASK STUDENTS: If you were starting a restaurant business, would you open corporate locations or use franchisees? What are the trade-offs?]

[PAUSE — wait for student responses]

Good thinking. Both have advantages and disadvantages. That is what we explore today.

[SHOW SLIDE 1: Business Models Comparison]

There are four main ways a company can grow a business. First, a single independent location. You own one store, one restaurant, one location. You do everything yourself. Second, a corporate chain. You own multiple locations. You hire managers to run each location, but you own all of them. Third, franchising. You grant independent businesspeople the right to operate locations under your brand. Fourth, hybrid — you own some locations and franchise others.

[TIME: ~5 min]

---

[SHOW SLIDE 2: Single Location Model]

Let's start with a single independent location. Advantages: complete control. You decide everything — what products to sell, what prices to charge, how to market. You keep all profits. Disadvantages: limited scale. You can only grow as fast as you personally can work or as fast as you can hire managers. You assume all risk. If the location fails, you lose your investment. Most small businesses operate this way. Your local coffee shop, your neighborhood bookstore, your family restaurant. They are owned by individuals who have invested their capital and their time.

[TIME: ~10 min]

---

[SHOW SLIDE 3: Corporate Chain Model]

Now, corporate chains. A company owns multiple locations. Starbucks owns many locations directly. McDonald's owns some. Walmart owns all of its stores. Advantages: complete control over all locations. The company can ensure consistent quality. The company captures all profits. Disadvantages: capital intensive. Owning 100 locations requires enormous capital for real estate, equipment, inventory. High operational complexity. The company must hire and train hundreds or thousands of managers. If one location fails, the company absorbs the loss directly. High risk.

[WRITE ON BOARD: Corporate Model = Capital Intensive + High Risk + Full Control + All Profits]

[TIME: ~15 min]

---

[SHOW SLIDE 4: Franchising Model]

Now, franchising. A company grows by granting independent businesspeople the right to operate locations. Advantages: capital efficient. The franchisor does not pay for each location upfront. Franchisees do. The franchisor collects upfront fees and ongoing royalties. Distributed risk. If a franchisee's location fails, the franchisor is not destroyed. The franchisor loses some royalties, but the franchisor's core business is not threatened. Motivated operators. Franchisees own the location, so they are highly motivated to make it succeed. Disadvantages: less control. The franchisor cannot dictate every decision. Quality can vary if franchisees do not follow standards. The franchisor's reputation depends on franchisees' performance.

[WRITE ON BOARD: Franchise Model = Capital Efficient + Lower Risk + Shared Responsibility + Royalty-Based Revenue]

[TIME: ~25 min]

---

[SHOW SLIDE 5: Risk-Return Matrix]

Let me show you a risk-return matrix. On the x-axis, we have capital required. On the y-axis, we have potential return on investment.

Independent locations: low capital, low return. You invest in one location. The return is the profit from that one location.

Corporate chains: high capital, potentially high return. You invest in many locations. If they all succeed, the return is huge. But if they fail, the loss is catastrophic.

Franchising: moderate capital, moderate-to-high return. The franchisor invests in the system, marketing, support. Franchisees invest in locations. If franchises succeed, the franchisor's return scales with the number of franchisees. The risk is distributed.

[TIME: ~35 min]

---

[SHOW SLIDE 6: When Does Franchising Make Sense?]

When should a company choose franchising? When several conditions are met. First, the business model must be replicable. You cannot franchise a unique, handmade product that only one person can make. But you can franchise a restaurant or a retail concept that can be standardized. Second, there must be demand for expansion. If your business is already at its optimal scale, franchising does not make sense. Third, you must have capital to invest in systems, training, support. You cannot franchise if you are broke. Fourth, you must be willing to distribute control. You cannot be a micromanager and franchise successfully.

[TIME: ~42 min]

---

[ASK STUDENTS: Can anyone think of a business that could not be franchised? Why not?]

[PAUSE — wait for student responses]

Good answers. Consulting businesses are hard to franchise because they rely on individual expertise. Law practices are hard to franchise because of professional regulations. Boutique brands that depend on a unique founder's vision are hard to franchise. But fast-casual restaurants, fitness studios, salons — these franchise well because the model is teachable and replicable.

Thank you. Next session, we will look at real cases. We will study Krispy Kreme and other franchise brands. See you next time.
