## SECTION 2: FULL LECTURE SCRIPT (English)

### Opening (3 minutes)

Good morning, everyone. Welcome to Week 3. Over the past two weeks, we've built a foundation. We understand what franchising is. We understand why it exists. We've explored the ecosystem. We've seen how franchisees can be entrepreneurs.

But we haven't yet explained **how franchising actually works**. How do you design a franchise system that both the franchisor and franchisees want to be part of? How do you structure the relationship so both parties profit? How do you prevent conflict?

That's where we are now. This week and next, we're going to introduce the most important framework in this entire course: the **Franchise Relationship Model**, or FRM.

The FRM is the master framework. Everything else we'll study—real estate strategy, financial analysis, franchisee selection, international expansion—all of it will fit into the FRM.

Think of the FRM as the blueprint for a successful franchise. If you understand this framework deeply, you can diagnose why franchises succeed, why they fail, and how to build them better.

Let's dive in.

---

### Segment 1: The Six Components Overview (9 minutes)

[SHOW SLIDE: "The Franchise Relationship Model — Six Components"]

The Franchise Relationship Model has six components. These six components are interlocking. Together, they create the conditions for a sustainable franchise system.

Here they are:

[WRITE ON BOARD: 
1. Customer Definition
2. Opportunity Recognition
3. Service Delivery System (SDS)
4. Transaction Analysis
5. Financial Model
6. Monitoring & Adaptation
]

Let me introduce them briefly, then we'll dive deep into each one.

**Component 1: Customer Definition**

Every franchise starts with a clear answer to: *Who is the customer? What do they want? Why would they choose this brand?*

Starbucks customers want a comfortable third place—not home, not office—to work, study, or relax. With consistent quality coffee and WiFi. McDonald's customers want fast, affordable meals for families. CU convenience store customers want late-night access to everyday products without leaving their neighborhood.

Without a clear customer definition, nothing else works. The Service Delivery System will be confused. The franchisees won't know what to prioritize. The business will lose focus.

**Component 2: Opportunity Recognition**

Once the franchisor understands the customer, the franchisor identifies *Why now?* Why does this opportunity exist today?

In the 1990s-2000s, Starbucks recognized that American consumers wanted a new kind of coffee experience. Not the traditional diner coffee. Not the specialty coffee from boutique roasters. Something in between: consistent, comfortable, aspirational. McDonald's recognized that busy families wanted quick, affordable meals. CU recognized that Korean consumers wanted 24-hour convenience without going to a large supermarket.

Opportunity recognition is about timing, market conditions, and consumer trends. It's about seeing a gap in the market before competitors do.

**Component 3: Service Delivery System (SDS)**

The SDS is the *operational system* that delivers the promised customer experience. It's how the franchisor translates customer needs into action.

For Starbucks, the SDS includes: store design (open, comfortable, branded), equipment (espresso machines, brewing methods), product (bean selection, recipes), staff (baristas trained to specific standards), processes (order taking, drink making, customer interaction).

For McDonald's, the SDS includes: restaurant design (modular, consistent), equipment (grills, fryers, ordering kiosks), product (standardized burger recipes), staff (trained to specific procedures), processes (cooking, assembly, serving).

The SDS must be *documented, teachable, and replicable*. If the franchisor can't explain how to deliver the customer experience, it can't be franchised.

**Component 4: Transaction Analysis**

Transaction analysis answers: *Who does what? What are the responsibilities of the franchisor? What are the responsibilities of the franchisee?*

For a McDonald's franchise, transaction analysis specifies:
- **Franchisor**: Selects site, approves restaurant design, sets menu, provides training, maintains brand standards
- **Franchisee**: Constructs building, equips restaurant, hires staff, operates daily, manages customer service, maintains cleanliness

Clear transaction analysis prevents conflict. Both parties know what to expect.

**Component 5: Financial Model**

How do both parties profit? What are the revenue sources and costs?

For a Starbucks franchisee:
- **Revenue**: Coffee and food sales to customers
- **Costs**: Labor, supplies, rent, utilities
- **To Franchisor**: Initial franchise fee + royalties (usually 5-7% of revenue) + marketing fees (1-2%)
- **Profit**: Revenue - Costs - Royalties - Fees

The financial model must make sense for both parties. If the franchisor takes too much (high fees, high royalties), the franchisee won't profit and will fail. If the franchisor takes too little, it won't have resources to support the system.

**Component 6: Monitoring & Adaptation**

How does the franchisor monitor franchisees to ensure they follow the system? And how does the system adapt as market conditions change?

Monitoring includes: store inspections, financial audits, mystery shopper programs, customer feedback, compliance with brand standards.

Adaptation includes: menu changes (McDonald's adds new items), technology updates (Starbucks' mobile app), training updates (addressing new customer preferences), real estate strategy changes.

Without monitoring, franchisees cut corners and damage the brand. Without adaptation, the system becomes obsolete.

[ASK STUDENTS: Can anyone think of a brand where monitoring is weak? What happens?]

[PAUSE for student responses]

Great examples. Let's move into the deep dive on each component.

### Segment 2: Components Deep-Dive — Customer, Opportunity, SDS (13 minutes)

[SHOW SLIDE: "The Customer-Opportunity-SDS Sequence"]

These three components are sequential. You start with the customer. You identify an opportunity. You build a system to serve them.

**Component 1: Customer Definition — In Depth**

Let's make customer definition very concrete.

Starbucks didn't invent the coffee market. Coffee existed for centuries. But Starbucks redefined *what kind of coffee experience* consumers wanted.

**Pre-Starbucks coffee in America:**
- Diners: Coffee with donuts, cheap, functional, older customers
- Home brewing: Mr. Coffee machines, convenience, but no ambiance
- Specialty roasters: Expensive, intimidating for many customers, limited availability

**Starbucks redefined coffee as:**
- Aspirational (premium, not cheap)
- Accessible (easy to understand, simple menu at first)
- Community-oriented (place to sit, work, socialize)
- Consistent (same experience in Seattle or Singapore)
- Capitalized (premium price consumers would pay)

This customer definition became everything. It drove the store design, the training, the pricing, the brand.

Similarly, McDonald's defined their customer not as "people who want to eat" but as "busy families seeking quick, affordable, consistent meals where kids feel welcome."

[WRITE ON BOARD: Clear Customer Definition = Everything Else Follows]

**Component 2: Opportunity Recognition — In Depth**

Opportunity recognition requires answering three questions:

1. **Is there unmet market demand?** (Yes, people want premium coffee experiences)
2. **Why hasn't the market been served yet?** (Because it required a new system, not just selling coffee)
3. **Why now?** (Market conditions, consumer sophistication, income levels, urban growth, coffee culture maturity)

Starbucks' opportunity was recognized in the 1980s-1990s. Why then? Because:
- American consumers had growing disposable income
- Specialty food/beverage was becoming fashionable
- Coffee culture was maturing (influenced by Italian and European coffee traditions)
- Urban centers had populations that wanted "third places" (Putnam's concept)
- Real estate and commercial expansion made national expansion feasible

By contrast, someone trying to launch a similar coffee concept in 1960 would have failed. The market wasn't ready.

**Opportunity recognition also requires recognizing the TIMELINE:**
- When does growth happen?
- When does saturation occur?
- When do competitors arrive?

CU's convenience store opportunity was recognized in Korea in the 1990s-2000s. Why? Because:
- Korean urbanization created 24-hour lifestyles
- Korean real estate was expensive (unlike US), driving demand for small, nearby stores
- Long work hours meant demand for late-night access to necessities
- Cold climate in winter, hot in summer, drove demand for convenience

By 2020, the Korean convenience store market was saturated (40K+ stores for 50M people).

**Component 3: Service Delivery System (SDS) — In Depth**

Once you understand the customer and the opportunity, you must design an SDS that *reliably delivers* the promised experience.

The Starbucks SDS includes:

**Physical Design:**
- Open store layout (customers can see the espresso bar)
- Comfortable seating (chairs, tables, couches)
- WiFi (free, reliable)
- Brand signage (recognizable, premium)
- Music and ambiance (not loud, not silent)

**Product Specification:**
- Espresso-based drinks (standardized recipes)
- Coffee beans (sourced, roasted, consistent)
- Food (tested combinations, quality)
- Beverage sizes (tall, grande, venti — standardized)

**Staff Procedures:**
- Barista training (espresso pulling, customer service, brewing methods)
- Order-taking process (standardized terminology)
- Drink preparation (exact sequences, quality control)
- Customer service (warmth, efficiency, remember names)

**Operational Procedures:**
- Store opening (checklist, equipment startup)
- Inventory management (par levels, reordering)
- Food safety (handling, temperatures, shelf life)
- Cleaning and maintenance (daily, weekly, monthly)
- Cash handling (security, reconciliation)
- Customer feedback (surveys, complaint resolution)

The SDS is *not* just "serve good coffee." It's a detailed, documented, trainable system for consistent delivery.

Why is this important for franchising?

Because the franchisee needs to learn it quickly. A Starbucks franchisee doesn't invent coffee service. They learn Starbucks' proven SDS. They execute it. That's why consistency can be maintained across thousands of locations.

[ASK STUDENTS: Why do you think McDonald's hamburgers taste the same in Seoul and in New York? What system ensures that?]

[PAUSE for student responses]

Exactly. The SDS. McDonald's has documented every detail of burger preparation. Temperature, timing, ingredients, portion size, assembly sequence. When you standardize at that level, consistency follows.

The Korean convenience store industry has an excellent SDS. CU's SDS includes:
- Store layout (all CUs look similar, products in same locations)
- Point-of-sale system (unified ordering, inventory tracking)
- Supply chain (CU controls sourcing, delivery, restocking)
- 24-hour operation procedures
- Product range standardization

This allowed CU to scale to 13,000+ locations. Each franchisee doesn't reinvent convenience retail. Each franchisee executes CU's proven SDS.

### Segment 3: Components Deep-Dive — Transaction, Financial, Monitoring (13 minutes)

[SHOW SLIDE: "Transaction Analysis, Financial Model, Monitoring"]

Now let's examine the three components that make franchising economically work.

**Component 4: Transaction Analysis — In Depth**

Transaction analysis is the *assignment of responsibilities*. It answers: Who invests in what? Who decides what? Who bears what risk?

Let's be very specific with examples.

**McDonald's Transaction Analysis:**

| Activity | Franchisor | Franchisee |
|----------|-----------|-----------|
| Site Selection | ✓ (chooses) | (occupies) |
| Site Acquisition | (approves) | ✓ (leases/buys) |
| Building Construction | (approves design) | ✓ (builds) |
| Equipment Purchase | (specifies) | ✓ (buys approved) |
| Initial Training | ✓ (provides) | ✓ (franchisee attends) |
| Menu Setting | ✓ (decides) | (executes) |
| Daily Operations | (oversees) | ✓ (manages) |
| Staff Hiring | (approves criteria) | ✓ (hires) |
| Pricing | ✓ (sets standard) | ✓ (may vary locally) |
| Marketing (National) | ✓ (funds) | (benefits) |
| Marketing (Local) | (suggests) | ✓ (funds) |

This clear assignment prevents conflict. The franchisee knows: "I will build and operate the restaurant, hire staff, manage daily operations. McDonald's will choose the site, approve the design, set the menu."

**CU Convenience Store Transaction Analysis:**

| Activity | Franchisor | Franchisee |
|----------|-----------|-----------|
| Location Selection | ✓ (major input) | ✓ (must accept) |
| Lease Negotiation | ✓ (often involved) | (manages) |
| Store Design | ✓ (standardized) | (implements) |
| POS System | ✓ (provides/maintains) | (operates) |
| Inventory Sourcing | ✓ (contracts) | (orders, receives) |
| Daily Stocking | (supplies) | ✓ (receives, shelves) |
| Pricing | ✓ (sets) | (executes) |
| Staff Hiring | (approves training) | ✓ (hires) |
| Customer Service | (standard setting) | ✓ (executes) |
| Complaints | (some escalated) | ✓ (handles day-to-day) |

Notice that CU is more hands-on in location selection. This is because convenience store success is 50% about location. CU wants to optimize this.

Clear transaction analysis means both parties know the expectations. It's not: "CU will help me succeed." It's specifically: "CU chooses the location. I staff it and manage daily operations."

**Component 5: Financial Model — In Depth**

The financial model is the set of financial arrangements between franchisor and franchisee.

**Starbucks Licensed Store Model (2025 data):**

Initial Investment: $400,000-$700,000
- Lease deposits
- Equipment and furniture
- POS systems
- Initial inventory
- Working capital

Ongoing Monthly Costs (for $50,000/month in revenue):
- Rent: $3,000-$8,000
- Royalties: 5% of revenue = $2,500
- Marketing contribution: 1% of revenue = $500
- Technology fees: $500
- Labor: $15,000-$20,000
- Supplies/COGS: $20,000
- Utilities, insurance, other: $3,000
- **Total Monthly: ~$45,000**
- **Monthly Profit: ~$5,000**

**Annual profit: ~$60,000 on $700,000 investment = 8.6% return**

Is this good? Depends on your expectations. On a $700K investment:
- Stock market returns: ~10% (historically)
- Real estate: ~5-8% rent return (excluding appreciation)
- Small business: highly variable, but averages 10-20%

Starbucks' 8.6% return is moderate. The advantage: lower risk (proven system) vs. starting independently.

**CU Convenience Store Model (Korea, 2025 data):**

Initial Investment: 60-80 million won ($45,000-$60,000 USD)
- Franchise fee: 50-60M won
- Equipment, POS, initial stock: 10-20M won

Ongoing Monthly Costs (for 20 million won in revenue):
- Rent: 1-2M won
- Royalties/Fees: 2-3M won (varies by franchisor)
- Supplies/COGS: ~8-10M won
- Labor: 3-5M won (24-hour operation requires multiple staff)
- Utilities, other: 1-2M won
- **Total Monthly: ~16-17M won**
- **Monthly Profit: ~3-4M won**

**Annual profit: 36-48M won on 70M won investment = 50-70% return**

Wow! This is much higher than Starbucks. Why?

- Lower initial investment (Korean labor cheaper at time of analysis)
- Higher markup on convenience store products (100%+)
- Efficient supply chain (CU controls everything)
- But also higher risk (declining market, labor cost inflation)

The financial model must work for franchisee profitability, or franchisees won't invest. But if franchisees are too profitable, the franchisor gets too little (not sustainable for support).

The franchisor's profit comes from:
- Initial franchise fee (one-time, upfront)
- Ongoing royalties (recurring, scales with franchisee success)
- Marketing fees (recurring)
- Sometimes: volume purchasing margins (franchisor buys in bulk, resells to franchisees)

The best franchise systems achieve **aligned incentives**: Both parties succeed together.

**Component 6: Monitoring & Adaptation — In Depth**

Monitoring is how the franchisor ensures franchisees follow the system. Adaptation is how the system evolves.

**Monitoring mechanisms:**

1. **Inspections**: Regular (monthly, quarterly) visits to franchisee locations
   - Example: Starbucks coffee quality checks, McDonald's restaurant cleanliness audits, CU facility inspections

2. **Financial audits**: Review of franchisee revenue and costs
   - Example: Verify royalties are calculated correctly, check sales data from POS systems

3. **Mystery shoppers**: Undercover customers who evaluate the experience
   - Example: Starbucks mystery shoppers rate barista warmth, drink quality, store ambiance

4. **Customer feedback**: Surveys, online reviews, complaint monitoring
   - Example: McDonald's tracks customer satisfaction scores by location

5. **Compliance training**: Ongoing training to reinforce standards
   - Example: CU provides monthly training updates to franchisees on new products, procedures

6. **Performance metrics**: KPIs (Key Performance Indicators) that franchisees must meet
   - Example: Sales per square foot, customer satisfaction scores, food cost percentages

Why is monitoring critical?

Because without it, franchisees cut corners. A franchisee might:
- Use cheaper ingredients (damaging brand quality)
- Reduce staff (poor customer service)
- Defer maintenance (brand deteriorates)
- Cut training (inconsistent execution)

If even 10% of franchisees cut corners, the brand suffers. Customers lose confidence. The franchise system fails.

**Adaptation mechanisms:**

Systems must evolve as markets change.

Starbucks adaptation (2010-2025):
- Added mobile ordering (responding to smartphone era)
- Expanded food offerings (responding to consumer demand)
- Introduced delivery (responding to changing consumption patterns)
- Updated sustainability practices (responding to environmental consciousness)

McDonald's adaptation (2000-2025):
- Added healthier options (responding to obesity awareness)
- Adapted menus by country (responding to local preferences)
- Implemented kiosks and mobile ordering (responding to labor costs)
- Expanded breakfast and all-day dining (responding to consumer needs)

CU adaptation in Korea (2010-2025):
- Expanded fresh food offerings (responding to health trends)
- Increased alcohol selection (responding to demographics)
- Added delivery partnerships (responding to convenience demands)
- Updated technology for faster self-checkout (responding to labor cost inflation)

Without adaptation, franchises become obsolete. With constant adaptation, franchises stay relevant.

### Segment 4: Korean Franchise Application — CU Case Study (8 minutes)

[SHOW SLIDE: "How CU Uses the Franchise Relationship Model"]

Let me apply the entire FRM to a Korean franchise: CU.

**Component 1: Customer Definition**

CU's customer: Urban Korean consumers who want late-night access to everyday necessities. Not specialty products. Not premium experiences. Basic products (drinks, snacks, cigarettes, lottery tickets) available 24/7 at nearby locations.

**Component 2: Opportunity Recognition**

CU's opportunity was recognized in the 1990s. Why? Korean urbanization, long work hours, expensive real estate (limiting supermarket growth), and demand for convenience.

CU started franchising aggressively in the 2000s. By the 2020s, it had become Korea's largest convenience store chain.

**Component 3: Service Delivery System (SDS)**

CU's SDS is highly standardized:
- Store layout: Compact (30-40 square meters), products in standard positions
- POS system: Unified across all locations, owned by CU
- Supply chain: CU contracts with suppliers, franchisees don't negotiate
- Operating hours: 24/7 (unlike some competitors)
- Product mix: Standardized (food, beverages, basic household items)
- Staff procedures: Specific training on register, customer service, stocking

This SDS allowed CU to scale rapidly. Each franchisee doesn't invent convenience retail. They execute CU's proven system.

**Component 4: Transaction Analysis**

CU's transaction analysis:

| Activity | Franchisor (CU) | Franchisee |
|----------|-----------|-----------|
| Location Selection | ✓✓ (critical) | (accepts) |
| Lease Terms | ✓ (often negotiates) | (manages lease) |
| Store Build-Out | (approves) | ✓ (pays for) |
| Equipment | ✓ (specifies, some provides) | ✓ (installs) |
| POS System | ✓ (owns, maintains) | (uses) |
| Supplier Contracts | ✓ (negotiates) | (orders) |
| Pricing | ✓ (sets) | (executes) |
| Staff Hiring | (trains) | ✓ (hires) |
| Daily Operations | (oversees) | ✓ (manages) |

CU is very hands-on in location selection. This is a key differentiator.

**Component 5: Financial Model**

CU Franchisee (2025 data):

Initial Investment: 60-80 million won
Ongoing Monthly (20M won revenue):
- Monthly profit: 3-4 million won
- Annual profit: 36-48 million won
- ROI: 50-70%

CU's profit sources:
- Franchise fee: 50-60M won per franchisee (one-time)
- Royalties: 2-3M won per franchisee per month (recurring)
- With 13,000 franchisees: Enormous aggregate revenue

The financial model has worked well for CU. But franchisees are increasingly squeezed (labor costs, rent costs, market saturation).

**Component 6: Monitoring & Adaptation**

CU Monitoring:
- Store inspections (quality, cleanliness, compliance)
- POS data monitoring (sales trends, inventory levels)
- Franchisee training programs (regular updates)
- Customer feedback integration

CU Adaptation (2010-2025):
- Expanded fresh food offerings (gimbap, sandwiches, prepared foods)
- Added fresh coffee service (responding to coffee culture boom)
- Implemented self-checkout kiosks (reducing labor needs)
- Expanded delivery partnerships
- Updated mobile app for ordering and payment

**The CU Success Story:**

CU succeeded because:
1. **Clear customer definition** → understood urban Korean needs
2. **Perfect opportunity timing** → Korean urbanization, 24-hour culture
3. **Strong SDS** → highly standardized, teachable, scalable
4. **Clear transaction analysis** → franchisees knew their role
5. **Attractive financial model** → franchisees could profit 50-70% ROI
6. **Effective monitoring** → quality maintained across 13,000 locations
7. **Continuous adaptation** → evolved as market changed

The FRM in action.

But here's the question for today: **Is CU in 2025 still following the FRM well?**

Some challenges:
- Market saturation (opportunity component weakening)
- Franchisee profitability declining (financial model component weakening)
- Franchisees demanding more support and fairness (monitoring component questioned)
- Labor cost inflation (SDS execution costs rising)

We'll discuss this more next session.

### Summary & Q&A (4 minutes)

The Franchise Relationship Model is the master framework. It has six components:

1. **Customer Definition**: Who is the customer? What do they want?
2. **Opportunity Recognition**: Why does this opportunity exist now?
3. **Service Delivery System**: How is value delivered consistently?
4. **Transaction Analysis**: Who does what?
5. **Financial Model**: How do both parties profit?
6. **Monitoring & Adaptation**: How is compliance ensured? How does the system evolve?

All six components must be strong for a franchise to succeed. If any one is weak, the system fails.

CU is an excellent example of the FRM in action—and also a case where components are weakening as the market matures.

Next session, we'll explore the franchisor-franchisee relationship more deeply. We'll ask: How do they create value together? What can go wrong?

- CU is an excellent example
- But no franchise is static; components weaken and must be renewed

## SECTION 4: KEY NOTES & IMPROVISATION TIPS

### Core Concepts to Emphasize

1. **The FRM is Systemic** — All six components work together. You can't have a strong SDS without clear customer definition. You can't have good monitoring without clear transaction analysis.

2. **The FRM Reveals Why Franchises Succeed or Fail** — If you see a struggling franchise, ask: Which FRM component is weak? Customer definition? (Nobody wants the product) Opportunity? (Market is saturated) SDS? (System isn't teachable) Transaction? (Unclear who does what) Financial? (Franchisees can't profit) Monitoring? (Standards are slipping)

3. **CU is an Excellent Real-World Example** — Korean students will know CU well. Ask them where they shop, what they buy, why. Use this familiarity to make the FRM concrete.

4. **Components Must Evolve** — The FRM isn't static. As markets mature, components must be refreshed. CU's opportunity component (market growth) is weaker in 2025 than in 2010. This requires adaptation.

### Common Student Misconceptions to Correct

1. **"A franchise is just a business I can operate."**
   - Correct: A franchise is a system designed at the FRM level. You're executing that system, not inventing.

2. **"The franchisor will help me succeed."**
   - Correct: The FRM specifies exactly what the franchisor does. Usually less support than franchisees hope for. Check the transaction analysis.

3. **"The financial model is just about royalties."**
   - Correct: The financial model includes all revenue sources, all costs, and must align incentives.

4. **"CU is successful, so it must be doing the FRM perfectly."**
   - Correct: CU was successful, but components are weakening. Success is not permanent.

### Classroom Discussion Prompts

1. "For a business you know well (Starbucks, CU, Nene Chicken), can you identify the six FRM components? What's the customer? What's the opportunity?"

2. "Which FRM component do you think is most important? Why?"

3. "For CU, which component is weakening most in 2024-2025? What should CU do?"

4. "Can you think of a franchise that has weak monitoring? What happens?"

5. "How would you know if a franchise system's SDS is strong? How would you test it?"

### Real-World Examples to Emphasize

- **Starbucks FRM**: Perfect customer definition (third place), opportunity perfectly timed, SDS highly detailed and teachable, monitoring rigorous
- **McDonald's FRM**: Clear customer (families), proven across 80+ countries, monitoring is legendary (consistency across globe)
- **CU FRM**: Excellent in 2010, but customer demand saturating, opportunity weakening, financial model strained by labor costs

### Improvisation Tips for Live Teaching

1. **If students seem confused about the FRM complexity:** Simplify with a single example (Starbucks). Walk through each component one by one. Make it concrete.

2. **If students are very interested in CU:** Discuss current (2025) challenges. Look up recent news about CU franchisee associations, labor disputes, market saturation. Make it contemporary.

3. **If students are skeptical about monitoring:** Give them specific examples of what happens without it (franchisees cut corners, quality declines, brand suffers). Ask them for brands they've had bad experiences with due to franchisee quality failures.

4. **If time is running short:** Focus on the three big components: Customer/Opportunity/SDS (market definition), Transaction Analysis (who does what), and Financial Model (profit sharing). These are the most important.

### Pre-Class Preparation

- Prepare a one-page diagram of the six FRM components. Make it visual.
- Research one current CU news story (2025). Have it ready to discuss.
- Have specific financial data (Korean minimum wage, CU franchisee margins, etc.) ready.
- Prepare 2-3 competitor examples (GS25, Emart convenience) to compare to CU.

### Post-Class Follow-up

- Assign: "Choose a franchise you know (Korean or international). Map the six FRM components. Identify any weak components. Propose improvements."
- This deepens understanding of the framework.
