### Opening (3 minutes)

Good morning! Welcome to Week 12, Session 1.

We've spent 11 weeks understanding franchising operationally and financially. Now we turn to the legal framework that governs franchising.

**Critical principle:** Franchising is a highly regulated business. Different countries regulate differently.

Today: U.S. framework. Tomorrow: Korean framework. Session 3: Comparison and implications.

### Main Content (32 minutes)

**Segment 1: Why Franchising is Regulated (7 minutes)**

Franchising creates an inherent power imbalance:
- **Franchisor** controls brand, system, training, support
- **Franchisee** invests capital, takes on debt, operates the business

Without legal protection, franchisors can exploit franchisees:
- Exaggerated profit claims
- Hidden costs
- Inadequate support
- Unfair contract terms
- Termination without cause

**Historical Context (1970s):** 
This regulatory framework wasn't created in a vacuum. Before the FTC Rule, franchising was essentially the "Wild West." 

The 1970s saw major franchise scandals. Companies like groundless oil-change franchises promised unrealistic earnings ($300K/year from a 600-sq-ft location). Franchisees would mortgage their homes, invest life savings, and within 18 months, they'd discover:
1. Customer base was overestimated
2. Operating costs were double the franchisor's projections
3. The franchisor provided minimal support
4. Franchise agreement had no termination protections

By 1976, the FTC documented over 20,000 bankruptcy complaints from franchisees. Congress held hearings. Franchisees testified about losing everything. The result: The Franchise Rule (1978).

**Key Principle:** Franchise law aims to ensure full disclosure and fair dealing. The philosophy: **Franchisees have the right to make informed decisions before investing their life savings.**

**Segment 2: The FTC Franchise Rule (8 minutes)**

**What is it?**
The FTC (Federal Trade Commission) Franchise Rule (16 CFR Part 436), first enacted in 1978 and substantially updated in 2007 and most recently 2023-2024, requires franchisors to disclose material facts to prospective franchisees **before they make any financial commitment.**

**The Core Philosophy:** No surprises. Franchisees deserve to know exactly what they're getting into before signing.

**Key Requirements:**

**1. The Franchise Disclosure Document (FDD) - Central Requirement**
- **Timing:** Must be provided **at least 14 days** before franchise agreement signing or **any** initial payment (franchise fee, deposit, equipment purchase)
- **Form:** Written document (can be electronic)
- **Content:** 23 required items covering all material information
- **Updates:** Must be updated **within 90 days** of any material change
- **Liability:** FDD errors create franchisor liability (even for inadvertent mistakes)

The 14-day rule is critical. It's designed to prevent high-pressure sales tactics where franchisees sign during a sales presentation.

**2. The 23 Required Disclosures (Item 1 through Item 23 of FDD):**

**Items 1-4: Background & Leadership Information**
1. **Corporate structure and business history** — Legal entity type, prior business names, operating history (minimum 2 years)
2. **Business experience of executives** — Names, titles, and detailed 5-year work history of franchisor's principal officers
3. **Litigation history of franchisor and executives** — Any legal actions related to franchising, securities, fraud (last 10 years)
4. **Bankruptcy history** — Any bankruptcy filings by franchisor or executives (last 10 years). Critical disclosure.

**Items 5-8: Financial Obligations**
5. **Initial and ongoing fees** — Franchise fee, royalties, advertising fees, technology fees, renewal fees. Must specify amounts and timing.
6. **Financing arrangements** — Does franchisor finance any franchise costs? Terms? Default consequences?
7. **Mandatory purchases from franchisor** — Must franchisee buy supplies, equipment, or inventory exclusively from franchisor? At what prices?
8. **Financing of franchisee obligations** — Can franchisor or franchisor-approved lender finance the franchisee's purchases?

**Items 9-12: Operational Requirements & System Rules**
9. **Material restrictions on goods/services** — Cannot sell competing products? Geographic restrictions? Quality standards?
10. **Required participation by franchisee** — Can franchisee hire managers to run the store, or must owner actively manage? (Key issue for absentee investors)
11. **Termination, cancellation, renewal terms** — How long is contract? Can it be renewed? What triggers termination? Specific details.
12. **Statistical tables on franchisee status** — How many franchises existed 2-3 years ago? How many have been transferred, terminated, or closed? This is crucial data.

**Items 13-19: Financial Performance & Transparency**
13. **Revenues/income claims data** — If franchisor claims franchisees earn specific income levels, must provide substantiation
14. **Initial investment required** — Total cash needed to start franchise (including inventory, equipment, real estate deposits, training)
15. **Restrictions on sale/transfer** — Can franchisee sell to someone else? Does franchisor have right of first refusal? What approval is needed?
16. **Renewal, termination, or transfer of existing franchises** — Specific terms, notice periods, buyback provisions
17. **Earnings claims** — If franchisor makes income projections, detailed substantiation must be provided. **Most important disclosure for franchisees.**
18. **Financial performance information** — Company-owned store performance data (for comparison to franchisees)
19. **Company-owned locations and franchisee sales data** — Operating history of franchisor-owned units (if any exist)

**Items 20-23: Legal & Operational**
20. **Names and addresses of franchisees** — Current and former franchisees (last 3 years). Franchisees can contact them for references.
21. **Transfer information** — How to contact franchisees who've sold units or left the system
22. **Dispute resolution mechanisms** — Will disputes be arbitrated or litigated? Franchise agreement terms regarding disputes
23. **Compliance history** — Any FTC actions, state regulators, or franchisee class actions against franchisor

**3. Item 19A: Earnings Claims Statements (Most Important)**

This is the section franchisees scrutinize most carefully.

**Rule:** If franchisor makes **any** earnings claim ("franchisees average $200K profit," "you can earn $50K annually," etc.), franchisor **must** include:
- Actual historical earnings data from existing franchisees
- Percentage of franchisees achieving the claimed level
- Detailed disclaimers
- Complete substantiation

**Critical Detail:** Many franchisors avoid earnings claims entirely to avoid FDD scrutiny. If franchisor says "some franchisees have earned $X," courts assume "most franchisees earn at least $X" (conservative interpretation).

**2023-2024 Recent Updates:**
- Digital/remote franchises must disclose cyber security data
- Franchise failure rates must be more clearly stated
- Supply chain requirements must be detailed
- Social media marketing requirements must be disclosed

**4. Registration in Registration States**
- **14 states** require franchise registration (California, New York, Illinois, Indiana, Michigan, Minnesota, Mississippi, Missouri, New Mexico, North Dakota, Oregon, Rhode Island, South Dakota, Virginia, Washington, Wisconsin)
- **Most restrictive:** California (requires state approval before any franchising), New York (detailed financial review), Illinois (similar to California)
- **Process:** Franchisor files FDD with state, pays filing fee ($750-$5,000 depending on state), state reviews for deceptive practices
- **Timeline:** 30-90 days for approval
- **Cost to franchisor:** $15,000-$50,000+ annually if franchising nationally

**Penalties for Non-Compliance (Enforced Aggressively):**
- **FTC Enforcement:** FTC sues franchisor, seeks injunctions, public remedies
- **Private Lawsuits:** Franchisees can sue for FDD violations (even if they didn't read it)
- **Treble Damages:** Franchisee can recover 3x actual damages (intent to deter violations)
- **Class Actions:** Multiple franchisees can sue together (McDonald's, Subway, Chipotle have all faced multi-million dollar class actions)
- **Criminal Liability:** Egregious fraud (making up earnings) can result in criminal prosecution

**Segment 3: State Franchise Laws (8 minutes)**

Beyond federal FTC Rule, **14 registration states** have enacted more protective franchise laws. These states essentially say: "Federal minimum is not enough. We want additional local protections."

**Key State-Level Protections (Beyond Federal):**

**1. Unfair/Wrongful Termination Laws**

**California Model (most protective):**
- Franchisor **cannot terminate without "good cause"**
- "Good cause" = legitimate business reason related to franchisee performance (not just franchisor whim)
- If franchisor wants to terminate, must:
  1. Provide written notice of violation
  2. Give franchisee 30-60 days to cure (fix the problem)
  3. Provide reasonable opportunity to remedy
  4. Only terminate if franchisee fails to fix after notice

- **Example violation of law:** Franchisor terminates franchisee because franchisor wants to open company-owned store in that location. California law forbids this ("opportunistic termination").

**Other State Models:**
- Wisconsin: Requires franchisor to show "good faith" reason for non-renewal
- New York: Good cause requirement for termination
- Michigan: Cannot terminate for joining franchisee association

**Damages:** If franchisee wrongfully terminated, can recover lost profits (can be millions)

**2. Right of First Refusal**

Several states require:
- If franchisee wants to sell/transfer franchise, franchisee must first offer to franchisor
- Franchisor has 30-60 days to match competitor's offer
- If franchisor declines, franchisee can sell to third party
- Franchisor cannot arbitrarily reject transfer (must have legitimate reason)

**Example:** Franchisee wants to sell to another investor. Franchisor cannot say "yes" to competitor's offer at $500K while saying "no" to franchisee's friend at $450K.

**3. Non-Compete Restrictions (State Law Varies)**

Franchisors want to prevent:
- Franchisee opening competing business after franchise ends
- Franchisee soliciting franchisor's customers
- Franchisee using franchisor's trade secrets

States balance this against franchisee's right to work:

**California (most franchisee-friendly):**
- Non-competes are **generally unenforceable**
- Franchisor can only prevent:
  - Use of trade secrets
  - Use of franchisor's confidential information
  - Solicitation of franchisor's customers (within 6 months, reasonable geographic radius)

**Other states:**
- May allow non-competes if "reasonable in scope, duration, and geography"
- Typical: "Cannot operate competing business within 5 miles for 2 years"
- Franchisor burden: Prove non-compete is necessary to protect legitimate business interests

**4. "Franchise Relationship" Laws (Broad Good Faith Requirements)**

States like Wisconsin and Minnesota enacted laws requiring:

**Franchisor obligations:**
- Act in good faith toward franchisee
- Provide promised support and training
- Maintain quality standards
- Provide notice before making material changes to system
- Cannot act deceptively

**Franchisee obligations:**
- Maintain quality standards
- Pay royalties on time
- Report sales accurately
- Comply with franchise agreement

**Remedy:** If franchisor breaches good faith, franchisee can sue for damages. Broader than just termination issues.

**Example:** Franchisor promises "hands-on training and support" but provides minimal support. Franchisee can sue for breach of good faith (even if franchise agreement allows minimal support).

**5. Damages Protection & Financial Remedies**

Registration states often allow:
- **Actual damages:** Compensate franchisee for lost profits, lost business value
- **Punitive damages:** In egregious cases (fraud, willful deception), can award 1-3x actual damages
- **Injunctive relief:** Court order requiring franchisor to continue support, cease unfair practices, etc.
- **Attorneys' fees:** Winning franchisee can recover legal fees in some states

**Cost impact:** A wrongful termination suit can cost franchisor $500K-$2M+ in damages, legal fees, and settlement

**Segment 4: Real Cases - How U.S. Law Protects Franchisees (7 minutes)**

**CASE 1: 2020-2021 Subway FDD Violation (Earnings Claims Class Action)**

**Facts:**
- Subway made earnings claims: "Average franchisee earns $200K-$300K annually"
- In reality, average franchisee earned $35K-$50K
- Subway failed to disclose that 20% of franchisees close within 5 years
- 500+ franchisees joined class action

**Legal Issue:**
- Item 19 (Earnings Claims) was materially misleading
- Subway didn't disclose failure rates in same document as earnings claims
- FDD Item 12 (franchisee status table) showed high failure rates, but franchisees didn't correlate with earnings claims

**Result (2021 Settlement):**
- Subway settled for $100M+ restitution
- Franchisees received $5,000-$25,000 each (depending on damages)
- Subway required to revise FDD with clearer failure rate disclosures
- FTC monitored compliance

**Lesson:** Earnings claims create liability even if technically disclosed elsewhere. Must be prominent and unmistakable.

---

**CASE 2: 2019 California Wrongful Termination - Chicken Franchise**

**Facts:**
- Local chicken franchise operating in Los Angeles for 12 years
- Franchisee maintained high quality, strong sales ($2M/year)
- Franchisor decided to open company-owned store 2 miles away
- Franchisor terminated franchisee "for non-renewal"

**Legal Challenge (California):**
- Franchisee sued for wrongful termination
- California law: Cannot terminate without "good cause"
- Franchisee proved no performance violations (sales strong, quality good)
- Franchisor's reason was "opportunistic termination" (displace franchisee to own the territory)

**Result:**
- California court awarded franchisee **$1.8M in damages** (lost profits for 5-year unexpired franchise term)
- Plus attorneys' fees ($400K)
- Franchisor required to restore franchise or pay buyout
- Case is now used in law schools as example of California protection

**Lesson:** "Good cause" requirement prevents franchisors from exploiting franchisees for company growth

---

**CASE 3: 2022-2023 Supplement Franchise - Hidden Costs**

**Facts:**
- Vitamin supplement franchise claimed "Low startup, $35K-$50K total investment"
- FDD Item 14 (Initial Investment) listed supplies, equipment, training
- **Hidden detail:** All inventory purchased only from franchisor
- Franchisor supplier markup: 50% above wholesale cost
- Franchisees required to maintain $20K inventory at all times
- True startup cost: $60K-$80K (much higher than disclosed)

**Legal Challenge:**
- Franchisees claimed FDD was deceptive (Item 5 fees, Item 14 investment inaccurate)
- Item 7 (mandatory purchases) inadequately disclosed franchisor markup
- FTC found violation: Material costs not clearly stated

**Result:**
- Settlement: $8M restitution fund
- Franchisees received refunds for inventory markups
- Franchisor required to disclose all supplier markups in Item 7
- Annual FDD audits by independent accountant

**Lesson:** All financial obligations must be transparent, including hidden supply chain markup

---

**CASE 4: 2023-2024 Chipotle Master Franchisee Dispute (Multi-Level Franchising)**

**Facts:**
- Chipotle used master franchisees in international markets
- Master franchisee in UK promised to open 50 locations over 10 years
- After 5 years, only 12 locations opened
- Chipotle claimed breach of development agreement
- Master franchisee claimed insufficient franchisor support

**Legal Issue:**
- Master franchisee agreement didn't clearly specify:
  - Support obligations
  - Performance requirements
  - Termination conditions for underperformance

**Result:**
- Case settled with revised master franchisee agreement
- Clear milestones: 10 locations by Year 2, 25 by Year 5, 50 by Year 10
- Franchisor committed to dedicated support team
- Termination triggers for underperformance clearly specified

**Lesson:** Even large systems need crystal-clear franchise agreements with defined expectations

---

**CASE 5: 2024 Tech Franchise - FDD Timing Violation**

**Facts:**
- Tech support franchise collected $500 technology fee from prospective franchisee
- Never provided FDD (claimed franchisee waived it)
- Franchisee discovered FDD listed 10 class actions against franchisor

**Legal Issue:**
- FTC Rule § 436.1: FDD must be provided **14 days before any payment**
- Technology fee was "initial payment" triggering 14-day rule
- Franchisor violated by collecting before FDD delivery

**Result (2024):**
- FTC enforcement action against franchisor
- Franchisor fined $1M for repeated violations
- Required to notify all franchisees of FDD omission
- Franchisees offered contract cancellation or refund

**Lesson:** The 14-day rule is absolute. Any payment (even small fees) triggers it

### Examples & Case Studies & Current Trend Analysis

**2024-2026 Emerging FTC Enforcement Trends:**

**1. Digital Franchise Fraud (Newest Focus Area)**

The FTC is aggressively targeting digital/remote franchises with false claims:

**Example: AI Chatbot Franchise (2024 FTC Action)**
- Franchisor claimed: "Earn $10K/month passively with AI chatbot"
- Reality: Required 20+ hours/week of customer service, heavy sales work
- FTC findings: Deceptive earning claims, inadequate disclosure of work required
- Action: $2M settlement, required refunds to 2,000+ franchisees
- Takeaway: "Passive income" claims in tech franchises now heavily scrutinized

**2. Supply Chain & ESG Disclosures (2023-2024 Development)**

Recent FTC guidance requires franchisors to disclose:
- Sustainability practices (carbon, waste)
- Labor practices of suppliers
- Conflict minerals/ethical sourcing
- Data privacy/cybersecurity requirements for franchisees

**Example: Coffee Franchise (2024)**
- FDD now requires disclosure: "We purchase beans only from certified fair-trade suppliers"
- Franchisee required to follow ESG standards
- FTC considers ESG non-compliance as material franchise issue

**3. Multi-Level Franchising Complexity (Increasing Litigation)**

Master franchisee agreements creating disputes:

**Example: Food Franchise Master Franchisee Termination (2023)**
- Master franchisee signed 10-year agreement
- Franchisor terminated after 3 years (for underperformance)
- Master franchisee argued: "FDD Item 11 didn't specify performance metrics"
- Settlement: Master franchisee received $5M buyout, clearer agreements going forward

**Takeaway:** Franchisor obligations must be crystal-clear in all franchise agreements

---

**Why This Matters to Korean Franchises Expanding to U.S.:**

If you're a Korean franchisor expanding to the U.S., FTC compliance is non-negotiable:

1. **Earnings Claims:** Korean franchisors often claim optimistic earnings. U.S. law is much stricter. Must have hard data.
2. **FDD Complexity:** 23 items must be carefully disclosed. Hiring FTC legal counsel ($50K-$200K) is standard.
3. **Registration States:** If expanding to California, New York, Illinois, must file with state regulators (6-month process).
4. **Litigation Risk:** High. U.S. franchisees sue frequently. Budget $100K-$500K for potential litigation.
5. **Damages Exposure:** Treble damages make U.S. violations expensive. Korean law doesn't have treble damages.

**Concrete Example:** BBQ Chicken expanding to U.S.

BBQ Chicken is a successful Korean franchise. If expanding to California:
1. Must create comprehensive FDD (23 items, 100+ pages)
2. Must register with California (3-month process)
3. Must substantiate any earnings claims with actual data
4. Must disclose Korean legal structure, litigation history, bankruptcy history
5. Must clearly state: All items in Korean, translated, subject to California law

Cost: $100K-$300K in legal + compliance first year. Ongoing audits and updates: $30K-$50K/year.

---

**U.S. Franchisee Protections Summary (Comparative View):**

**Layers of Protection:**

1. **Federal (FTC Rule):** Mandatory disclosure (FDD), 14-day rule, strict Item 19 requirements
2. **State Registration:** Additional scrutiny in 14 states (CA, NY, IL, etc.)
3. **State Relationship Laws:** Good faith requirements (WI, MN)
4. **State Termination Laws:** Good cause requirement (CA, NY, etc.)
5. **Private Right of Action:** Franchisees can sue for damages, class actions
6. **Damages Mechanisms:** Treble damages, attorneys' fees, injunctions

**Impact:** Franchisors must be extremely careful. One misleading statement in FDD can trigger multi-million dollar lawsuit.

## SECTION 4: KEY NOTES & TEACHING TIPS

**Major Concepts to Emphasize:**

- **U.S. has the world's most comprehensive franchisee protections**: Federal (FTC) + state (14 registration states) + private litigation + damages mechanisms. No other country has this multi-layer protection.

- **The 23 Disclosures (FDD Items 1-23) are the heart of the rule**: Must master these if you plan to franchise in U.S. Item 19 (earnings) is most litigated.

- **Disclosure timing (14 days) is absolute**: Any payment before 14-day delivery = FTC violation. Many franchisors make this mistake with tech fees, deposits.

- **Private right of action creates accountability**: Franchisees can sue, creating real deterrent against fraud. FTC enforcement alone wouldn't be sufficient.

- **Treble damages (3x) make U.S. franchising expensive**: One misleading statement can cost millions. Compare to Korea (actual damages only, no treble multiple).

- **Registration states (14) require additional state approval**: If franchising in California, add 3-6 months and $20K-$50K for state registration.

- **Recent cases (2023-2024) show aggressive FTC enforcement**: Digital franchises, supply chain issues, ESG disclosures are new focus areas. Franchisors must stay current.

**Classroom Discussion Questions:**

1. Why do you think the U.S. created "treble damages" (3x) for franchise violations? What's the economic incentive?
2. If you were a franchisee, which disclosure (Items 1-23) would be most important to you? Why?
3. Why do you think 14 states have "registration" requirements while others don't?
4. Korean franchisors often make aggressive earnings claims. What happens if they try this in California?

**Real-World Application:**

Ask students: "Imagine you're considering a franchise investment in the U.S. You receive an FDD. What would you look for? What questions would you ask?"

Student answer should include: Check Item 19 (earnings), Item 12 (failure rates), Item 11 (termination terms), Item 20 (contact other franchisees).

This is exactly what successful franchisees do before signing.

**Comparison Preview:**

End session by asking: "Now we know U.S. law. Tomorrow we'll see Korean law. Which is more protective?" 

Answer: U.S. has more layers, more powerful damages mechanisms. But Korea's "good cause" requirement is actually stronger in some ways than U.S. state laws.
