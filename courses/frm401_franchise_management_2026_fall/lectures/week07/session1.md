## SECTION 2: FULL LECTURE SCRIPT (English)

### Opening (3 minutes)

Good morning! Welcome to Week 7.

In Week 5, we explored the fundamentals of SDS. Today, we address a critical challenge: **How do you maintain consistency while adapting to local markets?**

This is the SDS customization paradox. Global franchises like McDonald's, Starbucks, and Subway face this daily. Korean franchises expanding internationally face this too.

Today's question: **Which elements must be standardized globally, and which can be localized?**

The answer determines franchise success in new markets.

### Main Content (32 minutes)

**Segment 1: The Standardization vs. Localization Paradox (8 minutes)**

Here's the fundamental tension:

Global brands want consistency—same experience everywhere. This is the SDS promise. McDonald's in Seoul should taste like McDonald's in New York.

But local markets want customization. Korean customers have different tastes than American customers. If you don't adapt, you'll fail in local markets.

**The paradox:** How do you maintain consistency while adapting?

Answer: Distinguish between **core systems** (must be standardized globally) and **content** (can be localized).

**Core Systems (Must be Standardized Globally):**
- Processes and procedures
- Quality control methods
- Equipment and technology
- Training curricula
- Brand visual identity
- Customer service principles

**Content (Can be Localized):**
- Menu items (add local flavors)
- Product offerings (add local products)
- Pricing (adjust for local market)
- Store design (cultural elements)
- Marketing messages (local language, cultural context)

Example: McDonald's hamburger is standardized globally—same process, same beef quality. But menus are localized—bulgogi burgers in Korea, teriyaki in Japan, poutine in Canada.

**Segment 2: McDonald's Global SDS with Local Menus (8 minutes)**

McDonald's is the master of this balance.

**Global Standards (Cannot Change):**
- Burger cooking temperature (160°F internal temperature—cannot vary)
- Beef quality (USDA graded or equivalent)
- Frying oil temperature (350°F for fries)
- Cleaning standards
- Service speed targets (order to delivery in 2 minutes)
- Restaurant design principles (clean, bright, family-friendly)

**Localized Content:**
- Menu: Bulgogi burgers in Korea, curry items in India, McSpicy in Asia
- Pricing: $8 in US, 5,900 won in Korea (adjusted for local economy)
- Store design: Cultural symbols and colors adapted
- Marketing: Local holidays and cultural references

**How It Works:**
McDonald's provides the SYSTEM (process, equipment, training). Franchisees provide the CONTENT (menu execution, local marketing).

**Result:** McDonald's maintains consistent quality globally while respecting local preferences. Both sides win.

**Segment 3: Korean Brands Going Global—The Challenge (8 minutes)**

Korean chicken franchises (BBQ, Nene Chicken) are expanding globally. This is harder because their core product is culturally specific.

**Challenge 1: Product Authenticity**
Korean chicken recipes are distinctive (spicy sauces, specific marinades). These are harder to adapt than McDonald's hamburgers.

If you localize too much (make it less spicy for Western palates), you lose authenticity. Korean customers want authentic Korean chicken.

If you don't localize (keep it very spicy), Western customers won't buy it.

**Challenge 2: Brand Identity**
Korean chicken franchises are built on Korean identity. How do you maintain this while adapting?

**Challenge 3: Consistency in Global Operations**
If franchisees are changing recipes locally, how do you maintain the "Korean chicken" brand globally?

**Case Example: BBQ's Global Strategy**
BBQ has opened locations in the US, China, Vietnam. Their approach:

- **Core:** Korean chicken marinating process, signature sauces
- **Adapted:** Portion sizes (Americans eat larger portions), side dish selections
- **Challenge:** Western demand for milder spice levels

BBQ's solution: Offer multiple spice levels at same location (both authentic Korean and adapted versions). This lets customers choose.

**Segment 4: When to Standardize, When to Localize (8 minutes)**

Decision framework for SDS customization:

**Standardize these (No exceptions):**
1. Food safety procedures
2. Quality control specifications
3. Customer health standards
4. Equipment specifications that enable consistency
5. Core brand visual elements

**Can Localize these (Within guardrails):**
1. Menu items and offerings
2. Pricing (adjust for local purchasing power)
3. Store design (add local cultural elements)
4. Marketing messages (local languages, cultural contexts)
5. Operating hours (adapt to local customs)
6. Staffing approaches (local labor practices)

**Example Decision Tree:**
- Question 1: "Is this a food safety issue?" → If YES: Standardize globally
- Question 2: "Does this affect brand identity?" → If YES: May need to standardize
- Question 3: "Does this affect customer experience negatively if changed?" → If YES: Consider standardization
- If all are NO: Localize

### Examples & Case Studies (13 minutes)

**Case 1: Starbucks Global SDS with Localization**

**Global SDS (Non-negotiable):**
- Espresso machine calibration
- Milk steaming technique (175°F)
- Coffee bean quality standards
- Barista training (25 hours minimum)

**Localized (Varies by Country):**
- Menu: Red bean paste frappuccinos in Korea, sakura-flavored drinks in Japan
- Store design: Korean Starbucks often includes traditional aesthetic elements
- Pricing: 4,500 won for tall latte in Korea vs. $4.50 in US
- Marketing: Korean Starbucks focuses on gift-giving and meeting culture

Result: Starbucks is global with local character. Korean customers feel the brand respects Korean culture while maintaining coffee quality.

**Case 2: Lotteria Global Approach**

Lotteria (Lotte's fast food franchise) has locations across Asia.

**Core Standards:**
- Burger cooking process
- Quality ingredients
- Service speed

**Localized:**
- Menu: Burgers with bulgogi in Korea, rice burgers in some Asian countries
- Pricing: Adjusts per country
- Store design: Varies by location

Challenge: Lotteria lacks the global dominance to maintain pricing premium like McDonald's. In each market, local competitors undercut on price.

### 주요 내용 (32분)

**세그먼트 1: 표준화 vs. 지역화 역설 (8분)**

기본적인 긴장이 있습니다:

글로벌 브랜드는 일관성을 원합니다. 모든 곳에서 동일한 경험. 이것은 SDS 약속입니다.

그러나 지역 시장은 커스터마이제이션을 원합니다. 한국 고객은 미국 고객과 다른 취향을 가지고 있습니다.

**역설:** 당신은 일관성을 유지하면서 적응하는 방법?

답: **핵심 시스템** (글로벌로 표준화해야 함)과 **콘텐츠** (지역화 가능)를 구분합니다.

**핵심 시스템 (글로벌로 표준화해야 함):**
- 프로세스 및 절차
- 품질 통제 방법
- 장비 및 기술
- 교육 커리큘럼
- 브랜드 시각적 정체성
- 고객 서비스 원칙

**콘텐츠 (지역화 가능):**
- 메뉴 항목 (지역 맛 추가)
- 제품 오퍼링 (지역 제품 추가)
- 가격 책정 (지역 시장 조정)
- 매장 디자인 (문화적 요소)
- 마케팅 메시지 (지역 언어, 문화 맥락)

예: McDonald's 햄버거는 글로벌로 표준화되어 있습니다. 하지만 메뉴는 지역화되어 있습니다.
