### Main Content (32 minutes)

**Segment 1: Problem Set 1 — Applying Mechanisms to a New Scenario (8 minutes)**

Imagine a Korean cosmetics brand called "Beauty Seoul" wants to expand its retail presence through franchising. Currently, it has 50 company-owned stores in major malls. The CEO wants to grow to 500 stores in the next 3 years.

They're considering a franchise model with these terms:
- Initial franchise fee: 80 million won
- Royalty: 6% of monthly sales
- Franchisor responsibility: Supply all products, marketing support, staff training

Let me ask you: **Analyze this franchise model using the four wealth creation mechanisms.**

Here's my analysis:

**Mechanism 1 — Rapid Capital Growth:**
The franchisor (Beauty Seoul) needs to invest 400 million won to grow organically (80 million per store). Through franchising, they collect 80 million per store from franchisees. They avoid capital investment and gain instant capital from initial fees. This mechanism is strong—Beauty Seoul accelerates growth without debt.

BUT: Is 3 years realistic for 450 new stores? That's 150 stores per year, or about 3 new stores per week. This is aggressive.

**Mechanism 2 — Risk Distribution:**
Franchisees invest in store buildout (typically 200-300 million won per location in a mall). Beauty Seoul invests only in inventory supply. Franchisees bear location and operational risk. This seems fair—each party controls what they can.

**Mechanism 3 — Systems Replication:**
This is where I see a potential weakness. What system does Beauty Seoul have that's unique and replicable? If the system is just "sell cosmetics," that's weak. If the system includes proprietary displays, staff training, inventory management, and customer loyalty programs specific to Beauty Seoul, that's strong.

The makeup of cosmetics is highly replicable in Korea. Many competitors exist. Without a defensible system, this franchise could struggle.

**Mechanism 4 — Brand Building:**
Beauty Seoul has some brand recognition in Korea's cosmetics market, but it's not dominant like Amorepacific brands. Franchising could accelerate brand recognition if done well, but it's risky. Poorly executed stores in inferior locations could damage the brand.

**Overall Assessment:**
The model has potential, but I have concerns:
- Mechanism 1 works, but the 3-year timeline is aggressive
- Mechanism 2 is fair
- Mechanism 3 (systems) is potentially weak
- Mechanism 4 (brand) is at risk if locations aren't carefully selected

**My Recommendation:** This franchise could work IF:
1. Beauty Seoul is very selective about franchisee locations (not oversaturate)
2. They invest heavily in training and ongoing support (offset weak systems)
3. They monitor franchisee profitability closely and adjust royalties if needed
4. They maintain strong brand identity across all locations

**Segment 2: Problem Set 2 — Red Flag Identification (8 minutes)**

Now let's look at a real problem. A Korean delivery-based food franchise called "Quick Meal Korea" is offering franchises. Here's their pitch:

"Quick Meal Korea has 200 locations and is expanding to 1,000 locations in 2 years. Join our network! Initial franchise fee is only 30 million won (the cheapest in the market). You operate from a small kitchen; we provide all recipes and logistics. Projected monthly revenue: 30 million won. Projected profit: 8 million won per month."

The franchise requires franchisees to buy food from their central commissary at 40% markup compared to local suppliers.

Let me ask: **Identify the red flags in this franchise model.**

Here are the red flags I see:

**Red Flag #1 — Emphasis on Low Upfront Fee:**
The franchise fee is artificially low at 30 million won. Why? Because the real money is in the markup on supplies (40% above market). The franchisor isn't incentivized to ensure franchisee profitability; they make money from supply markups.

**Red Flag #2 — Aggressive Growth Timeline:**
200 to 1,000 locations in 2 years? That's 400 new franchises per year. This is unsustainable growth—similar to the cafe franchises that collapsed. The franchisor is prioritizing expansion over location quality.

**Red Flag #3 — Generic System:**
Delivery food preparation is not unique. Thousands of restaurants do this. There's no defensible system. The franchisee is simply renting a brand name.

**Red Flag #4 — Profit Projections Are Suspicious:**
8 million won per month after expenses? That's very high for a delivery kitchen. Most small kitchens make 3-5 million won profit per month. These projections are likely inflated to attract franchisees.

**Red Flag #5 — Supply Chain Control:**
The franchisor controls all supplies through their commissary with a 40% markup. This is a potential trap. If the franchise fails financially, the franchisor has already made money from markups. The franchisee loses.

**Red Flag #6 — No Mention of Franchisee Support:**
The pitch doesn't mention training quality, marketing support, or how the franchisor helps franchisees succeed. This is a red flag.

**My Assessment:** This franchise is a potential trap. The franchisor makes money from low franchise fees (to attract franchisees) and supply markups (to extract profit). Franchisees are the ones at risk. I would predict:
- Aggressive growth for 1-2 years (many franchisees attracted by low fee)
- Profit pressure as franchisees realize 8 million won is unrealistic
- High failure rate among franchisees
- Eventual saturation and collapse

This is the Cafe Bene pattern repeating.

**Segment 3: Problem Set 3 — Comparative Analysis (8 minutes)**

Let's compare two Korean chicken franchise models:

**Model A: BBQ (Established, conservative growth)**
- 1,500 locations nationally
- Franchise fee: 150 million won
- Royalty: 5% of sales
- Strong brand, unique recipes
- Slow, selective expansion
- High franchisee profitability expectation

**Model B: Chicken Startup "Fast Bird" (New, aggressive growth)**
- 50 locations, founded 2023
- Franchise fee: 70 million won
- Royalty: 3% of sales
- Generic chicken recipes
- Target: 500 locations by 2026
- Emphasis on speed over quality

Let me ask: **Compare these two models. Which is more sustainable long-term?**

**Analysis:**

**Mechanism 1 (Capital Growth):**
- BBQ: Strong—established brand attracts franchisees; they pay premium fees
- Fast Bird: Strong—low fees attract many franchisees quickly

Winner: Fast Bird (in the short term)

**Mechanism 2 (Risk Distribution):**
- BBQ: Fair—franchisees accept risk in exchange for strong brand and support
- Fast Bird: Unfair—franchisees bear high risk with no proven brand

Winner: BBQ

**Mechanism 3 (Systems Replication):**
- BBQ: Strong—unique recipes, proven operational systems, training
- Fast Bird: Weak—generic recipes, no defensible advantage

Winner: BBQ

**Mechanism 4 (Brand Building):**
- BBQ: Strong—brand equity is already established; franchising amplifies it
- Fast Bird: Weak—brand is new and unproven; aggressive expansion could damage it

Winner: BBQ

**Overall:**
BBQ is more sustainable. While it grows slower, each mechanism is strong. Fast Bird will grow quickly but is vulnerable to collapse if market conditions worsen or saturation occurs.

**Prediction:**
- BBQ: Will maintain 1,500-2,000 locations; stable, profitable
- Fast Bird: Will grow to maybe 300 locations, then face pressure; many locations will close

**Why?** Because long-term franchise success requires all four mechanisms to be strong. BBQ has them; Fast Bird doesn't.

**Segment 4: Review of Weeks 1-4 (8 minutes)**

Before we finish, let me recap the major concepts from Weeks 1-4 to prepare you for the midterm:

**Week 1: Course Introduction**
- What is franchising? A system where a franchisor allows franchisees to use their brand and system for a fee
- Why does franchising exist? Because it's more efficient than corporate expansion in many cases

**Week 2: Franchising as Entrepreneurship**
- Franchising enables entrepreneurs without brand creation to still own a business
- Examples: Krispy Kreme, Nene Chicken, BBQ (successful franchises; different markets)
- Challenge: Adaptation to local markets (pizza, chicken, coffee have different market dynamics)

**Week 3: Franchise Relationship Model**
- Franchisor and franchisee must have aligned interests
- Balance of control: Franchisor controls brand/system; franchisee controls local operations
- Legal framework: Contracts define the relationship; quality standards ensure consistency

**Week 4: Wealth Creation (this week)**
- Four mechanisms: Capital, Risk Distribution, Systems, Brand
- Balance is key: Both franchisor and franchisee must profit
- Red flags indicate unsustainable models
- Korean franchises are laboratories for learning what works and what doesn't

**Key Takeaway from Weeks 1-4:**
Franchising is not a magic formula. It's a system that works ONLY when:
1. The franchisor has a valuable system or brand
2. The relationship is fairly balanced
3. Both parties are financially incentivized to succeed
4. The franchisor genuinely supports franchisee success

### 요약 및 질의응답 (2분)
