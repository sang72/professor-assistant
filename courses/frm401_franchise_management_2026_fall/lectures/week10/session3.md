### Main Content

#### Segment 1: Investment Return Metrics (15 minutes)

**The Three Key Metrics:**

When evaluating any investment, you calculate:
1. **Payback Period:** How long until you recover initial investment?
2. **ROI (Return on Investment):** What % return do you earn annually?
3. **NPV (Net Present Value):** How much is the total profit worth in today's dollars?

Let me explain each one with a real franchise example.

**Case: Chicken Franchise in Seoul, Good Location**

Initial Investment: 300M won
- Equipment: 150M
- Deposits/Initial inventory: 100M
- Working capital: 50M

Monthly Profit: 30M won (from our Session 2 model)
Annual Profit: 360M won

[ASK STUDENTS] Before I calculate, what do you think? Is 360M annual profit on 300M investment good?

**METRIC 1: Payback Period**

**Definition:** How many months until cumulative profit equals initial investment?

Formula: Initial Investment ÷ Monthly Profit = Payback Period (months)

Calculation: 300M ÷ 30M = 10 months

**Interpretation:** 
- The franchisee recovers their entire investment in 10 months
- After month 10, they're operating with pure profit
- This is EXCELLENT (industry standard is 18-24 months)

**Red Flags:**
- Payback >36 months = risky (you're exposed for 3 years)
- Payback >60 months = probably not viable (your opportunity cost is too high)

**METRIC 2: Return on Investment (ROI)**

**Definition:** What % return does your money earn each year?

There are two versions:

**a) Annual ROI (simple version):**

Formula: Annual Profit ÷ Initial Investment × 100%

Calculation: 360M ÷ 300M × 100% = 120%

**Interpretation:**
- You earn 120% return on invested capital each year
- By year 2, your profit alone (360M × 2 = 720M) exceeds initial investment (300M)
- This is an exceptional return
- Comparison: Stock market average = 8-10% annually; bonds = 4-5%; real estate = 5-7%
- This franchisee is earning 10-15X market returns

**Why so high?**
Because the franchise operator is doing the work. The 30M monthly profit includes:
- Franchisee's labor (not paid separately)
- Franchisor's contribution (they're profitable on royalties)
- Customer demand (built by brand)

If you're comparing to passive investments (stocks, bonds), chicken franchise is much more labor-intensive but also much more profitable.

**b) Multi-Year Cumulative ROI:**

Let's look at 5-year scenario:

| Year | Monthly Profit | Annual Profit | Cumulative Profit | Total Capital Deployed | Cumulative ROI |
|------|----------------|----------------|-------------------|------------------------|-----------------|
| Year 1 | 30M | 360M | 360M | 300M | 120% |
| Year 2 | 30M | 360M | 720M | 300M | 240% |
| Year 3 | 30M | 360M | 1,080M | 300M | 360% |
| Year 4 | 30M | 360M | 1,440M | 300M | 480% |
| Year 5 | 30M | 360M | 1,800M | 300M | 600% |

**5-year total return: 1,800M profit on 300M investment = 600% cumulative ROI**

But wait—this assumes:
1. Profit stays constant at 30M/month (unrealistic)
2. You don't pay taxes (unrealistic)
3. Inflation is zero (unrealistic)

Let me recalculate realistically.

**REALISTIC SCENARIO: Accounting for Taxes and Inflation**

Let's assume:
- Profit stays at 30M/month for first 2 years
- Then declines gradually (market saturation, competition)
- Income tax: 20% (franchisee is self-employed, effective rate around 20%)
- After-tax profit: 30M × 80% = 24M/month
- Inflation: 2% annually (slightly eats into real value)

| Year | Monthly Profit | Annual After-Tax | Cumulative | Real Value (2% inflation) |
|------|----------------|------------------|-----------|--------------------------|
| Year 1 | 24M | 288M | 288M | 288M |
| Year 2 | 24M | 288M | 576M | 563M |
| Year 3 | 22M (decline) | 264M | 840M | 792M |
| Year 4 | 20M | 240M | 1,080M | 1,000M |
| Year 5 | 18M | 216M | 1,296M | 1,193M |

**More realistic 5-year value: 1,193M in today's dollars on 300M investment**

**Annualized ROI:** (1,193M ÷ 300M)^(1/5) - 1 = 30.8% annualized

Still excellent. But more realistic than 600%.

**METRIC 3: Net Present Value (NPV)**

**What is NPV?**

NPV converts all future profits into a single present-day number, accounting for:
1. The time value of money (money today is worth more than money tomorrow)
2. The investor's required rate of return (discount rate)

[ASK STUDENTS] Why is money today worth more than money tomorrow?

**Because:**
- You could invest it and earn interest
- Inflation erodes buying power
- Risk exists (franchise could fail)

**Formula (simplified):**

Let's say your required return is 15% annually (meaning: you won't invest unless you beat 15% annual ROI).

NPV = -Initial Investment + (Year 1 Profit ÷ 1.15) + (Year 2 Profit ÷ 1.15²) + ... + (Year 5 Profit ÷ 1.15⁵)

**Calculation for our chicken franchise:**

Year 0 (investment): -300M
Year 1 profit: 288M ÷ 1.15 = 250.4M
Year 2 profit: 288M ÷ 1.32 = 218.2M
Year 3 profit: 264M ÷ 1.52 = 173.7M
Year 4 profit: 240M ÷ 1.75 = 137.1M
Year 5 profit: 216M ÷ 2.01 = 107.5M

**NPV = -300M + 250.4M + 218.2M + 173.7M + 137.1M + 107.5M = 586.9M**

**Interpretation:**
- NPV is positive (586.9M > 0) = Good investment
- At your required 15% return rate, this franchise creates 586.9M in value beyond that requirement
- The larger the NPV, the better the investment

**Decision Rule:**
- NPV > 0: Accept the investment
- NPV < 0: Reject the investment
- Choose the project with highest NPV when comparing multiple options

#### Segment 3: Case Study - Korean vs. International Investment (13 minutes)

**Now let me show you why the same franchise looks completely different depending on where you invest.**

**Case: Nene Chicken Expansion Decision, 2025**

Nene Chicken is considering expansion into three markets:
1. **Korea** (domestic market)
2. **Vietnam** (Southeast Asia expansion)
3. **Japan** (competition with local players)

Let's model each one.

**Market 1: Seoul, South Korea (2025 Data)**

**Market Context:**
- Fried chicken is saturated category (1,200+ brands in Korea)
- Delivery platforms (Coupang Eats, Yogiyo) have driven price wars
- Real estate prices very high
- Minimum wage continues rising

**Financial Model:**

| Item | Amount | % of Sales |
|------|--------|-----------|
| Monthly sales | 25M | 100% |
| COGS (chicken, oil) | -8.75M | -35% |
| Labor (2 staff) | -7M | -28% |
| Rent (premium location) | -5M | -20% |
| Utilities | -0.75M | -3% |
| Equipment | -1M | -4% |
| Delivery platform fee | -2.5M | -10% |
| Franchisor royalty | -1.5M | -6% |
| **NET PROFIT** | **-1M** | **-4%** |

**WAIT—this franchisee is losing money.**

Why? Korea's market is oversaturated. Delivery platforms destroyed margins (they take 15-20% commission). Rent is high.

**This franchise is not viable in Korea anymore.**

This is a real problem: Nene Chicken's best market (Korea) is becoming unprofitable for new franchisees.

**Market 2: Ho Chi Minh City, Vietnam (2025 Data)**

**Market Context:**
- Fried chicken is GROWING category (Vietnamese love fried protein)
- Limited local competition (local players are lower quality)
- Real estate is cheap (1/5 the price of Seoul)
- Labor costs very low (1/3 of Korea)
- No delivery platform saturation (delivery is emerging)

**Financial Model (adjusted for Vietnam):**

| Item | Amount | % of Sales |
|------|--------|-----------|
| Monthly sales | 30M VND (~1.3M USD) | 100% |
| COGS | -9.6M | -32% |
| Labor (4 staff, lower wage) | -4.5M | -15% |
| Rent (prime location) | -3M | -10% |
| Utilities | -0.6M | -2% |
| Equipment | -0.8M | -2.7% |
| Delivery fees (emerging, lower) | -1.5M | -5% |
| Franchisor royalty | -1.8M | -6% |
| **NET PROFIT** | **8.6M** | **28.7%** |

**This franchisee makes 8.6M/month in Vietnam.**

Why? Lower costs (especially labor and rent), growing demand (not saturated), less delivery platform pressure.

**Comparison:**

| Metric | Korea | Vietnam |
|--------|--------|---------|
| Monthly profit | -1M (LOSS) | 8.6M (GAIN) |
| Profit margin | -4% | 28.7% |
| ROI on 300M investment | Negative | 34.4% annually |
| Payback period | Never pays back | 35 months |
| Market sentiment | Saturated, declining | Growing, optimistic |

**Investment Decision:**
- Korea: DO NOT INVEST (current market is dead for new franchisees)
- Vietnam: INVEST (strong returns in growing market)

[ASK STUDENTS] This is a dilemma Nene Chicken actually faces in 2025. Should they keep recruiting franchisees in Korea (domestic brand strength, familiar market) or pivot to Vietnam (better economics, but international risk)?

**Market 3: Tokyo, Japan (2025 Data - Most Complex)**

**Market Context:**
- Japanese consume fried chicken (successful local brands: KFC, local yakitori chains)
- But Japanese fried chicken (karaage) is different style
- Japanese customers have high quality standards
- Very expensive real estate in prime locations
- Strong labor regulations, high wages
- Fierce competition with established local brands

**Financial Model:**

| Item | Amount | % of Sales |
|------|--------|-----------|
| Monthly sales | 40M JPY (~280K USD) | 100% |
| COGS | -14M | -35% |
| Labor (strict regulations, high wage) | -12M | -30% |
| Rent (premium Shibuya/Shinjuku) | -10M | -25% |
| Utilities + taxes | -2M | -5% |
| Equipment | -1M | -2.5% |
| Royalty | -2.4M | -6% |
| **NET PROFIT** | **-1.4M** | **-3.5%** |

**Also unprofitable, but for different reason than Korea.**

Why?
- Rent is extremely high (25% vs. 20% in Korea)
- Labor is very high (30% vs. 28%)
- Japanese customers reluctant to try unfamiliar brand
- Competition fierce

**Investment Decision for Japan:**
- DO NOT INVEST in prime locations (rent destroys economics)
- MAYBE invest in suburban locations, but then sales drop
- Market not suitable for Nene Chicken

---

**Synthesis: Three Markets, One Franchise**

| Market | Viability | Why | Strategic Implication |
|--------|----------|-----|----------------------|
| Korea | ❌ No | Saturated, delivery destroyed margins | Stop recruiting; support existing franchisees |
| Vietnam | ✅ Yes | Growing demand, low costs, good margins | Expand aggressively |
| Japan | ❌ No | Expensive market, strong local competition | Enter only if can differentiate (premium positioning, different location strategy) |

**This is how international expansion works.** Same franchise, same product, completely different economics depending on local market conditions.

**For a franchisee, the lesson is:**
- Never invest in home market just because it's home
- Look at the economics of YOUR market
- If the franchise doesn't work in your location, don't invest just because you're familiar with the brand

### Vocabulary Emphasis

- **Payback period:** Time needed to recover initial investment from profits
- **ROI (Return on Investment):** Annual percentage return on capital deployed
- **Discount rate:** Required return rate that justifies the investment
- **NPV (Net Present Value):** Total value created in today's dollars, accounting for time value of money
- **Opportunity cost:** Profit you give up by choosing one investment over another
- **Market saturation:** Stage where market has too many competitors, prices decline
- **Macro factor:** Large economic force (inflation, wage growth, delivery platforms) affecting all franchisees similarly
- **Geographic arbitrage:** Profiting by deploying capital in low-cost, high-growth markets

### Common Mistakes to Watch For

1. **Confusing profit % with ROI %**
   - Profit margin 8% (8% of sales) ≠ 8% ROI on capital (could be 50%+ ROI if payback is fast)
   - Clarify: ROI uses CAPITAL as denominator, not sales

2. **Ignoring taxes**
   - Pre-tax profit ≠ what franchisee keeps
   - Always factor 20-30% tax hit depending on structure

3. **Assuming constant profit**
   - Real franchises have declining profit in years 2-3 as novelty wears off
   - Better to model declining profit: Year 1 = 100%, Year 2 = 95%, Year 3 = 85%

4. **Underestimating rent inflation**
   - Korea's biggest franchisee killer is rent renegotiation
   - Always include 10-15% rent increase scenario in analysis

5. **Not considering market changes**
   - Delivery platform fees didn't exist 5 years ago
   - A franchise viable in 2020 might not be viable in 2025
   - Always ask: "What's changed in this market since 2022?"

### Extension Questions

**If student asks:** "Why is Korea profitable in some cases but not others?"
**Answer:** Location, location, location. Prime location in Gangnam can generate 40M/month sales. Suburban location generates 15M. Fixed costs don't change much (rent is everywhere), so sales variation drives profit.

**If student asks:** "Should Nene Chicken just lower their royalty rate in Korea?"
**Answer:** They could, but then franchisees are still unprofitable (see the model—royalty is only 6%, not the main problem). The main killers are high rent + delivery fees + COGS + labor. Royalty reduction would help a bit but doesn't solve the structural problem.

**If student asks:** "How do established chains survive in saturated Korea?"
**Answer:** They don't—they're consolidating. Many Korean restaurant franchises are closing. Survivors are either:
1. Ultra-efficient operators who can profitably run on 10-15% margins
2. Premium brands that don't compete on price (BBQ Chicken is surviving because they're premium, not budget)
3. Diversified chains that make money on supply to franchisees (like CU earning royalties but also making money on product sales)

### Real-World Context

**2025-2026 Update:**
- Korean restaurant franchises are struggling. Many franchisees are closing.
- Vietnam is the hot market for Korean franchise brands. BBQ Chicken, Nene Chicken, etc., are all expanding Vietnam aggressively.
- Japan remains difficult because of competition and costs, but some brands are succeeding with niche strategies (e.g., focusing on delivery rather than dine-in).
- International expansion is increasingly essential for Korean franchise brands because domestic market is mature/saturated.
