## SECTION 2: FULL LECTURE SCRIPT (English)

### Opening (3 minutes)

Welcome to Session 3 of Week 12.

In Sessions 1 and 2, we studied U.S. and Korean franchise law in depth. Now the critical question: **What does this mean?**

When you're a franchisee, does the legal system protect you?
When you're a franchisor, does the legal system constrain you?

This session answers these questions through comparative analysis and real-world case studies.

### Main Content (32 minutes)

**Segment 1: Philosophical Foundations - Why Legal Systems Differ (7 minutes)**

Before diving into specifics, understand the philosophical difference:

**U.S. Philosophy: Caveat Emptor + Regulation for Market Failure**

**Caveat emptor** = "Buyer beware"—in principle, parties negotiate at arm's length. BUT when market failure exists (franchisor has monopoly power over franchisee), regulation corrects it.

U.S. approach:
- Trust markets and negotiation first
- Regulate only when abuse is documented
- Heavy reliance on private litigation (damages incentivize compliance)
- Treble damages = "punish violators into compliance"
- Disclosure (FDD) = market mechanism (franchisee can make informed choice)

**Korean Philosophy: Social Responsibility + Proactive Protection**

Korean legal culture emphasizes:
- Franchisor has power; with power comes responsibility
- Proactive protection (prevent fraud before it happens)
- Government plays active role (Fair Trade Commission)
- Cooling-off period = let franchisees reconsider without penalty
- Good faith requirement = broad behavioral obligation

Korean approach:
- Assume power imbalance is real; regulate it
- Prevent problems (cooling-off, good faith rules)
- Mediation-first culture (resolve disputes cooperatively)
- Lower damages awards (not about punishment, about compensation)

**Practical Impact:**

- **U.S.:** Franchisor invests heavily upfront in FDD compliance (prevents lawsuits). Once FDD is compliant, franchisor has relative freedom.
- **Korea:** Franchisor must maintain ongoing good faith behavior. Compliance is continuous, not just at FDD stage.

**Example:** Franchisor wants to raise royalty rate mid-contract.
- **U.S.:** If contract allows it, franchisor can usually do it (depends on state). Franchisor pays for FDD mistake, not for royalty increase.
- **Korea:** Even if contract allows it, franchisor might violate "good faith" requirement by acting unilaterally. Must negotiate or it's unfair practice.

**Segment 2: 12-Dimension Comparison (Detailed Breakdown) (8 minutes)**

**1. DISCLOSURE REGIME**

**U.S.:**
- Standardized FDD (23 items, same format nationwide)
- Mandatory Items 1-23 (no flexibility)
- Comprehensive (covers all material information)
- Burden: Franchisor must provide everything upfront
- Strength: Franchisees can easily compare FDDs across brands

**Korea:**
- No standardized format; law specifies ~15 key topics
- Franchisor customizes disclosure (tailored to their system)
- Burden: Franchisor must cover required topics, but can structure as they choose
- Strength: More flexible; less bureaucratic

**Winner for Franchisee:** U.S. (standardized = easier to verify)

---

**2. PROTECTION MECHANISM: Preventive vs. Punitive**

**U.S.:**
- **Punitive:** Treble damages (3x) = franchise violation must be expensive to deter future violations
- Franchisor compliance driven by fear of lawsuit
- Strong financial incentive to comply

**Korea:**
- **Preventive:** Cooling-off period (escape before sunk), good faith requirement (ongoing behavior standard)
- Franchisor compliance driven by legal obligation + Fair Trade Commission oversight
- Lower financial penalties; more emphasis on behavioral obligation

**Winner for Franchisee:** Tie (U.S. = bigger financial recovery if you win; Korea = easier to exit without litigation)

---

**3. SPEED OF RESOLUTION**

**U.S.:**
- Litigation: 2-5 years
- Class actions: 3-7 years
- Arbitration: 1-2 years
- Settlement usually happens after 1-2 years of discovery (expensive)

**Korea:**
- FTC investigation: 3-6 months
- Mediation: 3-6 months (FTC-facilitated)
- Arbitration: 3-6 months
- Litigation: 1-3 years (faster courts)
- Culture favors settlement over trial

**Winner for Franchisee:** Korea (4-6 months average vs. 1-2 years in U.S.)

---

**4. COST OF DISPUTE RESOLUTION**

**U.S.:**
- FTC action: Free to franchisee (government handles)
- Private litigation: $300K-$1M+ in legal fees (per side)
- Class action settlement: Defendants pay attorneys' fees, but individual franchisee recovery varies
- Arbitration: $50K-$200K (split between parties)
- **Total for franchisee to sue:** $200K-$500K (risks case)

**Korea:**
- FTC complaint: Free to franchisee (government investigates)
- FTC mediation: Low cost (~2-5M won = $1.5K-$4K)
- Arbitration: 20-50M won (~$15K-$40K, split between parties)
- Litigation: 50-200M won (~$40K-$150K, more affordable than U.S.)
- **Total for franchisee to pursue resolution:** 10-50M won (~$8K-$38K)

**Winner for Franchisee:** Korea (3-5x cheaper to pursue resolution)

---

**5. ENFORCEMENT MECHANISM**

**U.S.:**
- FTC enforcement (powerful government agency with 200+ franchise investigators)
- Federal Trade Commission can pursue nationally
- Private lawsuits (franchisees sue directly)
- Class action mechanism (multiple franchisees combine)
- Decentralized: Each state can also enforce its own rules

**Korea:**
- Fair Trade Commission enforcement (moderate government agency, 50-70 franchise investigators)
- National coverage (one agency, no state variation)
- Private lawsuits (less common cultural preference)
- Class arbitration (increasing but less common than U.S.)
- Mediation-first culture (reduce litigation volume)

**Winner for Franchisor:** U.S. (lower FTC enforcement if franchisor is careful); Korea (higher legal certainty due to uniform rules)

---

**6. FRANCHISEE RIGHTS TO INFORMATION**

**U.S.:**
- Can view franchisee list (Item 20 of FDD)
- Can contact other franchisees
- Can request performance data (if franchisor publishes)
- No explicit audit right (franchisee can request documents, may not always succeed)

**Korea:**
- Explicit right to information about franchisor finances
- Can audit franchisor records (if profit > 500M won annually)
- Can contact other franchisees (names available)
- Supplier contract transparency (can request to review)
- Increasing: Right to audit supplier markups (proposed 2024-2025)

**Winner for Franchisee:** Korea (more comprehensive information access)

---

**7. TERMINATION PROTECTION**

**U.S.:**
- Varies by state (California = good cause required; Texas = contract governs)
- 14 states have strong protections; 36 states = limited protection
- Some states require notice (usually 30-90 days)
- Unfair termination = damages claim, but standard varies

**Korea:**
- Universal "good cause" requirement (all territories)
- 6-month notice required
- Franchisor must show legitimate business reason
- Wrongful termination = damages claim

**Winner for Franchisee:** Korea (uniform protection, longer notice period)

---

**8. MID-CONTRACT CHANGES (Price, terms, etc.)**

**U.S.:**
- Contract governs; if silent, franchisor can usually change terms (varies by state)
- Some states (CA) require "good faith" = limits arbitrary changes
- Franchisee's main remedy: Refuse payment and litigate

**Korea:**
- Cannot change material terms without franchisee agreement
- "Good faith" requirement = unilateral changes are unfair
- Recent GS25 case: royalty increase without consent = illegal
- Franchisee remedy: Refuse payment, file FTC complaint, get refund

**Winner for Franchisee:** Korea (better protection against surprise mid-contract changes)

---

**9. DAMAGES AVAILABLE**

**U.S.:**
- Actual damages (lost profits, lost business value)
- Treble damages (3x multiplier for intentional violations)
- Attorney fees (in some cases)
- Class action settlements: Can be $10M-$100M+
- **Upside:** Very large potential recovery
- **Downside:** Must win lawsuit (high burden of proof)

**Korea:**
- Actual damages (lost profits, business value)
- No multiplier (yet; 2x proposed for 2025)
- Mediation/settlement often includes both sides' attorney fees
- Class awards: Smaller (100M-1B won typical)
- **Upside:** Easier to get actual damages through mediation
- **Downside:** Overall recovery smaller

**Winner for Big Cases:** U.S. (treble damages = bigger recovery)
**Winner for Average Cases:** Korea (mediation = easier settlement)

---

**10. COOLING-OFF/EXIT RIGHTS**

**U.S.:**
- 14-day FDD review period (but not a cancellation right; franchisee can just review)
- After signing: Can only exit by breach (franchisor violation) or buy-out negotiation
- No automatic right to exit

**Korea:**
- 7-day cooling-off period (automatic right to cancel, full refund, no penalty)
- Franchisee can exit even if business is profitable
- Most protective feature in Korean law

**Winner for Franchisee:** Korea (automatic exit right for 7 days)

---

**11. REGULATORY BODIES & ENFORCEMENT CULTURE**

**U.S.:**
- FTC (Federal) + State regulators (14 states)
- FTC aggressive on enforcement (200+ cases/year)
- Private lawsuits common (franchisees sue proactively)
- Litigation-oriented culture (win/lose mentality)

**Korea:**
- Fair Trade Commission only
- Moderate enforcement (50-70 cases/year, growing)
- Private lawsuits less common (prefer mediation)
- Settlement-oriented culture (both win/both benefit mentality)

**Winner for Franchisee:** U.S. (more regulatory resources)
**Winner for Franchisee Experience:** Korea (faster, cheaper resolution culture)

---

**12. EMERGING ISSUES (Digital, ESG, Data)**

**U.S.:**
- FTC issuing guidance on digital franchises (2023-2024)
- ESG disclosure emerging (not yet mandatory)
- Data ownership/privacy: General contract law applies
- No specific digital franchise regulation yet

**Korea:**
- FTC Korea issuing digital franchise guidance (2024)
- Formal digital franchise law expected 2025
- Data ownership, cybersecurity rules being developed
- ESG voluntary disclosure (becoming expectation)
- **Korea is ahead of U.S. in regulating emerging franchise models**

**Winner for Future:** Korea (proactive regulation of new models)

**Segment 3: Real-World Case Studies - How Multinational Franchisors Navigate (8 minutes)**

**CASE STUDY 1: McDonald's U.S. vs. Korea Franchise Strategy**

**U.S. Operation (Franchise structure):**
- Master franchisees in major metros
- Franchisee required to comply with FDD (23 items)
- U.S.: Heavily franchised (>70% franchisees, <30% company-owned)
- Earnings: Limited disclosure (McDonald's avoids Item 19 claims)
- Franchisor: Pays for FDD maintenance, state registration (CA, NY, IL)
- Strategy: Heavy disclosure upfront, then let franchisee operate

**Korea Operation (Mixed franchising + company-owned):**
- McDonald's Korea mostly company-owned (70% company-owned, 30% franchisees)
- Conscious choice: Avoids franchise law complexity
- Disclosure: Follows Korean law requirements
- Franchisor: Direct control over operations, brand, prices
- Strategy: Prefer company-owned to minimize legal risk, maintain quality

**Why the difference?**
- U.S. law allows franchising; McDonald's chose to franchise for expansion capital
- Korean law increasing; McDonald's chose company-owned to avoid cooling-off risk (franchisees could exit within 7 days)
- U.S.: Economics favorable to franchising (franchisee buys in, franchisor scales)
- Korea: Company-owned reduces legal risk from franchisee disputes

**Financial Impact:**
- U.S.: McDonald's franchisees typically earn $150K-$300K annually (after fees, expenses). Company makes money from royalties + supply.
- Korea: McDonald's company-owned stores earn similar, but company bears all risk/cost. Higher franchisor operating burden but zero franchisee litigation risk.

**Takeaway for Business:** Legal environment shapes franchise strategy. U.S. encourages franchising; Korea's protections encourage company-owned model.

---

**CASE STUDY 2: Starbucks U.S. Expansion vs. Starbucks Korea Expansion**

**U.S. Expansion (1987-present):**
- Started company-owned stores (brand building)
- Gradually added licensed partners (Walmart, airports, etc.)
- True franchising: Limited (mostly licensed partners, not full franchises)
- Why? Starbucks preferred to control brand/quality
- FDD: Starbucks did create FDD, but didn't heavy market franchises (not their strategic choice)
- Legal advantage: Limited franchise liability (fewer franchises = fewer disputes)

**Korea Expansion (1999-present):**
- Entered through company-owned stores first (proof of concept in new market)
- Gradually added some franchises (but minority)
- Mixed model: Mostly company-owned, some franchisees for specific locations
- Why? Both brand control AND adapt to Korean law

**Korea Strategic Decisions (2000-2026):**
- 2000-2005: Learned Korean law requirements; built FDD
- 2005-2010: Offered some franchises (learned about cooling-off period impact)
- 2010-2020: Slowed franchising; emphasized company-owned (more control)
- 2020-2026: Gradual franchise growth, but with clear contracts addressing cooling-off, good faith, support

**Financial Impact:**
- Korea company-owned: Starbucks bears all rent, labor costs. But controls everything. Prices set nationally (premium positioning maintained).
- Korea franchises: Franchisees bear rent/labor. Starbucks gets royalties (~5%). But 7-day cooling-off means 10-15% of new franchisees quit within week (customer acquisition cost for Starbucks).

**Takeaway for Business:** Starbucks adapted to Korean law by emphasizing company-owned model. U.S. choice (company-owned) driven by brand control; Korea choice (company-owned) driven by legal considerations + brand control.

---

**CASE STUDY 3: Korean Brand BBQ Chicken - Global Expansion**

**Korea Operation (Domestic market):**
- Heavy franchising (80% franchisees, 20% company-owned)
- Subject to Korean law
- Strong franchisee base (stable, long-term relationships)
- Challenge: Cooling-off period means 10-15% new franchisees cancel within 7 days
- Support requirement means BBQ Chicken must invest heavily in training, field support

**Vietnam Expansion (2010-present):**
- No specific franchise law in Vietnam
- BBQ Chicken can operate more flexibly
- Franchise terms: Can set their own (no regulation)
- Strategy: Simpler contracts, lower support requirements, faster franchisee recruitment

**U.S. Expansion (2015-present, Limited):**
- Must comply with FTC Rule + state laws
- California/New York: Registration required (expensive, slow)
- FDD: Had to create comprehensive disclosure (23 items)
- Challenge: U.S. franchisees expect higher support than Korea (different culture)
- Legal risk: High (U.S. franchisees litigate frequently)
- Strategy: Limited U.S. expansion; focus on master franchisee partnerships (reduce direct legal exposure)

**Comparative Challenges:**
- Korea: Cooling-off, good faith = continuous investment in franchisees
- Vietnam: Less regulation = flexibility but less franchisee protection = reputation risk
- U.S.: Heavy regulation = expensive compliance but clear rules = predictable

**Financial Impact:**
- Korea domestic: High franchisee satisfaction (protected by law, supported by franchisor)
- Vietnam: Lower support cost but higher failure rate, lower brand loyalty
- U.S.: High compliance cost (FDD, registration, legal counsel) but high franchisee quality/commitment

**Takeaway for Business:** Korean franchisors going global must adapt from protective Korean system to either less-regulated markets (Vietnam) or more-regulated markets (U.S.). Both require strategic adjustment.

**Segment 4: 2025-2030 Outlook & Convergence Trends (8 minutes)**

**Likely Trajectory (Based on current proposals and discussions):**

**U.S. Direction (2025-2030):**

1. **Digital Franchise Regulation (Likely):**
   - FTC likely to propose formal digital franchise rules
   - Will address: Data ownership, cybersecurity, platform requirements
   - Model: Similar to current franchising rules but updated for tech
   - Timeline: Proposed 2025-2026, finalized 2027-2028

2. **Cooling-Off Period (Unlikely but possible):**
   - No current momentum for U.S. to adopt 7-day cooling-off
   - But: Some franchisee advocates pushing for it
   - Argument: "Korea has it; we should too"
   - Counterargument: "Kills franchise model; creates economic waste"
   - Probability: 20% (unlikely given U.S. pro-business orientation)

3. **State Convergence (Likely):**
   - Non-registration states may adopt California/New York protections
   - Reason: Franchisee litigation increasing; states want to reduce disputes
   - Timeline: Gradual (2025-2030)

**Korean Direction (2025-2030):**

1. **Damages Multiplier (Likely):**
   - Proposed 2x damages (similar to treble damages concept)
   - Would incentivize franchisee lawsuits
   - Timeline: Expected in 2025-2026 amendment

2. **Digital Franchise Regulation (Very Likely):**
   - Formal law expected 2025
   - Will address: Data ownership, cybersecurity, franchisee autonomy
   - Korea ahead of U.S. here

3. **Registration System (Unlikely):**
   - No proposal for pre-franchise approval (California-style)
   - Korean culture prefers lighter regulatory touch
   - Probability: <10%

4. **Mediation Mandate (Likely to expand):**
   - Already required for some disputes
   - Likely to be extended to all franchise disputes by 2025
   - Would make mediation binding first step before litigation

**Convergence or Divergence?**

**Likely outcome: PARTIAL CONVERGENCE BY 2030**

**Areas of convergence:**
- Both will regulate digital franchises (new area)
- Both will maintain strong franchisee disclosure requirements
- Both will have mechanisms for franchisee to exit or recover damages
- Both will require franchisor good faith behavior (though mechanisms differ)

**Areas of continued divergence:**
- U.S. will maintain treble damages (Korea may adopt 2x); U.S. system remains more punitive
- Korea will maintain cooling-off period; U.S. won't adopt it
- Korea will maintain uniform national system; U.S. will maintain state variation
- Mediation culture: Korea stronger; U.S. more litigation-oriented

**By 2030 prediction:**
- U.S. and Korea both have strong franchisee protections
- Mechanisms remain different but outcomes similar
- Global franchisors will face comparable disclosure/support/termination requirements in both markets
- Digital franchises will have clear regulatory frameworks in both countries

### Summary & Strategic Implications (5 minutes)

**For Franchisees (Choosing which market to franchise in):**

**If you want maximum legal protection:** Korea (cooling-off, good cause, lower dispute costs)
**If you want maximum damages recovery:** U.S. (treble damages, but harder to win)
**If you want fastest resolution:** Korea (mediation-first, 4-6 month typical)
**If you want most predictable outcome:** U.S. (40+ years of case law, clear precedent)

**For Franchisors (Choosing where to franchise):**

**If you want regulatory clarity:** U.S. (FTC rules are crystal-clear, state registration is standardized)
**If you want operational flexibility:** Vietnam, Indonesia (minimal regulation)
**If you want ethical best practices:** Korea (good faith requirement forces transparency)
**If you want balanced growth:** Mix (company-owned in regulated markets, franchise in less-regulated)

**For Investors (Evaluating franchise investment):**

**U.S. advantages:** Established system, clear rules, large damages if franchisor violates
**U.S. disadvantages:** Litigation risk, complex state-by-state variation, expensive legal costs

**Korea advantages:** Fast dispute resolution, lower legal costs, clear good faith standard, cooling-off period protects you
**Korea disadvantages:** Smaller damages awards, less developed case law, regulatory landscape still evolving

**Key Takeaway:** No single "best" system. U.S. = large potential damages; Korea = better franchisee experience. Choose based on your risk tolerance.

## SECTION 4: KEY NOTES & TEACHING INSIGHTS

**Critical Concepts:**

- **Philosophy matters:** U.S. system = "trust markets with regulatory guardrails"; Korea system = "proactive protection from power imbalance." This philosophical difference explains all specific legal differences.

- **12-dimension comparison shows no clear "winner":** U.S. stronger on damages, precedent, enforcement resources. Korea stronger on speed, cost, cooling-off, information access. Neither system is objectively superior; they're different trade-offs.

- **Multinational franchisors must strategically adapt:** McDonald's/Starbucks chose company-owned model in Korea partly due to legal/cool-off risks. BBQ Chicken learned to navigate different regulatory frameworks. Strategy ≠ one-size-fits-all.

- **Convergence is partial, not complete:** By 2030, both systems will have strong digital franchise rules, good faith requirements, disclosure requirements. BUT: U.S. will maintain treble damages + state variation; Korea will maintain cooling-off + mediation culture. Systems stay distinct but increasingly compatible.

**Student Engagement Questions:**

1. **Which system would you rather franchise in, and why?** (Answers reveal risk tolerance)

2. **McDonald's chose company-owned in Korea. Was that the right choice? What would you do?**

3. **If you're a Korean franchisor expanding to California, what's your compliance budget?** (Answer: $100K-$300K first year, $30K-$50K annually after)

4. **Starbucks discovered that Korean franchisees prefer company-owned stores (higher security, brand consistency). Does legal environment drive this preference, or just business strategy?** (Discussion: Both. Law creates incentive for company-owned; business strategy aligns with it)

5. **Will U.S. ever adopt 7-day cooling-off period? Why or why not?** (Answer: Unlikely. Would disrupt franchise model. But possible if franchisee advocacy increases)

**Teaching Strategy:**

Present the 12-dimension comparison as a "balanced scorecard" — not winners/losers, but trade-offs. Then show how those trade-offs drive actual business decisions (McDonald's, Starbucks, BBQ Chicken case studies).

Conclusion: Legal environment is a critical strategic variable in international franchising. Successful global franchisors adapt their model to local legal constraints while maintaining brand consistency.
