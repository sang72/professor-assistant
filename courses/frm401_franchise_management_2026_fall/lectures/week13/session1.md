### Main Content

#### Segment 1: Four Entry Models (12 minutes)

**Why do franchises go global?**

1. **Market saturation at home:** Korean food franchise market is mature. Growth is capped.
2. **Demographic trends:** Korean population is aging/declining. Fewer new customers.
3. **Capital availability:** Korean franchises (BBQ Chicken, Nene Chicken) have capital to invest internationally.
4. **Brand strength:** Korean brands are increasingly respected globally (K-beauty, K-food, K-content).
5. **Geographic arbitrage:** Emerging markets (Vietnam, Indonesia, Philippines) are less saturated, have growing middle class.

**Four entry models, each with different risk-return profiles:**

---

**MODEL 1: DIRECT FRANCHISING**

**What is it?**
Franchisor directly recruits and manages franchisees in target country. Franchisor owns the brand, controls the system, maintains operations.

**How it works:**
1. Franchisor sets up office in new country (or hires local manager)
2. Franchisor recruits franchisees directly
3. Franchisor provides training, support, operations guidance
4. Franchisor collects royalties, fees
5. Franchisor is responsible for regulatory compliance, brand management

**Risk Profile:**
- **High risk:** Franchisor must understand local market, navigate regulations, support franchisees in unfamiliar market
- **Requires:** Deep local knowledge or hiring experienced local executives
- **Execution challenges:** Language barriers, regulatory differences, cultural misalignment

**Return Profile:**
- **High reward:** Franchisor keeps 100% of profit (minus local operations costs)
- **Control:** Franchisor maintains full system control
- **Scalability:** Can grow system quickly if successful

**Example: Starbucks Korea (1999-present)**

Starbucks entered Korea through direct franchising + company-owned stores:
1. Starbucks opened company-owned stores first (proof of concept)
2. After establishing brand recognition, opened franchising recruitment
3. Today: Mix of company-owned + franchised
4. Model worked because: Korea was wealthy market, Starbucks could invest, Korean culture adapted well to premium coffee

**Financial Economics:**
- Initial investment: 50B+ won (multiple stores to establish brand)
- Payoff: Korea is now Starbucks' fastest-growing market (outside China)
- Timeline: 5-7 years before profitability

---

**MODEL 2: MASTER FRANCHISING**

**What is it?**
Franchisor partners with a "master franchisee"—a strong local company that has territory rights and recruits/manages sub-franchisees. Master franchisee is like a "country manager."

**How it works:**
1. Franchisor signs agreement with master franchisee (local company)
2. Master franchisee has exclusive rights to territory (entire country or region)
3. Master franchisee invests in market development, franchisee recruitment, sub-franchisee support
4. Franchisor collects royalties from master franchisee (typically 3-5% of master's revenue)
5. Master franchisee collects royalties from sub-franchisees (typically 5-7% of sales)

**Risk Profile:**
- **Lower risk for franchisor:** Master franchisee bears market risk. Franchisor is more passive.
- **Higher risk for master franchisee:** They invest heavily, manage franchisee relationships, bear full market risk
- **Execution challenge:** Franchisor must find right master franchisee (partner quality matters enormously)

**Return Profile:**
- **Lower return for franchisor:** Takes percentage of percentage (3-5% vs. 5-7% direct)
- **Loss of control:** Franchisor can't directly manage franchisees in market
- **Slower scaling:** Limited by master franchisee's capacity

**Example: KFC in Asia**

KFC expanded through master franchisees in most Asian markets:
- India: Master franchisee is major Indian conglomerate (Hindustan Unilever)
- China: Started with master franchisees, shifted to direct as market matured
- Thailand: Master franchisee is local food company

**Financial Economics:**
- Franchisor initial investment: Minimal (just brand and support)
- Master franchisee initial investment: 100B+ won (market development, recruitment)
- Franchisor upside: Consistent royalties, minimal execution risk
- Master franchisee upside: Build large business in territory
- Timeline: Faster than direct (master franchisee has local expertise)

---

**MODEL 3: JOINT VENTURE**

**What is it?**
Franchisor and local company are equal (or negotiated) partners. Both invest, both control, both profit.

**How it works:**
1. Franchisor partners with local company
2. Both invest capital (franchisor brings brand/system, local brings real estate/market knowledge)
3. Both share ownership, decision-making, profits
4. Often structured as separate company (e.g., "McDonald's China" is joint venture)

**Risk Profile:**
- **Moderate risk:** Shared between franchisor and partner
- **Execution challenge:** Must align interests between two companies with different cultures, goals
- **Governance complexity:** Joint decision-making can be slower than unilateral

**Return Profile:**
- **Shared return:** Franchisor gets percentage of JV profit (not royalties), not full upside
- **Shared control:** Franchisor can't dictate strategy
- **Long-term partnership:** Usually multi-decade arrangements

**Example: McDonald's China (1990-present)**

McDonald's entered China through joint venture with state-owned company:
- JV structure: 50/50 initially, later shifted
- Investment: Both sides invested hundreds of millions
- Decision-making: Shared governance, but McDonald's had operational control (negotiated)
- Timeline: Started 1990, became fully company-owned by McDonald's, now shifting to franchising

**Financial Economics:**
- Franchisor initial investment: 50B+ won
- Local partner investment: Similar or larger
- Franchisor profit: Share of JV profits (40-50% typically)
- Timeline: 10+ years to profitability in large market like China

---

**MODEL 4: COMPANY-OWNED ENTRY**

**What is it?**
Franchisor opens company-owned stores first, establishes brand and operating model, THEN begins franchising.

**How it works:**
1. Franchisor invests in opening 5-10 company-owned locations
2. Proves business model works in market (sales, profit, operations)
3. Once proven, begins recruiting franchisees
4. Company-owned stores + franchised stores = hybrid model

**Risk Profile:**
- **Higher initial risk:** Heavy franchisor investment before franchise revenue
- **Execution challenge:** Must prove business model before asking franchisees to invest
- **Timeline challenge:** Takes 3-5 years of company investment before franchising begins

**Return Profile:**
- **Higher long-term return:** By time franchising begins, franchisor has deep market knowledge, proven operations, brand recognition
- **Franchisees have lower risk:** They buy into proven model, not unproven concept
- **Strong system:** Company-owned stores are training grounds for franchisees

**Example: Starbucks Korea again, but different angle**

Starbucks strategy:
- 1999-2002: Opened company-owned stores only (investment: 200B+ won)
- 2003-2006: Began selective franchising while expanding company-owned
- 2006-present: Growing franchising, but maintaining significant company-owned presence

**Financial Economics:**
- Franchisor initial investment: 200B+ won over 5 years
- Payoff: Established market, strong franchisees, minimal franchisee failure
- Timeline: 5-10 years to break even, 10+ years to significant profit

---

**Comparison Table: Four Models**

| Model | Initial Franchisor Investment | Risk | Return | Control | Timeline | Example |
|-------|------------------------------|------|--------|---------|----------|---------|
| Direct | High (100B+) | High | High (100%) | High | 5-7 yrs | Starbucks Korea |
| Master | Low (minimal) | Moderate | Moderate (3-5%) | Low | 3-5 yrs | KFC India |
| Joint Venture | High (50B+) | Moderate | Moderate (40-50%) | Moderate | 10+ yrs | McDonald's China |
| Company-Owned | Very High (200B+) | Moderate | High (over time) | High | 10+ yrs | Starbucks early strategy |

**Strategic Choice:** Which model for which situation?
- **Large, wealthy market (US, developed Asia):** Direct franchising or company-owned (franchisor can invest)
- **Emerging market with regulatory uncertainty:** Master franchising (local partner bears risk)
- **Large market with major competitor:** Joint venture with local partner for leverage
- **Strategic market:** Company-owned entry to control brand positioning

#### Segment 2: Standardization vs. Localization (13 minutes)

**Once you pick entry model, you face the SDS question on steroids: How much to standardize? How much to localize?**

**The Core Tension:**

**Standardization benefits:**
- Brand consistency globally
- Economies of scale (same suppliers, same operations)
- Quality control
- System efficiency

**Localization benefits:**
- Appeal to local tastes
- Comply with regulations
- Respect cultural preferences
- Compete with local brands

**The Tradeoff:**

Too much standardization: "We'll do exactly what we do in Korea. If Thai customers don't like it, that's their problem."
- Result: Product that doesn't sell, brand failure, loss of investment

Too much localization: "We'll customize everything for local market. No Korean DNA in the brand."
- Result: Loss of brand identity, cost explosion, system complexity

**Best practice: Core standardization, flexible localization**

**What MUST be standardized:**

1. **Core product quality:** If you're a pizza franchise, pizza must be excellent everywhere. No compromise.

2. **Brand identity & visual:** Logo, colors, store design language should be instantly recognizable. A Starbucks in Seoul should look like Starbucks in New York.

3. **Service philosophy:** If brand is "customer obsession," that must be true everywhere.

4. **Safety & hygiene standards:** Non-negotiable.

5. **Training & certification:** Standards for employee competence.

**What SHOULD be localized:**

1. **Menu variations:** Full menu? Limited menu? Added items?
   - Starbucks Korea has menu items not in US (red bean pastries, Korean rice cakes)
   - McDonald's India removes beef (respecting Hindu/Muslim customers)
   - KFC China added local spices

2. **Pricing:** Price point varies by market (purchasing power)
   - Starbucks coffee in Korea: Higher than US (labor expensive, premium market)
   - Starbucks Vietnam: Lower than Korea (emerging market, lower wages)

3. **Store design (secondary elements):** Decor, music, aesthetic should feel local while maintaining brand
   - Starbucks Seoul: Modern Korean design, Korean artists, Korean music
   - Starbucks Mumbai: Indian design elements, Indian art

4. **Marketing & messaging:** What resonates culturally?
   - US Starbucks: "Individualism, personal coffee choice"
   - Korea Starbucks: "Status, premium experience"
   - Vietnam Starbucks: "Modern aspirational brand"

5. **Staffing practices:** Labor laws, wage expectations, work culture differ
   - US Starbucks: Tips, flexible scheduling
   - Korea Starbucks: No tipping, structured hours (labor law)
   - Vietnam Starbucks: Wage is competitive advantage (lower than developed countries)

6. **Regulatory compliance:** Permits, health codes, employment law differ by country

**Real Example: 7-Eleven Global Adaptation**

7-Eleven operates in 20+ countries. Almost nothing is identical globally.

**US 7-Eleven:**
- Menu: Hot dogs, slurpees, american coffee
- Format: Small convenience store
- Revenue drivers: Gas, snacks, drinks

**Thailand 7-Eleven:**
- Menu: Pad Thai, mango sticky rice, Thai coffee
- Format: Central gathering place, places to sit
- Revenue drivers: Food, beverages, sit-in dining

**Japan 7-Eleven:**
- Menu: Bentos, onigiri, premium sake
- Format: Premium convenience store
- Revenue drivers: High-quality food, fresh inventory, restocking multiple times/day

**Korea 7-Eleven (GS25 competitor):**
- Menu: Kimbap, bibimbap, Korean snacks
- Format: Quick meal destination
- Revenue drivers: Prepared meals, delivery

**India 7-Eleven (trying to enter):**
- Menu: Samosa, dosa, Indian sweets
- Format: TBD (still experimenting)
- Revenue drivers: TBD

Same franchisor. Completely different execution by country. Why? Because 7-Eleven learned: **Local adaptation isn't weakness, it's strength.** The franchisee who respects local culture succeeds. The franchisee who imposes foreign model fails.

#### Segment 3: Case Studies—Expansion Models (17 minutes)

**Case 1: 7-Eleven Japan vs. Korea—Same Concept, Different Success**

**Background:**

7-Eleven is global convenience store chain. Japan and Korea both have 7-Eleven (and competing brands like Lawson, FamilyMart in Japan; CU, GS25 in Korea).

**Japan 7-Eleven (Highly Successful, 20,000+ locations):**

Entry model: Master franchising (initially), then shifted to company-operated
- 7-Eleven Japan is subsidiary of Seven & i Holdings (Japanese conglomerate)
- High standardization in operational discipline (quality, cleanliness, inventory)
- High localization in product (Japanese food, seasonal items, premium quality)
- Stores are gathering places, not just transactions (seating, services)

**Why successful:**
1. **Operational discipline:** 7-Eleven Japan is obsessed with supply chain. Stores are restocked multiple times/day. Inventory is current and fresh.
2. **Localization:** Menu is 80% Japanese. Food quality is premium. Customers see 7-Eleven as "convenience with respect."
3. **Real estate strategy:** Prime locations, often with residential access
4. **Franchisee quality:** High barriers to becoming franchisee. Training is extensive. Support is constant.

**Financial metrics:**
- Average location volume: Very high (70-100M yen/month vs. 30-40M in US)
- Franchisee profit: Strong (15-20% margins common)
- System health: Thriving. Waiting list to become franchisee.

---

**Korea 7-Eleven (Struggling, ~5,000 locations):**

Entry model: Direct franchising (7-Eleven tried to operate directly in Korea)
- 7-Eleven Korea is subsidiary of 7-Eleven Inc
- Initial strategy: Replicate Japan model (limited menu, global standards)
- Faced competition from local brands (CU, GS25, Emart24)

**Why struggling:**
1. **Wrong localization:** Didn't adapt menu to Korean preferences fast enough. Korean customers wanted Korean food, not global convenience store format.
2. **Operations complexity:** Tried to maintain global operations standards (strict inventory, limited SKUs) in market where speed and variety valued
3. **Real estate:** Locations less premium than Japan (lower quality areas, tougher real estate costs)
4. **Franchisee dissatisfaction:** Profit margins lower than competitors (due to limited localization). Franchisees unhappy.

**Financial metrics:**
- Average location volume: Lower (20-30M won/month vs. 40-50M for CU)
- Franchisee profit: Weaker (8-12% margins, vs. 15%+ for CU)
- System health: Declining. Franchisees exiting to CU/GS25.

**Key Lesson:** 7-Eleven succeeded in Japan by respecting Japanese culture and adapting aggressively. 7-Eleven failed in Korea by not adapting fast enough and ceding market to local brands that respected Korean preferences.

**What should 7-Eleven Korea do differently?**
1. Full menu localization (Korean food 60%+)
2. Shift operations model (embrace speed over standardization)
3. Franchisee support that competes with CU/GS25 (royalty cuts, better support)
4. Real estate focus on residential areas (where Korean customers congregate)

---

**Case 2: Korean Fried Chicken Going Global (BBQ Chicken, Nene Chicken) (2020-2026)**

**Background:**

Korean fried chicken (dakgangjung style) became trendy globally 2015-2020. Korean brands started expanding internationally.

**BBQ Chicken International Strategy:**

Entry model: Master franchising in Western markets, direct in Asia
- US: Master franchisee (BBQ Chicken USA)
- Southeast Asia: Direct franchising with local partnerships
- Strategy: Keep Korean brand identity, adapt menu partially for Western tastes

**Market performance (2024-2026):**
- Vietnam: Success (30+ locations, strong profit, expanding rapidly)
- Southeast Asia: Success (Thailand, Indonesia, Philippines growing)
- US: Moderate (15+ locations, learning market, slower growth)
- Japan: Slow (5+ locations, facing strong local competition)

**Why successful in Vietnam but slower in US?**
1. **Market readiness:** Vietnam is hungry for Korean food (K-content, aspirational). US already saturated with chicken chains.
2. **Localization:** BBQ adapted faster in Vietnam (adjusted spice, portion size) than in US (tried to keep Korean authentic at first)
3. **Master franchisee quality:** Vietnam master franchisee understood market. US master franchisee was learning market.
4. **Real estate:** Vietnam had available real estate in growth areas. US competitive for premium locations.

**Financial metrics:**
- Vietnam: Average location volume ~200M VND (~$9K/month), franchisee profit ~25% margin
- US: Average location volume ~$25K/month, franchisee profit ~8% margin

---

**Case 3: Nene Chicken—Failed US Entry, Successful Asia Expansion (2022-2026)**

**Background:**

Nene Chicken is Korean fried chicken, premium positioning.

**US Entry (2022-2023, then retrenched):**

Entry model: Direct franchising (Nene opened corporate office in LA)
- Targeted affluent Asian-American communities in California
- Maintained premium brand positioning ($20-25 per serving)
- Minimal localization (kept Korean menu, Korean operations)

**Result: Failed**
- Opened ~10 locations
- Franchisees struggling with low traffic, high rent, slow local brand awareness
- By 2024, most US franchisees exited or converted to other brands
- Nene closed US operations, refocused on Asia

**Why failed:**
1. **Wrong target market:** US chicken market is commoditized. Premium Korean chicken didn't command premium price in competitive market.
2. **Entry model mismatch:** Direct franchising requires heavy franchisor investment ($200B+). Nene didn't have capital or expertise for sustained US operations.
3. **Localization failure:** Didn't adapt to US market (menu, marketing, pricing strategy)
4. **Franchisee selection:** Recruited franchisees based on Korean background, not market expertise

---

**Southeast Asia Entry (2023-2026, successful):**

Entry model: Master franchising, then direct as network grows
- Vietnam: Master franchisee expanding rapidly (30+ locations planned)
- Thailand: Direct partnerships in Bangkok
- Indonesia: Master franchisee negotiating

**Why successful:**
1. **Right target market:** Southeast Asia hungry for Korean brands. Premium positioning viable.
2. **Entry model:** Master franchising reduced Nene's risk and investment
3. **Localization:** Adapted menu faster than US (clearer target market = clearer localization)
4. **Franchisee selection:** Chose experienced master franchisees, not novices

**Current trajectory:** Nene will likely have 200+ Southeast Asia locations by 2030. Zero US presence.

---

### Summary (5 minutes)

**Key Learnings:**

1. **Four entry models exist:** Direct, Master, JV, Company-Owned. Each has different risk-return profile.

2. **Model choice depends on:** Market attractiveness, franchisor capital availability, local partner quality, strategic importance

3. **Standardization vs. Localization:** Core (quality, brand, service, safety) must be standardized. Peripheral (menu, pricing, design, marketing) should be localized.

4. **7-Eleven Japan vs. Korea:** Japan succeeded by aggressive localization. Korea struggled by insufficient localization. Local adaptation is competitive advantage, not dilution.

5. **Korean brands globally:** BBQ Chicken successful in Vietnam through master franchising + localization. Nene Chicken failed in US by overestimating market demand and underestimating localization needs. Nene thriving in Southeast Asia.

6. **Strategic principle:** Enter markets where your brand resonates AND where you can execute. Better to dominate one market (Korea, Vietnam for Korean brands) than spread thin across weak markets (US).

### Vocabulary Emphasis

- **Direct franchising:** Franchisor directly recruits and manages franchisees in new market
- **Master franchising:** Franchisor partners with local master franchisee who manages territory
- **Joint venture:** Franchisor and local partner are equal partners, share investment and control
- **Company-owned entry:** Franchisor opens company-owned stores to prove model before franchising
- **Localization:** Adapting product, service, or operations to local market preferences
- **Standardization:** Maintaining consistent brand, quality, and operations globally
- **Geographic arbitrage:** Profiting by deploying capital in high-growth, low-saturation markets
- **Market readiness:** Degree to which market is receptive to product/brand

### Common Mistakes to Watch For

1. **Assuming brand success at home guarantees international success**
   - Reality: Market differences are fundamental. US chicken market ≠ Vietnam chicken market
   - Strong brand helps, but market fit matters more

2. **Confusing "localization" with "abandoning brand identity"**
   - Reality: You can respect local culture while maintaining brand essence
   - Starbucks Korea has Korean menu but is still clearly Starbucks

3. **Overestimating franchisor's ability to manage franchisees in unfamiliar market**
   - Reality: Direct franchising works if franchisor understands market deeply (Starbucks did, Nene didn't)
   - Master franchising is safer when franchisor lacks local expertise

4. **Underestimating execution complexity of operations across borders**
   - Reality: Supply chains are different, labor laws are different, customer preferences are different
   - Company-owned entry allows learning before betting on franchisees

5. **Assuming entry model is permanent**
   - Reality: McDonald's started with joint ventures, shifted to franchising as market matured
   - Franchisor should be willing to change model as market evolves

### Extension Questions

**If student asks:** "Why do franchises prefer master franchising in emerging markets?"
**Answer:** Lower risk for franchisor. Master franchisee understands local market, bears execution risk. If market doesn't work, franchisor hasn't invested 100B+ directly. Master franchisee shares risk because they benefit from success.

**If student asks:** "Isn't master franchising just a way for franchisor to avoid responsibility?"
**Answer:** Partially true. It's risk avoidance. But it's justified in unfamiliar markets where franchisor lacks expertise. The tradeoff: Lower return for lower risk. Smart franchisors use master franchising early, then shift to direct franchising once market is proven.

**If student asks:** "How do franchisors decide what to localize?"
**Answer:** Test framework:
1. Is this essential to brand identity? If YES → standardize
2. Does local market strongly prefer different? If YES → localize
3. Can we afford both? If NO → prioritize brand over localization
4. What's our competitive disadvantage if we don't localize? If HIGH → localize
5. Can franchisees profitably operate with our standard? If NO → localize
