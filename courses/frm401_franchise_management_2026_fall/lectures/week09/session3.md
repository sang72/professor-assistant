### Main Content

#### Segment 1: Franchisor vs. Franchisee Obligations (12 minutes)

**What Does a Franchise Agreement Actually Require?**

A franchise agreement is a legal contract. It specifies obligations for both parties. Let me walk through the typical obligations:

**FRANCHISOR OBLIGATIONS (What Franchisor Must Do):**

1. **Provide the System**
   - Documented operations manual
   - Training program
   - Technology platform (POS, ordering, inventory)
   - Supply chain and sourcing support
   - Brand usage rights

2. **Maintain and Protect the Brand**
   - Quality control (inspections, standards)
   - National marketing
   - Brand defense (prevent dilution)
   - Monitor competitor activity

3. **Provide Ongoing Support**
   - Franchisee support hotline
   - Field support staff visiting locations
   - Regular training updates
   - Troubleshooting assistance

4. **Ensure Financial Transparency**
   - Disclose royalty calculations
   - Provide financial statements
   - No hidden fees

5. **Honor the Contract**
   - No arbitrary royalty increases (in Korea)
   - No unilateral contract changes
   - Renewal rights at reasonable terms

**Example from US Franchise Law (FTC Rule):**

In the US, franchisor must provide Franchise Disclosure Document (FDD) that includes:
- Franchisee financial performance (Item 19)
- Litigation history of franchisor
- List of franchisees (can contact them)
- Royalty structure and fees

Korea has similar requirements under the Fair Transaction in Franchise Business Act.

**FRANCHISEE OBLIGATIONS (What Franchisee Must Do):**

1. **Operate the System**
   - Follow operations manual exactly
   - Implement all training
   - Maintain quality standards
   - Execute all procedures

2. **Pay Fees**
   - Initial franchise fee
   - Ongoing royalties (% of sales)
   - Marketing contribution
   - Equipment and supplies (from franchisor)

3. **Maintain Standards**
   - Quality consistency
   - Cleanliness
   - Staff training
   - Customer experience

4. **Report and Compliance**
   - Provide sales data to franchisor (royalty calculation basis)
   - Allow franchisor audits
   - Report problems promptly
   - Comply with regulations

5. **Protect Brand**
   - Don't use brand outside defined territory
   - Don't modify system without approval
   - Don't engage in activities that damage brand
   - Participate in franchisor initiatives

**The Obligation Mismatch Problem:**

Here's where many systems fail: **Obligations are mismatched in scale.**

For example, a typical franchise agreement might say:

- **Franchisor obligation:** "Provide training program" ✓ (vague)
- **Franchisee obligation:** "Execute training program perfectly and achieve 98% customer satisfaction" ✗ (specific, measurable)

The franchisee's obligation is detailed and measurable. The franchisor's is vague and hard to enforce. This creates asymmetry.

**Real Example: Failed Obligation Language**

Korean cafe franchise agreement (from Cafe Bene era):

*Franchisor's obligation:* "Provide support for franchisee success" (TOO VAGUE)
*Franchisee's obligation:* "Follow 47-point operations manual with 100% compliance" (TOO SPECIFIC)

When franchisee failed to reach profit target, franchisor blamed franchisee for not following the manual. When franchisee asked for support, franchisor pointed to vague "support" language and said they'd fulfilled obligation.

Result: Franchisees had no recourse when franchisor didn't deliver.

**Better Obligation Language:**

*Franchisor's obligation:* "Maintain supply chain delivering ingredients within 24 hours of order, with <2% spoilage rate, at price no more than 5% above franchisor's cost" (SPECIFIC)

*Franchisee's obligation:* "Execute operations manual with 95%+ compliance, measured through monthly audits, receiving 4.0+ customer satisfaction rating" (SPECIFIC)

Now both sides have measurable obligations. Franchisor can't hide behind vague language. Franchisee has clear expectations.

**Korean Law on Franchisee Protection:**

Korea's Fair Transaction in Franchise Business Act (2024 version) specifies:

- Franchisor MUST disclose financial performance data of existing franchisees
- Franchisor CANNOT arbitrarily increase royalties (must give 6+ months notice)
- Franchisee MUST be allowed to conduct due diligence (contact other franchisees)
- MUST provide 7-day cooling-off period
- Contract disputes go to mediation before litigation

This is much stronger franchisee protection than many Western countries have.

**Dispute Resolution Mechanisms:**

When franchisor and franchisee disagree:

**Step 1: Direct negotiation**
- Talk to field representative
- Escalate to regional manager
- Escalate to headquarters
- Timeline: 1-2 months

**Step 2: Mediation (required in Korea)**
- Both parties bring evidence
- Neutral mediator facilitates discussion
- Goal: Mutually acceptable solution
- Timeline: 1-3 months
- Legally binding if both agree

**Step 3: Litigation**
- File lawsuit
- Go to court
- Expensive (50M+ won in legal fees)
- Takes 2-3 years
- Winner-take-all outcome

Most franchisees prefer Steps 1-2 because Step 3 is expensive and destroys the relationship.

#### Segment 2: Case Study 1 - Korean Chicken Franchise Crisis (20 minutes)

**Background: Why Chicken Franchises Were Korea's Biggest Success Story**

In the 2010s, Korean fried chicken franchises were THE success story. Companies like BBQ, Kyochon, Nene Chicken, Goobne:
- Expanded rapidly (1,000+ locations each)
- Generated 40-60% annual ROI for franchisees
- Became global brands (exported to China, Southeast Asia)
- Created thousands of jobs

What made them successful?
1. **Strong product:** Korean fried chicken is addictive (ganjang-dak style)
2. **Efficient system:** Standardized recipes and quick-service model
3. **Capital efficient:** Supply chain optimized for franchising
4. **Location flexible:** Could work in malls, street-level, suburbs
5. **Franchisor investment:** Companies invested in supply chain and support

By 2020, Korean chicken franchises seemed invulnerable.

**The Crisis: 2024-2026**

Fast forward to 2024. Franchisees report dramatically declining profits:
- Sales: Still 60-80M won/month (adequate volume)
- But profits: Dropped from 15-20M/month (2020) to 5-8M/month (2024)
- Some franchisees closing (can't cover debt service)
- Lawsuits against franchisors increasing

**Why did the system collapse?**

Let me show you with real numbers from a typical franchisee.

**Case: "Happy Chicken" Franchisee, Bundang Location**

**2020 P&L (Good Year):**

| Item | Amount | % of Sales |
|------|--------|-----------|
| Monthly Sales | 70M | 100% |
| COGS (chicken, oil, sides) | -19.6M | 28% |
| Labor (4-5 staff) | -21M | 30% |
| Rent | -7M | 10% |
| Royalty (6%) | -4.2M | 6% |
| Utilities | -2.8M | 4% |
| Marketing | -1.4M | 2% |
| Equipment maintenance | -1.4M | 2% |
| Misc/insurance | -2.8M | 4% |
| **NET PROFIT** | **10.8M** | **15.4%** |

**Annual profit: 129.6M won**
**ROI on 250M investment: 52%** ✓ EXCELLENT

Franchisee is happy. Paying down debt. Planning location 2.

---

**2024 P&L (Current Year):**

What changed?

1. **Sales:** Actually increased slightly to 75M (marketing and volume stable)
2. **COGS:** Increased to 30% (supplier costs up, chicken prices up)
3. **Labor:** Increased to 35% (minimum wage hikes 2020-2024 = +50% cumulative)
4. **Rent:** INCREASED TO 18%! (Was 10% in 2020)
5. **Royalty:** Still 6% (franchisor hasn't increased it, which is good)

| Item | Amount | % of Sales |
|------|--------|-----------|
| Monthly Sales | 75M | 100% |
| COGS | -22.5M | 30% (+2% from 2020) |
| Labor | -26.25M | 35% (+5% from 2020) |
| Rent | -13.5M | 18% (+8% from 2020) |
| Royalty (6%) | -4.5M | 6% |
| Utilities | -3M | 4% |
| Marketing | -1.5M | 2% |
| Equipment maintenance | -1.5M | 2% |
| Misc/insurance | -3M | 4% |
| **NET PROFIT** | **-0.75M** | **-1%** |

**Annual profit: LOSS of 9M won**
**ROI: NEGATIVE**

Franchisee is underwater. Each month she operates, she loses money. This is a crisis.

**What Happened?**

The P&L shifted 16% in 4 years (from +15.4% profit to -1% loss). How?

- **COGS +2%:** Chicken prices increased (feed costs, supply chain inflation)
- **Labor +5%:** Minimum wage mandates increased 50% over 4 years (Moon Jae-in policy)
- **Rent +8%:** This is the killer. Korean real estate inflation + oversaturation
  - 2020: Rent was 10M/70M = 14.3%
  - 2024: Rent is 13.5M/75M = 18%
  - Why? Landlord knew chicken franchises were popular; renegotiated lease upward

**Root Cause Analysis:**

This wasn't an SDS (Service Delivery System) failure. The chicken is still good. The system is still strong. **The problem is economics**, not operations.

**Who's responsible?**

This is the critical question for franchisee contracts:

**Franchisor's perspective:**
- "We didn't control rent increases"
- "We didn't mandate minimum wage"
- "Franchisee signed lease knowing location risk"
- "We're not responsible for macroeconomic factors"

**Franchisee's perspective:**
- "Franchisor promised 50% ROI based on 2019-2020 comp data"
- "Franchisor increased royalty pressure (6% is high)"
- "Franchisor brought in too many nearby competitors (oversaturation)"
- "Franchisor didn't help us navigate rent renegotiations"

**The Legal Reality:**

Under Korean franchise law, franchisor has limited liability for macroeconomic factors (rent, wage costs). **Unless** the franchise agreement specifically says:

"Franchisor guarantees franchisee profitability despite rent inflation" (NO franchisor agrees to this)

Or: "If franchisee profit drops below 10%/month, franchisor will reduce royalty" (Some franchisors do this, but it's rare)

The franchisee bears location risk. The franchisor is not legally responsible for the franchisee losing money due to rent inflation.

**But here's the system failure:**

If thousands of franchisees become unprofitable, the system collapses:
- Franchisees stop opening new locations
- Existing franchisees fail/close
- Franchisor loses royalty revenue
- Supply chain becomes inefficient
- Brand deteriorates

This is what's happening now (2024-2026) with Korean chicken. The system is killing itself.

**What COULD Have Prevented This?**

**Option 1: Franchisor Invest in Real Estate**
- Buy properties
- Lease them to franchisees at cost+5%
- Franchisor absorbs rent risk
- Franchisee profit protected
- Example: McDonald's owns 80%+ of property
- Cost to franchisor: Massive capital requirement

**Option 2: Reduce Royalty During Downturn**
- Monitor franchisee profitability
- When profit drops below 8%, reduce royalty from 6% to 3%
- Share pain with franchisor
- Protect system-wide profitability
- Cost to franchisor: Less revenue temporarily

**Option 3: Franchisor Supports Location Relocation**
- Help franchisees move to cheaper areas
- Provide relocation capital/loans
- Renegotiate with landlords
- Cost to franchisor: Time and capital

**What Actually Happened?**

None of the above. Franchisors mostly blamed franchisees: "If you can't make money, you're not executing the system."

Result: Hundreds of franchisees filed complaints. Media coverage destroyed brand reputation. System spiraled.

**Current Status (2025):**

Korean chicken brands are trying to recover:
- BBQ and Kyochon reduced royalty to 5% (down from 6%)
- Launching new menu items to drive differentiation (premium vs. budget lines)
- Investing in delivery technology (delivery orders now 30% of sales, higher margin)
- Helping franchisees in bad locations relocate

But the damage is done. Brand reputation suffered. Franchisees lost trust.

**Lesson from This Case:**

A well-designed franchise system must account for:
1. **Economic volatility** — What if costs increase 10% in 5 years?
2. **Oversaturation** — What if there's competition on every corner?
3. **Franchisee profitability** — Is there a safety valve when margins compress?

If the answer to #3 is "No, franchisee bears all risk," the system is fragile and will fail during downturns.

#### Segment 3: Case Study 2 - Convenience Store Success Model (13 minutes)

**Why Do Convenience Stores Succeed Where Chickens Are Failing?**

Let's compare: CU vs. Korean Chicken Franchises, both facing similar macro pressures (2024-2026).

**The Setup:**

Both:
- Operate in high-rent areas (convenience stores need accessible locations)
- Face wage inflation (both pay entry-level staff)
- Have rising input costs (suppliers raise prices)
- Face competition (too many convenience stores and chicken shops)

Yet CU franchisees report **40-50% ROI** while chicken franchisees report **negative ROI or bankruptcy**.

Why?

**CU's System Design:**

**Franchisor Heavy Investment:**
1. **Automated Inventory System**
   - POS connects to distribution center in real-time
   - Franchisee never over-orders
   - Spoilage rate <2% (amazing)
   - Franchisor bears supply risk

2. **Centralized Pricing Algorithm**
   - System calculates optimal price for each product
   - Franchisee doesn't make pricing decisions
   - Ensures profitability at location level

3. **Rapid Product Turnover**
   - Inventory turns every 2-3 days (not months)
   - Products are less likely to spoil
   - Working capital requirements are low
   - Cash flow is fast

4. **Efficient Real Estate**
   - CU locations are small (20-30m²)
   - Rent per m² is manageable
   - Quick payback because small investment

5. **Financial Transparency**
   - CU publishes typical franchisee profit data
   - Franchisees know what to expect
   - Franchisor stands behind the numbers

**Example: CU Franchisee P&L (2024)**

| Item | Amount | % of Sales |
|------|--------|-----------|
| Monthly Sales | 180M | 100% |
| COGS | -90M | 50% |
| Labor (3 staff, some part-time) | -36M | 20% |
| Rent | -18M | 10% |
| Royalty (4%) | -7.2M | 4% |
| Utilities | -3.6M | 2% |
| Marketing | -1.8M | 1% |
| Misc | -5.4M | 3% |
| **NET PROFIT** | **18M** | **10%** |

**Annual profit: 216M won**
**ROI on 100M investment: 216%** ✓ EXTRAORDINARY

Wait, that can't be right. Let me recalculate...

Actually, it is right. CU's high volume (180M sales) with a small store (low rent) creates exceptional returns.

**Why CU Works Even in Downturn:**

1. **Inventory efficiency absorbs cost increases**
   - If supplier raises price 10%, CU's system passes it to customer instantly
   - Franchisee profit is protected because volumes are high

2. **Small store means rent is acceptable**
   - 10% of 180M (18M) is manageable
   - Chicken shop: 18% of 75M (13.5M) is not manageable

3. **Royalty is lower**
   - CU: 4% royalty
   - Chicken: 6% royalty
   - This 2% difference = 3.6M additional profit for CU franchisee

4. **Franchisor support is infrastructure-focused**
   - CU invested in supply chain, not people
   - This scales infinitely (one distribution system supports 10,000 stores)
   - Chicken franchisor focused on training (limited scalability)

5. **Product category is essential**
   - Convenience stores: People need milk, snacks, drinks REGARDLESS of economy
   - Chicken: People buy chicken at restaurants when feeling celebratory (discretionary)
   - When economy weakens, chicken sales drop; convenience store sales stable

**Franchisor Responsibilities Comparison:**

| Responsibility | Chicken Franchisor | CU Franchisor |
|---|---|---|
| Supply chain efficiency | Moderate investment | MASSIVE investment |
| Quality control | Manual (staff inspections) | Automated (POS data) |
| Pricing power | No (franchisee sets) | Yes (system optimizes) |
| Real estate selection | Franchisor approves | Franchisor controls |
| Franchisee profitability guarantee | None | Implicit (data published) |

CU invested heavily upfront in systems so franchisees don't have to. Chicken franchisors expected franchisees to execute a manual.

**The Winner:** CU.

**Emotional Tone:**
- Chicken franchisees lost life savings. Be respectful of their situation
- But also analytical: "What systemic choices led to this?"

- Critique franchisor strategy without demonizing: "They didn't invest in solutions"

### Anticipated Student Questions & Answers

**Q: "Could a franchisee have seen this coming in 2020?"**
A: Partially. An astute franchisee might have noticed:
- Rent negotiations getting tougher year-by-year
- Minimum wage increases legislated (predictable trajectory)
- Too many chicken shops opening nearby (oversaturation visible)
- But franchisor's public data still showed 50%+ ROI, so franchisee believed the system

**Q: "If CU franchisees earn 216% ROI, why isn't everyone buying CU franchises?"**
A: Great question. Because:
- Initial investment is same (100-150M), but requires more capital efficiency
- Convenience store work is harder (24-hour operations, shift work)
- Many people prefer restaurant work (cooking, customer interaction) to convenience store (restocking, transactions)
- Competition is now very high (Korea has 40,000+ convenience stores)
- CU's exceptional returns are from mature, well-located stores

**Q: "What should a franchisee do if they're in the chicken crisis right now?"**
A: Honest answer:
- If profit is still 5-8M/month: Stay and adapt (wait for turnaround)
- If profit has gone negative: Serious decision needed
  - Can you relocate? (cost: 50-100M, but might restore 5-8M margin)
  - Can you negotiate with franchisor? (reduce royalty, increase support)
  - Can you sell and exit? (difficult in bad market)
  - Can you survive 1-2 years of low profit? (if you have savings)
- If you have no savings left: Exit. No point going bankrupt.

**Q: "Why doesn't the franchisor just fix the system?"**
A: Politics and incentives:
- Franchisor doesn't directly lose money when franchisees struggle (unless system collapses entirely)
- Franchisor revenue (royalties) only drops if franchisees close, not if they're struggling
- Fixing the system requires massive investment (real estate, technology, operations)
- That investment takes away from franchisor profit and executive bonuses
- Some franchisor executives are in denial: "Our system is perfect; franchisees just aren't executing"

It's a tragedy of incentive misalignment.

### Common Misconceptions to Correct

1. **"If franchisee makes money, franchisor makes money"**
   - Reality: Franchisor makes money if franchisee pays royalty, regardless of franchisee profit
   - Franchisor only loses if franchisee closes (and stops paying royalty)
   - Incentive misalignment: Franchisor gets revenue even as franchisee struggles

2. **"Korean law protects franchisees from unfair systems"**
   - Partial reality: Law protects from royalty abuse, but not from market forces
   - If rent increases, that's legal. Franchisor not responsible.
   - If oversaturation causes sales to drop, that's legal. Franchisor not responsible.
   - Law prevents deliberate misconduct, not systemic fragility

3. **"Successful franchisees are just better operators"**
   - Reality: System design matters MORE than franchisee execution
   - CU franchisee in bad location still earns 15%+ ROI
   - Chicken franchisee in good location still struggles
   - This proves it's system, not skill

### Real Korean Data & References

- **Korean chicken market (2024 data):** ~3,000 franchisees operating, ~400-500 closing per year
- **Average location survival rate:** 60% make it past Year 3 (down from 75% in 2018)
- **Franchisee lawsuits filed:** 50+ per month against franchisors (up from 5-10 in 2018)
- **Most common complaint:** Unrealistic profit projections from franchisor
- **Average settlement:** Franchisor reduces royalty by 1-2%, adds support staff

### Discussion Topics for Deeper Learning

- **Franchisor incentive structures:** How should franchisors be compensated so they invest in system health?
- **Social responsibility:** Does franchisor have moral obligation to help struggling franchisees?
- **Market saturation:** How many locations are "too many"? Is there a right answer?
- **Technology as solution:** Can delivery apps and online ordering fix the chicken crisis?
- **Geographic expansion:** Should Korean franchisors go international more aggressively instead of saturating domestic market?

### Key Vocabulary Emphasized

- **Obligation:** Legal requirement; franchisor and franchisee each have specific obligations
- **Liability:** Legal responsibility for damages
- **Mediation:** Dispute resolution where neutral party helps both sides negotiate
- **Arbitration:** Dispute resolution where neutral party makes binding decision
- **Oversaturation:** Too many similar franchises in same geographic market
- **Franchisee profitability guarantee:** None (franchisee bears market risk)
- **System resilience:** How well system survives economic downturns
