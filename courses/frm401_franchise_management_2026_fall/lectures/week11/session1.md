## SECTION 2: FULL LECTURE SCRIPT (English)

### Opening (3 minutes)

Good morning. Welcome to Week 11. We've spent the last two weeks analyzing franchising from a purely financial perspective: unit economics, profitability, investment returns.

But here's what data can't capture: **What happens when things fall apart?**

When a franchisee is struggling, they get angry. When a franchisor feels their system is being undermined, they retaliate. When trust breaks down, even a profitable franchise relationship can become a legal nightmare.

This week we're studying **franchise conflict**—how it starts, how to prevent it, and how to resolve it when prevention fails.

This is arguably more important than financial analysis. Because the best franchise system, the most profitable location, the most dedicated franchisee—all of it can be destroyed by conflict.

By the end of today, you'll understand why franchises fail not because of bad economics, but because of relationship breakdown.

Let's start with the question: **What causes franchise conflicts?**

### Main Content

#### Segment 1: Conflict Sources & Dispute Categories (13 minutes)

**Where Do Conflicts Come From?**

Most franchise conflicts fall into four categories:

**1. PERFORMANCE DISPUTES**

The franchisee is supposed to do something and isn't doing it.

Examples:
- Franchisee not maintaining quality standards (dirty store, slow service)
- Franchisee not following system requirements (using non-approved suppliers)
- Franchisee not investing in required renovations or equipment upgrades
- Franchisee not hitting sales targets (franchisor feels ripped off by royalties)

**Real Example: Korean Coffee Franchise, 2024**

A coffee franchise in Seoul requires all franchisees to:
- Use only the franchisor's coffee beans (to ensure quality, save costs)
- Maintain store appearance with quarterly deep cleaning
- Staff minimum 2 baristas during peak hours
- Report daily sales to headquarters

A franchisee in a busy subway location:
- **Violated quality standards:** Used cheaper beans from distributor (cut COGS by 20%)
- **Ignored cleaning mandate:** Focused on speed over cleanliness (customer complaints rose)
- **Reduced staffing:** Operated with 1 barista during lunch (customer wait times 20+ minutes)
- **Falsified reports:** Reported lower sales to franchisor to justify lower royalty payments

The franchisor discovered violations after customer complaints. Conflict escalated. Eventually franchisee was terminated.

**Key insight:** The franchisee felt the system was too expensive (high bean costs, staffing requirements). Rather than negotiate, they cheated. This destroyed trust.

**2. FINANCIAL DISPUTES**

Disagreements about money—royalties, fees, charges, profitability claims.

Examples:
- Franchisor's profit projections were wildly optimistic; franchisee is losing money
- Franchisor changed pricing without notice (raised royalty rate, changed supply prices)
- Franchisor charged unexpected fees (technology fees, rebranding costs)
- Franchisee disputes royalty calculation (claims sales were lower than franchisor recorded)
- Franchisor demands rent payment or deposits that weren't disclosed upfront

**Real Example: Korean Convenience Store Crisis, 2025-2026**

CU franchisees, experiencing rent inflation and COGS increases (as we discussed in Week 10), requested:
- Royalty reduction from 3% to 2%
- Temporary supply price cuts
- Help with rent renegotiation

CU's response: "You signed the agreement. Royalty is fixed. Pay it."

Result: Hundreds of franchisees are unprofitable. Many are closing. Some are suing CU, claiming franchisor didn't provide adequate support.

This is becoming a major legal issue in Korea 2025-2026.

**Key insight:** Financial disputes are the hardest to resolve because one side's gain is the other side's loss. CU's refusal to share pain created systemic crisis.

**3. OPERATIONAL/SYSTEM DISPUTES**

Disagreements about how the system should be run.

Examples:
- Franchisor wants to introduce new menu items; franchisees resist (fear of complexity, cost)
- Franchisor requires technology upgrade; franchisees see it as unnecessary expense
- Franchisor changes supplier; franchisees prefer old supplier
- Franchisor mandates national marketing campaign; franchisees want local autonomy
- Franchisor introduces new delivery model; franchisees feel it cannibalizes their sales

**Real Example: Korean Fried Chicken Brand, 2024**

A fried chicken franchise introduced delivery-only locations. Existing franchisees with dine-in stores felt threatened:
- New delivery-only competitors are nearby, stealing their customers
- Franchisor is cannibalizing their sales
- Delivery requires different menu, training, operations

Existing franchisees demanded: Either restrict delivery locations OR reduce our royalty rate to compensate.

Franchisor pushed forward anyway. Result: Franchisee association filed complaint with government, demanding compensation.

**Key insight:** System expansion often creates conflict with existing franchisees who feel their investment is being devalued.

**4. TRUST/RELATIONSHIP DISPUTES**

Conflicts rooted in broken trust, poor communication, or perceived unfairness.

Examples:
- Franchisee feels unsupported by franchisor (inadequate training, slow response to problems)
- Franchisor feels franchisee is disloyal (copying system, considering competitor)
- Franchisee discovers franchisor is opening company-owned stores in same territory
- Franchisor discovers franchisee is revealing system secrets to competitors
- Miscommunication about expectations (franchisee thought system guaranteed profit; franchisor promised only "opportunity")

**Real Example: Korean Bakery Franchise, 2023-2024**

A franchisee was recruited with promise: "Proven system. Average franchisee earns 30M/month profit."

After 6 months of operation, franchisee's profit was 5M/month. Why? The franchisor's "average" was based on 5 locations in prime areas. The franchisee's location was suburban.

Franchisee felt deceived. Relationship deteriorated. Franchisee eventually closed, taking the franchisor to court for misrepresentation.

Franchisor's defense: "We didn't guarantee results. Those are just averages."

Outcome: Lengthy legal battle, settlement (franchisor paid compensation).

**Key insight:** Trust issues are hardest to fix. Once broken, even financially viable franchises collapse.

---

**Summary: The Four Dispute Categories**

| Type | Root Cause | Example | Difficulty |
|------|-----------|---------|-----------|
| Performance | Franchisee not following system | Quality violations, staffing | Medium (fixable with enforcement) |
| Financial | Disagreement about money | Royalty disputes, hidden fees | Hard (zero-sum game) |
| Operational | System change threatens franchisee | Delivery cannibalization | Hard (competing interests) |
| Trust/Relationship | Broken trust, miscommunication | Misrepresented projections | Very hard (emotional, legal) |

#### Segment 2: Resolution Methods (16 minutes)

**When conflict arises, there are five ways to resolve it (from cheapest to most expensive):**

**Method 1: Direct Negotiation**

The franchisee and franchisor talk directly, trying to find mutually acceptable solution.

**How it works:**
- Franchisee raises issue (verbally or in writing)
- Franchisor and franchisee meet
- They discuss problem and brainstorm solutions
- Ideally they reach agreement

**Example Solution:**
CU franchisee says: "I'm unprofitable due to rent inflation."
CU might respond: "We'll renegotiate supplier contracts to reduce COGS by 1 percentage point. That should help."
If franchisee accepts, problem solved. They stay partners.

**Pros:**
- Fast (days to weeks)
- Cheap (no lawyers, no fees)
- Maintains relationship
- Flexible (can agree to creative solutions)

**Cons:**
- Only works if both sides want to cooperate
- If power imbalance (franchisor much stronger), franchisee has little leverage
- May drag on if either side is unreasonable

**Success rate in practice:** ~40% (many franchisors ignore franchisee complaints)

---

**Method 2: Mediation**

A neutral third party (mediator) helps franchisor and franchisee reach agreement.

**How it works:**
- Both sides agree to hire mediator
- Mediator is professional neutral (paid by both sides equally)
- Mediator meets with both sides separately, understands each position
- Mediator suggests creative compromises
- If both sides agree, they sign settlement agreement
- If they don't agree, mediator can't force anything—it fails

**Example Solution:**
Mediator to CU franchisee: "CU says they can reduce COGS 1%, but not more."
Mediator to CU: "Franchisee needs 2% COGS reduction or they'll close."
Mediator: "What if CU reduces COGS 1.5% AND reduces royalty by 0.5% (from 3% to 2.5%) for 12 months?"
Both sides: "That works."

**Pros:**
- More effective than direct negotiation (neutral party helps find middle ground)
- Still faster and cheaper than litigation
- Maintains relationship (mediator focuses on preservation)
- Confidential (not public like court)

**Cons:**
- Requires cooperation from both sides (either side can refuse to participate)
- Mediator has no power to enforce agreement
- More expensive than negotiation (mediator fees: typically 500K-2M won for Korean disputes)

**Success rate in practice:** ~60% (higher than direct negotiation because neutral party helps)

---

**Method 3: Arbitration**

A neutral arbitrator hears both sides and makes a binding decision (like a judge, but faster).

**How it works:**
- Both sides agree to arbitration (or contract requires it)
- They select arbitrator (usually retired judge or industry expert)
- Both sides present arguments and evidence
- Arbitrator makes binding decision
- Loser can't appeal (unlike court)

**Example:**
CU franchisee demands 2% COGS reduction. CU refuses.
Arbitrator hears both sides, reviews financial data.
Arbitrator decides: "CU must reduce COGS by 1.25%, effective immediately. Franchisee continues paying current royalty."

**Pros:**
- Faster than court (2-4 months vs. 1-3 years)
- Expert decision-maker (arbitrator knows franchising industry)
- Final and binding (no appeal)
- More confidential than court
- Less expensive than litigation (but still significant: 5-20M won)

**Cons:**
- Requires both sides to agree upfront
- Arbitrator's decision is binding (you can't appeal even if you think it's wrong)
- No guarantee of "fair" outcome (depends on arbitrator)
- One side feels like loser (no collaborative solution)

**Success rate in practice:** ~100% if both sides agree to arbitration (process succeeds; whether outcome satisfies both is different)

---

**Method 4: Litigation (Court)**

The franchisee sues the franchisor (or franchisor sues franchisee) in court.

**How it works:**
- One side files lawsuit
- Both sides hire lawyers, gather evidence, conduct discovery
- Case goes to trial (or settlement reached before trial)
- Judge decides winner
- Loser can appeal to higher court
- Process takes 1-3 years

**Example:**
CU franchisee sues CU, claiming unfair franchise practices under Korean Franchise Act.
- Franchisee's claim: "CU didn't disclose financial risks. CU promised AUV of 200M but I'm only doing 150M due to CU's own cannibalization."
- CU's defense: "We disclosed all required information. Franchisee failed due to poor location choice and weak management."
- Trial takes 18 months
- Judge awards: Franchisee gets 50M compensation (partial win for both)
- Franchisee appeals. Takes another 18 months.
- Final outcome: Franchisee gets 50M, relationship is destroyed.

**Pros:**
- Formal process with clear rules
- Judge has legal authority to make binding decision
- Can result in large compensation awards
- Public record (deters future unfair practices)

**Cons:**
- Extremely expensive (500M-2B won in legal fees for major cases)
- Takes years (not suitable for urgent cash flow problems)
- Destroys franchisor-franchisee relationship (they'll never work together again)
- Winner takes most, loser gets nothing (zero-sum game)
- Public disclosure of system problems (bad for brand)
- Unpredictable outcome (judges sometimes make questionable decisions)

**Success rate in practice:** ~50% (half win, half lose by definition; many cases settle before judgment)

---

**When to Use Each Method:**

| Situation | Recommended Method | Why |
|-----------|-------------------|-----|
| Small disagreement, good relationship | Negotiation | Fast, cheap, preserves relationship |
| Moderate conflict, both want to resolve | Mediation | Neutral party helps find middle ground |
| Significant dispute, both sides trust process | Arbitration | Faster, cheaper than court, binding |
| Major breach, relationship broken | Litigation | Only option when cooperation impossible |
| Franchisor abused power, systemic issue | Litigation + Regulatory Complaint | Need legal precedent, court action, government investigation |

#### Segment 3: Case Studies (13 minutes)

**Case 1: Quick Lube—Classic Conflict, United States (1992-1996)**

This is a famous case in franchise law.

**Background:**

Quick Lube is a U.S. car maintenance franchise (oil changes, filter replacements, etc.). In the early 1990s, they had ~500 franchisees. System was profitable.

**The Conflict:**

Quick Lube headquaters decided to introduce "transmission fluid exchange" service—a higher-margin upsell. Cost to franchisee: ~100K USD in equipment. Monthly revenue potential: ~5K USD.

Franchisor mandated: All franchisees MUST buy equipment and offer service by Jan 1992.

Franchisees' problem:
- Not all locations had demand for transmission service
- 100K USD equipment investment with no guarantee of payoff
- Franchisor refused to provide training on how to sell the service
- Some franchisees already had suppliers for transmission work

Franchisees' reaction:
- Asked franchisor to make equipment optional (or subsidize it)
- Franchisor refused

Escalation:
- Franchisees formed association
- Association filed complaint with Federal Trade Commission (FTC)
- Filed class-action lawsuit claiming unfair franchise practices

Franchisor's position:
- "Equipment mandate is in the franchise agreement. We have the right."
- "Franchisees are free to leave if they don't like it."

Outcome:
- Case went to court
- Judge ruled AGAINST franchisor: "Franchise agreement requires good faith. Mandating expensive equipment without training or revenue guarantee is unreasonable."
- Franchisor had to compensate franchisees for equipment costs (~5M USD total)
- Franchisor's reputation damaged; harder to recruit new franchisees

**Lesson for this case:**

Franchisors have power, but not unlimited power. Courts will intervene if franchisor uses power abusively. This case established legal precedent that franchisors must act in "good faith" with franchisees.

---

**Case 2: Korean Chicken Franchise—Rent Cannibalization, 2024-2025**

**Background:**

A Korean fried chicken brand expanded rapidly, reaching 400 locations by 2023. System was highly profitable.

**The Conflict:**

In 2024, brand introduced delivery-only satellite locations (no dine-in). These small shops cost only 50M won to open (vs. 150M for full dine-in restaurant).

Headquarters' rationale:
- Delivery is growing market
- Can reach neighborhoods without high-rent locations
- Satellite shops feed main restaurants nearby

Existing franchisees' perspective:
- Satellite shops steal their delivery orders
- Nearby customers now order from satellite, not main restaurant
- Their sales dropped 10-20%
- Cannibalization wasn't disclosed upfront

Franchisee response:
- Demanded satellite shop closure or compensation
- Asked for reduced royalty (from 6% to 4%) to offset lost sales
- Franchisor refused

Escalation:
- 100+ franchisees formed association
- Filed complaint with Korean Fair Trade Commission (FTC equivalent)
- Threatened class-action lawsuit

Franchisor's position:
- "System expansion is our right. Franchisees benefit long-term from brand growth."
- "We're not responsible for your location choice. Choose better location next time."

Outcome (as of early 2026):
- Fair Trade Commission is investigating
- Multiple lawsuits pending
- Brand is under pressure to either close satellite shops or compensate franchisees
- Recruiting new franchisees has become difficult
- Brand's reputation damaged in Korea

**Lesson for this case:**

Franchisors can't grow the system at franchisees' expense without compensation. Courts increasingly recognize "duty to franchisees" extending beyond contract terms.

---

**Case 3: Korean Convenience Store Chain—Systemic Unprofitability, 2025-2026**

This is the most serious ongoing Korean franchise crisis.

**Background:**

A major Korean convenience store chain (not naming specifically, but based on real situations) has ~3,000 franchisees. For years, system was "win-win":
- Franchisors made money on royalties + supply markup
- Franchisees made 10-15M won monthly profit

**The Crisis:**

2023-2026: Macro shocks hit simultaneously
- Rent inflation (landlords raised rates 5-10%)
- COGS inflation (supplies up 20-30%)
- Labor inflation (minimum wage increased 5% annually)
- Delivery platform saturation (15-20% commission eating margins)

Result: Many franchisees flipped from profitable to unprofitable. By 2026:
- Estimated 30-40% of franchisees are unprofitable
- Franchisees are closing locations or filing bankruptcy
- Some franchisees are suing franchisor, claiming:
  - Franchisor didn't disclose economic risks upfront
  - Franchisor didn't provide support during crisis
  - Franchisor prioritized own profits over franchisee welfare

Franchisor's initial response: "We provide system, training, brand. You run business. Your results depend on your efforts."

Media pressure and government inquiry:
- Korean government is investigating unfair franchise practices
- Media is highlighting franchisee suffering
- Consumer sentiment turning negative (people sympathizing with franchisees)

Franchisor's revised response (2026):
- Began royalty reductions (from 3% to 2.5%)
- Negotiated supplier price cuts
- Provided more operational support
- But too little, too late (damage already done)

Outcome (ongoing):
- Tens of thousands of franchisees affected
- Government considering stricter franchise regulations
- Brand loyalty damaged
- Long-term impact: Fewer people want to become franchisees in Korea

**Lesson for this case:**

System resilience is franchisor's responsibility. When macro conditions change, franchisor must share pain with franchisees or system collapses. This is becoming Korean legal/regulatory reality.

---

### Summary (5 minutes)

**Key Learnings:**

1. **Franchise conflicts arise from four sources:** Performance issues, financial disputes, operational changes, broken trust

2. **Resolution methods exist on a spectrum:** From cheap/fast (negotiation) to expensive/slow (litigation)

3. **Negotiation works only if both sides cooperate.** Mediation helps when direct talks fail.

4. **Arbitration is faster alternative to court**, but requires upfront agreement

5. **Courts increasingly protect franchisees** from franchisor abuse of power

6. **Korean franchise crisis (2025-2026) shows:** Franchisors must share pain during downturns or face systemic collapse

7. **Prevention is always better than cure:** Clear communication, fair terms, transparency prevent most conflicts

## SECTION 4: KEY NOTES & IMPROVISATION TIPS

### Instructor Preparation Notes

**Before the Session:**
- Familiarize yourself with Korean Fair Trade Commission regulations on franchising
- Have recent news articles about Korean convenience store franchisee struggles (2025-2026)
- Prepare video clip of franchisee interview (if available) discussing hardship
- Research the Franchise Disclosure Act (FDD) requirements in various countries
- Prepare real settlement agreements (anonymized) showing typical conflict resolutions

**During the Session:**
- Emphasize that conflict is NOT always franchisor's fault (performance disputes often legitimately require franchisee correction)
- But emphasize franchisor's greater responsibility (they have more power, can articulate system better)
- Show how Korean convenience store crisis was predictable and preventable
- Ask: "If you were CU CEO in 2024, knowing franchisees were unprofitable, what would you do?"

**Real Data Context (2025-2026):**
- Korean Fair Trade Commission reported 300+ franchise-related complaints in 2025 (up from 180 in 2023)
- Average Korean franchisee now works 60+ hours/week for 5-10M won monthly profit (below minimum wage equivalent)
- Franchise recruitment rates in Korea dropping (fewer new franchisees willing to enter market)
- International franchises (like U.S. brands) facing fewer conflicts because better capitalized, more transparent from start

### Vocabulary Emphasis

- **Conflict of interest:** Situation where two parties' goals are opposed
- **Good faith:** Acting reasonably, fairly, with honesty (legal standard)
- **Breach of contract:** Violation of written agreement terms
- **Damages:** Monetary compensation for losses caused by other party
- **Mediation:** Process where neutral party helps disputants reach agreement
- **Arbitration:** Process where neutral arbitrator makes binding decision
- **Litigation:** Court process where judge decides winner
- **Class-action lawsuit:** Multiple parties suing together (common in franchising)
- **Discovery:** Exchange of evidence before trial
- **Settlement:** Agreement ending dispute before final judgment

### Interactive Elements

[ASK STUDENTS] "If you're a franchisee earning only 3M won/month due to macro shocks, what do you do?"
- Expected responses: Negotiate with franchisor, sue, close location, continue hoping market improves
- Key learning: All options have costs; negotiation is cheapest if franchisor cooperates

[PAUSE] "Write down: Would you invest in a Korean convenience store franchise in 2026?"
- Most should say "NO" based on current profitability crisis
- Discuss why people still invest despite poor economics (desperation, lack of alternatives, belief market will improve)

[GROUP DISCUSSION] "CU franchisees want royalty reduction. CU says no. What happens next?"
- Students should discuss escalation path: negotiation → mediation → arbitration → litigation
- Key learning: Without negotiation, disputes only get more expensive

[COMPARE CASES] "Quick Lube case vs. Korean convenience store case. Same issue?"
- Quick Lube: Franchisor imposed new requirement without franchisee input
- Korean convenience store: Macro conditions changed, franchisor didn't adapt
- Both show franchisor responsibility to franchisees

### Common Mistakes to Watch For

1. **Blaming franchisees for all conflicts**
   - Reality: Some conflicts are franchisee's fault (poor performance)
   - But many are franchisor's fault (unrealistic projections, system changes, lack of support)
   - Judge both sides fairly

2. **Underestimating litigation costs**
   - Students might think "just sue," not realizing legal fees are massive
   - A 3-year litigation costs more than franchisee's entire 5-year profit
   - This is why mediation/arbitration better for most cases

3. **Assuming franchisee always loses**
   - Reality: Courts increasingly favor franchisees, especially in Korea
   - Quick Lube case showed franchisor lost despite having "right" in contract
   - Courts interpret contracts in context of good faith

4. **Not considering relationship preservation**
   - Most franchisors assume "if we win the lawsuit, we win"
   - Reality: Winning in court but destroying brand image is net loss
   - Better to settle, preserve system, continue growing

5. **Ignoring system-level crises**
   - Korean convenience store situation wasn't about one bad franchisee
   - It was systemic: macro conditions made system unviable for many
   - Franchisor must respond at system level (not just individual dispute level)

### Extension Questions

**If student asks:** "Why doesn't a franchisee just leave if they're unprofitable?"
**Answer:** Sunk costs. Franchisee has invested 100-300M won. If they leave, they lose everything. They're trying to make it work even at low profit. This is why mediation often succeeds—both sides prefer continuing to loss of capital.

**If student asks:** "Why don't all franchises use arbitration instead of court?"
**Answer:** Some disputes require court jurisdiction (disputes about law itself). Also, franchisor often refuses arbitration (they have power, so they prefer court where they can afford better lawyers). Arbitration is great when both sides want it.

**If student asks:** "How can franchisors prevent conflicts?"
**Answer:**
1. Clear, honest disclosure upfront (AUV based on specific location types, not averages)
2. Transparent fee structure (no surprise charges)
3. Regular communication (check-ins with franchisees)
4. Flexibility (allow system adjustments when market changes)
5. Fair problem-solving (shared responsibility for franchise success)
6. Clear performance standards (franchisee knows what's expected)

### Real-World Context

**2025-2026 Korean Franchise Situation:**
- Korean government considering stronger franchisee protection laws
- Some franchisors voluntarily improving royalty rates (hoping to avoid regulation)
- International franchises entering Korea, sometimes treating Korean franchisees better than Korean brands do
- Recruitment declining; few new franchisees willing to invest
- Existing franchisees facing choice: accept low profit, exit market, or go to court

This represents a turning point in Korean franchising. The post-COVID era has exposed system vulnerabilities. Franchisors who adapt will survive; those who don't will face regulatory pressure.
