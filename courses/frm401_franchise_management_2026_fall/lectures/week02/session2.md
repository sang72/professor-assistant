### Opening (3 minutes)

Good morning, everyone. Welcome to Week 2, Session 2. Last session, we defined franchising precisely. We explored the six essential elements: trademark, documented system, fees, royalties, support, and control.

But defining what franchising IS doesn't answer the critical question: **When should a company franchise?** What makes it a good opportunity? Why do some franchises succeed and others fail?

That's what we're exploring today. We're going to move from definitions to strategy. We're going to ask: What market conditions create franchise opportunities? How do you recognize a real opportunity versus hype?

This matters because Korea in 2025 has both: real opportunities (some market segments are still growing) and massive hype (some franchisors are selling dreams, not realities). By the end of today, you'll understand how to distinguish between them.

### Segment 1: Opportunity Recognition Framework (10 minutes)

[SHOW SLIDE: "What Makes a Franchise Opportunity?"]

Opportunity recognition is the first component of the Franchise Relationship Model that we'll study more deeply in Week 3. But let me introduce it now.

An opportunity exists when three conditions are met:

1. **Unmet market demand** — There are customers who want something they're not currently getting
2. **Favorable timing** — Market conditions make NOW the right time to serve that demand
3. **Competitive advantage** — Your concept can serve that demand better than competitors

[WRITE ON BOARD: Opportunity = Unmet Demand + Favorable Timing + Competitive Advantage]

Let me be concrete. Consider **Starbucks in the 1980s.**

**Unmet demand:** Americans wanted a coffee experience that was:
- Not the cheap diner coffee (low quality, low ambiance)
- Not the boutique specialty coffee roasters (too intimidating, too expensive, limited locations)
- Something in between: premium quality, comfortable environment, accessible locations, community-oriented

Was this demand real? Yes. Coffee consumption was rising. Disposable income was rising. Americans were becoming more sophisticated consumers. Coffee culture was maturing (influenced by European and Italian traditions). Urban professionals wanted places to work, study, and socialize outside of home or office.

[ASK STUDENTS: Do you think that demand was obvious to everyone in 1980, or did Starbucks founders have to convince people?]

[PAUSE for response]

Exactly. It wasn't obvious. Most people thought coffee was coffee. Starbucks had to educate the market. But the underlying demand was there—they just articulated it first.

**Favorable timing:** The 1980s-1990s were ideal for Starbucks because:
- Urban commercial real estate was plentiful and affordable
- The service economy was growing (more white-collar jobs needing office environments)
- Specialty food and beverage was becoming trendy
- Coffee culture from Europe was diffusing into America
- Disposable income was growing

By contrast, if Starbucks had tried to launch the same concept in 1960, it would have failed. The market wasn't ready.

**Competitive advantage:** Starbucks' competitive advantages were:
- Founder knowledge (Howard Schultz had lived in Italy, understood espresso culture)
- System design (standardized recipes, training, store design)
- Real estate expertise (selecting locations)
- Brand building (early investment in marketing and identity)

Other coffeehouses existed in 1980 (Berkeley, Seattle). But Starbucks combined the three elements: they recognized unmet demand, they entered at the right time, and they had competitive advantages.

Now consider **Krispy Kreme trying to enter Korea in the 2010s.** 

Unmet demand? Not really. Donuts weren't central to Korean eating culture. Korea has many pastry and coffee alternatives. Did Koreans desperately want American donuts? No.

The timing wasn't right. Krispy Kreme launched stores in Seoul, attracted huge initial interest (novelty factor), but then declined. By 2020, most Krispy Kreme locations in Korea had closed. The demand wasn't real; it was novelty-driven.

[ASK STUDENTS: Can you think of a franchise that entered Korea or your country and failed? What was missing—unmet demand, timing, or competitive advantage?]

[PAUSE for response]

Great examples. This is the key skill: distinguishing between real opportunity and hype.

### Segment 2: Market Structure & Size Analysis (14 minutes)

[SHOW SLIDE: "Analyzing Market Opportunity"]

Once you've identified an opportunity, you must analyze the market to understand its potential.

**Three questions:**

1. **What is the market structure?** (Who are the competitors? How concentrated is the market?)
2. **What is the market size?** (How many potential customers? What is total revenue available?)
3. **What is market profitability?** (Can franchisees make money? Or is the market too fragmented?)

**Question 1: Market Structure**

Market structure determines competitive intensity. Is the market concentrated (few big players) or fragmented (many small players)?

**Concentrated markets** (few players):
- Advantages: Less competition, potential for pricing power
- Disadvantages: Harder to enter, incumbents have advantages
- Example: Korean convenience stores (CU, GS25, Emart, Lotteria dominate ~80% of market)

**Fragmented markets** (many small players):
- Advantages: Easier to enter, less head-to-head competition
- Disadvantages: Price wars, difficulty scaling
- Example: Cafes in Korea (thousands of independent cafes, thousands of small chains)

Understanding market structure is critical because it determines whether franchising works.

Franchising works best in **moderately concentrated markets** where:
- There are 5-15 significant competitors
- The market is consolidating (small players are being acquired or failing)
- Franchising can enable a brand to scale and consolidate
- Network effects matter (having many locations strengthens the brand)

Example: **U.S. fast-casual restaurants in the 2000s.** The market had many small independent restaurants, but no dominant chains. Chipotle, Panera, and similar concepts started franchising and consolidated the market. Customers preferred consistent quality over independent restaurants. Franchising enabled scale.

Example: **Korean chicken franchise market (2005-2015).** The market was fragmented—many small chicken restaurants, but no major national chains. Nene Chicken and BBQ Chicken franchised aggressively, consolidated the market, and created national brands. By 2015, the top 10 chicken chains dominated 60%+ of the market.

By contrast, franchising in **highly concentrated markets** is harder because incumbents (Starbucks, McDonald's) already own most locations and have massive brand advantages.

**Question 2: Market Size**

You must understand:
- Total addressable market (TAM): How many potential customers exist?
- Revenue potential: How much money can this market generate annually?
- Growth rate: Is the market growing, stable, or declining?

Let me use Korean convenience stores as an example.

**Korean Convenience Store Market, 2025:**

- **Market size:** 40,000+ locations, ~12 trillion won annual revenue (~$9 billion USD)
- **TAM:** 50 million Koreans, 80% urban dwellers. Convenience store usage: 60-70% of urban population visits at least weekly. Average transaction: 8,000-10,000 won. 
- **Implied market:** 50M × 70% × 52 weeks × (customer visits) = massive TAM
- **Growth rate:** DECLINING. 2020-2025, market growth was 0-2% annually (basically flat). Between 2025-2030, expected to decline 1-3% annually due to:
  - Saturation (too many stores)
  - E-commerce and delivery (online groceries, food delivery reducing convenience store traffic)
  - Labor cost inflation (reducing franchisee profitability)

This market size analysis is critical for franchise opportunities. A declining market is dangerous for new franchisees because:
- Revenue is harder to achieve
- Competition intensifies (franchisees fight for shrinking pie)
- Franchisee failures increase (which damages brand reputation)

Compare to **Korean chicken franchise market (2020-2025):**

- **Market size:** ~5,000+ chicken restaurants, ~3 trillion won annual revenue
- **Growth rate:** Growing 3-5% annually (still healthy)
- **TAM:** Koreans love fried chicken. Market expansion potential through delivery, international expansion, non-restaurant channels (convenience stores)

This market is healthier for franchising.

**Question 3: Market Profitability**

Can a franchisee make money in this market? This requires unit economics analysis.

Unit economics: Revenue per location, minus all costs, minus franchisor fees, equals franchisee profit.

Example: **CU Convenience Store, 2025 (Korean market)**

- **Average revenue per store:** 20 million won/month
- **COGS and supply:** 8-10 million won (40-50%)
- **Labor:** 3-5 million won (20-25%, though this is rising)
- **Rent:** 1-2 million won (5-10%, varies by location)
- **Franchisor fees (royalty + extras):** 2-3 million won (10-15%)
- **Utilities, other:** 0.5-1 million won (2-5%)
- **Total costs:** ~16-17 million won
- **Franchisee profit:** 3-4 million won/month (~18% margin)

**Annual profit:** 36-48 million won on ~70 million won investment = 50-70% ROI initially

But here's the critical part: This assumes 20 million won monthly revenue. If the location is in a slower area, revenue might be 15 million won/month. Then:
- **Franchisee profit:** Only 1-2 million won/month
- **Annual profit:** 12-24 million won
- **ROI:** 17-34%

And if labor costs keep rising (which they are), profitability falls further.

By 2026, many CU franchisees reported profitability below 30% ROI, making the investment return below stock market historical averages.

This is why opportunity analysis is critical. Even in an established, well-known franchise system (CU), unit economics are declining.

[ASK STUDENTS: If you could invest 70 million won and get either:
- Convenience store franchise: 25-35% ROI, declining
- Stock market index fund: 8-10% ROI, stable
- Real estate: 4-6% ROI, stable, but appreciating asset

Which would you choose?]

[PAUSE for discussion]

The answer is: It depends on your risk tolerance and time horizon. But increasingly, convenience store franchising is looking less attractive.

### Segment 3: Profitability & Growth Analysis (11 minutes)

[SHOW SLIDE: "Market Growth vs. Market Saturation"]

Let me introduce a critical concept: **The Market Life Cycle.**

Every market goes through stages:
1. **Introduction:** New market, unproven demand, high risk
2. **Growth:** Demand proven, multiple competitors entering, rising profitability
3. **Maturity:** Market saturated, commoditized, intense competition, declining margins
4. **Decline:** Customer demand shifting, old technology/models becoming obsolete

Franchising works best in the **Growth stage.**

In Introduction stage: Risks are too high. Few franchisees want to invest because demand is unproven.

In Growth stage: Perfect for franchising. Demand is proven. Customers want the product. Multiple franchisees can expand the brand. Everyone profits.

In Maturity stage: Franchising becomes difficult. Market is saturated. New franchisees struggle. Existing franchisees fight for revenue. Franchisee failures increase.

In Decline stage: Franchising often fails. The franchisor tries to support too many struggling franchisees.

**Korean Example: Nene Chicken Market Life Cycle**

Nene Chicken was founded in 1991. Let me trace its life cycle:

**1991-2000 (Introduction):** Nene Chicken created the Korean fried chicken category as a casual restaurant experience. Before Nene, Korean fried chicken was a side dish or street food. Nene elevated it to a main dining experience. They franchised slowly, building brand.

**2000-2010 (Growth):** Explosive growth. Nene Chicken franchised aggressively. Thousands of franchisees invested. Revenue grew 20-30% annually. Profitability was high. New franchisees could achieve 40-50%+ ROI.

Why? Because:
- Demand was proven and growing (Koreans loved fried chicken)
- Competitors existed but market had room for many winners
- Franchisees had opportunities in every city/neighborhood
- No location was "saturated" yet

**2010-2020 (Maturity):** Growth continued but slowed. By 2015, market matured. Chicken was now a crowded category—dozens of chicken chains. Price wars intensified. Franchisee margins compressed. ROI fell from 40%+ to 25-30%.

Why? Because:
- Market demand was satisfied (everyone who wanted fried chicken could find it)
- Too many competitors in many locations (cannibalization)
- New franchisees faced harder market conditions
- Delivery and online ordering changed the game

**2020-2026 (Maturity/Decline):** Market is saturated. Too many chicken restaurants. COVID-19 accelerated consolidation. Smaller chains closed. Nene Chicken still leads, but growth is 3-5% (vs. 20%+ earlier). New franchisees struggle. ROI dropped to 15-25%.

[SHOW SLIDE: "Nene Chicken Revenue & Franchisee Profitability Decline (2005-2025)"]

(Chart showing growth declining over time, profitability per unit falling)

This life cycle is crucial. If you're considering buying a Nene Chicken franchise in 2025, you're entering a **Mature market**, not a Growth market. Your ROI will be 15-25%, not 40-50%. That's the reality.

**Korean Example: BBQ Chicken Market Challenge (2020-2026)**

BBQ Chicken is another major Korean chicken franchise. Launched in 2000s, grew rapidly through 2010s.

But BBQ Chicken faces challenges:
- Market saturation (too many chicken options)
- Aggressive competition from CJ Foodville, Kyochon, and others
- Franchisee profitability declining
- Some recent franchisee closures and lawsuits (2024-2025)

Why highlight BBQ Chicken? Because it shows: **Even successful franchises face market maturity challenges.**

BBQ Chicken had:
- Strong brand
- Good system
- Proven concept
- Thousands of franchisees

But when the market matured, franchisees' unit economics deteriorated. Many left the system. The franchisor had to adjust strategy (lowering fees, providing more support, opening fewer new locations).

### Segment 4: Korean Market Examples & Critical Analysis (8 minutes)

[SHOW SLIDE: "Evaluating Franchise Opportunities: Three Cases"]

Let me give you three Korean examples so you can practice opportunity analysis.

**Case 1: Nene Chicken (2025) — Mature Market Opportunity**

Nene Chicken in 2025 is the **largest chicken franchise in Korea** with 2,000+ locations and $800M+ annual revenue.

But is this a good franchise opportunity for a new franchisee in 2025?

**Analysis:**

- **Market demand:** ✓ Proven (Koreans love fried chicken)
- **Timing:** ✗ Late (market is mature, not growing)
- **Competitive advantage:** ✓ Strong brand (Nene is market leader)
- **Market structure:** ✗ Fragmented (dozens of chicken competitors)
- **Franchisee profitability:** ✗ Declining (ROI has fallen from 40%+ to 20-25%)
- **Growth potential:** ✗ Limited (market is maturing)

**Verdict:** Opportunity exists, but it's a MATURE market opportunity, not a growth opportunity. New franchisees face lower returns. Better for existing franchisees scaling their portfolio.

**Case 2: Korean Premium Coffee Franchises (2024-2026) — SATURATED Market**

Korea has a massive coffee culture. Starbucks has 2,000+ locations. But so do Hollys, Ediya, Caffè Bene, Compose, and dozens of independent chains and cafes.

By 2025, the Korean coffee franchise market is **over-saturated.**

**Analysis:**

- **Market demand:** ✓ Proven (Koreans consume massive coffee)
- **Timing:** ✗ Too late (market is oversaturated)
- **Competitive advantage:** ✗ Hard to differentiate (Starbucks dominates, specialty segments exist)
- **Market structure:** ✗ Highly fragmented (thousands of competitors)
- **Franchisee profitability:** ✗ Declining (oversupply has destroyed margins)
- **Market life cycle:** Decline stage

**Verdict:** This is a **declining opportunity.** New coffee franchisees face intense competition, low differentiation, and poor profitability. Unless you have a unique concept (e.g., Korean traditional coffee or specialized niches), don't invest.

**Case 3: Korean Plant-Based / Vegan Restaurants (2024-2026) — EMERGING Opportunity**

Korean plant-based food is a **nascent market.** The trend is emerging in major cities (Seoul, Busan) but hasn't spread nationwide. Few major franchise chains dominate this space.

**Analysis:**

- **Market demand:** ? Emerging (Korean consumers are becoming more health-conscious, but plant-based is still niche)
- **Timing:** ? Possibly right (health trends are accelerating globally, Korea is following)
- **Competitive advantage:** ? Possible (first-mover advantage in Korea if you execute well)
- **Market structure:** ✓ Fragmented (few major players, room for growth)
- **Franchisee profitability:** ? Unknown (untested concept in Korea, unit economics unclear)
- **Growth potential:** ✓ High (if plant-based trend continues)

**Verdict:** This is a **growth opportunity, but risky.** If plant-based food becomes mainstream in Korea, early franchisees could scale and profit hugely. If the trend doesn't take hold, you lose. Only invest if you believe in the trend and can tolerate risk.

[ASK STUDENTS: Of these three, which would YOU invest in as a franchisee? Why?]

[PAUSE for discussion]

The point: Opportunity recognition is hard. You must distinguish between:
- Real, growing opportunities (take risks, invest capital)
- Mature opportunities with declining profitability (be cautious, expect lower returns)
- Saturated opportunities (avoid unless you have unique competitive advantages)
- Emerging opportunities (exciting but risky)

### Summary & Q&A (4 minutes)

Opportunity recognition consists of three elements:
1. **Unmet market demand** — Do customers actually want this?
2. **Favorable timing** — Is NOW the right time?
3. **Competitive advantage** — Can you serve this demand better than competitors?

To evaluate a franchise opportunity, analyze:
1. **Market structure** — Is it concentrated or fragmented? Consolidating or atomizing?
2. **Market size** — How many customers? How much revenue potential?
3. **Unit economics** — Can franchisees actually make money?
4. **Market life cycle** — Growth, Maturity, or Decline stage?
5. **Franchisee profitability trends** — Rising, stable, or declining?

In Korea, 2024-2026, we see:
- **Nene Chicken, BBQ Chicken:** Mature markets, declining franchisee profitability
- **Convenience stores (CU, GS25):** Saturated market, declining ROI, labor cost crisis
- **Coffee franchises:** Over-saturated, poor profitability for new entrants
- **Emerging opportunities:** Plant-based, health-focused, niche concepts with growth potential

The critical skill is distinguishing between hype and reality. Don't be seduced by a franchise brand's name. Analyze the market fundamentals.

## SECTION 4: KEY NOTES & IMPROVISATION TIPS

### Core Concepts to Emphasize

1. **Opportunity is Not Obvious** — Starbucks didn't invent coffee. They recognized that unmet demand existed. This requires both market insight and luck with timing.

2. **Market Timing Matters More Than Most People Realize** — Krispy Kreme in Korea failed because the timing was wrong, even though the brand is strong globally. Nene Chicken succeeded because the timing was perfect (Korean chicken culture + urban development + disposable income rising).

3. **Market Saturation is Real** — Korea in 2025 has TOO MANY convenience stores, too many coffee shops, too many chicken restaurants. New franchisees face declining unit economics, not rising. Don't assume historical profitability will continue.

4. **Life Cycle Matters** — A franchise that was a great opportunity in 2010 (Nene Chicken) may be a poor opportunity in 2025 (same brand, same system, different market stage).

### Common Student Misconceptions to Correct

1. **"If a franchise is famous, it must be a good opportunity."**
   - Correct: A famous franchise may be in a mature or declining market. CU and Nene are famous, but franchisee profitability is declining.

2. **"If the market is big, there's room for everyone."**
   - Correct: Big market size doesn't equal easy profitability. If there are 40,000 convenience stores in Korea, each one gets a shrinking pie.

3. **"Historical growth rates will continue forever."**
   - Correct: Markets follow life cycles. Growth stage can't last forever. Plan for maturity and decline.

### Classroom Discussion Prompts

1. "For CU convenience stores in 2025, what would convince you to invest 70 million won? What would discourage you?"

2. "Why did Krispy Kreme fail in Korea, while Nene Chicken succeeded? What was different about the opportunity?"

3. "Looking at plant-based restaurants in Korea (emerging opportunity), what factors would make you bullish or bearish?"

4. "If you were Nene Chicken's founder in 2010 (market maturing), what would you do to sustain the franchise system?"

### Real-World Examples to Emphasize

- **Starbucks (1980s-1990s):** Perfect opportunity recognition (unmet demand + timing + competitive advantage)
- **Nene Chicken (2000-2010 vs. 2020-2026):** Growth stage vs. Mature stage, same brand, different opportunities
- **Korean Coffee Franchises (2025):** Over-saturation case study, why new entrants struggle
- **Convenience Stores Korea (2020-2026):** Labor cost crisis destroying unit economics

### Improvisation Tips for Live Teaching

1. **If students seem overwhelmed by market analysis:** Focus on unit economics. Show the simple math: Revenue - Costs - Franchisor fees = Franchisee profit. Everything else flows from this.

2. **If students ask about opportunities in their hometown:** Ask them to do a market analysis. Count competitors, estimate market size, think about growth. This makes it concrete.

3. **If discussion gets too abstract:** Bring it back to Korea. Ask: "Would you invest 70 million won in a CU franchise in 2025?" This forces decisions based on data.

4. **If time is running short:** Focus on the market life cycle and why Nene Chicken is a good/bad investment depending on the year. Skip detailed financial analysis.
