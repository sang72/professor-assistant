## Using Transaction Analysis to Improve the Service Delivery System

**Definition**: Deconstructing the SDS into a series of transactions, then assigning each transaction to either the franchisee or franchisor based on who does it best.

**Two types of advantage:**
1. **Cost advantage**: Enhanced purchasing power of multiple franchisees vs. individual
   - Example: Jiffy Lube negotiated multi-million-gallon motor oil purchases; standalone competitors were buying oil in 55-gallon drums
2. **Execution advantages**: Networked group of stores sharing knowledge and best practices

**When tasks are clearly allocated:**
- Training becomes more focused
- Efficiency increases
- Analysis and changes are easier
- The franchise can grow better and faster

**How finely should transactions be defined?**
Start with bigger tasks, then refine into smaller and more refined experiences.

**Rule for allocation:**
- If centralizing the function lowers the overall or unit cost → **franchisor** is likely best-suited
- If the transaction needs intense or focused management, or local knowledge affects execution → **franchisee** is best-suited

## Transaction Analysis for Generic Franchise SDS (Table 7-1)

| Transaction | Franchisor Role | Franchisee Role |
|-------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Site criteria | Define key elements of location that best serve target customer | Apply the site criteria to the specific local market |
| Site acquisition | Advise; offer lease and purchase agreement templates | Negotiate and execute the lease or purchase agreement |
| Zoning and permitting | Advise on zoning laws; provide professional contacts | Manage the local zoning process; hire professional consultants |
| Construction | Prepare blueprints; create standardized contract and PERT chart; management support | Obtain local government approvals; solicit bids from local contractors; manage contractor relationship |
| Equipment purchase | Negotiate national contracts | Establish purchase timing and installation; make payments |
| Building/leasehold improvement | Create standardized plans and blueprints; manage contractor | Modify plans for local building codes; monitor contractor relationship |
| Inventory purchase | Establish efficient inventory levels; negotiate national contracts | Place local orders; manage unit level inventory |
| Employee training | Establish training programs; deliver franchisee and founding employees' training | Deliver ongoing training for initial staff; training for new/replacement employees |
| Grand opening advertising | Design a grand opening marketing template; advise on local customization | Customize and execute the marketing plan; develop local promotions |
| Ongoing advertising | Creative development and national advertising brand support | Local media purchase and promotional execution |

## Support Systems and the Franchisor-Franchisee Relationship

**Transaction Analysis for Ongoing Outlet Operations (Table 7-2)**

| Transaction | Franchisor | Franchisee |
|-------------|-----------|------------|
| Operating manual | Develop business model; document in manual for franchisees | Adhere to business model; maintain its confidentiality |
| Training manual | Create training department for operations R&D and development of training materials | Train employees |
| Advertising manual | Create advertising department to develop national advertising program with an agency | Purchase local media and execute through media purchase and local promotions |
| Location inspection/monitoring | Write evaluation criteria based on operations manual; field support personnel visit and consult | Cooperate and communicate with franchisor; discuss operational problems and solutions |
| Research and development | Operate company-owned outlets; share knowledge with franchisees | Problem solving and communication; sometimes acts as test location for new products |
| Inventory | Establish, review, and approve product specifications; manage national contracts | Develop local vendors for non-national contract supplies; manage inventory |

**Transaction Analysis for Advertising and Public Relations (Table 7-3)**

| Transaction | Franchisor | Franchisee |
|-------------|-----------|------------|
| Brand definition/message | Develop the advertising theme or message | Provide advice and feedback |
| Material development | Manage marketing material production by managing the advertising agency | Contribute contractually agreed amount to national advertising fund |
| Regional marketing | Provide input to and exercise voting rights in regional advertising councils | Contribute to regional advertising fund; participate in cooperative governance |
| Media placement | Make national advertising (TV and radio) purchases utilizing national advertising fund | Make regional media purchases through cooperative and local media purchases individually |
| Promotions | Develop national promotional events; provide advice on local promotions | Evaluate "participating store" consent for national promos; create local promotions |

**Transaction Analysis for Product Supply (Table 7-4)**

| Transaction | Franchisor | Franchisee |
|-------------|-----------|------------|
| Product specification | Prioritize product usage and detail specification | Provide feedback on product performance |
| Approved suppliers | Investigate vendors who meet specification criteria | Introduce new suppliers for franchisor approval |
| Supply contract development | Negotiate national buying contracts | Purchase for local needs; make payment |
| Research and development | Field test new products; report new product introductions; make appropriate changes to operations manual | Field test new products; communicate product success/failure; recommend product needs and changes |

## Case Study: Bagelz

*(Note to students: This case illustrates complex franchise decision-making, not to judge the adequacy of those decisions.)*

### Background

Mike Bellobuono and his three partners — Joe Amodio (president), Wes Becher (VP), and Jamie Whalen (territory development manager) — built Bagelz, a Connecticut-based bagel chain, to seven retail locations in just three years.

### Key Decision: To Franchise or Not?

**Competitive pressure:**
- Bruegger's Bagels and Manhattan Bagel were expanding throughout New England
- Industry-wide bagel sales had exploded
- Operating as a chain store constrained potential growth rate

**The Bagelz Competitive Advantages:**
1. Irving Stearns: Chief bagel-maker with 20+ years experience — could quickly develop new products
2. Flexibility of management: Quick to spot and react to new market trends
3. Ground floor opportunity

### Mike's Background

- Graduated from Babson College (B.S., May 1996)
- With college friend Jamie Whalen, studied food-service industry as a class project
- Identified bagels and chicken as next high-growth segments
- Met Wes Becher and Joe Amodio who had opened Bagelz a year earlier
- Bought into the company by arranging financing through their fathers

### The Fred DeLuca Offer (Spring 1998)

Fred DeLuca, founder of Subway franchise (10,000+ locations by 2000; rated #1 franchise by Entrepreneur Magazine six times between 1993-1998), contacted Bagelz after a vendor mentioned them.

DeLuca challenged: "Do you know what you are getting yourself into? Are you sure you really want to deal with all the problems that arise from franchising?"

**Decision**: After 6 months of meetings, Fred DeLuca and Peter Buck purchased 50% stake in the franchise company. Mike, Jamie, Joe, and Wes retained the other 50% + 100% of the commissary.

### Advantages of Aligning with DeLuca:
- Access to large amounts of capital
- Advertising and legal support
- Experience (Subway had more locations in U.S. than any other franchisor)
- Use of Subway's legal department to write the UFOC
- Use of Subway's architects for designing new stores
- Significantly lower corporate franchise-development costs

### Financial Performance (Estimated per Store)

**1998 Income Statement (estimated):**
- Total Revenue: $8,000/week ($416,000/year) = 100%
- Total COGS: $4,800/week = 60%
- Gross Profit: $3,200/week = 40%
- Total Operating Expenses: $1,596/week = ~20%
- Total Income from Operations: $1,604/week ($83,408/year) = ~20%

**1999 Income Statement (with royalties — estimated):**
- Total Revenue: $8,000/week ($416,000/year) = 100%
- Royalties added: $640/week ($33,280/year) = 8%
- Total COGS: $4,320/week = 54%
- Gross Profit: $3,680/week = 46%
- Total Operating Expenses: $2,236/week = ~28%
- Total Income from Operations: $1,444/week ($75,088/year) = ~18%

### Key Innovations

**Baking technology**: Shifted from boil-and-bake to bake-only operation without compromising taste
- Advantages: Faster, cheaper, safer (no injury from boiling), lower energy use (25% of previous)
- Baker shift alarm: Store alarm set for 3:00 AM to alert team if baker wasn't on site

**Poppy seed savings**: Dampened bagels with sponge → dipped in seed bins → covered each bagel with 1/4 oz of seeds → leftover seeds remain in bin → eliminated waste → reduced costs by 50%

**Operations**: Stores opened at 6 AM; closed at 5 PM weekdays, 2 PM weekends; 85% of business in morning; average customer service throughput: 2 minutes

### Marketing Innovations

- Travel coffee mugs with Bagelz name: Given away in exchange for competitor mugs
- Emphasizing the "Z" in Bagelz: Put the Z in front of words ("Z sandwiches")
- The Quarterloaf: One-fourth size of a loaf of bread, shaped like a roll, for additional 25 cents
  - Used same sandwich-making area as bagels
  - Gave impression of many different menu items without extra preparation processes
- Presliced meats and premixed pasteurized eggs: Reduced prep time; no injury risk from slicer blade
- Self-serve drink fountains
- Custom-printed cream cheese containers with Bagelz logo placed near register

**Television advertising strategy**: Decided to wait because:
- High-quality food production is expensive
- Instead: Strategic alliances with companies already advertising on TV

### The Commissary

Mike, Jamie, and Wes (NOT Fred DeLuca) owned the central baking facility.

**Franchisees required to purchase from commissary:**
- Cream cheese
- Bagel dough
- Average cream cheese sales: 3,500 pounds per week (as of early 1996)

**Competitive quality choices:**
- No cutting cream cheese with water (adds cost but maintains quality image)
- Larger bagels: 4.25 ounces vs. industry standard 4 ounces → longer shelf life → fresher bagels

**Critical innovation: Frozen dough development**
- Previously: Fresh dough had only 3-day shelf life → limited distance from commissary
- Solution: Developed frozen dough with large manufacturing companies
  - Frozen dough: 3-month shelf life
  - Cream cheese: 30-day shelf life
  - Result: Growth no longer limited to clusters around commissaries

**Co-branded units**: Opened first co-branded store with Subway (adjoin a Subway location)

**Financial statements:**
- 10-year growth: 2 service centers ($500K) → 47 service centers ($30M)
- Balance sheets and income statements showing franchise system growth

### Transaction Analysis of Bagelz

**Franchise SDS:**
- Centralized baking facility
- Additional dough-making machine at commissary
- Irving Stearns (bagel maker extraordinaire)
- Refrigerated cases near cash registers
- Self-serve beverages and cold drink cases
- Pre-sliced meats, premixed pasteurized eggs, prepackaged cream cheeses
- Fresh vs. frozen dough production
- New style oven in bake locations
- New style of adding toppings to bagels
- Removal of "boiling" aspect from production
- New double defrost rack system
- Use of Subway's legal and architectural departments
- New product development

**Ongoing Outlet Operations:**
- Bake and nonbake units
- Field visits by Bagelz managing partners
- Alarm system for 3:00 AM baking shift
- Hours of operation
- Operations manual
- Bagel sales-demand charts
- Sandwich line-assembly process

**Advertising and Public Relations:**
- Franchising advertising fund
- Use of Z at the end of sandwich names
- Travel coffee mugs with Bagelz logo
- Development of quarterloaf bread concept
- Co-branding: Bagelz and Subway

**Product Supply:**
- Franchisee cooperative for raw materials
- Vendor-based product discounts negotiated by Bagelz
- Franchisor-owned commissary
