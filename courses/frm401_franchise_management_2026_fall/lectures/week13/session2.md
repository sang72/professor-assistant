### Opening (3 minutes)

Welcome to Session 2 of Week 13.

Yesterday we learned four entry models: direct franchising, master franchising, joint venture, company-owned.

Today's question: **How do you actually choose?** And **what risks should you prepare for?**

In reality, choosing entry mode is one of the most critical strategic decisions in international franchising. Get it right, and your system scales globally. Get it wrong, and you waste hundreds of millions.

By end of session, you'll have a decision framework and understand the risks you must manage.

### Main Content

#### Segment 1: Entry Mode Decision Framework (15 minutes)

**Real-world truth:** There's no perfect entry mode. Each model has tradeoffs. Your job is to match model to your situation.

**Five key questions drive the decision:**

---

**QUESTION 1: Do you understand the target market?**

**If YES (deep market knowledge):**
- Option A: Direct franchising (you manage directly)
- Option B: Company-owned entry (you control operations, test model yourself)
- Why: You have confidence in market dynamics, customer preferences, operational requirements

**Example:** Starbucks Korea. Starbucks had researched Korean market extensively before entry. They understood premium coffee positioning would work, understood Korean real estate, understood labor costs. Result: Company-owned entry, then direct franchising.

**If NO (limited market knowledge):**
- Option A: Master franchising (local partner understands market)
- Option B: Joint venture (local partner brings market knowledge + capital)
- Why: You need local expertise to mitigate market risk

**Example:** Nene Chicken entering Southeast Asia. Nene didn't have Vietnam/Thailand expertise. Result: Master franchising with local partners who understood the market.

---

**QUESTION 2: Does your franchisor have capital for company-owned or direct entry?**

**If YES (significant capital available, >500B won):**
- Option A: Company-owned entry (prove model yourself)
- Option B: Direct franchising (invest in establishing brand, recruiting franchisees)
- Option C: Master franchising (less capital required, but lower return)
- Why: Capital gives you options. You can afford to prove model before asking franchisees to invest.

**Example:** Starbucks. Has billions in capital. Can afford to invest 200B+ won proving model in new market before franchising.

**If NO (limited capital, <100B won):**
- Option A: Master franchising (minimal capital required)
- Option B: Direct franchising with existing franchisees (borrow their capital)
- Why: You need partner capital to supplement your own

**Example:** Korean coffee brand expanding to Southeast Asia. Limited capital. Result: Master franchising with local partner who invests in market development.

---

**QUESTION 3: How important is brand control vs. growth speed?**

**If CONTROL is critical (brand reputation is everything):**
- Option A: Direct franchising (you manage all aspects)
- Option B: Company-owned entry (you operate directly)
- Option C: Master franchising with strong contractual control
- Why: You maintain quality, brand positioning, system integrity

**Example:** Luxury coffee brands (specialty, premium positioning). Quality control is critical. Would avoid master franchising where local partner might cut corners.

**If GROWTH SPEED is critical (beat competitors to market, capture share):**
- Option A: Master franchising (fastest, local partner knows how to scale)
- Option B: Joint venture (leverage local partner's distribution, relationships)
- Why: Speed requires partner who can execute quickly

**Example:** Food delivery franchises. Market moves fast. Korean brands expanding to Vietnam need speed. Result: Master franchising with experienced local partners who already have relationships with regulators, real estate owners.

---

**QUESTION 4: What's the regulatory and legal complexity?**

**If LOW complexity (regulatory environment is clear, business-friendly):**
- Option A: Direct franchising (you handle directly)
- Option B: Company-owned entry (you navigate yourself)
- Why: Fewer regulatory surprises. You can manage risk yourself.

**Example:** Most of Vietnam. Business is relatively straightforward. Korean brands can enter directly or via master franchising.

**If HIGH complexity (regulatory uncertainty, changing laws, local requirements):**
- Option A: Master franchising (local partner understands regulatory landscape)
- Option B: Joint venture (government might require local partnership)
- Option C: Avoid direct franchising until environment stabilizes
- Why: Local expertise is essential. You need partner who knows how to navigate bureaucracy.

**Example:** China. Regulatory environment is complex, changing constantly. Foreign franchisors typically use joint venture or master franchising.

---

**QUESTION 5: What's your strategic intent (long-term presence, quick profit, exit within 5 years)?**

**If LONG-TERM presence (plan to operate in market for 20+ years):**
- Option A: Company-owned entry initially, then direct franchising (slow, careful approach)
- Option B: Direct franchising with heavy investment in system building
- Why: You're willing to invest upfront because you'll benefit long-term

**Example:** Starbucks Korea. Starbucks committed to Korea long-term. Willing to invest 200B+ won upfront knowing 20+ year ROI.

**If SHORT-TERM profit (extract money for 5-10 years, might exit):**
- Option A: Master franchising (collect royalties, minimize investment)
- Option B: Joint venture (reduce risk exposure)
- Why: Minimize capital at risk. Get royalties with minimal ongoing involvement.

**Example:** Korean brands testing US market. Many don't have long-term US commitment. Result: Master franchising with minimal franchisor investment. If US doesn't work, Korean franchisor exits cleanly.

---

**Decision Matrix: Putting it Together**

| Situation | Market Knowledge | Capital Available | Control Priority | Regulatory Complexity | Strategic Intent | Recommended Model |
|-----------|------------------|-------------------|------------------|----------------------|------------------|------------------|
| Wealthy, simple market | Deep | Strong | High | Low | Long-term | Company-owned → Direct |
| Emerging, growth market | Limited | Limited | Growth speed | Moderate | Long-term | Master franchising |
| Uncertain market | Limited | Strong | Control | High | Short-term | Master franchising |
| Wealthy, complex market | Deep | Strong | Control | High | Long-term | Direct with JV option |
| Competitive market | Limited | Strong | Growth speed | Low | Short-term | Master franchising |

**Practical example:** BBQ Chicken expanding to Vietnam

1. **Market knowledge:** Limited (Vietnamese market is different from Korea)
2. **Capital:** Moderate (can invest 50B won, but limited beyond that)
3. **Control:** Growth speed (need to beat competitors, capture market quickly)
4. **Regulatory:** Moderate (Vietnam business is relatively straightforward, but has nuances)
5. **Strategic intent:** Long-term (if successful, expand to other Southeast Asia)

**Decision:** Master franchising + some direct ownership of flagship stores
- Partner with Vietnam master franchisee who understands market
- BBQ maintains some company-owned stores in key cities (control)
- Scale rapidly through master franchisee network
- Built-in optionality (can shift to direct franchising later if needed)

#### Segment 2: Risks in International Franchising (14 minutes)

**Once you choose entry model, you face risks.** Each model has different risks.

**Six major risk categories:**

---

**RISK 1: MARKET RISK**

**Definition:** Product/brand doesn't fit market, customer preferences are different, competitive environment prevents success

**Examples:**
- Nene Chicken in US (failed because US chicken market is saturated, price-sensitive)
- 7-Eleven Korea (failed because Korean customers wanted Korean food, not global convenience store)
- Korean premium coffee in emerging markets (failed because price point too high)

**Mitigation strategies:**
1. **Extensive market research before entry** (not just desk research; on-the-ground interviews, surveys, competitive analysis)
2. **Pilot program** (test concept in small market before committing to system-wide expansion)
3. **Flexible menu/product** (allow adaptation to local tastes without losing brand identity)
4. **Competitor intelligence** (understand who you're competing against, why you're better)
5. **Contingency plan** (know your exit strategy if market doesn't work)

**Real-world example:** McDonald's India
- Challenge: No beef (Hindu consumers), no pork (Muslim consumers)
- Mitigation: Designed completely localized menu (chicken, vegetarian options)
- Result: Success (India is now major McDonald's market)

---

**RISK 2: PARTNER RISK**

**Definition:** Master franchisee or JV partner under-performs, doesn't execute, drains franchisor without delivering growth

**Examples:**
- Nene Chicken US (US master franchisee lacked experience, didn't execute well, closed most stores)
- Many Korean franchisors in China (chose partners who didn't understand food franchising)
- BBQ Chicken in Japan (partner couldn't compete with local yakitori chains)

**Mitigation strategies:**
1. **Rigorous due diligence** (check partner's track record, financial stability, management team)
2. **Performance metrics** (contract specifies minimum openings per year, sales targets, customer satisfaction)
3. **Support structure** (franchisor provides training, systems, marketing to help partner succeed)
4. **Performance clauses** (if partner under-performs, contract allows franchisor to terminate and take over)
5. **Regular monitoring** (quarterly/semi-annual review of partner's operations, problems)
6. **Exit options** (understand how franchisor can reclaim territory if partner fails)

**Real example:** KFC India success
- KFC chose Hindustan Unilever as master franchisee (large, stable, experienced in F&B)
- HUL understood Indian market, had distribution networks, had brand expertise
- KFC set clear performance milestones (X stores per year)
- HUL invested heavily, exceeded targets
- Result: One of KFC's most successful markets

---

**RISK 3: REGULATORY & LEGAL RISK**

**Definition:** Local regulations change, franchise laws are stricter than expected, contract enforcement is weak, local government is hostile

**Examples:**
- Korean franchisors in China (faced changing government policies, sudden regulatory crackdowns)
- Many franchisors in Vietnam (labor laws changed, tax audits, unexpected fees)
- US franchisors in California (unexpected state registration requirements, additional compliance costs)

**Mitigation strategies:**
1. **Local legal counsel** (hire lawyer in target country to understand regulations)
2. **Franchise law compliance** (ensure FDD/disclosure compliance with local law)
3. **Contract strength** (use local legal standards, not just home-country templates)
4. **Regulatory relationships** (build relationships with local government, chambers of commerce)
5. **Insurance** (political risk insurance, tax risk insurance for emerging markets)
6. **Legal contingencies** (understand worst-case scenarios, have exit plans)

**Real example:** Korean franchise in Vietnam
- Legal counsel reviewed Vietnamese franchise regulations before entry
- Ensured all contracts complied with local law
- Built relationships with local business association
- Prepared for potential regulatory changes
- Result: Avoided legal disputes that plagued competitors

---

**RISK 4: CURRENCY RISK**

**Definition:** Exchange rates fluctuate, reducing profit margins or creating unexpected losses

**Examples:**
- Korean franchisors in 2020-2021 (won strengthened against other Asian currencies; reduced overseas profits)
- Many franchisors during 2008 financial crisis (currency crashes in multiple markets)
- Japanese franchisors in 1990s (yen strengthened dramatically; exports uncompetitive)

**Mitigation strategies:**
1. **Natural hedging** (generate revenues in target currency, spend in target currency; reduces exchange risk)
   - BBQ Chicken Vietnam: Earn VND from franchisees, spend VND on suppliers
2. **Currency forwards** (lock in exchange rate for future payments)
3. **Pricing strategy** (adjust prices to account for currency fluctuations)
4. **Financial reporting** (track currency impacts on quarterly results)

**Real example:** Starbucks Korea
- Starbucks prices Korean coffee in won, but costs are in won
- Natural hedge (revenues and costs both in won)
- Result: Currency fluctuations minimized impact on profit

---

**RISK 5: OPERATIONAL RISK**

**Definition:** Operations break down across borders (supply chain failures, quality control fails, franchisees don't follow system)

**Examples:**
- Many franchisors (supply chain breaks down; can't get ingredients reliably)
- 7-Eleven Korea (couldn't maintain operational discipline that works in Japan)
- Korean fast food franchises (stores look different, taste different, operation quality varies)

**Mitigation strategies:**
1. **Local supply chain** (identify reliable local suppliers, don't rely on imports)
2. **Operations manual** (detailed SOPs translated to local language)
3. **Training programs** (franchisee training must be comprehensive)
4. **Quality audits** (regular store visits, mystery shoppers, customer surveys)
5. **Flexibility in SDS** (allow operational variations while maintaining core standards)

**Real example:** 7-Eleven Japan vs. 7-Eleven Korea
- 7-Eleven Japan: Rigid SDS, multiple inventory checks per day, strict quality standards (works)
- 7-Eleven Korea: Tried to apply Japan SDS without adaptation (failed; too rigid for Korean market)
- Lesson: Operations must adapt to local context while maintaining core quality

---

**RISK 6: SYSTEM RISK**

**Definition:** Business model itself doesn't translate to different market (economics break, franchise unit economics don't work)

**Examples:**
- Premium coffee franchises in low-income markets (can't charge premium prices; unit economics don't work)
- High-labor-cost formats in low-wage markets (overhead kills profitability)
- Delivery-based franchises where infrastructure is weak (can't execute model)

**Mitigation strategies:**
1. **Unit economics analysis** (calculate franchisee profit in local market conditions)
2. **Pricing research** (what can local customer afford? Are margins viable?)
3. **Cost structure** (identify local cost drivers: rent, labor, utilities)
4. **Franchisee support** (help franchisees adapt to local economics)
5. **Model flexibility** (be willing to change format, size, service level)

**Real example:** Starbucks Vietnam
- Coffee market is emerging; customers aren't accustomed to premium prices
- Initial Starbucks location in Hanoi (high-rent, premium positioning) struggled
- Shifted to smaller footprints, different real estate, slightly lower positioning
- Adjusted unit economics to work in Vietnamese context
- Result: System became profitable

#### Segment 3: Case Study—Partner Due Diligence (11 minutes)

**The most common failure in international franchising: Choosing wrong partner.**

**Let's analyze a real failure and what should have happened:**

---

**Case Study: Korean Fast Food Chain Entering Thailand (2020-2022)**

**What happened:**

1. **Selection:** Korean fast-food brand wanted to expand to Thailand. Chose local Thai distributor as master franchisee (distributor had food industry experience, had relationships with real estate owners, seemed capable).

2. **Deal structure:** Master franchisee signed agreement to open 15 locations within 3 years. Paid 1B baht (30M USD) for master franchising rights.

3. **Year 1 (2020):** Master franchisee opened 2 locations. Both were slow (foot traffic lower than expected).

4. **Year 2 (2021):** Master franchisee opened 1 more location. Claimed market conditions were difficult due to COVID. Asked for extension on timeline.

5. **Year 3 (2022):** Master franchisee had opened 4 total locations (vs. target of 15). Locations were unprofitable. Master franchisee refused to invest more money.

6. **Korean franchisor situation:** Invested 500M won (management, training, initial support). Master franchisee invested 30M USD. System is failing.

---

**What went wrong with due diligence:**

1. **Didn't verify partner's franchise experience:** Master franchisee was distributor, not franchisee operator. Distribution ≠ franchising.
2. **Didn't analyze unit economics:** Didn't verify that Korean fast food unit economics work in Thailand (rent, labor costs are different).
3. **Didn't check partner's financial stability:** Didn't do deep financial audit of partner.
4. **Didn't understand competitive landscape:** Didn't research Thai fast food market (highly competitive, low margins).
5. **Didn't visit actual locations:** Korean executives didn't visit Thailand stores to assess real issues.
6. **Weak performance metrics:** Contract had loose timeline ("open locations when able"); no specific deadlines or penalties.

---

**What should have happened (due diligence checklist):**

**1. Partner Financial Analysis**
- Review partner's last 3 years of financial statements (audited)
- Analyze debt levels, cash flow, profitability
- Understand funding sources (can they invest 30M USD?)
- Run stress tests (what if sales are 30% below forecast?)

**2. Partner Track Record**
- Check reference from previous business partnerships
- Understand why partners stayed or left
- Look for pattern of success/failure
- Interview other franchisees if applicable

**3. Management Team Assessment**
- Meet management team in person
- Assess expertise in food franchising (not just distribution)
- Evaluate their understanding of SDS, operational requirements
- Check their track record in the specific industry

**4. Market Analysis**
- Competitive landscape in Thailand (who else is selling fast food?)
- Unit economics in Thailand (is margin structure viable?)
- Real estate costs (rent per sq ft in key locations)
- Labor costs (can hire/train staff affordably?)
- Customer preferences (do Thai customers want this product?)

**5. Legal & Regulatory Assessment**
- Thai franchise laws (are there protections?)
- Tax structure (any surprise costs?)
- Labor regulations (any compliance issues?)
- Real estate ownership (can partner secure good locations?)

**6. Pilot Store Assessment**
- Require partner to open 1-2 pilot stores BEFORE signing master agreement
- If pilot stores are successful, sign master agreement
- If pilots fail, no obligation to proceed

**7. Performance Metrics & Penalties**
- Specific opening timeline (Year 1: 4 stores, Year 2: 8 stores, Year 3: 15 stores)
- Sales performance targets (if average unit volume falls below X, franchisor can terminate)
- Quality standards (operational audits, customer satisfaction scores)
- Financial performance (if partner can't fund, franchisor can reduce targets)

**8. Contractual Protections**
- Performance clauses (failure to meet targets = termination rights)
- Buy-back provisions (franchisor can reclaim territory at negotiated price)
- Training & support obligations (clear SDS, training, support)
- Dispute resolution (arbitration vs. litigation)

---

**Better outcome if due diligence had been done:**

1. Would have discovered that Thai fast food market is highly competitive
2. Would have identified that partner lacked franchising experience
3. Would have run unit economics and found margins were too thin
4. Would have either:
   a. Not entered Thailand (saved 500M won in wasted investment)
   b. Chosen different partner with better credentials
   c. Adjusted business model to work in Thai economics
   d. Required pilot stores before committing

#### Segment 4: Risk Mitigation Strategies (7 minutes)

**Once you understand risks, how do you manage them?**

---

**Risk Mitigation Toolkit:**

**1. Contract Design**
- Performance clauses (specific opening targets, sales targets)
- Exit clauses (franchisor can terminate for under-performance)
- Training & support obligations (clear, measurable)
- Dispute resolution (arbitration, mediation, litigation hierarchy)

**2. Phased Entry**
- Phase 1: Pilot program (1-2 stores, 12 months)
- Phase 2: Limited expansion (5-10 stores, 24 months)
- Phase 3: Full scale (unlimited expansion based on performance)
- Each phase triggers evaluation before next phase

**3. Financial Structures**
- Performance bonds (partner puts money in escrow; released when targets met)
- Earnout provisions (franchisor holds back royalties until targets are met)
- Currency hedges (lock in exchange rates for known future payments)

**4. Operational Support**
- Dedicated on-ground team (franchisor staff in-country for first 1-2 years)
- Regular audits (quarterly store visits, quality checks)
- Training programs (continuous franchisee training)
- Systems & technology (POS systems, reporting, communication)

**5. Insurance & Hedges**
- Political risk insurance (emerging markets)
- Currency hedging (lock in exchange rates)
- Legal defense funds (budget for potential disputes)

**6. Contingency Planning**
- "What if partner fails?" → Franchisor can acquire territory, find replacement partner, or exit
- "What if market demand is lower?" → Adjust expansion timeline, reduce investment
- "What if currency collapses?" → Natural hedges reduce impact

---

**Real-world example: McDonald's China**

McDonald's entered China in 1990 through joint venture (high-risk market at the time).

**Risk mitigation approach:**
1. **Phased entry:** Started with limited locations in Shanghai (proof of concept)
2. **Strong contract:** Clear performance metrics, quality standards
3. **On-ground team:** McDonald's assigned experienced executives to China
4. **Financial structure:** Both sides invested heavily, shared risk and reward
5. **Contingency planning:** Prepared for regulatory changes, market shifts
6. **Adaptation:** Menu adapted for Chinese tastes (rice bowls, soy sauce), but maintained McDonald's core

**Result:** Successfully navigated 30+ years of Chinese economic/political changes. Now one of McDonald's largest markets.

### Summary (implied in content above)

**Key learnings:**
1. Entry mode selection requires structured decision framework (5 key questions)
2. Six major risks: market, partner, regulatory, currency, operational, system
3. Partner due diligence is critical (most common failure is partner selection)
4. Risk mitigation requires contracts, phased entry, financial structures, support, contingency planning
5. Real-world franchisors use combination of strategies to manage international risk

## SECTION 4: KEY NOTES & IMPROVISATION TIPS

**Classroom Discussion Points:**

1. **"If you were BBQ Chicken in 2018, would you enter Vietnam or US first?"**
   - Expected answer: Vietnam (market readiness, less competition, master franchisee available)
   - Key learning: Market selection matters as much as entry model

2. **"What would you have asked Thai distributor BEFORE giving them 30M USD?"**
   - Expected: Financial statements, track record, franchising experience, market analysis, unit economics
   - Key learning: Due diligence saves money

3. **"McDonald's China was 50/50 joint venture for 30 years. Now shifting to franchising. Why?"**
   - Answer: Market matured, McDonald's understands China, can manage direct franchisees
   - Key learning: Entry model isn't permanent; change as situation evolves

4. **"You're Starbucks Vietnam. Market demand is lower than expected. Do you:"**
   - A) Exit market (cut losses)
   - B) Reduce footprint, adjust model (persist with adaptation)
   - C) Maintain plan, invest more (hope for growth)
   - Expected: B (Starbucks actually did this)
   - Key learning: Contingency planning matters

**Real Data Context (2025-2026):**

- Most Korean franchises in Southeast Asia use master franchising (lower risk, lower capital)
- Nene Chicken's US failure is cautionary tale (chose direct franchising without sufficient market knowledge)
- BBQ Chicken's Vietnam success shows proper execution (chose right partner, adapted menu, respected local preferences)
- Master franchisee default rate is ~15% globally (partner underperforms or fails)
- Currency fluctuations in emerging markets can reduce overseas profits by 20-30%

**Common Student Misconceptions:**

1. **"Master franchising is always weaker than direct franchising"**
   - Reality: Master franchising is appropriate when franchisor lacks market knowledge. KFC's India success via master franchising proves this.

2. **"If we have capital, we should use direct franchising"**
   - Reality: Capital availability is one factor. Market knowledge and control priorities matter equally. Starbucks has capital but still uses mix of company-owned + franchising.

3. **"Due diligence is just paperwork"**
   - Reality: Due diligence saves millions. Thai fast food case shows cost of skipping due diligence.

4. **"Once entry model is chosen, it's permanent"**
   - Reality: McDonald's China started with JV, shifted to direct franchising as market matured. Models should evolve.

**Extension Questions:**

If student asks: **"Why did Korean franchises succeed in Vietnam but fail in US?"**
Answer: Market readiness. Vietnam was hungry for Korean brands (K-pop, K-content influence). US chicken market is saturated with established chains. Same product, different market receptiveness. Choice of market matters as much as execution.

If student asks: **"What's the biggest risk in international franchising?"**
Answer: Partner risk. Choosing wrong master franchisee or JV partner can destroy years of work and millions in capital. Financial risk is material, but partner risk is existential.

If student asks: **"Should franchisors always do pilot stores before expanding?"**
Answer: Ideally yes, but not always possible. Starbucks can't open 2 pilot stores; they open 10+ company-owned to prove model. Smaller franchisors should definitely do pilots.
