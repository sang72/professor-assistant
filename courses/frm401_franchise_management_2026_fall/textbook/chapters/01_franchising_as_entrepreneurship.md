## What is Franchising?

Franchising happens when someone develops a business model and sells the rights to operate it to another entrepreneur — a franchisee. The company selling the rights is the franchisor. The franchisee usually gets the rights to the business model for a specific time period and in a specific geographic area.

**Two types of franchising:**
- **Business format franchising**: The way the product is delivered is as important to the brand as the actual product (e.g., McDonald's golden arches)
- **Product franchising**: The actual product — not the way it is delivered — is the focus (e.g., auto dealerships)

The key feature of the franchise system is that the ownership of the brand and the modus operandi for the delivery of the product are retained by the franchisor, and execution is a franchisee responsibility.

**Scale of franchising (as of publication):**
- 4,500 franchise businesses
- 600,000 outlets
- Accounts for a third of all retail sales nationwide
- Expected to grow to 40% of U.S. retail sales

## What It Means to Be Entrepreneurial

According to Howard Stevenson of the Harvard Business School, entrepreneurship is "exploiting an opportunity regardless of the resources currently available."

**Key principle:** Focus on OPPORTUNITY, not on resources.

"Entrepreneurship is a way of thinking and acting that is opportunity obsessed, holistic in approach, and leadership balanced — for the purpose of wealth creation."

**Defining wealth:**
- Profit and capital gain are easily measured benchmarks
- Stakeholders must see intrinsic value in the exploitation of the opportunity
- Ultimately, the stakeholders must define wealth

**Components of the entrepreneurship definition:**
- **Opportunity obsession**: We are never finished shaping the deal
- **Thought and action link**: Rigorous planning process, not seat-of-the-pants behavior
- **Holistic**: The business environment is constantly changing
- **Leadership**: Entrepreneurial activity is focused by the vision of a single person or committed team

## Modeling Entrepreneurship

### The Opportunity: Core of Entrepreneurship

Three subsets of opportunity recognition:
1. **Market demand**
2. **Market size and structure**
3. **Margin analysis**

### Five Key Market Demand Questions

1. **Who is the customer?** Geographic, demographic, and psychographic data
2. **Is the customer reachable?** Channel development — build a roadway to customers
3. **Is the price-value relationship attractive?** The faster customers receive value, the greater the likelihood of increased market demand
4. **Can the business achieve a 20% market share?** Proof of sustainable market demand
5. **Can the business grow at an aggressive annual rate?** Historical norm is approximately 20% annual growth

### Geographic Profiles
- Local, regional, national, or international scope
- U.S. divided into nine regions
- Subdivisions: urban, suburban, rural
- Franchises define a market by the number of people necessary to support a single unit

### Demographic Profiles
Key variables: age, gender, income, home/work address, marital status, family status, occupation, race and ethnicity, religion, nationality

**Example — TIP 1-1: Radio Shack's Moving Target Market**
- 1970s-80s: Target market = technophiles (young men, 29 years old)
- 1990s shift: Target market = 44-year-old upper middle-class males
- Result: Dramatic improvements in profitability by adapting to changing demographics

### Psychographic Profiles
Segment potential customers based on:
- Social class (7 categories: Upper uppers 1% through Lower lowers 7%)
- Lifestyle (health consciousness, fashion orientation)
- Personality traits (self-confident, conservative, independent)

### Behavioral Profiles
Segment by knowledge, attitudes, and use of products.
Example: High-line automobile franchise customers care most about pricing and appearance → design ads with clean image and aggressive price.

## Opportunity Recognition: Market Structure and Competition

Two most fertile market structures for entrepreneurs:
1. **Fragmented market**: Opportunity to consolidate around a brand — provide consumer with a positive price-value relationship embodied in a trademark
   - Example: Jiffy Lube consolidated the fragmented automotive maintenance market
2. **Emerging market**: Aging population creating emerging market in health care

## Opportunity Recognition: Market Size

Is the juice worth the squeezing?

**Jiffy Lube market size calculation:**
- 120 million cars and light trucks in the United States
- Average car owner required preventive maintenance service 2.8 times per year
- Market size = 336 million services per year

**Five stages of the business cycle:**
1. Research and development
2. Launch
3. **Growth** ← most fertile entrepreneurial territory
4. Maturity
5. Decline

**TIP 1-2: How Jiffy Lube Exploited a Fragmented Market**
- 30-second elevator pitch: Check all fluid levels + vacuum + wash windows + inflate tires — all within 10 minutes, at one fixed price, no appointment, in a bi-level drive-through facility
- Per-unit breakeven: approximately 30 cars per day
- Many stores doubled or tripled breakeven within first year

## Opportunity Recognition: Margin Analysis

The final differentiator between an opportunity and an interesting idea.

Must demonstrate: You can provide a product or service that is faster, better, or cheaper than what currently exists.

**Key financial metrics:**
- Gross margin (basic ratio in opportunity assessment)
- Net profit
- Cash flow
- Time to break even

Common indicators of competitive advantage:
- Being the low-cost provider
- Having a lower capital requirement than competition
- Many franchisors use **20% ROI** as benchmark for successful store-level economics

## Resource Marshaling: The Service Delivery System (SDS)

**Entrepreneur's mantra**: "Minimize and control rather than maximize and own."

The SDS is the way resources are arrayed so that demand can be extracted from the marketplace.

**"Think cash last" principle**: When you have defined the appropriate resources for your opportunity, ask: How do I gain access to these resources without writing a check?
- Begin by leasing the building and equipment
- Set up payment terms so vendors can be paid with customers' cash
- Only when there is no other way, obtain cash from an investor

**Example: Panera Bread franchisee Mitch Roberts**
Instead of paying $600,000 for land + building:
- Approached landowner: "Would you build us this building and lease it to us?"
- Real estate costs reduced to $50,000 per year with no cash up front

## The Entrepreneurial Team

High-potential ventures are best brought to fruition by management teams, not individuals.

**"Vital organs" of entrepreneurs:**
1. **Opportunity obsession**: Do you view the world in terms of opportunity or resources?
2. **Flexibility**: Can you deal with ambiguity and constant change?
3. **Leadership**: Stellar leadership skills to steer the ship

**Key questions for team assessment:**
- Do team members have relevant experience?
- Can the team take advantage of each other's competitive strengths?
- Is there mutual passion and shared responsibility?

**Relevant experience categories:**
- Perfect relevance: Track record of launching a new venture in the same industry
- Still relevant: Startup experience + bottom-line responsibility + managed people and hard assets
