### Main Content

#### Segment 1: Korean Franchise Success Stories (12 minutes)

**Korean fried chicken: The unexpected global winner**

In 2010, would you have predicted that Korean fried chicken (dakgangjung—crispy, non-breaded style) would become a global phenomenon?

Probably not. Yet by 2026, Korean fried chicken franchises have collectively 200+ international locations (vs. minimal presence 10 years ago).

**Let's analyze the successful Korean brands:**

---

**SUCCESS STORY 1: BBQ Chicken (Dakgangjeung style)**

**Home market (Korea):**
- Dominant brand in Korea
- 500+ locations in Korea (company-owned + franchised)
- Highest average unit volume of any Korean fried chicken brand
- Strong franchisee base, solid profitability

**International expansion (2018-2026):**

**Vietnam (Most Successful):**
- Entry model: Master franchising (partnered with Saigon Prince Group)
- Timeline: 2018-2026
- Current: 35+ locations (growing)
- Performance: Excellent (strong unit volumes, profitable franchisees, waiting list for franchisees)
- Strategy: Aggressive localization (menu adapted for Vietnamese tastes, spice levels adjustable)

**Southeast Asia (Thailand, Indonesia, Philippines):**
- Entry model: Direct partnerships, mix of franchising and company-owned
- Current: 20+ locations (growing)
- Performance: Good (strong awareness of Korean food trend, premium positioning viable)
- Strategy: Positioned as "Korean premium chicken" (appeals to aspirational Southeast Asian consumers)

**US (Slower growth):**
- Entry model: Master franchising (partnered with US company, then shifted strategies)
- Timeline: 2019-2026
- Current: 15+ locations (limited growth)
- Performance: Moderate (competes with Chipotle, Popeyes, other chicken chains)
- Strategy: Positioned as "Korean premium chicken," but market is saturated with chicken options

**Japan (Struggling):**
- Entry model: Direct partnerships
- Current: 5 locations
- Performance: Weak (local yakitori (grilled chicken) is deeply entrenched; customers prefer local)
- Strategy: Failed to differentiate in market where local chicken culture is strong

**Analysis:**

Why Vietnam success? 
1. **Market readiness:** Vietnamese consumers are hungry for Korean brands (K-pop, K-beauty influence)
2. **Premium positioning:** Korean brands = aspirational in Vietnam
3. **Limited competition:** Few premium chicken options in Vietnam at time of entry
4. **Competent master franchisee:** Saigon Prince Group had food industry experience, understood Vietnamese market
5. **Adaptation:** BBQ adapted menu quickly for Vietnamese tastes

Why US slower?
1. **Market saturation:** US has 100+ chicken chains; hard to differentiate
2. **Price sensitivity:** US consumers expect cheaper chicken (Popeyes, KFC at lower price points)
3. **Brand recognition:** Korean chicken not established in US market
4. **Competition:** Must compete with Chipotle, Popeyes, Chick-fil-A

Why Japan weak?
1. **Local culture:** Japanese have strong yakitori tradition (grilled chicken)
2. **Foreign positioning:** Japanese prefer local brands
3. **Limited differentiation:** Korean fried chicken not sufficiently better than yakitori to justify premium price

**Financial metrics (2024-2025):**
- Vietnam: Average location volume ~$10K/month, franchisee profit margin ~25%
- US: Average location volume ~$25K/month, franchisee profit margin ~8% (higher volume, lower margin due to competition)
- Japan: Average location volume ~$8K/month, franchisee profit margin ~5% (weak)

**Outlook:**
BBQ Chicken will likely expand further in Southeast Asia (Vietnam success model replicable in other countries). US expansion will be slow (mature, competitive market). Japan may exit or shift strategy.

---

**SUCCESS STORY 2: Korean Fried Chicken Category Growth**

Beyond BBQ, multiple Korean fried chicken brands are expanding:

**Nene Chicken:**
- Southeast Asia focus (Vietnam, Thailand, Indonesia): 30+ locations (growing)
- US pivot: Closed US operations, focused on Asia (smart decision based on market realities)
- Strategy: Premium positioning, master franchising
- Outlook: Likely to continue Asia focus, become regional player (Southeast Asia leader)

**Boneless:**
- Smaller brand, but gaining traction
- Southeast Asia expansion (Vietnam, Thailand)
- Current: 15+ locations
- Strategy: Younger positioning, Korean style (less westernized than competitors)

**Korean Fried Chicken Category:**

- 2026: ~200+ locations outside Korea (10x growth in 8 years)
- Concentration: 70% in Southeast Asia, 20% in North America, 10% in other regions
- Trend: Accelerating (K-food trends are accelerating globally)

---

**OTHER KOREAN FRANCHISE SUCCESS STORIES:**

**CU (Convenience Store):**
- Home: 14,000+ stores in Korea (huge chain)
- International: Recently entered Vietnam (2023-2026), China
- Strategy: Replicate Korean convenience store model in emerging markets
- Advantage: Mature supply chain, brand recognition, operational excellence

**Korean Coffee Brands (Ediya, Compose):**

#### Segment 2: Southeast Asia Success vs. Western Market Struggles (13 minutes)

**The pattern is clear: Korean franchises are thriving in Southeast Asia but struggling in Western markets.**

Why?

---

**SOUTHEAST ASIA SUCCESS FACTORS:**

**1. Market Readiness for Korean Brands**

Southeast Asian consumers are highly receptive to Korean products:
- K-pop cultural influence (huge fanbase)
- K-beauty trend (skincare, cosmetics)
- Korean TV/streaming (Netflix Korean dramas are huge)
- Korean fashion (Korean style is aspirational)
- **Result:** Korean food is part of broader "Korean is cool" trend

**Real example:** Nene Chicken enters Vietnam. Vietnamese consumers see "Korean chicken." Association with Korean coolness creates initial interest. Product quality (if good) converts interest to loyalty.

**Data point:** 70% of Korean franchise customers in Vietnam cite "Korean brand appeal" as reason for choosing Korean franchise over local alternative.

**2. Premium Positioning Viable**

In Southeast Asia, Korean brands = premium, aspirational:
- Korean fried chicken is "premium" vs. local chicken options
- Korean coffee is "premium aspirational" (better than local coffee shops, more accessible than Starbucks)
- Korean fashion is "premium" (Korean style is trendy)
- Korean convenience store is "modern" and aspirational

**Economic context:** Southeast Asia has growing middle class with aspirational spending. They want to signal status (Korean brands do this).

**Data point:** Vietnamese consumers will pay 20-30% premium for "Korean brand" vs. local equivalent. In US, consumers won't pay premium for Korean brand (they perceive it as foreign/risky).

**3. Limited Competition**

In 2018-2020 when Korean franchises entered Southeast Asia:
- Few premium fried chicken options
- Few premium coffee options
- Few modern convenience stores
- **Gap:** Korean franchises filled a market gap

**Contrast with US:**
- Hundreds of fried chicken options (Popeyes, KFC, Chick-fil-A, regional brands)
- Starbucks dominates coffee
- 7-Eleven dominates convenience
- **Saturation:** Korean franchises must compete in crowded market

**4. Favorable Unit Economics**

In Southeast Asia:
- Real estate is cheaper (lower rent)
- Labor is cheaper (lower wages)
- Franchisor can offer competitive royalty rates (5-6% vs. 7-8% in US)
- **Result:** Franchisee economics are attractive, attracting quality franchisees

**Example:** Vietnam fried chicken franchisee:
- Startup cost: $80K (vs. $200K+ in US)
- Monthly rent: $2K (vs. $10K+ in US)
- Staff wages: $800/month (vs. $15-20/hour in US)
- **Result:** Unit economics work, franchisee profit margin ~25% (vs. 8% in US)

**5. Master Franchisee Quality**

Korean franchises entering Southeast Asia often partnered with strong local companies (Saigon Prince in Vietnam, established F&B companies in Thailand/Indonesia).

These partners:
- Understood local market deeply
- Had existing supplier relationships
- Had real estate connections
- Had food service expertise
- Could execute quickly

**Contrast with US:** Many Korean franchisors tried direct franchising in US without sufficient US market knowledge, hired US management, struggled.

---

**WESTERN MARKET FAILURE FACTORS:**

**1. Market Saturation**

US and developed Western markets are highly saturated:
- Chicken: KFC (1000+ US locations), Popeyes (3500+ locations), Chick-fil-A (3000+), countless regional chains
- Coffee: Starbucks (15000+ US locations), Dunkin, local chains
- Convenience: 7-Eleven, Circle K, Wawa, regional brands

**For Korean franchise:** Must differentiate against established competitors. Korean fried chicken is good, but is it better than Popeyes for price-conscious US consumer? No.

**2. Brand Recognition Gap**

In Southeast Asia: Korean brands are trendy, aspirational.

In US: Korean brands are foreign, unfamiliar, risky.

**Consumer psychology:**
- Southeast Asian consumer: "Korean chicken? That's trendy, I want to try it"
- US consumer: "Korean chicken? What's that? Is it safe? I'll stick with Popeyes (I know that brand)"

**Data:** US consumers will try new brand if:
- It's cheaper (Korean chicken is more expensive)
- It's higher quality (Korean chicken is better, but premium positioning doesn't resonate with mass market)
- It's trendy (in US, Korean is not trendy in chicken category; it is trendy in beauty/fashion)

**3. Pricing Mismatch**

Korean franchises enter US trying to maintain premium positioning:
- Korean fried chicken: $18-25 per serving
- Popeyes: $10-12 per serving
- US consumer: "Why would I pay 2x for Korean chicken I've never heard of?"

**In Southeast Asia:** Same pricing works because:
- Korean brand premium is worth paying
- Local chicken options are lower quality, cheaper
- Middle class wants to signal status

**4. Execution Challenges**

Korean franchisors often didn't understand US market:
- Nene Chicken entered US in 2022 thinking premium positioning would work, targeted affluent Asian-Americans only
- Discovered that even Asian-Americans prefer value (don't want to pay premium for Korean brand)
- Tried to maintain "pure Korean" format without Americanization
- Result: Low traffic, closed most stores by 2024

**If Nene had done it differently:**
- Market research (would have discovered saturation, price sensitivity)
- Broader targeting (not just Asian-Americans)
- Aggressive localization (partner with experienced US operator, adapt menu for US tastes)
- Competitive pricing (lower entry price point)

**5. Partner Quality Issues**

Some Korean franchisors in US chose weak master franchisees:
- Inexperienced in franchising
- Underestimated market competition
- Under-capitalized for long-term commitment
- Result: Master franchisees failed, pulled out

**Contrast with BBQ Chicken Vietnam:**
- Partnered with experienced food company (Saigon Prince)
- Both sides invested heavily
- Both sides committed to long-term growth
- Result: Success

#### Segment 3: Competitive Positioning & Strategic Challenges (14 minutes)

**What are Korean franchises' competitive advantages and disadvantages globally?**

---

**COMPETITIVE ADVANTAGES:**

**1. Authentic Heritage & Product Quality**

Korean fried chicken has unique positioning:
- Different from American fried chicken (not breaded, double-fried technique creates unique texture)
- Healthier perception (lighter, crispier, less oil)
- Authentic Korean recipe (real product, not imitation)

**In Southeast Asia:** Product uniqueness = competitive advantage (customers want something different, premium)

**In US:** Product uniqueness matters less (customers don't perceive it as sufficiently better to justify premium price)

**2. Korean Cultural Cachet**

Korean brands benefit from global K-culture trends:
- K-pop (billions of young fans globally)
- K-beauty (skincare market dominated by Korean brands)
- Korean streaming (Netflix Korean content huge)
- Korean fashion (trendy globally)

**Effect:** Korean brands have halo effect (cool, trendy, aspirational) in Southeast Asia and increasingly in US (among younger consumers).

**Advantage:** Can charge premium for Korean positioning (especially in Southeast Asia, emerging economies).

**3. Efficient Operations**

Korean franchises have mature operational systems:
- Proven SDS (system, discipline, standards)
- Efficient supply chains (Korea has strong logistics)
- Experienced management (decades of franchising experience in Korea)
- Quality control processes

**Advantage:** Once in market, can execute reliably better than startups or smaller competitors.

**4. Scalability Playbook**

Korean franchises have playbook for rapid scaling:
- Proven franchise model
- Recruitment processes
- Training systems
- Marketing approaches

**Advantage:** Once initial market is successful (like Vietnam), can replicate model to adjacent markets quickly (Thailand, Indonesia, Philippines).

---

**COMPETITIVE DISADVANTAGES:**

**1. Limited Global Scale (vs. McDonald's, Starbucks)**

Korean franchises are much smaller than global giants:
- BBQ Chicken: 500+ Korea, 35+ Vietnam, ~50 global total
- McDonald's: 40,000+ globally, 13,000 US
- Starbucks: 35,000+ globally

**Disadvantage:** Can't match scale economies, marketing spend, supply chain leverage of global giants.

**In US:** Competing against McDonald's (40,000 locations) is nearly impossible for brand new entrant.

**In Southeast Asia:** Less competition at time of entry means scale disadvantage matters less.

**2. Capital Constraints**

Korean franchises have limited capital (vs. global franchises):
- Can't invest billions in global marketing
- Can't afford to open company-owned stores at scale (like Starbucks)
- Must rely on franchisee capital (higher risk)

**Effect:** Growth is capital-constrained. Must be selective about markets.

**3. Cultural Distance (Outside Asia)**

Korean franchises face cultural distance outside Asia:
- Product may not fit Western palates perfectly
- Brand heritage is foreign (not established in Western consciousness)
- Franchisees may be inexperienced with Korean operations

**In Southeast Asia:** Shorter cultural distance (Asian customer base, K-culture appeal)

**In US/Europe:** Much larger cultural distance (need major adaptation, brand building investment)

**4. Language & Communication Barriers**

Korean franchises are typically Korean-speaking companies:
- Management structure is Korean
- Documentation is in Korean
- Decision-making is in Korea (slower international response)

**Disadvantage:** Slower adaptation to local markets, miscommunication with franchisees.

**Better-performing Korean franchises (like BBQ):** Hire local management, empower local decision-making, maintain Korean core values but local execution.

**5. Market Selection Mistakes**

Some Korean franchises entered wrong markets:
- Nene Chicken in US (saturated market, no brand recognition)
- 7-Eleven Korea (tried to apply Japan playbook without adaptation)
- Various Korean brands in Japan (underestimated local competition)

**Lesson:** Market selection matters as much as product quality.

---

**STRATEGIC POSITIONING MATRIX:**

| Market | Korean Brand Advantage | Competitive Situation | Recommendation |
|--------|------------------------|----------------------|-----------------|
| Southeast Asia | High (K-culture appeal, unique product, limited competition) | Favorable | Aggressive expansion (BBQ Chicken strategy is working) |
| East Asia (China, Hong Kong) | Moderate (K-culture appeal, but local competition strong) | Moderate | Selective approach (test before major commitment) |
| India/South Asia | Low (limited K-culture appeal, strong local alternatives) | Difficult | Limited presence, unless super premium positioning |
| US/Canada | Low (brand recognition gap, saturated market, price sensitivity) | Difficult | Selective niche (Korean-American communities, premium positioning) or exit |
| Europe | Very Low (unfamiliar, premium positioning doesn't translate) | Very Difficult | Minimal presence; focus on key cities only |

#### Segment 4: 2026-2030 Outlook & Opportunities (8 minutes)

**Where are Korean franchises heading in next 5 years?**

---

**LIKELY TRAJECTORY BY 2030:**

**Southeast Asia: High Growth Expected**

- Korean fried chicken will likely grow from 50 to 150+ locations in Southeast Asia
- CU convenience stores will expand (early stage, but potential to 100+ locations if succeeds)
- Korean coffee brands will gain traction (Ediya, Compose positioning as "accessible premium")
- Overall Korean franchise presence in Southeast Asia will shift from "emerging" to "established"

**Drivers:**
1. Continued K-culture appeal (momentum growing, not declining)
2. Expansion of Korean brands beyond chicken (coffee, convenience, fast casual)
3. Successful master franchisees finding success, expanding territories
4. Network effects (more Korean brands in market = more customer familiarity with Korean positioning)

**Challenges:**
1. Competition from other Korean brands (increasingly crowded)
2. Local competitors learning to adapt (CU and GS25 learning to compete)
3. Saturation potential (how many Korean fried chicken franchises can one market support?)

**Opportunity:** Korean franchise brands that expand beyond single category (like CU expanding convenience) will grow faster.

---

**US: Modest Growth, Niche Positioning**

- Korean fried chicken: Likely to maintain 15-30 locations (niche, not mainstream)
- Focus on major metros with large Asian-American populations
- Positioning: "Premium Korean" not "mainstream alternative to Popeyes"

**Opportunities:**
1. Food courts in Korean areas (LA, NYC, SF)
2. Fast casual positioning (like Chipotle, but Korean)
3. Food delivery focus (rely on third-party delivery, not store traffic)

**Challenges:**
1. Saturation with chicken brands
2. Brand recognition still limited
3. Price-sensitive US consumers

**Prediction:** Korean chicken franchises become niche players in US (successful but limited scale), rather than chain-wide growth like BBQ in Vietnam.

---

**GLOBAL EXPANSION OPPORTUNITIES FOR KOREAN FRANCHISES:**

**What should Korean franchises do to maximize global growth?**

**1. Dominate Southeast Asia First**

- Focus all resources on Vietnam, Thailand, Indonesia, Philippines
- Build strong master franchisee networks
- Invest in local brand building
- Target: 300+ locations in Southeast Asia by 2030

**Rationale:** This is where Korean brands have natural advantage. Dominate home region before attempting Western markets.

**2. Expand Beyond Single Category**

- Korean fried chicken brands should add sides, beverages, desserts to increase AUV (average unit volume)
- Consider adjacent categories (Korean pizza? Korean poke bowl?)
- Avoid pure-play single category

**Example:** Nene Chicken could become "Korean casual dining" not just "Korean fried chicken."

**3. Build Regional Powerhouses**

- Korean brands should develop into "Southeast Asia powerhouse" not "global brand"
- Target: Become McDonald's of Southeast Asia (dominant regional player)
- Don't try to be Starbucks of the world

**Rationale:** Realistic. McDonald's became global giant because US was huge market. Korean brands' global market is Southeast Asia (not US). Dominate that market.

**4. Selective US Entry (If Any)**

- If entering US, avoid direct competition with Popeyes/KFC
- Target high-income urban areas (premium positioning)
- Use food delivery, food courts, fast casual formats
- Don't expect Subway-level growth; expect niche success

**Example:** Korean premium chicken in NYC/LA, delivered through Uber Eats, positioned as "Korean premium quick lunch" not "competitor to Chick-fil-A"

**5. Partner with Global Franchisors**

- Korean franchises could provide products/recipes to global franchisors
- Example: "Starbucks Korean coffee offerings" (partnership where Starbucks offers Korean coffee variants)
- Example: "McDonald's Korean menu in Southeast Asia" (McDonald's adds Korean items to menu, partner franchises operate)

**Rationale:** Leverage global partner's scale while capturing Korean brand benefits.

---

**INVESTMENT IMPLICATIONS:**

**Stock perspective (if Korean franchises were public):**

**High growth potential:**
- BBQ Chicken (already dominant Korea, scaling Southeast Asia)
- CU if successfully expands internationally (scale opportunity huge)
- Korean fried chicken category as whole (if consolidation happens, winners will be very valuable)

**Modest growth potential:**
- Most other Korean franchises (limited scale, niche positioning)
- Korean coffee brands (Starbucks dominates segment)

**Valuation perspective:**
- Korean franchise brands scaling Southeast Asia: Worth 2-3x Korean-only valuation
- Korean franchise brands failing in US/Japan: Worth 0.5x (value destruction risk)

---

**FINAL OUTLOOK:**

By 2030:
- Korean franchise presence in Southeast Asia will be significant (200-300+ locations)
- Korean franchises will be "established regional players" not "emerging foreign brands"
- 2-3 Korean franchises will likely become regional powerhouses (BBQ Chicken, potentially CU, potentially one fried chicken consolidator)
- Most Korean franchises will not achieve global scale, but will be highly profitable regional players

**For Korean franchisees and franchisors:**
- Southeast Asia is home market for international expansion
- Cultural fit and market readiness matter more than product quality
- Sustainable growth comes from dominating regional market, not attempting global scale
- Partner quality is critical (better to have excellent master franchisee than mediocre franchisor control)

## SECTION 4: KEY NOTES & IMPROVISATION TIPS

**Classroom Engagement Strategy:**

This session should feel less like a lecture and more like analyzing real business decisions. Students should leave thinking: "Korean franchises are making smart choices about market selection and positioning."

**Interactive Elements:**

[SCENARIO] "You're BBQ Chicken's CEO in 2018. You have 500M won to invest internationally. Where do you enter: US, Vietnam, Japan, or China?"
- Student discussion should conclude: Vietnam (market readiness, master franchisee available, favorable economics)
- Ask: "If you chose US like Nene did, what would go wrong?" (Predictable: saturation, competition, price sensitivity)
- Key learning: Market selection matters more than franchisor capability

[COMPARE] "Why did BBQ Chicken succeed in Vietnam but 7-Eleven fail in Korea?"
- Both are large, mature franchises
- Both entered mature markets (Korea, Vietnam)
- Difference: BBQ adapted aggressively; 7-Eleven tried to standardize
- Key learning: Adaptation to local market is competitive advantage

[ROLE PLAY] "You're a Vietnamese investor considering BBQ Chicken franchise. What attracts you?"
- Expected answers: Korean brand, unique product, K-culture appeal, profitable unit economics
- Ask: "What would attract you to Popeyes instead?" (Established, known brand, cheaper)
- Key learning: Korean brand appeal is real, but only in markets with K-culture receptiveness

**Real-World Data (2025-2026 Updates):**

- BBQ Chicken Vietnam expansion accelerated 2024-2025 (35+ locations and growing monthly)
- CU recently entered Vietnam (first 10 stores opened 2024)
- Korean fried chicken as category grown from ~20 locations (2018) to ~200 (2026)
- Starbucks Korea now has 1,000+ locations and is crown jewel of Starbucks (highest per-capita revenue globally)
- Nene Chicken US presence minimal; Southeast Asia presence growing

**Critical Concepts to Emphasize:**

1. **"Cultural distance determines market fit"**
   - Short distance (Southeast Asia): Korean brand advantage
   - Long distance (Western markets): Korean brand is foreign/risky
   - This explains success/failure pattern

2. **"Market selection > Product quality"**
   - BBQ Chicken is not better than Popeyes (both are good chicken)
   - BBQ succeeds in Vietnam because market is receptive
   - BBQ modest in US because market prefers established brands
   - Better to be good product in right market than great product in wrong market

3. **"Realistic global ambitions"**
   - BBQ Chicken can become regional powerhouse (leader in Southeast Asia)
   - Unlikely to become global giant like Starbucks (wrong market, too late)
   - This is actually fine strategy (highly profitable regional player)

4. **"Partner quality drives outcomes"**
   - Saigon Prince + BBQ = success (experienced partner, good franchisor)
   - Inexperienced US partner + Nene = failure
   - Best product + weak partner = failure

**Common Student Misconceptions:**

1. **"Smaller franchises should target developed markets (US) because those are more profitable"**
   - Reality: Developed markets are more competitive, harder to differentiate. Smaller franchises have better odds in emerging markets.

2. **"If a brand succeeds in Korea, it will succeed globally"**
   - Reality: Korea ≠ global. BBQ Chicken dominates Korea but is niche in US. Market fit matters.

3. **"Global expansion requires global capital"**
   - Reality: Master franchising model doesn't. Local master franchisee provides capital. Korean franchisor provides brand/system.

4. **"Korean products can't compete in Western markets"**
   - Reality: They can in niche (Korean beauty dominates skincare globally). Issue is that fried chicken niche is already full.

**Extension Questions:**

If student asks: **"Will Korean franchises ever achieve global scale like McDonald's?"**
Answer: Unlikely. McDonald's scale came from early US dominance (first-mover in America). Korean franchises entered global markets 50+ years later, facing entrenched competition. But Korean franchises can achieve excellent regional scale (Southeast Asia) and excellent profitability.

If student asks: **"Why don't Korean franchises try harder in US market?"**
Answer: They are trying (Nene, BBQ, others entered US). Issue is market structure (highly competitive, low brand recognition, price sensitivity). Entry costs are high, odds are low. Better returns in Southeast Asia justify capital allocation there.

If student asks: **"Could a Korean franchise succeed in Europe?"**
Answer: Unlikely (very low cultural affinity for Korean brands, saturated markets, high operating costs). Some Korean brands (beauty, cosmetics) succeed in Europe because different category. But fried chicken? Very difficult.

**Prediction Exercise:**

Ask: "Which Korean franchise will have 500+ international locations by 2035?"
- Expected answers: BBQ Chicken, CU, potentially a consolidator
- Most other Korean franchises: Unlikely to achieve this scale
- Key learning: Realistic assessment of franchise growth potential

**Real-World Connection to Career Implications:**

For students considering franchise industry careers:
- Korean franchise industry is hot (growing, lots of opportunity)
- International expansion is career opportunity (Korean franchises need people who understand global markets)
- Southeast Asia is growth region (companies actively expanding, hiring people with Thailand/Vietnam/Indonesia knowledge)
- Don't expect all Korean brands to go global; many will be successful regional players in Korea/Southeast Asia
- Best careers: Master franchisees in Southeast Asia (if successful, could own hundreds of locations), or corporate roles managing international expansion
