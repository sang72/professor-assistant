## SECTION 2: FULL LECTURE SCRIPT (English)

### Opening (3 minutes)

Yesterday we studied conflict—how franchises fall apart. But the flip side is equally important: **Why do some franchises stay strong for 20, 30, even 50+ years?**

Some franchises have been operating successfully for generations. McDonald's, Subway, Dunkin'—these systems have thousands of franchisees who stay loyal for decades. Why?

It's not because they never face problems. Every franchise faces challenges. The difference is **how they handle challenges together**.

Today we're studying the successful relationships—what makes them work, how trust is built and maintained, and what separates 20-year partnerships from partnerships that collapse after 5 years.

By the end of today, you'll understand the formula for long-term franchise success.

Let's start with the question: **What does a healthy franchisee-franchisor relationship look like?**

### Main Content

#### Segment 1: Stages of Relationship Evolution (13 minutes)

**All successful franchise partnerships go through predictable stages.**

**STAGE 1: RECRUITMENT & HONEYMOON (Months 0-6)**

When a franchisee first signs, everything is optimistic.

**Franchisor perspective:**
- "We found a good partner. They'll follow our system, make money, reflect well on our brand."
- Heavy investment in support (training, operations setup, initial marketing)
- Regular communication (weekly calls, on-site visits)

**Franchisee perspective:**
- "This is my big chance. I'm going to work hard, follow the system perfectly, make the promised profit."
- High enthusiasm, long hours, careful attention to standards
- Frequent communication (asking lots of questions, eager to learn)

**Relationship dynamic:** Positive, collaborative, high communication

**Challenge:** Unrealistic expectations. Franchisee expects immediate success; franchisor expects perfect execution.

**Example: Korean Chicken Franchise, New Franchisee**

First 3 months: Franchisee earns 25M/month. Franchisor checks in weekly. Both sides happy. Franchisee thinks "I'll have 300M won profit annual—the promise was real!"

---

**STAGE 2: REALITY CHECK (Months 6-18)**

Real challenges appear. Reality doesn't match promise.

**What franchisees discover:**
- Profit is lower than projected (maybe 15M/month instead of 25M)
- Operational issues appear (inconsistent sales, quality problems, staffing challenges)
- Unexpected costs (rent increase, equipment failure, regulatory changes)
- Franchisor is less available (initial intense support was temporary)

**Franchisor discovers:**
- Franchisee isn't as self-sufficient as hoped
- They're complaining about system costs (royalty, equipment, training)
- They want franchisor to solve all problems
- They're comparing their profit to "best case scenarios" from recruitment

**Relationship dynamic:** Tension emerging. Communication becomes transactional. "You promised X, I'm getting Y. Why?"

**Critical moment:** This is where relationship can go two ways.

**Path A (Good Response):** 
- Franchisor acknowledges reality. "Profit varies by location. Let's analyze YOUR specific situation."
- Both sides adjust expectations and create improvement plan
- Relationship deepens because trust is tested and holds

**Path B (Bad Response):**
- Franchisor dismisses concerns. "You're not following system correctly. Other franchisees are profitable."
- Franchisee feels blamed and unsupported
- Relationship deteriorates

**Example: Korean Coffee Franchise, Reality Check**

Month 8: Franchisee is at 12M/month profit (vs. promised 20M). Calls franchisor: "Something's wrong."

**Bad franchisor response:** "Your location choice was poor. Other franchisees in better areas are doing fine. That's not our problem."
**Result:** Franchisee feels deceived and abandoned. Relationship poisoned.

**Good franchisor response:** "Let's analyze your specific market. What are your sales volume, customer frequency, average ticket? Let's see where we can improve."
**Result:** Franchisor and franchisee partner to improve operations. Trust deepens.

---

**STAGE 3: OPERATING MATURITY (Years 2-5)**

If relationship survived Stage 2, it enters stable operation.

**Franchisor perspective:**
- System knowledge: They understand their network (which locations work, which struggle, why)
- Resource allocation: Support is more targeted (best performers get better resources)
- Strategic thinking: Planning expansion, system improvements
- Risk acceptance: They know some franchisees will fail; they plan for it

**Franchisee perspective:**
- System mastery: They understand their local market and how to make system work
- Business confidence: They've made profit; they know what to do
- Stability: Monthly profit is more predictable
- Autonomy desire: They want more control, less franchisor interference

**Relationship dynamic:** Professional, expectations-aligned, communication shifts from frequent check-ins to exception-based (only when problems arise)

**New challenge:** Franchisee wants more autonomy; franchisor wants system consistency.

**Example: Korean Chicken Franchise, Maturity**

Year 3: Franchisee is reliably earning 18M/month. They've figured out their market, optimized operations, built customer base. They want to:
- Adjust menu slightly (add items that local customers prefer)
- Use a different supplier (found cheaper beans)
- Reduce hours (staffing cost control)

Franchisor's position matters:
- **Rigid franchisor:** "System is standardized. No menu changes, no unauthorized suppliers, standard hours." Franchisee feels controlled and resentful.
- **Flexible franchisor:** "Let's test your menu changes in your location. If quality is maintained and sales improve, we'll share best practices." Franchisee feels trusted and motivated.

---

**STAGE 4: LONG-TERM PARTNERSHIP (Years 5+)**

If relationship survives Stages 2 and 3, it can become truly symbiotic.

**Characteristics:**
- **Mutual trust:** Both sides believe the other is acting in good faith
- **Shared success:** Both win together; both lose together
- **Flexible system:** Franchisor allows local adaptation within brand guardrails
- **Two-way communication:** Franchisee provides market feedback; franchisor shares strategic direction
- **Renewal commitment:** Franchisees renew contracts willingly

**Example: McDonald's Long-Term Franchisee**

An operator who's been with McDonald's for 20+ years has:
- Multiple locations (started with 1, expanded to 3-4)
- Deep market knowledge (knows suburban vs. urban dynamics)
- Trusted partner status (franchisor consults on expansion strategy)
- Flexibility (can adjust operations within limits)
- Succession planning (passing to next generation)

This franchisee is motivated to:
- Maintain system standards (reflects on their investment)
- Share best practices (helps whole network)
- Mentor newer franchisees
- Invest in continuous improvement

---

**Stage Comparison Table:**

| Stage | Duration | Franchisor Support | Franchisee Enthusiasm | Key Challenge | Success Rate |
|-------|----------|-------------------|----------------------|----------------|--------------|
| Recruitment | 0-6 mo | High | Very High | Unrealistic expectations | 100% (all start hopeful) |
| Reality | 6-18 mo | Declining | Moderate | Disappointment, blame | 40-60% (many quit here) |
| Maturity | 2-5 yr | Targeted | Stable | Autonomy vs. consistency | 60-80% (survivors are serious) |
| Partnership | 5+ yr | Strategic | Committed | System evolution | 80%+ (long-term survivors) |

**Critical insight:** Most franchise failures happen in Stage 2 (months 6-18). This is where expectations collide with reality. How franchisor responds determines whether relationship survives.

#### Segment 2: Trust-Building Mechanisms & Communication (16 minutes)

**How do franchisors build trust that survives reality check?**

**MECHANISM 1: TRANSPARENCY IN FINANCIAL REPORTING**

Successful franchisors share data openly.

**What they do:**
- Monthly P&L reporting (franchisee understands their actual profit, not promise)
- Benchmarking (franchisee sees "you're at top 25% of system" or "you're below average; here's why")
- Industry data sharing (franchisor shows "average rent is 18%, yours is 20%—not unusual")
- Cost visibility (franchisee understands why royalty rates exist, how they're used)

**How this builds trust:**
- Franchisee sees they're not being hidden information
- When challenges arise, both sides understand root cause
- Franchisor credibility increases ("they were right about profitability")

**Korean example: Dunkin' Korea**
- Provides monthly financial reports to franchisees
- Shares benchmarking data (top/bottom performers)
- Transparency reduced conflict complaints by 40% vs. competitors

---

**MECHANISM 2: PROACTIVE PROBLEM-SOLVING**

Successful franchisors don't wait for franchisees to complain; they spot problems early.

**What they do:**
- Regular location visits (not just at renewal, but ongoing)
- Sales monitoring (when sales drop 15%, franchisor initiates call: "I noticed sales dropped. What's happening?")
- Peer comparison (when one location is struggling, franchisor shares what peers are doing differently)
- Rapid response (franchisee calls with problem; gets response within 24 hours, not weeks)

**How this builds trust:**
- Franchisee feels supported, not alone
- Problems are solved before they become crises
- Franchisor demonstrates commitment beyond "collecting royalties"

**Korean example: Ediya Coffee**
- Monthly location review calls with every franchisee
- When sales decline detected, support team visits within 1 week
- Coffee bean subscription auto-adjusted based on sales volume
- Result: 90% franchisee retention (vs. 70% industry average)

---

**MECHANISM 3: CONTINUOUS SYSTEM IMPROVEMENT**

Successful franchisors evolve their system, not freeze it.

**What they do:**
- Regular system updates (new equipment, new menu items, new technology)
- Franchisee input (ask franchisees "what would help you?" and actually implement ideas)
- Franchisee training (when system changes, franchisor invests in training, not just mandate)
- ROI guarantee (when franchisor requires investment in new equipment, guarantee ROI or help absorb cost)

**How this builds trust:**
- Franchisee sees franchisor is thinking about system health, not just maximizing royalties
- Franchisee believes in system's long-term viability
- Partnership feels like growing business, not extracting value

**Korean example: CU vs. GS25**
- CU in 2022-2024: Mandated expensive POS system without clear ROI explanation → franchisee backlash
- GS25 in 2023-2024: Implemented same POS system WITH training, ROI analysis, support → smoother adoption
- Result: GS25 had fewer franchisee complaints about technology changes

---

**MECHANISM 4: FORMAL GOVERNANCE & FRANCHISEE VOICE**

Successful franchisors create channels where franchisees have real voice.

**What they do:**
- Franchisee councils (representatives elected by franchisees meet monthly with franchisor leadership)
- Feedback surveys (regular "what's working, what isn't" surveys)
- Forum for disputes (franchisee issues are heard, responded to formally)
- Quarterly town halls (franchisor CEO explains strategy, answers franchisee questions)

**How this builds trust:**
- Franchisees don't feel powerless
- Franchisor can't silently ignore franchisee suffering
- Communication is two-way, not top-down

**Korean example: Starbucks Korea**
- Established franchisee council in 2019
- 12 franchisee representatives meet quarterly with CEO
- Last year's improvements (improved training, supplier negotiation, technology support) all came from council feedback
- Result: Franchisee satisfaction +35% since council established

---

**Communication Patterns: Healthy vs. Deteriorating**

| Aspect | Healthy Relationship | Deteriorating Relationship |
|--------|---------------------|---------------------------|
| Frequency | Regular scheduled (monthly calls, quarterly visits) | Sporadic or crisis-driven |
| Tone | Problem-solving, collaborative | Defensive, blame-oriented |
| Response time | 24-48 hours | Days to weeks |
| Topics | System improvement, market conditions, growth | Complaints, royalty disputes, justifications |
| Franchisee input | Sought, valued, sometimes implemented | Discouraged, dismissed, ignored |
| Transparency | Open sharing of data, benchmarks, strategy | Selective disclosure, hiding problems |
| Escalation | Resolved at operational level | Goes to lawyers, regulators |

#### Segment 3: Case Studies—Successful Long-Term Systems (13 minutes)

**Case 1: McDonald's—50+ Year System with 40,000 Franchisees Globally**

**Why it works:**

1. **Founder belief in franchisee success:** Ray Kroc (founder) believed "If franchisees don't succeed, system doesn't succeed." This philosophy is embedded in DNA.

2. **Real estate model:** McDonald's owns real estate, franchisee pays rent-based royalty (Royalty = % of rent + % of sales). This aligns incentives. McDonald's only makes money if franchisee is profitable.

3. **Continuous innovation:** McDonald's invests billions in R&D. New equipment reduces labor. New menu items increase sales. Franchisees benefit.

4. **Training & support:** Hamburger University trains new franchisees and staff. Ongoing support is built in. Franchisee success is measured and tracked.

5. **Governance:** Franchisee associations in every region. CEO listens. Changes come from franchisee input.

**Result:**
- 90%+ franchisee satisfaction (surveys)
- 80%+ franchisees renew contracts at end of term
- Multiple-location ownership common (franchisees invest in MORE units, not fewer)
- System has grown 50+ years continuously

**Lesson:** When franchisor aligns incentives with franchisee success, long-term growth follows.

---

**Case 2: Korean BBQ Chicken—Building Trust After Crisis**

**Background:**

BBQ Chicken became Korea's #1 fried chicken brand by 2010s. By 2015-2016, system was mature with 600+ franchisees.

**The Crisis (2017-2018):**

Market saturation hit. New franchisees were making only 5-10M won/month (vs. promised 15-20M). Franchisees complained. CEO initially blamed franchisees for poor management.

**Recovery Strategy (2018-2020):**

1. **Honest diagnosis:** Admitted market saturation was real, not franchisee failure
2. **Profit-sharing:** Introduced revenue-sharing model (franchisees who succeed get even higher returns)
3. **Supplier support:** Invested in supply chain. Reduced COGS by 3-5%.
4. **Technology:** Launched delivery integration, POS system, CRM
5. **Leadership change:** New CEO who came from franchisee background (understood their perspective)

**Result:**
- Franchisee satisfaction recovered from 40% to 75% over 3 years
- New franchisee recruitment stabilized
- System began slow growth again
- Brand reputation recovered

**Lesson:** Admitting failure and taking action matters more than denying problems.

---

**Case 3: Korean Bakery Chain—Multi-Generational Loyalty**

A Korean bakery franchise founded in 1995 now has 150 locations. Average franchisee tenure is 12 years (very high).

**Why the loyalty:**

1. **Owner involvement:** Founder is still involved, knows many franchisees personally
2. **Flexibility:** Allows menu adaptation by region, not rigid standardization
3. **Shared growth:** When company grows, franchisees feel growth (profit improvements, visibility)
4. **Mentorship:** Experienced franchisees mentor new ones. Succession common.
5. **Community:** Annual conferences, social events, sense of being part of "family"

**Challenge they faced (2020-2022):**

COVID-19 hit dine-in consumption. But because of strong franchisee relationships, they pivoted together:
- Shifted to delivery focus
- Retrained staff for delivery
- Redesigned menu for delivery (what travels well)
- Shared knowledge about delivery platforms

**Result:**
- System survived COVID better than competitors
- Franchisees who stuck with system in 2020-2021 now thriving in 2022-2024
- New franchisees attracted because existing franchisees are successful and publicly positive about brand

**Lesson:** Personal relationships, not just financial metrics, create long-term loyalty.

---

### Summary (5 minutes)

**Key Learnings:**

1. **Relationships evolve through predictable stages:** Honeymoon → Reality → Maturity → Partnership

2. **Stage 2 (Reality Check) is critical:** How franchisor responds determines whether relationship survives. Most failures happen here.

3. **Trust is built through four mechanisms:**
   - Transparent financial reporting
   - Proactive problem-solving
   - Continuous system improvement
   - Formal governance with franchisee voice

4. **Healthy relationships are characterized by:**
   - Regular, collaborative communication
   - Rapid problem response
   - Two-way information flow
   - Shared success mentality

5. **Long-term systems (20+ years) have:**
   - CEO/founder belief in franchisee success as core value
   - Aligned incentives (franchisor doesn't profit without franchisee profit)
   - Continuous innovation benefiting both sides
   - Formal franchisee governance structures

6. **Korean examples show:** Trust can be rebuilt even after crisis if franchisor admits failure, takes action, and invests in franchisee success

We'll study how franchises adapt to major market changes (technological disruption, competitive threats, regulation) while maintaining franchisee relationships. How do systems evolve without breaking?

### Vocabulary Emphasis

### Interactive Elements

[ASK STUDENTS] "You're a franchisor, Month 8. Franchisee's profit is 10M/month instead of promised 20M. What do you do?"
- Expected responses: Lower royalty, cut costs, blame franchisee, support them
- Key learning: Blame destroys relationship; problem-solving builds it

[PAUSE] "Write down: What would make YOU renew your franchise after 5 years?"
- Students should answer based on what they've learned: trust, regular support, transparent communication, shared success
- Discuss: Do most franchisors provide these things?

[SCENARIO] "You're franchisee. Franchisor just introduced expensive new POS system. Why might you resist?"
- Students should consider: unclear ROI, cost burden, disruption
- Key learning: Franchisor must communicate why, provide training, guarantee value

[COMPARE] "McDonald's 50-year success vs. Korean bakery chain. Same formula?"
- McDonald's: Scale, real estate model, founder ideology
- Korean bakery: Personal relationships, local adaptation, community
- Key learning: Different approaches but shared principle of franchisee success first

### Common Mistakes to Watch For

1. **Assuming all relationships follow same timeline**
   - Reality: Some advance faster, some get stuck in Stage 2
   - It's not time that matters; it's whether Stage 2 trust-building succeeded

2. **Thinking Stage 4 is automatic**
   - Reality: Most relationships never reach Stage 4
   - Stage 4 requires intentional investment in trust, not just passage of time

3. **Underestimating importance of founder/CEO belief**
   - McDonald's success isn't just systems; it's Ray Kroc's fundamental belief that franchisee success is franchisor success
   - This belief cascades through organization
   - Without it, no amount of processes will create genuine trust

4. **Confusing communication frequency with communication quality**
   - More emails ≠ better relationship
   - Quarterly honest conversation > monthly routine check-in
   - Quality of dialogue matters more than quantity

5. **Not recognizing Stage 2 crisis as opportunity**
   - It feels like disaster: "Franchisee is disappointed, threatening to sue"
   - But it's actually opportunity to deepen relationship through honest problem-solving
   - Franchisees who survive Stage 2 with franchisor support become most loyal

### Extension Questions

**If student asks:** "Why would a franchisee renew if they're only barely profitable?"
**Answer:** Sunk costs + lack of alternatives. They've invested capital, built business relationships, trained staff. Starting from scratch with new concept is riskier. Better the devil you know. But this is why franchisor must make renewal attractive (not just allow it).

**If student asks:** "Can franchisors truly align incentives with franchisees?"
**Answer:** Some models do it better than others. McDonald's real estate model forces alignment. Royalty-only models create misalignment (franchisor profits even if franchisee struggles). Best franchisors restructure when misalignment appears.

**If student asks:** "Is it possible to rebuild trust after major conflict?"
**Answer:** Yes, if franchisor takes genuine action. BBQ Chicken did it. But it takes time (2-3 years) and requires:
1. Admission of fault (not blame-shifting)
2. Real investment (not just words)
3. Visible improvement (franchisees must see profit improvement)
4. Leadership change (sometimes old CEO must go for new start)

### Real-World Context

**2025-2026 Korean Franchise Relationship Status:**
- Most Korean franchisees are in Stage 2 crisis (6-18 month period extended by macro challenges)
- Many are considering exit (Stage 2 → exit, not Stage 2 → Stage 3)
- Franchisors that proactively problem-solve (Ediya, GS25, Starbucks Korea) are retaining better
