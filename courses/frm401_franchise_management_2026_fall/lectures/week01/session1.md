### Part 3: Course Structure & 15-Week Schedule (0:10–0:18)

[SHOW SLIDE 2: 15-Week Course Schedule Overview]

Let me show you the big picture. This course runs for 15 weeks. We meet every week, and each week has three sessions — three separate class meetings. In each session, we will study the textbook, work through case studies, participate in discussions, and develop your understanding of franchising.

[WRITE ON BOARD: 15 Weeks = 3 Sessions per Week = 45 Total Class Sessions]

The 15 weeks break down into three major phases. Weeks 1 through 6 are our first phase. In this phase, we are building the foundation. We learn what franchising is, we understand the relationship between the franchisor — that is, the company that owns the franchise system — and the franchisee, who is the individual or business that buys the right to operate under that brand. We study how franchises create wealth. We explore something called the Service Delivery System, or SDS, which is how a franchise deploys resources to serve customers consistently. And we learn how franchisors select and monitor their franchisees.

At the end of Week 6, we have finished the first major section of the course. Then, in Week 7, we stop and review. We spend all of Week 7 preparing for the Midterm Exam. The Midterm Exam happens in the third session of Week 7. It covers everything from Weeks 1 through 6. The exam is 75 minutes long. You will answer multiple-choice questions, short-answer questions, and one short essay question. All together, the exam is worth 30 points toward your final grade.

[PAUSE 3s]

[TIME: ~8 min]

Then, in Weeks 8 through 14, we move into the second major section of the course. Here, we continue our study of franchising, but we go deeper. We study marketing within a franchise system. We learn transaction analysis — how to assign tasks and responsibilities between franchisor and franchisee. We study the financial side of franchising, including profitability and investment returns. We examine the dynamics of the relationship between franchisees and franchisors — what happens when they face conflicts or need to adapt. We study franchise law — both in the United States and in Korea — because understanding the legal framework is absolutely critical. And we explore international franchising, including how American and global brands expand across borders.

In Week 14, we finish the new material. Then, in Week 15, we review again. Week 15 is Final Exam Preparation Week. The Final Exam happens in the third session of Week 15. It is 90 minutes long and covers Weeks 8 through 14, with 20 percent of the material coming from Weeks 1 through 6 as cumulative review. The Final Exam is also worth 30 points.

[SHOW SLIDE 3: Assessment Timeline]

On this slide, you can see the full assessment calendar. Notice that Assignment Number 1 is due at the end of Week 6. This assignment is an individual assignment where you choose to analyze either a Korean franchise brand or a global franchise system. More details on that in Week 5.

Does the overall timeline make sense so far? Any questions before we move to grading?

### Part 4: Grading Policy & Breakdown (0:18–0:28)

[SHOW SLIDE 4: Grading Breakdown Pie Chart]

Now, let us talk about grades. Your final grade is composed of five components. Let me list them:

[WRITE ON BOARD: Attendance = 20 points | Midterm = 30 points | Final = 30 points | Assignment = 10 points | Attitude = 10 points | TOTAL = 100 points]

First, Attendance is 20 points. That is 20 percent of your grade. This is important. You are expected to be present and on time to every class. If you arrive more than 10 minutes late, you will be marked as one-half absent. If you arrive more than 20 minutes late, you will be marked as fully absent. If you attend fewer than 40 minutes of the 50-minute session, that counts as an absence. More than five absences, and your grade will be severely impacted. In fact, six absences may result in an automatic F. So, please prioritize attendance.

[PAUSE 3s]

Second, the Midterm Exam is 30 points, and the Final Exam is 30 points. Together, the two exams are worth 60 points out of 100 — that is 60 percent of your grade. This shows you how important it is to prepare for these exams. Both exams are cumulative. Both exams have three parts. Part A is multiple choice — 30 questions, each worth 1.5 points, for a total of 45 points in Part A. Part B is short answer — 5 questions, each worth 8 points, for a total of 40 points in Part B. Part C is one short essay question worth 15 points. All together, that is 100 points per exam, which then converts to your 30-point share of the course grade.

[SHOW SLIDE 5: Multiple Choice vs. Short Answer vs. Essay]

The reason for this structure is that it tests you at different levels. Multiple choice tests whether you can recognize correct definitions and concepts. Short answer tests whether you can explain concepts in your own words. And the essay tests whether you can synthesize ideas and apply them to real situations.

[PAUSE 3s]

Third, you have one major Assignment, worth 10 points. You can choose either to analyze a Korean franchise brand or to analyze a global franchise system. You have flexibility here — pick what interests you. More on this in Week 5.

Fourth, your Attitude and Participation is worth 10 points. This is evaluated throughout the semester based on whether you come to class prepared, whether you participate in discussions, whether you ask thoughtful questions, and whether you treat your classmates and me with respect. At the middle of the semester, in Week 8, I will give each of you feedback on your current participation score so you know where you stand and have a chance to improve.

[TIME: ~18 min]

[SHOW SLIDE 6: Final Grade Scale]

Now, what do these points mean in terms of a letter grade? Let me show you the scale. One hundred points is an A-plus. Ninety to 92 is an A-minus. Eighty to 82 is a B-minus. And the minimum passing grade is a C-minus, which is 70 to 72 points. Anything below 60 points is an F, a failure. I hope you will aim for much higher than 70, of course. The good news is that this course is designed to be achievable. If you attend class, prepare your assignment, and study for the exams, you should do well.

Does the grading breakdown make sense? Is anyone unsure about how their grade will be calculated?

### Part 5: Course Policies & Expectations (0:28–0:36)

[SHOW SLIDE 7: Course Policies Overview]

Now, let me explain four important course policies. You will receive a printed syllabus with all of this written down, so you can refer to it anytime. But I want to emphasize these points in person.

**First — Academic Integrity.** All work you submit must be your own original work. Plagiarism is not tolerated. If you copy work from another student, if you submit work that was generated by an AI tool without permission, or if you present someone else's ideas as your own, you will receive a zero for that assignment. This is non-negotiable. And it may result in further disciplinary action by the university. So, if you are unsure about what counts as plagiarism, please come talk to me before you submit work.

**Second — Late Submission Policy.** Assignments have firm due dates. If you submit late — even one day late — your grade will be reduced by 10 percent. Two days late, 20 percent reduction. Three days late, 30 percent reduction. After three days, the assignment will not be accepted. Exams have no make-up option unless you have a documented medical emergency. So, please start your work early and plan ahead.

**Third — Communication.** If you need to reach me, send me an email. I will respond within 48 hours. If you have questions about grades, send an email within one week of receiving your grade. Office hours will be posted on the syllabus, and I am happy to discuss any course material with you.

**Fourth — Accommodation for Students with Disabilities.** If you have a documented disability or learning need, please contact me within the first two weeks of the semester. The university disability services office will provide documentation, and we will work together to ensure you have the support you need.

[PAUSE 3s]

Let me repeat the most important one: **Academic integrity.** If you are unsure whether something is okay, ask me. I would rather you ask than risk an F on an assignment.
