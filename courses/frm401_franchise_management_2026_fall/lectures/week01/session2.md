## SECTION 2: FULL LECTURE SCRIPT

### Opening (5 minutes)

Welcome back, everyone! Yesterday we covered what franchising is conceptually. Today we're zooming out to see the big picture: How big is franchising? Where does it thrive? What does it look like in the real world?

Let me start with a reality check. By the time you finish your lunch today, you will have probably interacted with at least two or three franchises. The coffee you drank this morning? Likely a franchise. The convenience store you visited? Franchise. The gym you work out at? Franchise. We don't always realize it, but franchising is woven into our daily lives.

So let's quantify it.

### Main Content (32 minutes)

#### Segment 1: The Global Scale of Franchising (8 minutes)

[SHOW SLIDE 1: Global Franchising Scale]

Franchising is **enormous**. Let me give you the numbers:

**United States:**
- 750,000+ franchise units
- $900+ billion in annual sales (as of 2024-2025)
- Approximately 1 out of every 2 retail sales happens through a franchised business
- Employs over 8 million people

**Globally:**
- Present in 150+ countries
- Billions of people interact with franchises daily
- Growing rapidly in Asia, Latin America, and Africa

[WRITE ON BOARD: 750,000 US units | $900B+ annual sales | 1 of 2 retail sales | 150+ countries globally]

Think about this. $900 billion. That's not $900 million. That's $900 billion. If you stacked $100 bills, one million dollars is about the height of a refrigerator. A billion dollars is about the height of a skyscraper. Nine hundred billion dollars is — well, you get the picture. This is an enormous portion of the global economy.

And it's growing. Franchising has grown at approximately 5-10% annually over the past decade. During economic downturns when overall retail struggles, franchising often holds up better. Why? Because franchisees are owners. They're committed. They weather downturns differently than salaried managers.

The point: **Franchising is not a niche business model. It's mainstream. It's how the modern economy works.**

#### Segment 2: Industries Where Franchising Thrives (9 minutes)

Now, franchising doesn't appear equally across all industries. Some industries are franchising-friendly. Others are not. Why?

[SHOW SLIDE 2: Industry Distribution]

Let me break down the industry landscape:

[WRITE ON BOARD: Food Service 40% | Retail 25% | Services 20% | Other 15%]

**Food Service (40% of franchising) — The Dominance of QSR**

Food service is the largest franchising sector. Why? Quick-service restaurants are the perfect franchising model.

Think about McDonald's. Every location:
- Fries are cooked at exactly 325 degrees for exactly 3.5 minutes
- Burgers are assembled identically
- Counter layout is standardized
- Training is standardized
- Customer experience is predictable

A franchisee in Seoul, Korea knows exactly what they're buying. They know the system works because it works in 40,000 other locations. They know the product quality. They know the customer expectations. This certainty reduces risk.

Other examples: Subway, Chipotle, Dunkin' Donuts, Starbucks, Nene Chicken, Lotteria. Food franchising thrives because:
1. **The product is standardizable** — You can document a recipe and train someone to make it identically
2. **The brand drives traffic** — Customers choose McDonald's specifically because they know what they'll get
3. **Location matters** — A good location can guarantee traffic
4. **Margin structure works** — Food service has healthy margins that can support franchisee profit AND franchisor royalties

**Retail (25%) — The Convenience & Apparel Model**

7-Eleven is the gold standard. Every 7-Eleven store:
- Looks identical
- Stocks similar products
- Uses identical technology
- Has standardized operations

But retail franchising also includes apparel (The Gap, Zara), fitness (CrossFit, Gold's Gym), salons, and bookstores. These work because inventory and layout can be standardized, and customer experience is predictable.

**Services (20%) — The Less Standardized Model**

Hotels (Marriott, Hilton), car rentals, real estate agencies, hair salons, cleaning services. These work differently from food/retail because the service delivery depends more on people than on products. But franchising still works because:
1. **Brand sets expectations** — A Marriott customer knows what to expect
2. **Standards ensure consistency** — Training and auditing maintain quality
3. **Franchisees operate locally** — While maintaining brand standards

A Marriott in Korea operates differently from a Marriott in the U.S. (different food in breakfast, different décor touches). But both deliver the core Marriott promise: clean rooms, professional service, reliable experience.

**Other (15%) — Emerging Categories**

Education franchises, automotive, home services, consulting. These are growing but smaller currently.

[ASK STUDENTS: "Can anyone think of a franchise you used recently? What industry? Why do you think franchising works for that business?"]

[PAUSE — wait for responses, then continue]

The lesson: **Franchising works best when:**
- The product/service can be standardized
- The brand has value
- Quality matters to customer decisions
- Margins can support both franchisor and franchisee

Franchising struggles in luxury (where customization matters), highly local services (where location reputation matters more than brand), or highly regulated professional services (like law).

#### Segment 3: Real-World Success Stories (10 minutes)

Let me show you examples of franchise success. And more importantly, what makes them successful.

**Case 1: McDonald's — The Global Powerhouse**

[SHOW SLIDE 3: McDonald's Success]

Numbers:
- Founded: 1955 (one restaurant)
- Current: 40,000+ locations
- Countries: 130+
- Annual Revenue: $20+ billion
- Employees: 200,000+

McDonald's is the franchise success story. But here's what's important: **McDonald's didn't own most of those 40,000 locations.** Franchisees do. McDonald's owns the system and the brand.

How McDonald's makes money:
1. **Franchise Fees:** Initial $45,000-$200,000 from new franchisees
2. **Royalties:** 5.5-6% of gross sales from every franchisee
3. **Rent & Service Fees:** McDonald's often owns or controls the real estate, charging franchisees rent
4. **Supplies:** Markup on supplies McDonald's provides

A McDonald's franchisee invests $1-2.5 million to build a location. McDonald's doesn't invest that capital. Franchisees do. But McDonald's gets ongoing income from every location forever.

Why does this work? **McDonald's has absolute standardization:**
- Same menu (mostly)
- Same equipment
- Same training
- Same quality control

A customer in Tokyo expects the same experience as in New York. And they get it. This global consistency is why McDonald's brand is worth $200+ billion.

**Case 2: 7-Eleven — The Convenience Store Model**

[SHOW SLIDE 4: 7-Eleven Mastery]

Numbers:
- Founded: 1946 (as Southland Corporation)
- Current: 16,000+ locations
- Countries: 20+
- Annual Revenue: $35+ billion
- Note: 7-Eleven uses master franchisees in many countries

7-Eleven perfected the convenience retail model. Every store is visually identical. Same logo. Same color scheme. Same shelf layout (mostly). Same product mix (adapted locally).

A 7-Eleven franchisee isn't buying freedom to innovate. They're buying **a proven machine**. They execute it locally.

**Why 7-Eleven succeeds:**
1. **Clarity of expectation** — Franchisee knows exactly what they're buying
2. **Centralized supply chain** — Franchisees benefit from huge economies of scale
3. **Centralized quality control** — Bad stores are fixed or closed
4. **Local adaptation** — Product mix changes by location, but format doesn't
5. **Technology platform** — Consistent POS, inventory, ordering across all stores

A 7-Eleven franchisee in Korea operates differently than in Thailand. Product mix is different. Operating hours might differ. But the core formula is identical.

**Case 3: Starbucks — The Hybrid Approach**

[SHOW SLIDE 5: Starbucks Model]

Starbucks is interesting because it uses a **hybrid model**:
- Corporate-owned stores in major markets (U.S., U.K., etc.)
- Franchised stores in secondary markets and specific channels (grocery stores, airports)
- Licensed stores in partner locations (Target, grocery stores)

Why the hybrid?
- **Control:** Starbucks owns stores in markets where brand perception is most critical
- **Rapid growth:** Franchising in markets where they need speed
- **Capital efficiency:** Franchisees provide capital where appropriate

This shows that franchising isn't all-or-nothing. Companies can use different models in different contexts.

#### Segment 4: What Makes Franchises Succeed? (5 minutes)

Now here's the critical question: What do McDonald's, 7-Eleven, and Starbucks have in common? Why do these franchises succeed while others fail?

[SHOW SLIDE 6: Success Factors]

**Five Critical Success Factors:**

1. **Strong, Recognizable Brand**
   - Customers choose the brand specifically
   - The brand has a clear promise (McDonald's = consistency, 7-Eleven = convenience, Starbucks = quality coffee)
   - The brand is trustworthy

2. **Proven, Teachable System**
   - Operations can be documented
   - Standards can be trained
   - Quality can be measured
   - Success is replicable

3. **Ongoing Franchisor Support**
   - Training (initial and continuous)
   - Marketing and advertising
   - Supply chain management
   - Technology platforms
   - Problem-solving

4. **Aligned Financial Incentives**
   - Franchisees can actually profit
   - Royalty rates are sustainable
   - Startup costs are reasonable relative to profit potential
   - Both franchisor and franchisee win

5. **Centralized Quality Control**
   - Regular audits
   - Clear standards
   - Remediation for failing stores
   - Termination for bad actors
   - Bad stores don't damage the brand

Here's the thing: **If ANY of these five factors is weak, the franchise struggles.**

Imagine McDonald's with poor support. Franchisees fail. Brand deteriorates. Imagine 7-Eleven with no quality control. Some stores become dirty. Brand deteriorates. Imagine a franchise with weak brand (no customer preference). Even with a good system, franchisees struggle.

All five must work together.

#### Segment 5: Failure Case — What Goes Wrong (5 minutes)

Let me contrast success with failure. Why do some franchises fail?

**Real Example: Cafe Bene (Korean Coffee Franchise)**

Cafe Bene expanded rapidly in Korea and internationally (2000s-2010s):
- Peak: 500+ locations
- Growth: Very fast, aggressive international expansion
- What happened: Rapid decline starting ~2012

Why did Cafe Bene fail?

1. **Weak systems** — The coffee quality was good initially, but operational systems were not standardized. Different locations operated very differently.

2. **Poor franchisor support** — Cafe Bene grew too fast. They couldn't support franchisees adequately. Training was inadequate.

3. **Bad unit economics** — The franchise fee and royalties were high. Franchisees struggled to profit. When franchisees don't profit, they cut corners (worse coffee, less service).

4. **Brand dilution** — As franchisees failed and cut corners, the brand deteriorated. Customers stopped choosing Cafe Bene. Without customer choice, franchisees fail.

5. **Market saturation** — Cafe Bene didn't manage location density. Too many Cafe Bene locations cannibalized each other.

The lesson: **Franchising success requires discipline, support, and alignment. Growth for growth's sake leads to failure.**

Compare to Starbucks in Korea: Slow, controlled expansion. Strong brand. Strong support. Franchisees profitable. System works.

### Summary (4 minutes)

Let me recap what we've covered:

1. **Franchising is enormous** — $900B+ in annual sales in the U.S. alone, 750,000+ units globally
2. **Industries differ** — Food service dominates (40%), but retail (25%) and services (20%) are significant
3. **Success requires five factors** — Strong brand, proven system, franchisor support, aligned incentives, quality control
4. **Failure is preventable** — When franchisors neglect these factors, systems collapse

But before we go, any questions? What surprised you today? What franchise would you want to own? What seems problematic about franchising?
