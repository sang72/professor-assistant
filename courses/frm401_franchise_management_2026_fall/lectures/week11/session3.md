## SECTION 2: FULL LECTURE SCRIPT (English)

### Opening (3 minutes)

Welcome to the final session of Week 11. We've studied conflict resolution and relationship building. Now we're asking: **How do franchises stay relevant and profitable for 20, 30, 50+ years as markets change?**

Here's the reality: **Businesses that don't adapt, die.**

Five years ago, no one expected delivery to dominate food franchises. Ten years ago, no one predicted social media would drive brand preference. Franchisors who are rigid, who say "we've done it this way for 20 years so we'll keep doing it," are heading toward bankruptcy.

But change is also dangerous. If franchisors change too much, too fast, franchisees feel destabilized. Investment uncertainty grows. Franchisees exit the system.

The challenge is: **How do you evolve the system while keeping franchisees on board?**

Today we're studying adaptation—how successful franchises manage change while maintaining franchisee relationships.

By the end, you'll understand why some franchises thrive through disruption and others collapse.

### Main Content

#### Segment 1: Change Management Framework & Franchisee Buy-In (12 minutes)

**When a franchise needs to adapt, there's a formula that works:**

**STEP 1: HONEST DIAGNOSIS**

First, franchisor must acknowledge the market reality—to themselves, and to franchisees.

Bad franchisor response: "Business is fine. Let's keep current model."
Result: System slowly dies. Franchisees gradually leave.

Good franchisor response: "We're facing [specific challenge]. Here's how it affects us. We need to adapt. Here's what we're thinking."

**Example: Korean Delivery Platform Challenge**

Problem appeared 2015-2020:
- Delivery platforms (Coupang Eats, Yogiyo) took 15-20% commission
- This destroyed restaurant margins (franchisees going unprofitable)
- Original model (dine-in focused) wasn't working

Bad franchisor response (some Korean brands): "Delivery is just a channel. Dine-in is still core. Keep paying your royalty as if sales are normal."
Result: Franchisees blamed, left, system contracted.

Good franchisor response (smart brands): "Delivery commission is real problem. We need to shift model. Less dine-in capacity, more delivery-optimized. Let's figure this out together."
Result: Franchisees felt heard, cooperated, system adapted.

---

**STEP 2: TRANSPARENCY WITH FRANCHISEES**

Franchisor must share the problem diagnosis with franchisees.

What to share:
- Market data (delivery is up X%, dine-in is down Y%)
- Financial impact (franchisees losing Z won due to commission)
- Franchisor's proposal (here's what we're considering)
- Franchisee input (what do you think? what would help?)

What NOT to share:
- Undecided options (don't propose 5 scenarios, ask franchisees to decide—that's franchisor's job)
- Threats ("If you don't adapt, you'll lose your franchise")
- Blame ("This is your fault. You should have adapted faster.")

**Example: CU vs. GS25 Approach, 2024**

CU (poor transparency):
- Mandated new delivery model
- Implemented POS system without explanation
- Didn't ask franchisees for input
- Result: Franchisee backlash, complaints to regulators

GS25 (good transparency):
- Shared market data showing delivery growth
- Explained why new delivery model benefits franchisees (longer operational hours, different profit profile)
- Provided training and ROI analysis on new POS
- Asked franchisees for feedback and adjusted accordingly
- Result: Smoother adoption, fewer complaints

---

**STEP 3: SHARED RISK IN TRANSITION**

When franchisor asks franchisees to invest in change (new equipment, retraining, operational pivot), franchisor must share the risk.

**Risk-sharing approaches:**

1. **Equipment subsidy:** Franchisor covers part of cost
   - Example: New delivery kitchen requires 50M investment. Franchisor subsidizes 20M. Franchisee invests 30M.
   - Signal: "We believe in this. We're investing too."

2. **Profit guarantee during transition:**
   - Example: "During first 3 months of new delivery model, if profit drops below baseline, we'll reduce royalty by 1 percentage point."
   - Signal: "We're here if transition goes wrong."

3. **Training investment:**
   - Example: Franchisor sends team to each location. 2 weeks of intensive training. Staff development support.
   - Signal: "We want you to succeed. We'll invest in your capability."

4. **Success metrics tied to franchisee outcomes:**
   - Example: "Our success is your profit. When your profit improves, we celebrate. When it drops, we problem-solve."
   - Signal: "We're not extracting value. We're building value together."

**How to know if franchisor is sharing risk:**
- Ask: "Would franchisor do this at corporate-owned location?" If no, then franchisee bears disproportionate risk.
- Better: Pilot at corporate locations first, prove concept works, THEN ask franchisees.

---

**STEP 4: PHASED IMPLEMENTATION**

Don't change everything at once. Phased approach allows learning.

**Good phased approach:**

Phase 1 (Pilot): Willing franchisees test new model. Franchisor provides extra support. Learn what works.

Phase 2 (Refinement): Based on pilot feedback, refine approach. Train second wave.

Phase 3 (Rollout): Most franchisees adopt. By now, best practices are clear.

Phase 4 (Optimization): Fine-tune continuously. System is now "new normal."

**Bad approach:** "Monday, everyone adopts new system. No pilot. No refinement."
Result: Chaos, failures, franchisee anger.

**Example: Starbucks Korea, Mobile Ordering Expansion, 2022-2024**

Phase 1 (2022, Pilot): 20 franchisees tested mobile ordering integration. Franchisor supported heavily. Discovered integration requires POS upgrade.

Phase 2 (2023, Refinement): Based on pilot, franchisor negotiated cheaper POS with vendor, subsidized 40% of cost for franchisees, trained staff.

Phase 3 (2023-2024, Rollout): 400+ franchisees adopted. By now, system was proven, staff training was smooth.

Phase 4 (2024+, Optimization): Continuous improvement. Mobile orders now 30% of sales. Franchisees see profit improvement.

Result: Successful technology transformation because of phased, transparent approach.

---

**STEP 5: ACKNOWLEDGE SUCCESS & SHARE BENEFITS**

When change is successful, franchisor must publicly acknowledge it and share the benefits.

What to do:
- Celebrate: "Our franchisees adapted. This generation is thriving."
- Share metrics: "Mobile orders increased by X%. Franchisee profit is up Y%."
- Reward: Consider profit-sharing increase, bonus, recognition.
- Learn: "What did we learn that applies to next challenge?"

What NOT to do:
- Keep all gain to franchisor (increase royalty because model now works)
- Ignore franchisees' contribution ("We made brilliant decision")
- Rush to next change without consolidation
- Blame franchisees if transition was hard ("You were too slow adapting")

#### Segment 2: Adaptation Case Studies (15 minutes)

**Case 1: COVID-19—Korean Franchises That Adapted vs. Those That Didn't (2020-2022)**

**Background:**

COVID-19 forced shift from dine-in to delivery overnight. Some franchises adapted. Some didn't.

**Failure Case: Traditional Korean Restaurant Franchise**

System was built entirely on dine-in:
- Seating capacity was 40 people
- Menu was for dine-in (full experience, presentation)
- Staff was trained for table service, not delivery
- Real estate was premium locations (high rent, built for foot traffic)

Franchisor response: "This is temporary. Wait it out. Keep everything same."

What franchisees needed: "Help us pivot to delivery. Menu packaging? Staff retraining? Delivery logistics?"

Result:
- Dine-in customers disappeared
- Franchisees couldn't quickly shift to delivery
- Sales dropped 70%
- Many franchisees closed
- System contracted from 150 to 40 locations by 2022

Lesson: Hoping problems disappear doesn't work. Adaptation is essential.

---

**Success Case: Korean Chicken Franchise (2020-2022)**

System had dine-in element, but was already moving toward delivery.

Franchisor response:
1. **Immediate pivot:** "Dine-in is gone for months. Full pivot to delivery model."
2. **Menu redesign:** "What travels well? What stays quality during delivery?" Redesigned packaging, menu items.
3. **Operations:** "Streamline kitchen. Remove dine-in constraints." Cut open kitchen time, focused on speed.
4. **Staff retraining:** Sent trainers to every location. 1-week intensive training on delivery operations.
5. **Technology:** Integrated with delivery platforms. Invested in POS integration.
6. **Financial support:** Royalty temporary reduced from 6% to 4% during 2020-2021 to help franchisees survive transition.

Result:
- Franchisees saw revenue dip but adapted quickly
- By 2021, delivery revenue exceeded pre-COVID dine-in revenue
- Franchisees thrived in "new normal"
- System maintained 400+ locations, in fact expanded
- When dine-in reopened 2021-2022, franchisees had diversified revenue (dine-in + delivery)

Lesson: Franchisor adaptation + franchisee support = system resilience.

---

**Case 2: Technology Disruption—Convenience Store Adaptation (2018-2025)**

**Background:**

Korean convenience stores faced multiple technology disruptions:
1. Payment technology (mobile payment replaced cash)
2. Inventory management (automation)
3. Customer service (self-checkout)
4. Logistics (delivery, unmanned shops)

**CU's Approach (Mixed Results):**

Good elements:
- Invested heavily in POS systems
- Integrated delivery platforms
- Trained franchisees on new technology
- Provided equipment upgrades

Bad elements:
- Mandated expensive POS without clear ROI
- Rapid changes without phased implementation
- Technology costs increased franchisee burden
- Didn't reduce other costs to offset technology spend

Result: Franchisees felt technology was being forced on them to make franchisor's job easier, not franchisee's job better.

**GS25's Approach (Better Results):**

- Pilot first: Tested new technology in select locations (franchisees volunteered)
- Transparency: Showed franchisees exact ROI ("This will reduce labor cost by 2 points in 6 months")
- Subsidy: Franchisor covered 50% of POS cost
- Training: Intensive on-site training, not just videos
- Support: Dedicated tech support team for franchisees

Result: Smoother adoption, higher franchisee satisfaction, franchisees saw value.

Lesson: Franchisor's technology agenda must align with franchisee's profitability agenda.

---

**Case 3: Regulatory Change—Adaptation to New Franchise Laws (2023-2026)**

**Background:**

Korean government tightened franchise regulations starting 2023:
- Mandated transparency (franchisors must disclose AUV, cost structures honestly)
- Restricted unfair practices (royalty changes, supply mandates)
- Required dispute resolution processes
- Allowed franchisee associations formal representation

**Impact on Franchisors:**

Old approach: "We operate as we choose. Franchisees have no power."
New reality: "Franchisees have legal rights. System must be transparent and fair."

**Adaptation by Smart Franchisors:**

1. **Pre-emptive transparency:** Many franchisors voluntarily disclosed more before government mandates
2. **Dispute processes:** Established mediation, arbitration processes before required
3. **Franchisee councils:** Created representation structures proactively
4. **Contract fairness:** Updated contracts to remove potentially unfair clauses

**Result:** Franchisors that adapted early faced fewer regulatory investigations. Brand reputation improved. Franchisees felt respected.

**Non-adaptation by Slow Franchisors:**

1. **Resistance:** "We shouldn't have to disclose. Franchisees should just trust us."
2. **Minimum compliance:** "We'll do absolute minimum required by law."
3. **Litigation:** When franchisees filed complaints, franchisor fought rather than settled

**Result:** Investigations, negative media, brand damage, franchisee departures.

Lesson: Regulatory change is signal to improve system, not threat to resist.

#### Segment 3: Korean Franchise Sustainability Analysis (15 minutes)

**Current State (2025-2026): Korean Franchises at Inflection Point**

Korean franchising in 2025-2026 is at a critical moment:

1. **Macro economic pressures:** Rent inflation, wage increases, delivery platform dominance
2. **Regulatory tightening:** Government protection of franchisees strengthening
3. **Generational shift:** Younger franchisees demand transparency and flexibility
4. **Technology disruption:** AI, unmanned operations, new delivery models emerging
5. **Competitive intensity:** More franchise brands, more oversaturation, margin compression

**Franchises that will THRIVE (2026-2030):**

1. **Adaptable systems:**
   - Allow local menu variation
   - Embrace new technology quickly
   - Invest in franchisee capability
   - Share pain during downturns

2. **Transparent organizations:**
   - Disclose financial data openly
   - Honest profit projections
   - Clear cost structures
   - Regular communication

3. **Franchisee-first mindset:**
   - Franchisor profit comes from franchisee profit
   - Investment in franchisee training and support
   - Formal governance structures
   - Shared risk in change initiatives

Examples: Some international brands (Starbucks Korea, Dunkin' Korea, McDonald's Korea) + enlightened Korean brands (GS25, some newer food concepts)

---

**Franchises that will STRUGGLE (2026-2030):**

1. **Rigid systems:**
   - "We've done it this way forever"
   - No system adaptation to market change
   - Franchisees must adapt alone
   - Franchisor prioritizes efficiency over franchisee profit

2. **Opaque organizations:**
   - Hidden fee structures
   - Misleading profit projections
   - Selective disclosure to franchisees
   - Communication only during crises

3. **Franchisor-first mindset:**
   - Franchisor maximizes own profit regardless of franchisee welfare
   - Minimal investment in support
   - Individual franchisee disputes, not system solutions
   - Franchisor passes all risk to franchisees

Examples: Many mid-size Korean food concepts built 2010-2015. Good in stable market. Struggling now in disrupted market.

---

**The Korean Convenience Store Example—Microcosm of Larger Trend**

Korean convenience stores (CU, GS25, Lotte) faced same macro pressures:
- Rent inflation (unavoidable)
- COGS inflation (unavoidable)
- Wage increases (unavoidable)
- Delivery platform dominance (unavoidable)

But responses differed:

**CU (Slow adaptation):**
- 2024-2025: Royalty reduced slightly (3% → 2.8%)
- Invested in technology but didn't explain ROI
- Media portrayed CU as ignoring franchisee suffering
- Result: Hundreds of franchisees unhappy, government investigation, brand damage

**GS25 (Faster adaptation):**
- 2023-2024: Proactively reduced royalty (5% → 4.5%)
- Renegotiated supplier contracts (reduced COGS)
- Technology investments with clear ROI
- Transparent communication with franchisees
- Result: Better franchisee satisfaction, fewer departures, slower but healthier decline

Both systems will need further adaptation. But GS25's approach preserves more franchisees and allows better long-term positioning.

---

**Long-Term Sustainability Formula:**

For franchises to survive 20+ years in disrupted market:

1. **Financial transparency:** Franchisees understand economics, can make informed decisions
2. **System flexibility:** Franchisor allows adaptation to local market, technology changes
3. **Shared investment:** When change is needed, franchisor invests in franchisee capability and success
4. **Governance:** Franchisees have formal voice in system direction
5. **Continuous improvement:** System evolves based on market, not inertia
6. **Crisis management:** When macro shocks hit, franchisor shares burden, not just mandates

Franchises that have this formula survive. Those that don't, decline.

### Summary (5 minutes)

**Key Learnings:**

1. **Stagnation is death:** Franchises that don't adapt to market change eventually fail

2. **Adaptation requires buy-in:** Franchisees must understand and support system changes

3. **Change management formula:**
   - Honest diagnosis
   - Transparent communication
   - Shared risk in transition
   - Phased implementation
   - Celebration of success

4. **COVID-19 case showed:** Franchises that pivoted quickly (with franchisor support) thrived. Those that waited, died.

5. **Technology disruption requires:** Alignment of franchisor's tech agenda with franchisee's profit agenda

6. **Regulatory change is opportunity:** Transparent, fair franchisors adapted easily. Resistant ones faced investigation and damage.

7. **Korean franchise market 2025-2026:** Inflection point. Adaptable, transparent franchisors will thrive. Rigid, opaque ones will decline.

8. **Long-term sustainability:** Requires financial transparency, system flexibility, shared investment, governance, and continuous improvement.

### Vocabulary Emphasis

- **System adaptation:** Strategic change in how franchise operates (technology, business model, menu)
- **Change management:** Process of guiding stakeholders through transformation
- **Franchisee buy-in:** Franchise accepting and supporting system change
- **Phased implementation:** Rolling out change in stages (pilot → refine → rollout → optimize)
- **Risk-sharing:** Franchisor and franchisee both invest in change, both benefit if successful
- **Macro shock:** Large external change (COVID, regulation, technology) affecting all franchisees
- **System resilience:** Franchise's ability to adapt and survive external disruptions
- **Sustainability:** Long-term viability of franchise system through market changes

### Common Mistakes to Watch For

1. **Underestimating franchisee resistance to change**
   - Reality: Change threatens franchisee's business, capital, livelihood
   - They need to understand "why" and see "how this helps me"
   - Mandates without explanation = guaranteed backlash

2. **Thinking adaptation is only operational**
   - Reality: Fundamental shifts (delivery dominance, technology) require financial changes
   - If you add technology costs but don't adjust royalty or reduce other costs, franchisee math doesn't work
   - Adaptation often requires franchisor sacrifice (lower royalty, equipment subsidy)

3. **Rushing implementation**
   - Reality: Franchisees need time to adapt, learn, build confidence
   - Phased approach takes longer but succeeds. All-at-once fails.
   - Franchisees often become advocates after successful phased implementation

4. **Ignoring early warning signs**
   - Reality: Franchisee suffering appears months before system crisis
   - If franchisor sees 30% of franchisees declining and does nothing, system contracts
   - Proactive diagnosis in Year 1 prevents Year 3 collapse

5. **Confusing "adaptation" with "blame franchisee"**
   - Bad: "Market changed. Franchisees need to be better operators."
   - Good: "Market changed. System needs to evolve. We'll evolve together."
   - Difference: Shared responsibility vs. individual blame

### Extension Questions

**If student asks:** "How do franchisors balance system consistency with local adaptation?"
**Answer:** Guardrails approach. Franchisor says: "These things are non-negotiable for brand (core menu, quality standards, pricing, brand image). Within these guardrails, adapt to your market (menu additions, local marketing, operations timing)." Starbucks does this well.

**If student asks:** "Why don't franchisees just adapt on their own if market changes?"
**Answer:** Two reasons:
1. Franchisor brand is what they bought. They can't just change brand.
2. Often adaptation requires investment (technology, retraining, equipment) that's risky for individual franchisee. Franchisor can support and risk-share, making it viable.

**If student asks:** "What if franchisee refuses to adapt?"
**Answer:** Franchisor has options:
1. Provide more support/training (many just need help)
2. Share more risk (lower royalty during transition)
3. If truly stubborn, franchisee-specific plan (maybe lower-cost option)
4. As last resort, non-renewal (but do this only after good-faith attempts)
