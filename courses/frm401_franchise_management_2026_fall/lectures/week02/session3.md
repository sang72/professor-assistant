## SECTION 2: FULL LECTURE SCRIPT (English)

### Opening (3 minutes)

Good morning, everyone. Welcome to the final session of Week 2. Over the past two weeks, we've answered the fundamental question: **What IS franchising?** We've defined it precisely, explored the ecosystem, and compared it to other business models.

Today, we ask a more personal question: **What does it mean to be an entrepreneur in a franchise system?** This is perhaps the most important question for anyone considering buying a franchise.

The American dream is entrepreneurship. Start a business. Build something from nothing. Control your destiny. Take all the risk, reap all the reward.

But there's a catch: most new businesses fail. Within five years, roughly 50% of small businesses don't survive. The risk is enormous. The capital requirement is substantial. The hours are brutal. The failure rate is high.

Then along comes franchising, which asks: *What if you could have entrepreneurship with less risk? What if you could own and operate a business using a proven system, with support, with brand recognition?*

That's the promise of franchisee entrepreneurship. But is it reality? And what are the real risks? That's what we explore today.

### Segment 1: Traditional Entrepreneurship vs. Franchisee Entrepreneurship (9 minutes)

[SHOW SLIDE: "Two Paths to Ownership"]

Let me contrast two paths. 

**Path 1: Traditional Entrepreneurship**

You identify a market opportunity. You develop a product. You raise capital (maybe from your own savings, maybe from investors). You launch your business. You hire staff. You build systems. You market your product. You compete against existing players.

The advantages are clear: complete freedom. You build something entirely yours. You keep all the profits. You control every decision. If you succeed, you might build a billion-dollar company.

The disadvantages are equally clear: enormous risk. You have no proven system. You're competing against established players. Your failure rate is 50% within five years. You will likely work 60-80 hours per week. You're completely personally liable.

**Real example:** Someone in Korea decides to open an independent coffee shop in 2025. They invest 100 million won (roughly $75,000 USD). They find a location. They design the menu. They hire baristas. They market locally. They compete against Starbucks, CU convenience stores, and 20 other coffee shops on the same block.

The probability they're still open and profitable in five years? Studies suggest about 50-50. It's entrepreneurship, but it's high-risk.

[ASK STUDENTS: Why do you think independent businesses have such a high failure rate? What are the main challenges?]

[PAUSE for student responses]

Exactly. Lack of proven systems. Competition from established brands. Capital constraints. Difficulty hiring and retaining quality staff. Inability to purchase supplies at bulk prices.

**Path 2: Franchisee Entrepreneurship**

Now consider an alternative. You identify a franchise opportunity. You evaluate several franchises. You choose one that you believe in. You pay the franchise fee. You receive the entire system: operations manual, training, site selection help, continuing support, brand recognition.

You still invest capital. You still operate the location. You still hire and manage staff. You still assume significant risk.

But you're not starting from zero. The system exists. The brand is established. The supply chain is developed. The customer expectations are clear. The failures points (what goes wrong) are known, and the franchisor has solutions.

**Real example:** Someone in Korea decides to buy a Nene Chicken franchise in 2025. They invest 80 million won (initial franchise fee, equipment, working capital). They attend training. They receive the operations manual. They understand the chicken recipe (which is proprietary). They understand the pricing, marketing approach, and customer service standards.

Nene Chicken's data shows that franchisees have a higher success rate than independent restaurants. Why? Because the system is proven.

[WRITE ON BOARD: Traditional Entrepreneurship = Freedom + Control + High Risk | Franchisee Entrepreneurship = System + Brand + Moderate Risk]

The key insight here is that franchisee entrepreneurship is a *different kind* of entrepreneurship. You're not inventing the business. You're executing an established system, with local adaptation. This trades some autonomy for lower risk.

### Segment 2: Risk-Return Analysis (13 minutes)

[SHOW SLIDE: "Risk-Return Comparison"]

Let me make this concrete by analyzing the financial risk-return profile.

**Traditional Entrepreneurship Financial Profile:**

Imagine you open an independent coffee shop with 100 million won investment.

- **Best case scenario (20% probability):** The shop succeeds brilliantly. Annual revenue is 500 million won. After all expenses and staff costs, your profit is 100 million won per year. Over five years, you profit 500 million won. Return on investment: 500%. Success!

- **Middle case scenario (30% probability):** The shop survives but struggles. Annual revenue is 300 million won. Profit is 30 million won per year. Over five years, profit is 150 million won. Return on investment: 150%. You're breaking even and building some wealth, but it's slow.

- **Worst case scenario (50% probability):** The shop fails within five years. You lose your 100 million won investment. Return: -100%. You're back to zero.

Expected return = (500 × 0.20) + (150 × 0.30) + (-100 × 0.50) = 100 + 45 - 50 = **95 million won over 5 years, or about 19 million won per year.**

But this assumes you're working 60-70 hours per week. So your hourly return might be $15-20 per hour. (Less than you could earn as a salaried manager!)

[WRITE ON BOARD: Traditional Entrepreneurship = High Upside + High Downside + Uncertain Middle]

**Franchisee Entrepreneurship Financial Profile:**

Now imagine you buy a Nene Chicken franchise with 80 million won investment.

- **Best case scenario (30% probability):** The franchise succeeds. Annual revenue is 400 million won. After royalties (8%), marketing fees, supplies, and staff costs, your profit is 50 million won per year. Over five years, profit is 250 million won. Return on investment: 312%.

- **Middle case scenario (50% probability):** The franchise succeeds moderately. Annual revenue is 300 million won. Profit is 25 million won per year. Over five years, profit is 125 million won. Return on investment: 156%.

- **Worst case scenario (20% probability):** The franchise fails or you exit. You lose your 80 million won investment. Return: -100%.

Expected return = (250 × 0.30) + (125 × 0.50) + (-80 × 0.20) = 75 + 62.5 - 16 = **121.5 million won over 5 years, or about 24 million won per year.**

At 60 hours per week, that's about $20-25 per hour.

[WRITE ON BOARD: Franchisee Entrepreneurship = Moderate Upside + Lower Downside + Higher Certainty]

**The Critical Difference:**

Franchisee entrepreneurship has:
1. **Lower downside risk** — The system is proven, so the worst-case scenario is less likely
2. **More predictable middle case** — Many franchisees succeed; it's not 50-50
3. **Slightly lower upside** — You share profits with the franchisor (royalties), so your absolute maximum is lower
4. **Lower personal workload** — The franchisor provides systems, so you're not inventing everything
5. **More passive income potential** — As your business runs smoothly, you can reduce hours

[ASK STUDENTS: If you had to choose between these two paths, which would you choose? Why? What factors matter most to you: maximum upside, security, or something else?]

[PAUSE for student responses]

This is why franchising appeals to many people. Not everyone wants to take the maximum risk for maximum reward. Some people want a reasonable return with lower risk. That's franchisee entrepreneurship.

### Segment 3: Case Studies — Success & Failure (15 minutes)

[SHOW SLIDE: "Franchise Success & Failure Stories"]

Let me illustrate with real cases.

**Case 1: Krispy Kreme Franchisee Success (United States)**

Krispy Kreme is a donut franchise that started in North Carolina and expanded nationally.

A franchisee in Texas opened a Krispy Kreme location in 2010. Initial investment: $450,000. The first year was slow — people didn't know the brand. But Krispy Kreme's marketing support helped. The company provided regional marketing. The brand reputation grew.

By year three, the location was generating $3 million in annual revenue. The franchisee's profit was about $400,000 per year (after all costs and royalties). By year five, the franchisee had earned back the initial investment and was building substantial wealth.

**Why did this work?**
- Krispy Kreme had a proven system
- The brand was strong and differentiated
- The product was addictive (literally — donuts have high sugar!)
- The location was chosen carefully by Krispy Kreme's real estate team
- The franchisee executed the system well

**Case 2: Subway Franchisee Struggle (United States)**

Subway is a sandwich franchise that exploded in the 1990s-2000s. The company promised low startup costs and high returns. Millions of people bought Subway franchises.

A franchisee in Ohio opened a Subway location in 2005 with a $150,000 investment. Subway promised that the location would generate $2 million in revenue quickly. But reality was different.

The location was in a mall that was declining. Foot traffic was lower than expected. Subway's corporate support was minimal — mostly just royalty collection. The franchisee struggled to compete against local sandwich shops and Quiznos.

By 2012, the franchisee closed the location. Total loss: $150,000. The franchisee had worked 50+ hours per week for seven years and had nothing to show for it.

**Why did this fail?**
- Poor location selection (Subway left this to franchisees)
- Low differentiation from competitors
- Franchisee was inadequately trained
- Subway didn't provide adequate support
- The company oversold the opportunity

[PAUSE — let this sink in]

These two cases illustrate a critical point: **Franchisee success depends on THREE factors: the franchisor's system quality, the location, and the franchisee's execution.** All three must be strong.

**Case 3: CU Convenience Store Success (Korea, 2010-2020)**

CU is a convenience store franchise in Korea. It grew from 3,000 locations in 2010 to over 13,000 by 2020. Many franchisees prospered.

A franchisee opened a CU location in Seoul in 2012 with an 60 million won investment. The location was in a busy residential area. The franchisee hired and trained staff well. The system was straightforward: stock products, manage registers, maintain cleanliness.

By 2015, the location was highly profitable, generating about 12 million won in annual profit. The franchisee opened a second location. Then a third. By 2025, the franchisee operated five CU locations and was a successful multi-unit operator.

**Why did this work?**
- CU's system was proven and scalable
- The business model was simple and teachable
- The Korean market's convenience store culture was strong
- The franchisee was capable and motivated

**Case 4: Korean Coffee Franchise Saturation (Korea, 2020-2026)**

But the Korean market has also seen franchisee failure. In the coffee franchise boom of 2015-2020, hundreds of people opened coffee franchises (Hollys, Cafe Coffee Bene, etc.). Franchisees invested 80-100 million won each.

By 2024-2026, many of these franchises struggled. Why? Market saturation. Too many coffee franchises. Too little differentiation. Oversupply of franchisees. Many franchisees closed their locations and lost their investment.

A franchisee opened a Hollys Coffee franchise in 2016 with a 90 million won investment. The location was decent. The franchisee executed well. But by 2023, the neighborhood had 12 other coffee shops (including Starbucks, CU, local cafes). Competition was intense. Revenue stagnated. The franchisee closed the location in 2024 with a loss of about 40 million won.

**Why did this fail?**
- Market saturation (too many franchisees in too small an area)
- Overselling by the franchisor
- Insufficient differentiation
- Lack of unique brand loyalty

[ASK STUDENTS: In the Korean coffee case, who bears more responsibility for the failure — the franchisor (for overselling) or the franchisee (for choosing a saturated market)?]

[PAUSE for discussion]

This is a real tension in franchising. The franchisor wants to recruit franchisees to grow. The franchisee wants to believe the franchisor's projections. But when markets become saturated, both parties suffer.

**Key Lesson:** Franchisee entrepreneurship success requires:
1. **System quality** — Is the franchisor's system truly proven?
2. **Location selection** — Is the market demand real?
3. **Franchisee selection** — Does the franchisee have capital, skill, and commitment?
4. **Market conditions** — Is the overall market growing or saturated?
5. **Honest representation** — Does the franchisor give realistic projections?

### Segment 4: Korean Franchise Challenges (2024-2026) (8 minutes)

[SHOW SLIDE: "Korean Franchising in 2024-2026"]

Let me address the specific challenges facing Korean franchisees in 2024-2026. The Korean franchise market is mature and has unique challenges.

**Challenge 1: Market Saturation**

Korea's convenience store market has reached saturation. There are over 40,000 convenience stores in Korea for a population of 50 million. That's 1 convenience store per 1,250 people. Many franchisees report declining profitability.

Similarly, the coffee franchise market exploded from 2010-2020, but by 2024, the market is oversaturated. Many franchisees are exiting.

**Challenge 2: Labor Costs and Wage Increases**

South Korea has aggressive minimum wage increases. The minimum wage has risen from 6,300 won/hour in 2015 to 11,100 won/hour in 2024 — a 76% increase in nine years.

For franchisees, this eats into profit margins. A convenience store franchisee needs to staff 24 hours per day. Labor costs have become the biggest expense, often exceeding 40-50% of revenue.

[WRITE ON BOARD: Rising Labor Costs = Lower Franchisee Profitability]

**Challenge 3: Rising Rents**

Real estate prices in Korea have soared, especially in Seoul and major cities. Franchisees are locked into long-term leases. Some franchisees report that their rent increased 30-50% when leases were renewed.

Combined with labor costs, many Korean franchisees are squeezed.

**Challenge 4: Franchisor Support & Fairness Issues**

Some Korean franchisees report that franchisors provide inadequate support. The franchisor collects royalties but invests little in local marketing, training, or problem-solving.

Also, some franchisors are accused of opening competing franchises in the same area, cannibalizing franchisee revenue. This has been a contentious issue in Korea.

**Example:** In 2023, CU and GS25 (the two largest convenience store chains) faced criticism from franchisees for allegedly opening locations too close together, stealing from existing franchisees' revenue.

**Challenge 5: Social and Political Pressure**

Korean franchisees have become politically vocal. They've organized associations to demand more support and better terms.

In 2024, the Korean government passed new franchise protection laws to require franchisors to:
- Provide more transparent financial disclosures
- Give franchisees territorial protection (no nearby competing locations)
- Provide adequate support
- Give franchisees recourse for grievances

These regulations are designed to protect franchisees from exploitation.

[ASK STUDENTS: Do you think these regulations help or hurt franchising in Korea? Will they make franchising more attractive or less attractive as a career path?]

[PAUSE for discussion]

**The Bottom Line on Korean Franchising:**

Being a franchisee in Korea is increasingly challenging. The easy money has been made. The low-hanging fruit has been picked. Today's franchisees need to be much more careful:
- Evaluate location demand (not just franchisor promises)
- Understand true labor and rent costs
- Check franchisee satisfaction with the franchisor
- Have adequate capital for 2-3 years of operations
- Be prepared to work long hours

For ambitious entrepreneurs, franchising in Korea is still viable. But it requires careful analysis, not just hope.

Let me summarize Week 2.

We've defined franchising precisely. We've explored the ecosystem. We've compared it to other business models. And today, we've analyzed what franchisee entrepreneurship really means.

**Key takeaways:**

1. **Franchisee entrepreneurship is real entrepreneurship**, but constrained. You own and operate a business. You invest capital. You assume risk. You build wealth if you succeed.

2. **But it's different from traditional entrepreneurship.** You're executing a proven system, not inventing from scratch. This lowers your downside risk but also limits your upside.

3. **Success requires three things:** a strong franchisor system, good location selection, and capable franchisee execution. All three matter equally.

4. **Korean franchising has unique challenges** in 2024-2026: saturation, rising labor costs, competitive real estate, and demand for franchisor fairness.

## SECTION 4: KEY NOTES & IMPROVISATION TIPS

### Core Concepts to Emphasize

1. **Franchisee Entrepreneurship is Different, Not Lesser**
   - Franchisees own and operate businesses, invest capital, assume real risk
   - But they execute proven systems vs. inventing from scratch
   - This is attractive to many people who want ownership with lower risk
   - Note: Some students will see this as "not real entrepreneurship" — correct this misconception

2. **The Three-Factor Success Model**
   - System quality (franchisor's responsibility)
   - Location/market (shared responsibility)
   - Franchisee execution (franchisee's responsibility)
   - All three must be present; one failure point = failure

3. **Risk-Return Trade-offs**
   - Traditional entrepreneurship: Lower baseline success, higher upside, personal wealth building
   - Franchisee entrepreneurship: Higher baseline success, shared upside, more predictable
   - Neither is "better" — depends on person's risk tolerance

4. **Korean Market Maturation**
   - The 2010-2020 golden era of Korean franchising is ending
   - Market saturation is real in convenience stores and coffee
   - New franchisees must be more analytical, less hopeful
   - Government regulation is increasing (better for franchisees long-term)

### Common Student Misconceptions to Correct

1. **"Franchisees are just employees."**
   - Correct: Franchisees own businesses, assume risk, keep profits. Very different from employees.

2. **"Franchisors always support franchisees."**
   - Correct: Some do excellently. Others collect royalties and provide minimal support. Check before buying.

3. **"Franchising is a guaranteed path to wealth."**
   - Correct: It reduces risk, but failure is still possible. Location, execution, and market matter.

4. **"The franchisor's revenue projections are realistic."**
   - Correct: Many franchisors oversell. Always talk to existing franchisees about actual revenue.

### Classroom Discussion Prompts

1. "If you had 100 million won to invest, would you start your own business or buy a franchise? Why?"
2. "What would you need to see from a franchisor before investing? What information?"
3. "In the Korean coffee case, who should bear more responsibility for franchisee losses — franchisor or franchisee?"
4. "How would you evaluate a franchising opportunity? What due diligence would you do?"
5. "Do you think the new Korean franchisee protection laws are good or bad?"

### Real-World Examples to Emphasize

- **Krispy Kreme success:** Proves that when system + location + execution align, franchisees prosper
- **Subway struggle:** Proves that low brand differentiation + poor location = failure, regardless of franchisor reputation
- **CU multi-unit operator:** Shows the path from single franchisee to small franchisee-entrepreneur empire
- **Korean coffee saturation:** Illustrates the danger of oversaturation and franchisor overselling

### Improvisation Tips for Live Teaching

1. **If students seem skeptical about franchisee entrepreneurship:** Show the Krispy Kreme example. Ask them to calculate the return on investment. Show that it's real wealth building.

2. **If students are interested in Korean franchising:** Discuss the 2024 regulatory changes. Ask them to research CU and GS25 franchisee associations. Show them YouTube videos of Korean franchisee protests (real and recent).

3. **If students want to evaluate specific franchises:** Create a framework together in class:
   - What's the franchisor's financial disclosure? (FDD in US, etc.)
   - What do CURRENT franchisees say about it? (Talk to them!)
   - What's the location strategy?
   - What support does the franchisor really provide?
   - What are the true costs (including labor, rent, etc.)?

4. **If time is running short:** Focus on the three success factors and the Korean challenges. These are the most important takeaways.

### Pre-Class Preparation

- Research one current Korean franchisee satisfaction issue (2024-2026). This makes it real.
- Prepare 2-3 minutes of video of Korean franchisees discussing challenges (YouTube has many).
- Have current financial data on Korean minimum wage, convenience store revenue, coffee franchise trends.

### Post-Class Follow-up

- Assign: "Find a franchise you're interested in. Research: (1) Franchisor's FDD or Korean equivalent, (2) Talk to 3 current franchisees, (3) Evaluate: System quality, location market, franchisee support. Report back next week."
- This gives them real due diligence experience.
