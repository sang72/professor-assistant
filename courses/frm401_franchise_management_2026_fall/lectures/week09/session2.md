### Main Content

#### Segment 1: Building Responsibility Matrices (12 minutes)

**Review: What is a Responsibility Matrix?**

Yesterday we introduced responsibility matrices as a 2x2 framework showing who controls what. Let's formalize this and build some actual matrices for real franchise systems.

A complete responsibility matrix includes three dimensions:
1. **Task**: What needs to be done?
2. **Control**: Who decides how it's done?
3. **Financial responsibility**: Who pays for it?

Let me show you a detailed matrix for a Korean Fried Chicken franchise:

**MATRIX 1: Korean Fried Chicken Franchise Responsibility Analysis**

| Task | Franchisor Control | Franchisor Pays | Franchisee Control | Franchisee Pays | Outcome |
|------|------------------|-----------------|------------------|-----------------|---------|
| **Recipe & QA** | Yes (defines) | Partial | No (executes) | Partial (labor) | Well-aligned. Franchisee receives detailed manual; must follow exactly |
| **Site Selection** | Partial (approval) | No | Yes (chooses) | Yes (lease cost) | Risk: Franchisee might choose bad location; franchisor has veto |
| **Equipment Setup** | Partial (specifies) | No | Yes (purchases) | Yes (150-200M) | Franchisee bears capital risk; franchisor reduces risk through standard specs |
| **Supply Chain** | Yes (centralized) | Yes (system cost) | No (orders) | Yes (per unit cost) | Strong alignment. Franchisor invests in efficiency; franchisee gets competitive pricing |
| **Staff Training** | Yes (curriculum) | Yes (materials) | Yes (executes) | Yes (labor cost) | Shared responsibility. Franchisor designs; franchisee implements and pays for staff time |
| **Daily Operations** | Partial (standards) | No | Yes (executes) | Yes (labor) | Franchisee-controlled. Franchisor sets standards; franchisee manages staff and execution |
| **Marketing** | Yes (national brand) | Yes (major) | Yes (local) | Yes (local budget) | Shared. Franchisor builds national brand; franchisee amplifies locally |
| **Customer Service** | Partial (standards) | No | Yes (delivers) | Yes (staff) | Franchisee-controlled. Quality depends on franchisee's staff hiring and management |

**How to Read This Matrix:**

- **Well-aligned tasks** (both or one party): System works well. Clear accountability
- **Misaligned tasks** (split responsibility): Risk of failure. Neither party fully owns outcome
- **Franchisor-heavy** (franchisor controls and pays): Franchisor invests heavily; franchisee gets support
- **Franchisee-heavy** (franchisee controls and pays): Franchisee bears risk; franchisor expects compliance

For the chicken franchise above: Mostly well-aligned. Franchisor invested heavily in supply chain. Franchisee bears real risk but has clear manual to follow. This is why Korean chicken franchises generally succeed compared to cafes.

**Contrast: A Poorly Designed Matrix (Generic Cafe Franchise)**

| Task | Franchisor Control | Franchisor Pays | Franchisee Control | Franchisee Pays | Outcome |
|------|------------------|-----------------|------------------|-----------------|---------|
| **Recipe & QA** | No (generic) | No | Yes (invents own) | Yes (trial/error) | Misaligned. Each cafe is different. No consistency |
| **Site Selection** | No | No | Yes | Yes (full risk) | Franchisee fully at risk. No franchisor guidance |
| **Equipment** | No (no standard) | No | Yes | Yes (200M+) | Misaligned. Franchisee doesn't know optimal setup |
| **Supply Chain** | No | No | Yes (sources independently) | Yes (full cost) | Misaligned. Franchisee might pay 2x what optimized chain would cost |
| **Training** | Minimal | Minimal | Yes (self-taught) | Yes (no structure) | Dangerous. Franchisee learns by failing |
| **Operations** | No standards | No | Yes (total) | Yes (total) | Franchisee-only. This is basically starting a cafe from scratch |
| **Marketing** | Generic materials | Minimal | Yes (all local) | Yes (all cost) | Franchisee bears full marketing burden |
| **Customer Service** | No standards | No | Yes | Yes | Franchisee-only. Quality varies wildly |

**What's Wrong with This Matrix?**

Everything. The franchisor provided a brand name and that's it. The franchisee has to figure out everything else. This is why generic cafe franchises fail. Franchisees expect support; they get a logo.

**Key Insight:**

The best franchises (Dunkin', CU, Chicken) have invested heavily in:
1. Clear supply chains (franchisor responsibility)
2. Detailed operations manuals (franchisor responsibility)
3. Training programs (shared responsibility)
4. Site selection support (franchisor approval, franchisee execution)

The failing franchises (Cafe Bene, generic cafes) left franchisees to figure out everything.

Franchise: **Korean Skin Care Salon**

| Pricing strategy | Sets range | Operates within | Franchise fee needs minimum price to be viable |
| Staff certification | Monitors | Hires & trains | Franchisor sets standard; franchisee responsible for compliance |
| Local advertising | Provides materials | Executes locally | Franchisor builds brand; franchisee amplifies locally |
| Equipment quality | Specifies | Purchases | Franchisor prevents cheap shortcuts; franchisee bears cost |

This creates shared responsibility and accountability. Good system design.

#### Segment 2: Location Comparison & Site Selection Analysis (15 minutes)

**Why This Matters:**

Location is the #1 profit driver, as we learned yesterday. Today we're going to **compare two locations using real P&L data** and **make a recommendation** on which one a franchisee should choose.

This is exactly what franchisees face when considering a franchise investment. Real scenario.

**Problem 1: Coffee Franchise Location Decision**

A prospective franchisee is choosing between two locations for a Korean specialty coffee franchise. Upfront investment is the same for both (200M won). She needs to choose one location.

**Location A: Premium (Gangnam, GBC Building)**

Geography: High-traffic business district
- Estimated monthly sales: 55M won (lots of office workers)
- Rent: 13M won/month (expensive but high traffic)
- COGS (coffee beans, milk, cups, etc.): 11M (20% of sales)
- Labor (3-4 staff): 15M (27% of sales)
- Utilities, insurance, misc: 3M
- Royalty: 6% = 3.3M

**Calculation:**
```
Revenue:                55M
- COGS:                -11M
- Labor:               -15M
- Rent:                -13M
- Royalty:            -3.3M
- Utilities/misc:      -3M
————————————————————————
NET PROFIT:           9.7M
PROFIT MARGIN:        17.6%
```

**Location B: Suburban (Jamsil, Residential)**

Geography: Quiet residential area
- Estimated monthly sales: 28M won (neighbors + foot traffic)
- Rent: 5M won/month (cheap, but lower traffic)
- COGS: 5.6M (20%)
- Labor (2 staff): 7M (25%)
- Utilities, insurance, misc: 2M
- Royalty: 6% = 1.68M

**Calculation:**
```
Revenue:                28M
- COGS:                 -5.6M
- Labor:                -7M
- Rent:                 -5M
- Royalty:             -1.68M
- Utilities/misc:       -2M
————————————————————————
NET PROFIT:            6.72M
PROFIT MARGIN:         24%
```

**Analysis:**

Wait. Location B has a **higher profit margin** (24% vs. 17.6%), but Location A has **higher absolute profit** (9.7M vs. 6.72M).

Which should franchisee choose?

**Financial metrics:**

| Metric | Location A | Location B | Winner |
|--------|-----------|-----------|--------|
| Monthly profit | 9.7M | 6.72M | A (higher by 3M) |
| Profit margin | 17.6% | 24% | B (higher % but lower $ amount) |
| Payback period | 20.6 months | 29.8 months | A (faster recovery) |
| Annual ROI | 58.2% | 40.3% | A (higher return) |

**Recommendation:**

Choose **Location A**. Although profit margin % is lower, absolute profit is higher (9.7M vs. 6.72M). Franchisee recovers investment faster and earns more.

**Why Location A wins despite lower margin %:**

The absolute profit amount matters more than percentage. An 9.7M monthly profit generates 116.4M annually; at 200M investment, that's 58% ROI. That's excellent. Location B generates 80.6M annually; at 200M, that's 40% ROI. Good, but not as good.

Also, Location A reaches payback in 21 months (1.75 years). Location B takes 30 months (2.5 years). If the franchisee has kids in school or personal obligations, 21-month payback is much safer than 30-month payback.

**The Complicating Factor: Rent Uncertainty**

But here's the real-world complication. When franchisee signs lease for Location A, the landlord might:
- Require a 20% rent increase after 2 years
- Demand renovations (extra 10M won)
- Ask for long-term contract with price escalation

Location B's landlord might be more flexible because the space is less competitive.

If Location A's rent goes from 13M to 16M (20% increase), monthly profit drops from 9.7M to 6.7M. Now both locations are similar.

**Franchisee Strategy:**

Before choosing Location A, negotiate:
- Long-term rent lock (fixed rate for 5 years)
- Renovation cost-sharing (landlord pays half)
- Flexible renewal (opt-out clause if sales don't meet projections)

This is where site selection is as much **negotiation** as it is **analysis**.

**Problem 2: Chicken Franchise Rent Negotiation**

A franchisee has secured Location A from above and is in final rent negotiation. Landlord initially wanted 13M/month. Franchisee is countering.

**Scenario 1: Franchisee accepts 13M**
- Monthly profit: 9.7M (as calculated above)
- Negotiation took 1 month (lost time-to-market)

**Scenario 2: Franchisee negotiates down to 11M**
- Monthly profit: 11.7M (13M higher per month)
- Extra profit: 1.56B additional over 3 years
- But negotiation might fail; franchisee loses location to another bidder

**Scenario 3: Franchisee accepts higher but longer-term lease**
- Rent: 13M now, capped at 14M in year 3 (instead of market rate 16M)
- Security: Predictable costs
- Savings: 24M over 3 years (2M/month savings if market goes to 16M)

**What should franchisee do?**

Most financial advisors would say: **Accept 13M with long-term lock.** Here's why:

1. Profit is still excellent (9.7M/month)
2. Rent predictability reduces risk
3. Time-to-market matters (every month of delay = lost sales)
4. Aggressive negotiation (scenario 2) risks losing location to competitor

A franchisee who takes 3 months to negotiate down from 13M to 11M might lose Location A to someone who moves fast. Then franchisee ends up at Location B with 6.72M profit. That's worse than accepting Location A at 13M.

**Key Lesson:**

In franchise site selection, **speed and certainty often beat maximum negotiation**. A 9.7M bird in hand beats trying to get 11.7M and ending up with nothing.

#### Segment 3: Financial Scenario Analysis & Decision-Making (15 minutes)

**Problem Set: Real Decision Scenarios**

Now we're going to work through three financial scenarios that franchisees actually face. I'll describe situation, students suggest answers, then we discuss.

**Scenario 1: Royalty Rate Increase**

*Situation:*
A franchisee has operated a coffee shop for 2 years. Initial royalty was 5%. Business is healthy: 40M monthly sales, 4M monthly profit.

Now the franchisor announces: "We're raising royalty from 5% to 7% effective next month. This funds our new national marketing campaign."

Current royalty payment: 2M/month
New royalty payment: 2.8M/month
Additional cost: 0.8M/month = 9.6M/year

This would drop monthly profit from 4M to 3.2M. Franchisee is furious.

*Question: Is this justified? Should franchisee accept or fight back?*

**Analysis:**

**Franchisor's argument:**
- National marketing benefits all locations
- Marketing budget was too low at 5%
- Franchisees benefit long-term from stronger brand
- Industry standard is 6-7% for mature systems

**Franchisee's counter-argument:**
- I signed contract for 5% and built business around that
- Retroactive increase is unfair; I made investment decisions based on 5%
- My profit margin is now compressed to 8% (dangerously thin)
- Other franchises offer lower royalty rates

**What actually happens in Korea:**

Korean franchise law (Fair Transaction in Franchise Business Act) has clauses about royalty changes:
- Cannot be retroactively applied to existing franchisees without consent
- Can be applied to new franchisees
- Disputes often go to mediation

**My recommendation:**

**Accept the increase, but negotiate terms:**
1. Phase in the increase (5% → 5.5% → 6% → 6.5% → 7% over 4 years)
2. Require franchisor to provide financial proof that marketing is effective (sales data)
3. Secure reduced royalty if profit margins drop below 7% threshold
4. Renegotiate if marketing doesn't deliver promised results

This balances franchisor's need for capital with franchisee's need for profit stability.

**What NOT to do:**

- Don't refuse and breach contract (franchisor can terminate you)
- Don't accept without negotiating (8% royalty is unsustainable)
- Don't sue immediately (litigation costs >50M, takes 2-3 years)

---

**Scenario 2: New Location Investment**

*Situation:*
A successful franchisee is earning 7M/month from one location (initial investment: 250M, paid back in 36 months, now in year 4).

She wants to open a second location. Franchisor is encouraging this. New location would cost 280M won to open.

The question: **Can she afford it? Should she do it?**

**Financial analysis:**

First location generates: 7M/month × 12 = 84M/year profit

To invest 280M, she needs:
- Option 1: Use 84M profit from Year 1 + borrow 196M
- Option 2: Wait 3-4 years and self-fund with accumulated profit
- Option 3: Get franchisor financing (sometimes available)

**If she borrows 196M at 5% interest:**
- Annual interest cost: 9.8M
- New location needs to generate: 9.8M + payback amount

If new location generates 5M/month profit (smaller location than first):
- Annual profit: 60M
- After interest: 50.2M
- Payback period: 280M ÷ (5M/month - 0.82M/month interest) = 63 months (5.2 years)

**Problem:** Payback takes too long. She'd still be paying off debt for location 2 while wanting to open location 3. This is dangerous.

**If she waits 3 years and self-funds:**
- First location has generated: 7M × 36 = 252M profit
- Plus original investment: 250M = 502M total capital
- Can open location 2 (280M) and still have 222M working capital

- New location generates 5M/month: 60M/year
- Combined profit from both: 144M/year
- This is sustainable and diversified

**Recommendation:**

Wait 3 years and self-fund location 2. Avoid debt and the risk of overlapping payback periods. She'll be on track to open location 3 in year 6-7 with multiple profitable locations.

**What NOT to do:**

Don't borrow aggressively or expand too fast. Many franchisees have failed by opening location 2 too soon, going into debt, and then having both locations struggle during recession.

**Scenario 3: Franchise System at Risk**

*Situation:*
A franchisee has been operating for 1 year with reasonable profit (4M/month). Suddenly, she notices:

1. New franchises are opening 200 meters away (oversaturation)
2. Franchisor just increased royalty from 5% to 7% (from Scenario 1)
3. Franchisor's national marketing spend is decreasing (they're spending less on national ads)
4. Franchisor just hired new CEO (leadership instability)

Her monthly profit is now declining: 4M → 3.5M → 3M over three months.

*Question: Should she exit the franchise? How?*

**Red flag analysis:**

- **New locations too close:** Oversaturation weakens brand value
- **Rising royalty + declining profit:** Unsustainable
- **Reduced marketing spend:** Franchisor is struggling financially
- **Leadership change:** Potential strategic shift; uncertain future

This franchise system is showing signs of decline.

**Exit strategies:**

**Option 1: Sell the franchise location**
- Find another franchisee willing to buy
- Sell for reduced price (location is now riskier)
- Time-consuming (3-6 months)

**Option 2: Wait and see**
- Monitor profit for 6 more months
- If stabilizes at 3M+, stay and adapt
- If continues declining, exit then
- Risk: Location becomes even harder to sell

**Option 3: Negotiation**
- Go to franchisor, provide profit data
- Propose: Reduce royalty to 5% for next 2 years, then reassess
- Or: Get franchisor approval to rebrand or modify system
- Upside: System improves; Downside: Franchisor might refuse

**My recommendation:**

File this as a "situation to monitor." Don't panic-sell yet. But do:

1. Build financial reserves (don't spend profit for 6 months)
2. Explore alternative franchise systems (what would you switch to?)
3. Prepare exit plan (identify potential buyers, know market value)
4. Set decision date (if profit hits 2.5M/month, you leave; if it stabilizes, you stay)

This gives you agency and time to make rational decision instead of emotional reaction.

### Anticipated Student Questions & Answers

**Q: "Why did Location A pay higher rent despite worse profit margin?"**
A: Good question. Location A has higher traffic (office workers), so rent is higher. But higher sales volume (55M vs. 28M) means rent is actually a SMALLER percentage of sales at Location A (24% vs. 18%). That's why absolute profit is higher.

**Q: "In Scenario 1, could the franchisee just refuse to pay the increased royalty?"**
A: Legally? They could try. But franchisor would likely terminate the franchise agreement. You'd be forced to close. Franchisees tried this in Korea in 2014-2015 (Cafe Bene, GS25). Most lost legal battles. Better strategy: Negotiate terms upfront.

**Q: "How did you know Location A would generate 55M sales?"**
A: I didn't. That's an estimate based on location characteristics and franchisor data. Real franchisees verify this by:
- Counting foot traffic at the location
- Asking franchisor for comp stores in similar locations
- Seeing actual transaction counts during peak hours
- This is called "site validation" and it's critical.

**Q: "In Scenario 3, what if franchisor goes bankrupt?"**
A: You own the location and equipment, so you can:
- Rebrand as independent (lose brand but keep location)
- Pivot to different franchise system
- Sell the location and equipment
- Worst case: You lose investment, but you own physical assets

This is why multiple locations > one location. One fails, others survive.

### Common Misconceptions to Correct

1. **"Higher profit margin % always means better investment"**
   - Reality: 24% of 28M (6.7M) < 17.6% of 55M (9.7M). Absolute profit matters more

2. **"Location negotiation is about winning the most rent reduction"**
   - Reality: It's about certainty. Fixed rent for 5 years > fighting for 1M reduction and losing location

3. **"Rising royalties are always bad for franchisees"**
   - Reality: If franchisor uses capital to improve system, it can increase total value for all. The question is: Does improved system justify higher royalty?

4. **"Franchisees can always exit easily"**
   - Reality: Selling a struggling location is very difficult. Exit planning should start early

### Vocabulary Emphasis

- **Responsibility matrix:** Who does what, who pays for what
- **Task assignment:** Which party performs each function
- **Payback period:** Time to recover investment through profit
- **Profit margin:** Profit as percentage of sales
- **ROI:** Return on investment, typically expressed as annual percentage
- **Oversaturation:** Too many similar franchises in same market
- **Exit strategy:** Plan to leave franchise investment
- **Debt service:** Monthly payment on borrowed capital
- **Red flags:** Warning signs of system failure
