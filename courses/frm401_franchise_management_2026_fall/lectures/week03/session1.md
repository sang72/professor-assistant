## SECTION 2: FULL LECTURE SCRIPT

Welcome to Week 3. We have spent two weeks on fundamentals. Now we dive into the framework that governs franchise relationships. It is called the Franchise Relationship Model, or FRM. This framework is central to everything we will study for the rest of this semester.

The Franchise Relationship Model has six components. Let me introduce them clearly.

[SHOW SLIDE 1: FRM Overview]

First: the customer. What does the customer want? Why would a customer choose a Starbucks over a local café? Consistency, convenience, quality.

Second: opportunity recognition. The franchisor identifies an opportunity in the market. There is demand. The franchisor builds a system to serve that demand.

Third: the Service Delivery System. How does the franchisor ensure that every franchisee location serves customers identically?

Fourth: transaction analysis. Who does what? What tasks does the franchisor perform? What tasks does the franchisee perform?

Fifth: the financial model. How do franchisors and franchisees make money? What are the fees, royalties, profit sharing?

Sixth: agency issues and monitoring. How does the franchisor monitor franchisees to ensure they follow the system?

[WRITE ON BOARD: FRM = Customer + Opportunity + SDS + Transaction Analysis + Financial Model + Monitoring]

[TIME: ~10 min]

---

[SHOW SLIDE 2: The Customer at the Center]

Let me start with the customer. Every franchise exists because there is customer demand. Starbucks exists because customers want coffee and a comfortable place to work. McDonald's exists because customers want quick, affordable meals. The entire system flows from understanding customer needs.

This is the foundation of the FRM. Everything else flows from this understanding.

[TIME: ~20 min]

---

[SHOW SLIDE 3: Opportunity Recognition & SDS]

When a franchisor recognizes an opportunity, they build a Service Delivery System to serve customer needs. The SDS is how the franchisor deploys resources — real estate, equipment, people, marketing — to deliver consistent value. If the SDS is clear and teachable, it can be franchised.

[TIME: ~30 min]

---

[SHOW SLIDE 4: Transaction Analysis]

Transaction analysis answers: who does what? For example, in a McDonald's franchise, the franchisor selects the site. The franchisee constructs the building and hires staff. The franchisor designs the menu. The franchisee manages daily operations. Clear assignment of responsibilities prevents conflict.

[TIME: ~40 min]

---

[ASK STUDENTS: Based on the FRM, why do you think the Financial Model and Monitoring are critical? What could go wrong if those components are weak?]

[PAUSE — wait for student responses]

Great insights. If the financial model is unclear, franchisees will not invest. If monitoring is weak, franchisees will cut corners and damage the brand. Both are essential.

The FRM is not just a model. It is a roadmap for building successful franchises. Next session, we will dive deeper into the franchisor-franchisee dynamics and how they create value together.

Thank you. See you next session.

[TIME: ~50 min]
