## SECTION 2: FULL LECTURE SCRIPT

Welcome back, everyone. Today we shift from course logistics to the exciting world of franchising itself. Yesterday we covered grading, policies, and course structure. Today we explore the business landscape.

[SHOW SLIDE 1: Global Franchising Scale]

Franchising is enormous. In the United States, there are over 750,000 franchise units generating more than $900 billion in annual sales. That is trillion with a B. Nine hundred billion dollars. Globally, franchising is present in 150+ countries and represents one of the largest business models on Earth.

[WRITE ON BOARD: 750,000+ US units | $900B+ annual sales | Global presence in 150+ countries]

Why is franchising so prevalent? Because it works. It allows companies to grow rapidly without needing to own every location. A franchisor can expand to 1,000 locations through franchisees. Franchisees get to own a business with a proven system. When done correctly, it is a genuine partnership.

[TIME: ~10 min]

---

Franchising is not distributed equally across industries. Let me show you the breakdown.

[SHOW SLIDE 2: Industry Distribution]

[WRITE ON BOARD: Food Service 40% | Retail 25% | Services 20% | Other 15%]

Food service dominates at 40 percent. Think McDonald's, Subway, Chipotle, Dunkin' Donuts. Quick-service restaurants are perfect for franchising. The brand is recognizable. The product is consistent. A franchisee knows exactly what they are buying.

Retail is 25 percent. 7-Eleven, Starbucks, fitness brands, salons. These businesses have clear service models that can be replicated.

Services are 20 percent. Hotels like Marriott, hair salons, car rentals, real estate agencies. The franchisor controls the brand and system. The franchisee operates locally.

[ASK STUDENTS: Can anyone think of a franchise they have used this week? What industry was it?]

[PAUSE — wait for student responses]

Good examples. These are all franchises. And they succeed because they have built strong systems.

[TIME: ~18 min]

---

[SHOW SLIDE 3: McDonald's Success]

Let me give you specific examples. McDonald's began in 1955 with one restaurant. Today, 40,000 locations in 130+ countries. Most are franchised. McDonald's makes money through fees, royalties, and rent. Franchisees operate locations and keep profits above costs. Why does this work? Strong brand. Standardized system. Ongoing support. Aligned incentives.

[SHOW SLIDE 4: 7-Eleven Mastery]

7-Eleven mastered convenience retail. Every store looks identical. Every store stocks similar products. Uses similar technology. A franchisee buys into a machine. Supply chain is centralized. Quality control is centralized. The franchisee executes the system locally.

[SHOW SLIDE 5: Starbucks Hybrid Model]

Starbucks owns some stores directly and franchises others. This hybrid approach gives the company control while enabling rapid growth.

Why do these franchises succeed? Five common factors. [SHOW SLIDE 6: Success Factors]

First: strong, recognizable brand. Second: proven, replicable system. Third: ongoing franchisor support. Fourth: aligned incentives — both parties win together. Fifth: effective quality control. A bad franchisee hurts the brand for everyone.

[TIME: ~40 min]

---

[SHOW SLIDE 7: Key Takeaways]

Let me recap. Franchising is a global, multi-billion-dollar business model. It is most prevalent in food, retail, and services. Successful franchises like McDonald's and 7-Eleven have strong brands, standardized systems, and clear support structures. When you see a franchise succeed, look for these factors.

Next session, we will define franchising formally. We will ask: what exactly is a franchise, legally? What is the relationship between franchisor and franchisee? Prepare for some conceptual depth.

Thank you. See you next session.
