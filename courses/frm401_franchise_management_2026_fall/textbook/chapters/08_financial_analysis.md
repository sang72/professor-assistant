## The Six Key Transactions

### 1. Revenue

**Unit of analysis**: The customer.

**Key questions:**
- How much will the average customer spend?
- How many customers can be served in a day or day part?

**Critical focus — throughput during peak periods:**
- Bagelz: Lunchtime
- Dunkin' Donuts: Morning drive time
- Jiffy Lube: Saturday morning

**Charge to every SDS**: Sell more to each of an increasing number of customers.

**Warning**: McDonald's engaged in a $0.99 price war in 2002 and suffered its first quarterly loss ever in that year. Pushing down price pushes up labor as a percentage of revenue.

### 2. Labor

**What human assets are required to earn revenue?**

**Key principle**: A strong brand attracts customers who will pay a premium.

**Labor cost dynamics:**
- Strong brand → commands premium pricing → pushes up unit revenue → forces DOWN labor percentage
- More streamlined SDS → more efficient labor usage → higher capacity utilization

**Industry benchmark**: For Bagelz, labor is 22% of revenue.

### 3. Cost of Goods Sold

**After paying for product components, how much revenue remains?**

**Economies of scale in supply are a key competitive advantage:**
- Dunkin' Donuts: One of the largest buyers of coffee in the world
- Jiffy Lube: One of the largest buyers of motor oil in the world
- Virtually every franchise has opportunity for regional or national supply contracts

**For Bagelz**: Food and beverage are key supply needs. Everything from flour to tomatoes should be bought under a regional or national contract.

**Financial benefit**: Advantageous national contracts often result in more money for store operators at the bottom line.

### 4. Royalties

**The simple test of a franchise**: Do the advantages in the franchise pay for the royalty?

**If the advantages are not sufficient to rationalize the ongoing royalty payment:**
- Tension will exist between franchisee and franchisor
- Franchisee may wonder if they'd be better off as an independent operator

**Comparison method**: Show net revenue as sales minus royalties to compare with non-franchised outlets.
- Both franchisee AND franchisor should do this calculation
- If comparison doesn't yield positive comparisons, why would anyone buy the franchise?

**Bagelz royalty**: 8% of revenue ($33,280/year per store)

### 5. Rent (Occupancy Cost)

**Key principle**: The most appropriate real estate location is the one that produces the LOWEST RENT AS A PERCENTAGE OF REVENUE.

**Critical distinction**: Do NOT confuse percentage of rent with absolute cost.

**Example:**
- Location A: Rent = $100,000/year; Revenue = $1,000,000 → Rent as % of revenue = 10%
- Location B: Rent = $5,500/year; Revenue = $50,000 → Rent as % of revenue = 11%
- Location B has lower absolute rent but is RELATIVELY MORE EXPENSIVE

**Best real estate**: Attracts the most revenue at the lowest price when real estate price is calculated as a PERCENTAGE of revenue.

**Failed locations reflect poorly on the brand and hurt everyone associated with the franchise.**

**Bagelz rent**: 6% of revenue ($26,208/year per store)

### 6. Advertising and Promotion

**Bagelz allocation:**
- Advertising line (2.5%): System fund used by franchisor to create materials and sometimes buy media
- Local advertising line (1%): Monies spent by franchisee in surrounding markets
- Local advertising can be sponsorship of local youth sports or purchase of electronic media

## Case Study: Panera Bread / PR Restaurants LLC

*(All information based on 2001 data)*

### Background: The Saint Louis Bread Company

Ken Rosenthal, 1987:
- Sold his apparel business after experiencing sourdough bread in San Francisco
- Took out second mortgage on his house, started first bakery — Saint Louis Bread Company (SLBC)
- Located in a nondescript strip mall, but was an immediate hit
- Grew organically to 19 company-owned stores + 1 franchised = 20 units by 1993
- Company-owned store sales: approximately $20 million
- Lacked capital to fuel further growth

### Au Bon Pain Acquisition (1993)

Ron Shaich (CEO of Au Bon Pain): Saw something that captured his imagination — the vision of a "neighborhood bakery-café" not constrained to downtown high-rise buildings.

**Key advantage over Au Bon Pain**: Suburban concept + higher top-line sales revenue + lower cost structures + attractive bottom-line figures.

**Au Bon Pain purchased SLBC for approximately $24 million.**

By 1995: Systemwide sales $44 million; grew from 20 to 45 units.

### Fast-Casual Dining Segment

Fast-casual accounts for **$19 billion (5%)** of the $380 billion restaurant industry.

Key elements of success (survey of fast-casual consumers):
1. Good quality food
2. Convenience to home
3. Good value for money

**Panera grew at 56% from 1999 to 2000** — targeting aging baby boomers and young mothers tired of fast-food chains.

Fast-casual market share:
- 2000: 18.1% of restaurant sales
- 1999: 14.9% of restaurant sales
- All other types lost market share over past three years

### The Panera Brand

**Name derivation**: Play on "pain" = "bread" in French; also "basket of bread" in Spanish.

**The neighborhood bakery-café concept:**
- Fresh baked goods
- Hot and cold beverages
- Sandwiches, soups, salads
- Fast, convenient, friendly atmosphere
- Self-serve hot beverage stations with free refills
- Ambience: warm yellow/orange walls, hardwood tables, working fireplace in select locations, light music, trendy lights, couches, colorful wall art
- Creates an inviting respite for main mealtimes AND "chill-out" times

**1999 timeline**: Ron Shaich sold Au Bon Pain division to Bruckmann, Rosser, Sherrill & Co.
**1999**: System-wide sales of $171 million from 181 units
**2001**: Net income rose 92% to $13 million; revenues increased 33% to $201 million from 369 units in 30 states

### The Panera Franchise Concept: Area Development Agreements (ADAs)

**Strategic decision**: Sell ADAs instead of individual units → avoid the mom-and-pop environment of inexperienced entrepreneurs → more sophisticated, experienced, multi-unit franchisees.

**ADA Terms:**
- Development fee: $5,000 per Panera to be opened under the agreement
- Initial franchise fee: $35,000 per bakery-café (including $5,000 development fee)
- Royalty: 5% of gross sales annually
- National advertising fund: up to 2.6% of gross sales (when called for by corporate)
- Local advertising: 2% of gross sales
- Marketing administration fees: 0.4% of gross sales

**By end of 2002**: 525 units open; 39 territories under agreement; committed units: ~733

**Note**: The largest Panera franchisee (39 units) was previously one of McDonald's largest franchisees (40 units).

### Service Delivery System: The Dough Problem

Every Panera location bakes all breads and other products FRESH DAILY.

**Two types of dough:**
1. **Sourdough-based products**: Fresh dough produced at one of Panera's 11 company-owned dough commissaries → delivered DAILY to each unit
2. **Non-sourdough products**: Frozen dough produced in Missouri commissary → delivered TWICE PER WEEK through independent food distributor

**Commissary investment**: Approximately $1 million to build and equip a sourdough commissary.

**Expansion strategy**: Expand ADAs around EXISTING commissaries to maximize capacity and distribution.

**Ideal distribution**: 20-30 units per commissary.

**Dough pricing**: Uniform across system at 30% of retail prices regardless of transportation distance.

**Commissary-less cafés** (under consideration): On-site dough production + existing baking functions
- Additional cost: ~$125,000 per year in annual unit operating expenses
- Additional consideration: $40,000 oven for new product introductions

### PR Restaurants LLC

Founded 1997 by:
- **David Peterman**: 29 years in food industry
  - Started as IHOP manager
  - 15 years at Taco Bell (district manager → regional VP)
  - Senior VP of operations at Au Bon Pain (overseeing 186 cafés, 4,000 employees)
- **Mitchell Roberts**: 
  - 5 years in real estate development after college
  - 8 years at Au Bon Pain (head of corporate real estate development)
  - MIT Sloan MBA

**First store**: Negotiated deal directly with Ron Shaich while still at Au Bon Pain.
**Break-even**: Achieved in 5 WEEKS.

**ADA**: Covers area outside Route 128 in Massachusetts + all of New Hampshire, Vermont, and Maine.

**Funding**: Raised $1.3 million from friends, family, and angel investors.

Initial seed capital allocation:
- $100,000: Franchise fees for entire 20-unit ADA
- $50,000: Startup costs including attorneys
- $60,000: David's salary for 6 months
- $600,000: First store's leasehold improvements
- $500,000: Franchisee fees and working capital

**Ownership structure**: Mitch 40%, David 40%, investors 20%

### PR Restaurant Financial Performance

**Favorable royalty**: PR Restaurants pays 4.5% (not 5%) as early adopters.
- Benefit: With $2M in sales, the 0.5% reduction = additional $10,000 net income per year

**Average unit sales**: $2.1 million (vs. Panera system average of $1.7 million)
- System same-store sales increase: 6.6% in new units
- PR Restaurants: Panera's leading franchisee in same-store sales

**Space**: Between 3,500 and 4,500 square feet; typical 10-year lease with two 5-year options

**Average ticket sales:**
- Breakfast: ~$3 per person
- Lunch: ~$8 per person (slightly misleading due to larger party sizes)

**Comparison metric**: Panera's average sales per square foot = $475 vs. Chili's = $240

**Management:**
- General manager salaries (with bonuses): Average $68,000 (high end ~$80,000)
- Assistant manager salaries: $35,000 to $50,000
- General manager turnover: ZERO
- Assistant manager turnover: ~30%
- Hourly staff turnover: 60-70% (vs. industry average of 100%+)

**Working capital required to open a new location**: ~$765,000 (includes real estate development, leasehold improvements, preopening salaries, startup marketing costs, and losses to breakeven)

### The Harvest Question (Foreshadowing Chapter 12)

With 9 stores open and plans for 20 total by end of 2004, Mitch and David faced the question:

"We can't grow fast enough [to meet the market demand for Panera]."

Key harvest options to consider:
- Sell at end of year with 13 units operating (new owner develops remaining 7)?
- Sell once 20-unit obligation is complete?
- Renegotiate for larger territory and additional ADA?
- Run 20 units as lifestyle business with healthy positive cash flow?
