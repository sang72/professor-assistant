## SECTION 2: FULL LECTURE SCRIPT

Welcome to Week 2. Last week, we covered the landscape of franchising globally and course logistics. This week, we dive into the heart of franchising. We ask fundamental questions: What exactly is franchising? How is it defined? How is it different from other business models?

Let me start with a review question.

[ASK STUDENTS: Based on what we learned last week, can anyone give me a one-sentence definition of franchising?]

[PAUSE — wait for student responses]

Good attempts. Those are all reasonable. But there is a precise legal and business definition. That is what we are learning today.

[TIME: ~3 min]

---

[SHOW SLIDE 1: The Franchise Definition]

Here is the formal definition. A franchise is a contractual relationship between a franchisor — the company that owns the brand and system — and a franchisee — an independent businessperson who operates under that brand. The franchisor grants the franchisee the right to do business under the franchisor's trademark and system. In exchange, the franchisee pays an initial fee and ongoing royalties. The franchisee agrees to follow the franchisor's operational system.

[WRITE ON BOARD: Franchise = Brand Owner (Franchisor) + Operator (Franchisee) + Contract + Trademark + System + Fees]

Let me break down the key elements. First, there must be a trademark or brand. McDonald's, Starbucks, 7-Eleven — these are trademarks. The franchisor owns these marks. The franchisee operates under these marks. Second, there must be a system. The franchisor has a proven way of doing business. The franchisee follows that system. Third, there must be financial terms. Typically, an initial franchise fee — maybe $20,000, maybe $500,000, depending on the brand. And ongoing royalties — typically 5 to 7 percent of revenue. Fourth, there is control. The franchisor has the right to inspect, audit, and enforce standards. The franchisee has the right to operate the business and keep profits.

[PAUSE 3s]

[TIME: ~10 min]

---

[SHOW SLIDE 2: Franchising vs. Licensing]

Now, how is franchising different from licensing? Good question. Licensing is when a company allows another company to use its intellectual property — maybe a patent or a design — in exchange for a royalty. But licensing does not usually include the full operational system. A franchisee is not just using a name. A franchisee is using a complete, proven business system.

[SHOW SLIDE 3: Franchising vs. Wholesaling]

How is franchising different from wholesaling? In wholesaling, a wholesaler buys products from a manufacturer and sells them to retailers. The relationship is transactional. In franchising, the franchisor and franchisee are long-term partners. The franchisor is invested in the franchisee's success because the franchisor's revenue depends on the franchisee's sales.

[SHOW SLIDE 4: Franchising vs. Employment]

How is franchising different from hiring employees? An employee works for a company and earns a salary. A franchisee is an independent businessperson. The franchisee owns the business. The franchisee assumes the risk. If the business succeeds, the franchisee profits. If it fails, the franchisee loses their investment. An employee cannot lose their job and lose their life savings. A franchisee can. This distinction is crucial.

[TIME: ~18 min]

---

[SHOW SLIDE 5: What Does It Mean to Be Entrepreneurial?]

Now, let us talk about entrepreneurship in franchising. What does it mean to be entrepreneurial? It means recognizing an opportunity, assembling resources, and taking risk to pursue that opportunity.

In traditional entrepreneurship, you start a business from scratch. You invent the product. You build the system. You establish the brand. You recruit customers. This is enormously risky. Most new businesses fail. But if you succeed, you can build something transformative.

In franchise entrepreneurship, you are still entrepreneurial, but in a different way. You are not inventing the product. That is done. The franchisor has already tested the model. You are not building the system from zero. The system exists. What you are doing is recognizing the opportunity to own a business in a particular market. You are assembling resources — maybe a loan from a bank, or savings. You are taking risk. If you operate the franchise poorly, you lose your investment. If you operate it well, you profit.

[PAUSE 3s]

So franchisees are entrepreneurial. But their entrepreneurship is more focused and less risky than traditional entrepreneurs.

[TIME: ~28 min]

---

[SHOW SLIDE 6: Opportunity Recognition]

How do you recognize an opportunity in franchising? Our textbook describes three key factors. First, market structure and competition. Is the market growing or shrinking? Are there many competitors or few? Is the market fragmented — many small players — or consolidated — a few large players? If the market is growing and fragmented, that might be a good opportunity for a franchise to consolidate the market.

Second, market size. How big is the addressable market? How many potential customers are there? If you are thinking about buying a Starbucks franchise in a small town of 5,000 people, is that enough market? Maybe. Maybe not. You need to estimate demand.

Third, margin analysis. What are the costs of operating a franchise location? What is the revenue? What is the profit? If you buy a Subway franchise, the initial investment might be $250,000. Annual revenue might be $500,000. Costs — food, labor, rent — might be $400,000. That leaves $100,000. After expenses, loan payments, and taxes, what is left? Is that enough to justify the risk?

[WRITE ON BOARD: Opportunity Recognition = Market Structure + Market Size + Margin Analysis]

When all three factors align — growing market, large addressable market, healthy margins — that is when an opportunity exists.

[TIME: ~38 min]

---

[SHOW SLIDE 7: Franchisee Risk & Reward]

Let me be clear about franchisee risk. When you buy a franchise, you are investing your money. You are opening a business. That business could fail. Even though the franchisor has a proven system, local factors matter. The location might be poor. The local economy might decline. A major competitor might open nearby. So franchisees face real risk.

But they also have advantages compared to traditional entrepreneurs. The system is proven. The brand is established. Customer demand is somewhat predictable. You are not inventing from zero.
