## Opening Case: Pizza Hut vs. Domino's (1986)

In 1986 Pizza Hut was struggling with upstart competitor Domino's. Franchisor management was convinced that pizza delivery was a real threat and that Pizza Hut needed to respond.

**The problem:**
- Pizza Hut had over 1,000 franchises with territory rights that were highly successful sit-down restaurants
- Delivery system added costs with uncertain revenue
- Tension grew as franchisor began to experiment with centralized phone ordering and special delivery procedures
- Pizza Hut teetered on the brink of massive litigation

**The lesson**: If you don't pay attention to key aspects of the franchise partnership, the system will fray.

**Solution**: The Franchise Relationship Model (FRM) makes these details understandable.

---

## The Franchise Relationship Model (FRM)

The FRM takes the entrepreneurial framework from Chapter 1 and incorporates specific processes unique to franchising.

**The FRM provides:**
- A blueprint for developing and analyzing all aspects of the franchise format
- A way to distinguish tasks best executed under corporate umbrella vs. individual franchisee
- Anticipation of practical breakdowns in the franchise relationship
- Avenues for appropriate modifications for both parties

**The model begins in the center with the CUSTOMER and moves to the SDS.**

The value of the FRM: It enables you to identify the nature of consequences when changes occur in your target customer, customer needs, or the SDS.

## The Customer: How Does Opportunity Recognition Work in Franchising?

**Key principle**: There is no such thing as a great idea for a franchise — only a great opportunity.

Assessment of a concept starts with defining the opportunity and is validated in one or more outlets.

**Domino's example**: Exposed pizza delivery as a customer need that Pizza Hut wasn't meeting. The 30-minute guarantee changed the rules about how people could eat pizza.

**Revenue projection formula:**

Target Customer × Estimated Revenue per Transaction × Transactions per Year = Annual Revenue

**Example — Kinder Care (daycare):**
- Average revenue per transaction = base cost of care per day + extra services
- Annual revenue = number of days per year × average price per day

## Establishing the Service Delivery System (SDS)

The SDS is called a proprietary design because it is not only the fundamental means by which customers' needs will be served, but it is also the way resources are arrayed to create competitive advantage.

**"The devil is in the details" — Jiffy Lube SDS story:**

Steve Spinelli recalls that specific location criteria had to be strictly adhered to:
- High volume of car traffic
- Which side of the street (outbound or inbound)
- High-profile retail area
- Far corner of any given street or block
- Exact angle of the building
- Width, depth, and angle of the entrance
- Degree bend of the driveway (to allow cars to "stack" in line)

The bend in the driveway was too sharp at some locations → gave the impression to oncoming traffic that the lot was full → had to adjust several driveways.

**The bi-level SDS innovation:**
Problem: How to offer 30 minutes of labor in only 10 minutes?
Solution: Cars drive through the bay and stop above an opening in the floor:
- Technician 1: Services car from below
- Technician 2: Services car underneath the hood
- Technician 3: Services car's interior

This "disruptive" system was confirmed when competitors (gas stations and car dealers) insisted they could offer a "quick lube" in 30 minutes using hydraulic lifts and traditional bays — and simply could not compete.

**TIP 2-1: How Wendy's Used Its SDS to Enter a Saturated Market (1972)**
Dave Thomas entered what many called an already crowded hamburger fast-food market.
- Introduced the FIRST DRIVE-THROUGH in a national fast-food chain
- Kitchens designed to mass-produce hamburgers
- Large front window: grill cooks visible to customers to observe traffic flow
- Offered double and triple patties (custom feel)
- Result: Successfully competed against McDonald's and Burger King's huge market share

## Transaction Analysis: Allocation of Responsibilities

**Transaction analysis**: The assignment of tasks and responsibilities to the franchise partner best suited to execute them.

**General guide:**
- **Franchisor tasks**: Those that carry economies of scale (size or bulk requirement)
- **Franchisee responsibilities**: Tasks executed on site in the local market requiring local knowledge

**Sample Transaction List (Table 2-1)**

| Franchisor (Centralized economies of scale) | Franchisee (Local entrepreneurial intensity) |
|---------------------------------------------|----------------------------------------------|
| Establish site acquisition criteria | Locate site that fits criteria |
| Design building blueprint specifications | Initiate local contractor bidding |
| National contracts for inventory and equipment | Onsite installation and inventory management |
| Centralized training of franchisee | Onsite training of franchise employees |
| Detail marketing plan template | Placement of promotions in local media |

**Key insight**: The transaction analysis defines the financial status of both franchisor and franchisee — every transaction incurs a cost or requires investment.

## Financial Structure: The Financial Model of the Franchise Concept

**Building the financial picture:**
- Customer definition → understanding of revenue
- Marshaling resources to establish SDS → understanding costs
- Known revenue + known costs = understanding of net revenue

**From transactions to financial statements:**
- Cost of resources → develop balance sheet
- Revenue from product delivery → develop income statement
- Pro forma financial statements → test viability before launch

**Key insight from Wendy's**: The whole concept is greater than the sum of the individual component parts. The competitive advantages of a franchise's business format come from the successful combination of parts.

## Agency Issues: How to Find the Right Franchise Partner

A key issue in any strategic business interdependency is the partner selection process and the ensuing principal-agent relationship.

**The agent** (most cases: the franchisee) agrees to act on behalf of **the principal** (the franchisor).

### Is This Franchise Right for You?
- Considerable due diligence information is available
- Potential franchisees sometimes fall in love with a concept and forget to do detailed due diligence
- Must study the revenue growth in existing stores and examine how fast new stores reach breakeven

### How Will a Franchisor Select You?
- Tendency for franchisor to "sell" franchises rather than cultivate long-term relationships
- Need for urgency to grow can result in relaxed standards for choosing franchisees
- Example: Dunkin' Donuts conducts extensive personality profiling of prospective franchisees

### Two Biggest Hindrances to Successful Partnership:
1. **Free riding**: Franchisee benefits from association with the franchise brand but neglects to pay for this benefit
   - Example: Accepts co-op advertising funds from a vendor in exchange for endorsement but doesn't promote the product as agreed
2. **Shirking**: Franchisee neglects a specific task or duty as outlined in the franchise agreement
   - Example: Using slightly less beef in a hamburger to save cost
   - Most prevalent in tourist areas with non-repeat customers

## Relational Dynamics: How Are Tasks Best Monitored?

**Jack in the Box incident (1993)**: A franchisee inadvertently served tainted meat to a customer who died. Brand equity was so severely damaged it took seven years to reach pre-incident systemwide revenue.

Most conduct detrimental to the franchise is less significant but still gnaws away at brand equity:
- Slightly slower counter service
- Dirty restrooms
- Inventory shortages

**McDonald's hula burger example**: Ray Kroc insisted on introducing a pineapple-covered hamburger that never gained public acceptance → franchisees lost a lot of money.

**Relational dynamics definition**: The active management of the franchisee-franchisor relationship, both formal and informal.

**Key principle**: If the franchisee or franchisor insists on a very strict interpretation of the license agreement, conflict is almost assured and will likely result in litigation.

**Effective monitoring system must:**
1. Monitor negative behavior
2. Examine exceptional performance and feed it back to the rest of the franchise operators

**Cost considerations**: Monitoring all activities is cost prohibitive and would erode confidence in the relationship. Tasks should be prioritized according to their potential impact on brand equity.

## Information Systems

**The key to building and maintaining information flow:**
- Must adequately police the operational standards of the system
- Must provide feedback of exceptional performance to the franchisor for systemwide implementation

**TIP 2-2: Monitoring Tasks — Jiffy Lube Point-of-Sale System**

If store A serviced 2,481 cars:
- Should use 2,655 gallons of motor oil (4.1 quarts per car)
- Should use 2,481 oil filters
→ Discrepancies detected immediately

**Information flow also uncovers innovations:**
A franchisee in Baltimore reported lower labor costs:
- Franchisor dispatched team to investigate
- Franchisee had invented a strap-like wrench that easily slipped into tight places for small oil filters in compacted engines
- Franchisor further engineered the device and made it available to all franchisees
→ Problem converted into competitive advantage

**Key insight**: Each franchisee benefits from every other franchisee's effort to solve problems and exploit opportunities.

## The Dynamics of the Franchisee-Franchisor Relationship

**The question**: How does the franchise entrepreneurial alliance adjust for changes in the market?

**ServiceMaster example**:
- Founded 1929 as moth-proofing company by Marion E. Wade
- Expanded to carpet cleaning in 1952 (one of America's first franchise businesses)
- 1962: Pioneered outsourcing in healthcare
- 1980: Began serving education market
- Result: Transformed from moth-proofing company into a Fortune 500 company

**Key principle**: When market dynamics or organizational design cause the majority of franchisees to lose money, this dis-equilibrium must be addressed.

**Methods for dealing with changes:**
1. Formal renegotiation of the license agreement
2. Informal adjustments (improving supplier relations, greater control over advertising funds)
3. Litigation (last resort — financially and relationally costly)

**Field personnel quality**: A leading indicator of the quality of the franchise organization as a whole.

## Conclusion

The FRM is dynamic — as events affect one aspect, all other aspects must be reviewed in a recurring iterative process.

**The FRM progression:**
1. Opportunity recognition and shaping
2. Competitive advantages and costs of the SDS
3. Emergent financial model (interaction between primary target customer and SDS)
4. Delineation of responsibilities between franchisor and franchisee
5. Partner selection and monitoring

**Tolerance zone**: An informal interpretation of the rights and responsibilities of each partner; allows for a margin of error in daily activities. Failure to recognize the need for dynamic management can often result in litigation.
