## Global Expansion Models

**Model 1: Direct Franchising**
- Franchisor directly recruits franchisees in target country
- Franchisor maintains control of system
- Requires franchisor to understand local market
- Example: Starbucks in Korea

**Model 2: Master Franchising**
- Franchisor partners with master franchisee (local company)
- Master franchisee recruits and manages sub-franchisees
- Lower franchisor risk, lower franchisor control
- Example: KFC in Asia (often via master franchisees)

**Model 3: Joint Venture**
- Franchisor partners with local company as equal partner
- Shared investment and decision-making
- Used for large market entries
- Example: McDonald's early China expansion (now direct)

**Model 4: Company-Owned Entry**
- Franchisor opens company-owned stores first
- Proves business model before franchising
- Higher initial investment, lower risk of franchisee failure
- Example: Starbucks' early Korea strategy

## International SDS Adaptation

**Must Standardize:**
- Core product quality
- Brand visual identity
- Service philosophy
- Safety standards

**Should Localize:**
- Product variations (menus, flavors)
- Marketing messages
- Store design (local aesthetics)
- Staffing practices (labor laws differ)

---

## Real Examples

**Starbucks Global:**
- Present in 80+ countries
- Highly standardized processes
- Heavily localized menus
- Model: Direct franchising + company-owned stores

**Nene Chicken (Korean) Global:**
- Expanding to US, Southeast Asia
- Challenge: Korean product in Western market
- Strategy: Master franchisees in US, direct in Asia
- Product: Keeping Korean recipes, adapting portions/pricing

**7-Eleven Global:**
- Operates in 20+ countries
- Highly localized by country
- Model: Master franchising in most markets
- Product: Heavily adapted (US 7-Eleven very different from Thai 7-Eleven)
