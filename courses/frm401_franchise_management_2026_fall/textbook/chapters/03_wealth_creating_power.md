## Understanding the Motivations of an Entrepreneur to Become a Franchisor

### Boston Chicken Case Study

Boston Chicken opened its first restaurant in 1985. Eight years later (1993) it had an IPO.

**The growth strategy failure:**
- Transformed from restaurant franchise into more of a real estate mortgage company
- Signed up Financed Area Developers (FADs) who put up only 20% of required development costs
- Boston Markets provided the remaining loans
- 1997: Raised over $400 million in bond offerings and convertible debt
- FADs were losing increasing amounts of money: $156.5 million in 1997 alone

**Lesson**: The growth strategy is always dependent on the religious devotion to the business model and store-level economics. Success can be turned into dramatic failure when you forget that.

**Result**: Boston Chicken filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy and was purchased by McDonald's Corporation.

## Agency Problems and Administrative Efficiency

**The core agency question**: How does the business owner know that delegated tasks are performed exactly as he or she prefers?

**The management performance problem:**
- When organizations use hierarchical management (assistant managers → managers → general managers → district managers → national managers)
- Management performance becomes divorced from ownership
- Outlet managers in company-owned stores do not directly reap rewards or suffer consequences
- The feedback mechanism to reduce inappropriate management behaviors (shirking, free riding) becomes ineffective

**The monitoring cost problem:**
- Correcting this effect requires high system-monitoring costs
- May solve behavioral problems but restricts speed and extent of growth

## Buy a Franchise, or Launch a Standalone?

**Present Discounted Value (PDV) calculation (Figure 3-1):**

A franchise should be bought instead of an independent operation when the PDV of the income stream from the franchised outlet is greater than the PDV of the income stream from the standalone operation.

**Two key concepts in PDV:**
1. **Discount**: Reduction in future income based on chance we will not achieve expected results. A new business idea is often "discounted" up to 70%. A franchise lowers this rate because a "proven" business model has less risk.
2. **Income**: The income stream itself

**Additional benefits of franchising vs. standalone:**
- Economies of scale in marketing, purchasing, and property, plant, and equipment (PP&E)
- Having a recognized franchise brand results in greater income sooner

## How Do Franchisors Determine the Amount of Franchisee Fees and Royalties?

**Example calculation:**
- Franchise PDV = $1,000,000
- Standalone PDV = $700,000
- Theoretical maximum franchise fee + royalty = $299,999

**What part should be franchise fee vs. royalty?**
- Higher franchise fee = signal to potential franchisee that the franchise is of high quality
- Example: Many home-cleaning franchises have fees under $20,000; Merry Maids is $32,500
- Like the $10 vs. $20 bottle of wine: price signals quality

## Size and Risk Management

**General principle**: The larger the system becomes:
- The larger the economies of scale
- The lower the costs
- The higher the profits
- The less risky the franchise opportunity

**Why concentration matters:**
A system with 100 units spread across the country does NOT have the same market presence as a system with 100 units in one geographic region.

Concentration of outlets creates:
- Marketing economies of scale
- Increased administrative efficiency
- Therefore, the system with dominant market share in multiple markets is the LEAST risky

**TIP 3-2: Panera Bread — An Intelligent Growth Strategy**
- Originally Saint Louis Bread Company
- Expanded to 20 company-owned stores (1987-1993)
- Used St. Louis to discover economies of scale and refine SDS
- Then began franchising
- By early 2003: over 400 stores, $350 million in revenue
- Franchisee commitment for over 700 new outlets
- Using franchising, accelerated growth by 250%

## Balancing Company and Franchised Outlets

**Key statistics:**
- 13% of all outlets in franchise companies are company-owned stores
- For every store a franchisor develops, seven more franchised stores are built
- The average franchise system's investment capital can produce 7x the number of stores that a wholly owned independent chain can

**Example of leverage:**
- 4 corporate-owned units vs. 1 corporate + 3 franchised
- Former: High capital investment, retards growth
- Latter: Increased cash flow from franchise fees enables further expansion

**Diversification consideration:**
Franchisees who invest significant portions of net worth in a single outlet leave themselves exposed to high franchisor-specific risk (similar to an investor holding only one stock).

## Resource Constraints

**Capital influx from franchise fees:**
- Reduces the need for the franchisor to raise money to grow
- Conserves the franchisor's capital
- Establishes a distribution network more quickly than company-owned outlets

## Franchise Disclosure: An Insight into Individual Franchisor Health and Wealth

**Informational asymmetry**: The franchisor and franchisee do not have access to the same knowledge about the strengths and weaknesses of the franchise.

**The FTC's position**: Does not require franchisors to disclose franchisee earnings because the wide range of earnings makes the average relatively meaningless.

**The "lemons" problem (Akerlof, 1970)**: Good firms are pooled with bad firms; unable to attract capital at reasonable rates, good firms progressively disappear from the market.

**How franchisors signal superior quality**: By building their brand through successful stores.

## License Agreement: How the Franchise Shares Responsibilities and Wealth

**Six key sections of the license agreement (Table 3-2):**

| License Agreement Item | Impact | Marketplace Signal |
|------------------------|--------|-------------------|
| TERM: Length of contract in years | Defines years used in PDV calculation | Longer = positive |
| RENEWAL: Ability to add additional years | Increases number of years of potential income stream | Renewal = positive |
| FRANCHISE FEE: One-time upfront fee | Impacts initial investment; signals quality | Higher = positive |
| ROYALTY: % of outlet revenue paid to franchisor | Establishes linkage between franchisee success and franchisor success | Higher = positive |
| MARKETING FEE: % of revenue for marketing | Signals firm's commitment to building brand | Higher = positive |
| SUPPLY REQUIREMENTS: Rights/responsibilities in purchasing | Key to understanding economies of scale potential | National contracts with third-party vendors = positive |

| Factor | Average | Range |

| Percent outlets franchised | 74.25% | 12.1-100% |

**Key interpretations:**
- Top-performing franchisors have a MIX of company-owned and franchised outlets
- Company stores signal ability to do point-of-sale research and development
- Average marketing fee + total number of outlets explains the firm's marketing power

## Public Capitalization: An Expanded View of the Franchise Company

**Research finding**: From January 1990 through December 2001 (excluding 1999's "irrational Internet exuberance"), public franchisors as a group OUTPERFORMED the S&P 500 in total return to shareholders for 10 of 11 years.

**Strategic use of public capital:**
- May allow franchisor to lower the franchise fee
- Effect: Accelerates growth because lower startup costs attract more franchisees
- Can then back-load income stream with higher royalty

**Boston Chicken's mistake**: Derived more income from financing franchisee development (interest on loans) than from royalties → forgot about franchisee success.
