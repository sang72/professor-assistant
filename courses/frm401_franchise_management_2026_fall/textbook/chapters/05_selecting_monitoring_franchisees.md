## Opening Context

The average franchise license agreement term is almost 15 years. Choosing the right franchise and choosing the right franchisee are very difficult.

**Key challenge**: Creating the best business format with the highest potential is an important start, but:
- There are no perfect selection methods
- Monitoring behavior and performance becomes a high priority
- The most astute monitoring, control, and feedback loops are critical components of a high-quality and evolving SDS

---

## Selection and Monitoring: Franchising's Dual Challenge

**Why franchising minimizes risks of poorly chosen managers:**
- Goals of franchisor and franchisee are more congruent than in employer/employee relationships
- Franchisees share wealth with franchisors through profits
- If an employee steals $1.00 by pocketing customer payment: Employer loses entire $1.00
- If a franchisee steals $1.00: Franchisor loses only the royalty percentage (5-8% = $0.05-$0.08)
- Franchisees usually invest significant portion of personal wealth → have more to lose than the franchisor

**However, incentives are still not perfectly aligned:**
- Franchisors earn profits primarily through ROYALTY payments → incentive to maximize systemwide SALES
- Franchisees motivated by NET PROFITS → incentive to maximize profit through revenue enhancement AND cost-saving measures

**Classic example**: Franchisor wants to promote a discount to drive traffic; franchisee is concerned the discount will erode profits → "Only at participating stores" small print in advertisements

**Result**: Franchising does NOT eliminate the need for monitoring and controlling operational systems.

## The Tolerance Zone

**Definition**: An informal performance standard on many dimensions; allows for a margin of error or tolerance in daily activities.

**Key insight**: An hour spent in almost any franchisee outlet will uncover numerous breaches of the license agreement that could result in legal action. By the same token, a franchisee who strictly interprets the license agreement can easily highlight franchisor breaches. The result: a litigious environment that serves no one.

### Services That Define the Tolerance Zone

The following represent the franchisee's perception of dealings with the franchisor that constitute the tolerance zone:

- Access to franchisor management
- Accounting systems
- Credit policies
- Equipment
- Fair dealings in the relationship
- Financial management assistance
- Inventory assistance
- Local advertising
- Management systems
- Market information
- National advertising
- New store development
- New product development
- Support in operations
- Problem-solving assistance
- Promotions
- Purchasing
- Training

### Two Major Priority Categories (Table 5-1):

**Operations Interaction (Highly Sensitive):**
- Field personnel visits to outlets
- Formal outlet inspections
- Field personnel hours in outlets
- Field personnel response and documentation of franchisee concerns
- Field personnel response to emergencies
- Overall field personnel impact on franchisees success

**Marketing Interaction (Highly Sensitive):**
- Value of marketing materials
- Overall helpfulness of field marketer
- Timeliness of response to needs
- Collection and sharing marketing data/information
- Days per month franchisee spends with field marketing personnel
- Overall impact of field marketing personnel on the franchisee

**Key message to franchisors**: Prioritize efforts in OPERATIONS and MARKETING categories; franchisees will be forgiving when you perform less well in other areas.

## Monitoring and Controlling Execution of the SDS

**Why brand equity must be protected:**
Companies work to build brands and trademarks because they communicate to potential customers the promise of a certain level of quality. Failure to fulfill this promise erodes brand equity.

**TIP 5-1: Jiffy Lube — Monitoring and Control: Supporting the Brand**

The threat to the brand was significant if franchisees didn't perform to basic standards: 14-point service in 10 minutes.

Monitoring methods used:
- Field personnel brought STOPWATCHES into stores
- Point-of-sale computers TIMED entry into and exit from service bays
- Timing complaints from customers were quantified and shared within the franchise system
- The 10-minute, 14-point service message dominated advertising

**How to choose the right franchisee:**

Two schools of thought:
1. Do NOT want franchisees with related prior experience (might bring bad habits)
2. Prior related business experience IS essential

Allied Domecq VP states: "While there is not universal agreement on whether a franchisee must have experience in the field of the franchise, franchisors generally look for overall business experience."

**For new franchises:**
- Start by recruiting would-be entrepreneurs comfortable in an operating role with some experience in marketing
- Established franchisors should analyze most successful franchisees to develop a prospective franchisee profile

**Initial control**: Require franchisees to follow current operations manuals and subsequently updated manuals.

### Developing the Operations Manual:

The operations manual captures fundamental tasks of the SDS. It serves as both an information resource and a training tool.

Best practices for development:
- Document all functions, changes, and reasons for changes as you develop your SDS
- Write a manual for executing the SDS in the first location, detailing all actions required
- Keep in mind that new franchisees must be trained using this manual
- Use professional trainers and writers working together to produce it
- Produce in a binder that allows for frequent use and easy page changes

## Monitoring Methods

Multiple tools exist, typically used together:

### 1. Field Support

**Function**: Act as liaison between franchisor and franchisee.
- Main responsibilities: Communicating franchisee needs to company + ensuring company policies are upheld
- Similar to district or regional managers in non-franchise companies but with LESS direct enforcement authority
- Often function as internal auditors

**Rating systems**: Communicate to franchisees how they are performing relative to company standards:
- General Nutrition Centers: Product category comparisons
- Friendly's Restaurants: Day-part revenue comparisons
- Jiffy Lube: Labor hours per car shared nationally

**Consequences of underperformance**: Given time and support to improve; failure to improve risks franchise agreement termination.

**Critical quality factor**: Ratio of field support personnel to franchised stores — higher ratio = more effective monitoring BUT more expensive.

**Key finding**: Franchisees rate franchisors more favorably when they perceive a high degree of communications with field support personnel.

### 2. External Service Audits and Peer Review

**External service audits**: Contract with outside agency (economies of scale = less expensive on contractual basis).

**"Mystery shopping"**: Representative poses as customer without franchisee's prior knowledge.
- Advantages: Evaluates typical customer experience
- Limitations: Subjective; limited view of operations (no access to back-end operations like inventory)

**Peer review (more thorough and cost-effective)**:
- Conducted by and between franchisees in the same system
- Results are CONFIDENTIAL — not shared with franchisor
- Franchisees visit locations, work in the operation, review back office, interview employees and customers
- WHY IT WORKS: Each franchisee's performance affects other franchisees' brand value

**Example — Jack in the Box**: A single franchisee's failure affects ALL franchisees because customers rely on the brand name, not the local entrepreneur.

**Franchise Advisory Council (FAC)**: IFA recommends franchisors support establishment of FACs.
- FACs help establish criteria for both minimum AND exceptional performance
- Help build a line of communication with the franchisor

### 3. Analytical Tools

**Franchisor performance expectations** — using company stores as baseline:
- Compare actual franchisee results to expectations
- Develop "exception" reports for stores falling outside parameters

**Managing by exception also means investigating SUPERIOR performers:**

**McDonald's example**: A franchisee reported lower than normal labor expense.
- Investigation revealed a specialized intercom system for the drive-through that increased productivity
- Eventually became standard operating procedure in ALL restaurants
- Saved millions of dollars in labor costs systemwide

**Key principle**: Innovative work flow, specialized tools, and incentive systems developed by one franchisee can be analyzed by the franchisor and exported to the rest of the system.

### 4. Customer Feedback

**Best way to monitor franchisee performance**: Ask customers whether they are being well served.

**Current customers**: Provide information about how well a store meets expectations.

**Potential customers (secondary and tertiary markets)**: Survey customers who fit PTA description but don't currently frequent the franchise.
- If feedback identifies actionable issues, communicating that concerns have been addressed may increase customer base

## Monitoring and Controlling Financial Reporting

**Franchisor's primary concern**: Efficient reporting of sales (because compensation is based on percentage of sales).

**Three means for controlling financial reporting:**

### 1. Point-of-Sale Systems

**Basic function**: Capture sales data.

**Ancillary functions**: Collecting inventory data, employee information, customer and marketing data.

**Jiffy Lube example**:
- Customer's name and address captured
- Vehicle-specific information (year, make, model, mileage) recorded
- Employees who worked on vehicle tracked
- Productivity measured
- Inventory controls: correlation of oil filters used + gallons of used oil extracted

**Sophistication levels**: WAN-based systems to manual cash registers with journal tape — more sophisticated = harder for franchisees to misreport.

**Key questions for the franchise contract regarding point-of-sale:**
1. How often do franchisees have to report sales?
2. In what format must the reports be?
3. What supporting documentation must franchisees provide?
4. Does the franchisor have the right to perform sales audits?

### 2. Financial Reporting

**Budgets and forecasts** as monitoring tools:
- Make a good benchmark against which to compare actual financial results
- Significant deviations can identify stores warranting closer attention

**Critical requirement**: Expectations must be REALISTIC — company stores serve as laboratory for developing expectations, but differences between company stores and franchise (markets served, size, labor markets) must be factored in.

**TIP 5-2: Dunkin' Donuts — Reconciling Local and National Forecasts**

Process:
- Franchisees prepare sales and development plans
- Regional managers compile data into a master document
- Franchisor develops similar national forecast
- Two documents compared and eventually reconciled

Result: "Franchisor and franchisee goals are aligned. This process often creates stress but seldom engenders conflict."

When there is disagreement:
- Franchisor has contractual upper hand in development
- Franchisees dominate regarding individual store forecasts

Benefit: Actuals are compared to budgets that were created in partnership → more realistic strategic plan → growth is more effectively assessed.

### 3. Financial Audits

**Purpose**: Reserve the right to conduct an external audit when a franchisee is suspected of inaccurate reporting.

**Cost consideration**: Too expensive to use as SOLE monitoring tool; use as targeted investigation tool.

**Requirement**: Include in franchise contract the right to require an audit AND require franchisees to comply with requests for information from external auditors.

## Creating a Feedback Loop

**The bigger picture**: Monitoring and controlling franchisee behavior emphasizes inconsistencies, but the two parties have far more in common than they have as conflicts.

**Best interests of BOTH parties**: Brand equity is built and the end customer is better served.

**Great American Cookie Company (GACC) and Kampgrounds of America (KOA) examples:**

KOA:
- Franchise association had been requesting a central reservations system with a toll-free number for years
- Denied repeatedly by franchisor
- When rival camps implemented such systems, KOA had to play catch up
- When finally implemented: Immediate success

GACC:
- Franchisees alerted home office about packaging problem: Brownies in bags were too messy to eat
- Company switched to boxes
- Result: Sales increased

**Some franchisors fear franchise associations**: Fear of "union mentality"

**GACC CEO's perspective**: "The biggest benefit of the FAC is the teamwork and being able to work with franchisees towards a common goal. By helping franchisees organize a franchise advisory council and taking it seriously, franchisors can tap into a great resource."

## Conclusion

**The balance franchisors must strike**: Between policing franchisee behavior to protect their own interests AND partnering with franchisees to improve the system as a whole.

**Key principle**: The more that franchisees are treated as true partners, the less likely behaviors such as shirking and free riding will exist.

**TIP 5-3: Seven Ways to Build and Maintain a Lasting Franchise Relationship**
(Based on experience of Bob Rosenberg, former CEO of Dunkin' Donuts)

1. Franchisee attitudes are directly proportional to ROI and to the growth of profitability at the unit level
2. Many franchisees need to participate and self-actualize beyond store management — the best learn and grow as business leaders
3. Involvement in governance (advisory councils) can be beneficial, but it is very difficult for the franchisor to achieve the right balance between listening and leading
4. Transparency in contract and ongoing relationships is essential
5. Expectations must be set up front; franchisor and franchisee should share a common objective regarding unit profitability
6. New products and programs should be tested and meet financial tests before being rolled out
7. Franchising is a relationship business that takes continuity, face time, and delivering on promises to build trust
