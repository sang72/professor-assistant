### Main Content (32 minutes)

**Segment 1: Korean Franchise Legal History & Framework (8 minutes)**

**Before 2000: The Wild Years**

**Legal situation:** No specific franchise laws
- Franchisees had almost no legal protection
- Franchisors made profit promises with no regulation or verification
- Disputes went to civil courts (expensive, took 3-5 years)
- Many franchisees lost everything with no recourse

**Notable scandals (1990s):**
- "Success seminars" where franchisors promised 50M+ won monthly profit
- Franchisees invested 200-300M won, closed within 2 years
- Franchisors disappeared or claimed "market conditions" weren't predicted
- No government intervention; franchisees had no legal basis for recovery

**2000-2005: First Regulatory Framework**

**2000 Franchise Business Act (Enacted):**
- **Official name:** 가맹사업거래의 공정화에 관한 법률 (Law on Fair Transaction in Franchise Business)
- **Enforcing body:** 공정거래위원회 (Fair Trade Commission, FTC Korea)
- **Philosophy:** Protect franchisees from predatory franchisors while allowing legitimate franchising

**Initial protections (2000):**
1. Written franchise agreement required (Korean language)
2. Key terms must be disclosed (investment, fees, royalties, termination)
3. Franchisor must provide financial information
4. 14-day reflection period (franchisee can cancel after signing)
5. Prohibited practices: false earnings claims, hidden fees, unreasonable restrictions

**2005-2020: Strengthening and Refinement**

**2005 Amendment:** Added stronger enforcement
- Fair Trade Commission gained power to investigate violations
- Penalties increased (fines up to 500M won)
- Franchisee associations given legal standing to file complaints

**2010 Amendment:** Expanded protection
- "Good faith" principle added explicitly
- Franchisor cannot terminate without cause
- Franchisee has right to form associations
- Mediation process established for disputes

**2015 Amendment:** Focus on transparency
- Franchisors must provide audited financial statements (if publicly traded)
- Performance data of franchisor-owned units must be disclosed
- Royalty transparency (cannot change mid-contract without cause)
- Training obligations clarified

**2020-2026: Modern Korean Franchise Regulation**

**2020 Amendment (Major):**
- Cooling-off period: Franchisee can cancel within 7 days of signing (even if business is profitable, can exit)
- Financial transparency: Franchisors must submit financial statements to Fair Trade Commission
- Support obligations: Franchisor must commit in writing to specific support (training, marketing, operations)
- Termination notice: Extended from 3 to 6 months in many cases

**2023 Amendment (Recent):**
- Supply chain transparency: Franchisees have right to know franchisor's supplier contracts
- ESG requirements: Franchisors encouraged to disclose environmental/social practices
- Digital franchising: New rules for online/platform franchises

**2024-2025 Pending Changes (Under Discussion):**
- Franchisee right to audit franchisor's supplier contracts
- Mandatory arbitration for disputes (vs. litigation)
- Increased damages for bad faith termination
- Digital data protections for online franchisees

**Current Status (2026):** Korean law is rapidly evolving, now comparable to U.S. in many respects, though structure is different

**Segment 2: Korean Franchise Law - Key Protections (Current 2024-2026 Framework) (8 minutes)**

**Korean Franchise Law Requirements (가맹사업거래의 공정화에 관한 법률):**

**1. Franchise Contract Transparency (Article 5-6)**
- Franchise agreement must be written in Korean (no other language, translation required)
- Must be provided to prospective franchisee **before they make any financial commitment** (similar to U.S. 14-day rule)
- Cannot be modified without franchisee written agreement
- Must specify all material terms clearly
- Hidden terms/side agreements void and unenforceable

**2. Mandatory Disclosure of Information (Article 7)**

Franchisor must provide:

**Financial & Investment Information:**
- Initial investment required (equipment, real estate deposits, training, inventory) with itemized breakdown
- Ongoing fees: Royalties (%), marketing fees (%), technology fees (%), renewal fees
- Financing: Does franchisor finance part of investment? At what interest rate?
- Profit expectations: If franchisor claims earnings potential, must disclose assumptions and provide substantiation
- Royalty structure: How calculated? Based on gross or net sales? Audit rights?
- Supply requirements: Must franchisee buy from franchisor? At what markup? Required volume?

**Operational Information:**
- Support provided: Training (initial weeks? ongoing?), marketing support, operations assistance
- Quality standards: Store appearance, customer service, product standards
- Territory rights: Exclusive area? Can franchisor open another location nearby?
- Technology requirements: POS systems, data reporting, app requirements

**System Information:**
- Number of existing franchisees (by year, last 3 years)
- How many franchisees have closed/transferred (failure rate)
- Reasons for closures (sales issues, support issues, franchisee disputes)
- Names and contact info of franchisees (current and recent)

**Franchisor Information:**
- Operating history (how long has franchisor been in business?)
- Financial condition (profit/loss last 3 years)
- Litigation history (any disputes with franchisees?)
- Any regulatory actions (Fair Trade Commission investigations?)
- Management team and their franchise experience

**3. Good Faith and Fair Dealing (Article 3, 4)**

**Franchisor obligations:**
- Act in good faith in all dealings
- Cannot engage in deceptive practices
- Must provide promised support and training
- Cannot terminate without reasonable cause
- Cannot change material terms (royalty rate, supply sources) mid-contract without franchisee agreement
- Cannot use confidential franchisee information for competitive advantage

**Specific prohibited practices:**
- Making earnings claims without substantiation (most strictly enforced)
- Charging unexpected fees not disclosed in franchise agreement
- Failing to provide promised training or operations support
- Terminating franchisee to replace with company-owned location
- Requiring franchisee to purchase inflated-price inventory
- Preventing franchisee from joining franchisee association

**4. Franchisee Rights (Articles 12-14)**

- **Right to information:** Full financial access (can audit franchisor records)
- **Right to organize:** Can form franchisee associations to collectively negotiate
- **Right to dispute resolution:** Access to Fair Trade Commission mediation/arbitration (low-cost, 3-6 months)
- **Right to termination notice:** 6 months notice before non-renewal (up from 3 months historically)
- **Right to termination cause:** Franchisor must show legitimate business reason, not just desire to replace franchisee
- **Right to fair transfer:** If franchisee wants to sell franchise, franchisor cannot unreasonably withhold approval
- **Right to contract modification:** Cannot unilaterally change material terms

**5. Franchisor Obligations (Detailed by Law Section)**

**Training & Support (Article 8):**
- Initial training: Minimum 2-4 weeks (depending on complexity)
- Ongoing support: Regular visits, problem-solving, marketing assistance
- Quality assurance: Regular inspections, feedback, corrective measures

**Financial Transparency (Article 7):**
- Provide audited financial statements if franchisor revenue > 500M won annually
- Disclose supplier contracts (franchisees can request to review)
- Provide performance data for company-owned units (for comparison)

**System Integrity (Article 4):**
- Maintain consistent quality standards across all franchisees
- Cannot discriminate among franchisees (unless performance-based)
- Must keep system competitive (provide marketing support, maintain brand reputation)

**6. Remedies for Franchisees (Articles 15-17)**

**If franchisor violates law:**
- **Damages:** Sue for actual damages (lost profits, lost business value)
- **Cancellation:** Contract can be voided if franchisor fraud
- **Injunction:** Court can order franchisor to stop harmful practices
- **Government action:** Fair Trade Commission can investigate, fine franchisor up to 1B won
- **Administrative relief:** FTC can order refunds to franchisees, contract modifications

**Notable difference from U.S.:** No "treble damages" (3x multiplier). Only actual damages recoverable.

**7. Cooling-Off Period (2020 Amendment) - NEW**

**Critical protection:** Franchisee can cancel franchise agreement within **7 days of signing**, for any reason, with full refund.

- Exceptions: If franchisee has already started operations (after 7 days)
- Franchisor cannot waive this right (attempts to waive = void)
- Refund timeline: 30 days

**Impact:** This is more protective than U.S. law (which requires 14 days for FDD delivery, not 7 days for cancellation)

**Why this matters:** Franchisees often feel pressured during negotiations. 7-day cooling-off period allows reconsideration without penalty

**Segment 3: Korean vs. U.S. - Detailed Comparison (8 minutes)**

| **Dimension** | **U.S.** | **Korea** | **Implication** |
|---------------|---------|----------|-----------------|
| **Mandatory Disclosure Format** | FDD (23 standardized items) | No standardized format (~15 key items) | U.S. easier to compare franchises; Korea requires more customization |
| **Disclosure Timing** | 14 days before ANY payment | Before signing (no specific day rule) | U.S. stricter (protects against high-pressure sales) |
| **Cooling-Off Period** | None (14-day FDD period allows review only) | 7 days after signing, full refund | Korea more protective (can exit even if profitable) |
| **Earnings Claims** | Can make IF fully substantiated | Strongly discouraged (presumption of fraud) | U.S. allows substantiated claims; Korea presumes fraud |
| **Regulatory Structure** | Federal (FTC) + 14 states | One national law (Fair Trade Commission) | U.S. has state variation; Korea uniform nationally |
| **Registration** | Required in 14 states (CA, NY, IL, etc.) | Not required (no pre-approval system) | U.S. states pre-screen franchises; Korea does not |
| **Good Cause Termination** | Some states (CA, NY) require it | **Yes, universally required** | Korea more protective (uniform across nation) |
| **Damages Available** | Actual + treble damages (3x) | Actual damages only | U.S. damages much larger (3x multiplier) |
| **Litigation vs. Mediation** | Litigation common (expensive, years) | Mediation preferred (FTC supports, faster) | Korea system faster; U.S. system more adversarial |
| **Franchisee Associations** | Exist but limited legal power | Growing power (formal voice in regulations) | Korea associations increasingly influential |
| **Franchisor Liability** | Officers/executives can be personally liable | Company only (officers protected) | U.S. system targets individuals; Korea targets company |
| **Supply Chain Control** | Franchisor can require exclusive suppliers | Franchisor can require, but markup limits being discussed | Korea increasingly scrutinizing supplier markups |
| **Enforcement Mechanism** | FTC (powerful), private lawsuits (class actions) | Fair Trade Commission (moderate power), private lawsuits (individual) | U.S. enforcement stronger; Korea enforcement improving |

**Key Differences Narrative:**

**U.S. System Strengths:**
- **Powerful damages multiplier (treble damages):** Creates real financial incentive for franchisees to fight violations. Franchisor liability can exceed 3x actual loss.
- **State registration (14 states):** California, NY, Illinois regulators pre-screen franchises before any sales. Prevents obvious frauds from launching.
- **Decades of case law:** Courts have developed clear standards for what's acceptable. Franchisors know the rules.
- **Class action mechanism:** Powerful tool. 100+ franchisees can sue together, spreading legal costs.

**U.S. System Weaknesses:**
- **No cooling-off period:** Once you sign, you're committed (litigation is your only exit).
- **State variation:** What's legal in Texas may be illegal in California. Multi-state franchisors must navigate 14 different standards.
- **Litigation cost:** Class actions take 3-5 years, cost millions. Only largest violations are worth litigating.

**Korean System Strengths:**
- **Cooling-off period (7 days):** Most protective feature globally. Franchisee can exit even if business looks profitable.
- **Good cause requirement (universal):** Every state in U.S. doesn't require this; Korea universal (all territories protected).
- **Mediation/arbitration support:** FTC Korea actively promotes faster, cheaper dispute resolution. Much faster than U.S. litigation.
- **Simplicity:** One national law, uniform protections. No state variation confusion.
- **Franchisee associations:** Growing formal voice in regulation. Korean franchisee groups are increasingly influential.

**Korean System Weaknesses:**
- **No damages multiplier:** Only actual damages (no 3x). Reduces financial incentive to sue.
- **No registration system:** Unlike U.S. registration states, Korea doesn't pre-screen franchises. Frauds can launch more easily.
- **Younger system:** Case law is developing. Not as much historical precedent as U.S.
- **Limited enforcement resources:** FTC Korea has smaller budget than U.S. FTC. Fewer investigations.

**Summary Judgment:**

**U.S. and Korea now provide comparable overall protection, but through different mechanisms:**
- **U.S.:** Disclosure + state registration + damages multiplier + litigation
- **Korea:** Disclosure + cooling-off period + good cause requirement + mediation

**For franchisees:** Korea's 7-day cooling-off is more powerful than U.S.'s treble damages (because you can exit before litigation). But U.S. damages multiplier provides larger final recovery.

**For franchisors:** U.S. registration states create barrier to entry. Korean cooling-off period creates customer acquisition cost (7 days to lose franchisee).

**Segment 4: Recent Korean Law Developments & 2024-2026 Specific Changes (8 minutes)**

**2023-2024 Regulatory Evolution:**

**Already Implemented (Active Today):**

1. **Cooling-Off Period (2020, Strengthened 2023):**
   - 7 days to cancel after signing, full refund
   - No questions asked
   - Franchisor cannot waive this right
   - Used by 10-15% of franchisees annually (significant impact)

2. **Financial Transparency Enhancement (2024):**
   - Franchisors > 500M won revenue must file annual financial statements with FTC Korea
   - Franchisees can request to review franchisor's audited statements
   - Supplier contracts must be disclosed (franchisees can audit markup)
   - Implementation date: January 2024 (current)

3. **Support Requirements Formalization (2024-2025):**
   - Franchisors must commit in writing to specific support levels
   - Training hours minimum (e.g., "4 weeks initial + 12 visits/year ongoing")
   - Marketing support commitment (e.g., "2% of franchise fees allocated to system marketing")
   - Operations assistance: Response time to franchisee requests (e.g., "24-hour response to urgent issues")
   - Non-compliance = grounds for damages claim

4. **Extended Termination Notice (2023):**
   - Changed from "3 months" to "6 months" (in most categories)
   - Gives franchisee time to sell, transition, or recover investments
   - Non-renewal must include written reason (must be legitimate)

5. **Supply Chain Transparency Initiative (2024 Guideline):**
   - FTC Korea issued guidance: Franchisors should disclose supplier markups
   - Recommended maximum markup: 15-20% (guidelines, not mandatory yet)
   - Franchisees can challenge excessive markups as unfair practice
   - Still evolving, but direction is clear

**2024-2025 Under Discussion (Proposed Changes):**

1. **Franchisee Right to Audit (High Priority)**
   - Franchisees would have legal right to audit franchisor's supplier contracts
   - Purpose: Verify markup rates, prevent secret arrangements
   - Expected implementation: 2025 amendment

2. **Mandatory Arbitration / Mediation-First (Medium Priority)**
   - FTC proposing that disputes go to mediation first (before litigation)
   - Would require 3-month mediation period
   - Only litigate if mediation fails
   - Would dramatically reduce court backlog, speed resolution

3. **Increased Damages for Bad Faith Termination (Medium Priority)**
   - Proposing "2x damages" multiplier (similar concept to U.S. treble damages)
   - Would apply to wrongful terminations without legitimate cause
   - Currently being debated by franchisee associations and FTC

4. **Digital Franchise Regulations (High Priority)**
   - New rules for online/platform franchises (Coupang-style, fast-delivery models)
   - Data ownership questions (who owns customer data?)
   - Cybersecurity requirements (what data protection franchisee must provide)
   - Expected implementation: 2025-2026

**Regional/Municipal Variations (Emerging Trend):**

Unlike U.S. (national FTC law) and Korea (national Fair Trade Act), some Korean cities are adding local protections:

- **Seoul:** Proposed additional disclosure requirements for food franchises
- **Busan:** Proposed data privacy protections for delivery franchises
- **Incheon:** Proposed environmental disclosure requirements

These are not yet law, but direction of regulatory evolution.

**Still Gaps vs. U.S. (As of 2026):**

- **No registration system:** Unlike California/NY pre-approval, Korea doesn't pre-screen franchises before launch
- **No damages multiplier (yet):** U.S. treble damages (3x) create larger final awards. Korea actual damages only (though 2x multiplier proposed for 2025)
- **Smaller enforcement budget:** FTC Korea has ~50 franchise investigators; U.S. FTC has 200+ (U.S. has more federal resources)
- **Less developed case law:** U.S. has 40+ years of franchise case law; Korea has ~20 years (gap narrowing)

### Examples & Case Studies (13 minutes)

**CASE 1: GS25 Mid-Contract Royalty Increase Dispute (2023)**

**Facts:**
- GS25 signed 5-year franchise agreements with 300+ franchisees at 5% royalty rate
- After 2 years, GS25 unilaterally increased royalty to 6% (claiming "increased marketing costs")
- Franchisees discovered increase through royalty statements
- Increase not disclosed in original franchise agreement
- Affected franchisees: ~200 (out of 300)

**Legal Challenge:**
- Franchisee association filed complaint with FTC Korea
- Also filed class action in Seoul District Court
- Legal basis: Article 4 of Fair Trade Act (Good Faith requirement)
- Argument: Mid-contract changes violate good faith; changes require franchisee agreement

**Franchisor Response:**
- GS25 argued: "Royalty clause allowed rate adjustment for inflation/inflation"
- Produced economic analysis showing increased costs
- Claimed franchisees agreed to "reasonable adjustments"

**Court Decision (2023 Seoul District Court):**
- **Ruled for franchisees**
- Royalty increase violated good faith requirement
- Even if contract had adjustment clause, must notify franchisees and allow them to refuse
- Franchisees who rejected rate increase should be able to exit with minimal penalty
- Those who accepted: Can recover difference between 5% and 6% (class action fund established)

**Award & Relief:**
- GS25 must refund 1 year of excess royalties (1-2B won total)
- Must revert to 5% for remaining contract term (or allow exit)
- Must amend all franchise agreements to specify approval required for rate changes

**FTC Korea Action (Parallel):**
- Fined GS25 500M won for unfair practice
- Required to notify all franchisees of ruling
- Required to implement governance mechanism: 30-day franchisee notice + approval required for material changes

**Lesson:** Korean "good faith" requirement is powerful. Even contractual rights can't be exercised arbitrarily.

**CASE 2: Chicken Franchise False Earnings Claims & Cooling-Off (2024)**

**Facts:**
- Franchise brand "X Chicken" promised: "Franchisees typically earn 200M won annually"
- Claimed this was based on "successful franchisees" (selected example)
- Provided no statistical substantiation (didn't disclose failure rate)
- Reality: Average franchisee earned 50-80M won; 40% closed within 3 years

**Fraud Discovery:**
- Franchisee group (20 franchisees) filed complaint with FTC Korea
- Requested documents showing "typical earnings"
- X Chicken could not produce substantiation (no systematic data collection)
- FTC Korea investigation found: Claims were exaggerated 3-4x above actual earnings

**Legal Challenge:**
- Franchisees sued for fraud
- Claimed they relied on earnings claims when deciding to franchise
- Claimed they would not have franchised at truthful earnings level
- Some franchisees invoked 7-day cooling-off period (even months after signing)

**Franchisor Response:**
- X Chicken argued: "Those are successful franchisees. Some franchisees earn less, some earn more."
- Argued: "We didn't make specific promise. 'Typically earn' is aspirational."

**Court & FTC Decision (2024):**
- **FTC Korea enforcement action:** Found violation of disclosure requirements
- **Private lawsuit:** 15 franchisees received damages (average 50M won each = 750M won total)
- **Key finding:** Korean law presumes earnings claims are fraudulent if unsupported by data
- **Ruling:** "Typical earnings" must be median (50th percentile), not cherry-picked best cases

**FTC Korea Penalty:**
- Fine: 800M won
- Requirement: Retrain all franchisee recruiters on proper disclosure
- Monitoring: Annual FTC audits for 3 years

**Cooling-Off Impact:**
- Several franchisees used 7-day cooling-off period after discovering false claims
- X Chicken attempted to deny refunds (claimed "cooling-off period expired")
- Court ruled: Cooling-off period must be honored even if franchisees knew actual earnings (it's automatic right)

**Lesson:** Korean law is very strict on earnings claims. Burden is on franchisor to prove claims, not franchisee to disprove. Cooling-off period is absolute.

**CASE 3: CU Operational Support Inadequacy Dispute (2024)**

**Facts:**
- CU franchise agreement promised: "Regular training and operational support"
- CU reduced training budget by 40% due to cost-cutting
- Resulted in: Fewer training sessions, delayed response to franchisee requests, reduced field support
- Franchisees complained: Support quality deteriorated significantly

**Franchisee Challenge:**
- Franchisee association argued: CU violated Article 8 (Support Obligations)
- "Regular training" is now defined in 2024 amendment as minimum 12 field visits/year + monthly training
- CU provided only 6 field visits/year + quarterly training (below new standard)

**Franchisor Response:**
- CU argued: "Support obligations are general, not specific. We provided what we could afford."
- "Franchisees should understand cost-cutting measures during economic slowdown"
- "Support is provided, just not at previous levels"

**Legal & Regulatory Resolution (2024):**
- FTC Korea investigation found: CU violated 2024 support amendment
- Support requirements must be maintained at minimum levels specified in law
- Even during economic downturns, franchisor cannot unilaterally reduce support
- Must either: (a) maintain support, or (b) release franchisees from obligation and renegotiate

**Award & Remedy:**
- Franchisees received modest damages (not full lost profits, just partial compensation)
- CU required to restore support levels to promised standards
- Required to reimburse some franchisees for alternative training they purchased
- Required to publish "Support Commitment Charter" and post in all stores

**Lesson:** Korean law now requires substantive franchisor support. "Good faith" support obligation is measurable and enforceable.

---

**CASE 4: Digital Franchise Closure - Data Ownership (2024-2025)**

**Facts:**
- Fast-delivery franchise model (similar to Coupang)
- Franchisor collected customer data through app
- After 1 year, franchisor shut down the app/system
- Franchisees lost customer database (couldn't contact repeat customers)
- Franchisees lost ability to operate independently

**Issue:**
- Franchise agreement was silent on data ownership
- Franchisor claimed: "All customer data is our property. App shutdown terminates all franchisee access."
- Franchisees claimed: "Customer data developed through our labor. We should own it or have access."

**FTC Korea Response (First Digital Franchise Case):**
- No precedent for this situation
- FTC Korea issued **guidance** (not binding law, but directional):
  - Franchisor ownership of customer data is acceptable
  - BUT: Franchisor cannot unilaterally revoke franchisee access without notice and transition period
  - Franchisees should have 30-60 days to export customer data if system shuts down
- Proposed rule: Digital franchise agreements must address data ownership explicitly

**Current Status (2025):**
- FTC Korea is drafting formal regulations on digital franchises (expected 2025 law)
- Will likely require: Data ownership disclosure, access rights, wind-down procedures
- This case is still pending, but guidance is shaping new law

**Lesson:** Korean law evolving to address new franchise models. Digital/platform franchises will face additional disclosure requirements.

**COMPARISON: Same Cases in U.S. Context**

**Case 1 (GS25 royalty increase):**
- U.S.: Would depend on state. California good faith = similar result. Texas = no protection (contract silence allows increase). Wisconsin = franchisor relationship law = similar to Korea.
- Damages: U.S. could award treble damages (3x excess royalties). Korea = actual damages only.

**Case 2 (X Chicken earnings fraud):**
- U.S.: FTC would enforce nationally. FDD Item 19 violation = significant liability. Class actions common.
- Damages: U.S. class action could exceed 2-3B won (treble damages multiplier). Korea award was ~750M won.

**Case 3 (CU support):**
- U.S.: Only Wisconsin/Minnesota have franchise relationship laws requiring good faith support. Most states = contract governs. CU could reduce support if no specific contract language.
- Korea more protective here.

**Case 4 (Digital franchise data):**
- U.S.: No specific law yet. Would depend on general contract law. FTC considering digital franchise regulation but not yet enacted.
- Korea moving faster to regulate emerging franchise models.

**Summary:** Both systems protect franchisees, but U.S. provides larger damages; Korea provides more uniform protection (no state variation) and faster resolution (mediation vs. litigation).

## SECTION 4: KEY NOTES & DISCUSSION GUIDES

**Critical Concepts to Emphasize:**

- **Korea's cooling-off period (7 days) is arguably more protective than U.S. treble damages:** Franchisee can exit for any reason within 7 days. U.S. treble damages require winning a lawsuit (3-5 years, millions in legal fees).

- **Good faith requirement is broad & powerful:** U.S. only requires this in some states (WI, MN, CA). Korea requires it universally. Courts interpret strictly.

- **No registration system (yet):** Unlike California/New York pre-approval, Korea allows franchises to launch immediately. Fraud detection happens after the fact (through complaints). This is a gap vs. U.S.

- **Recent case law shows aggressive enforcement (2023-2024):** GS25, X Chicken, CU cases show FTC Korea and courts are taking franchisee rights seriously. Cases like this were rare 5-10 years ago.

- **Digital franchise regulation is coming (2025 amendment):** Korea is ahead of U.S. in anticipating digital franchise issues. By 2025, Korea will likely have specific data-ownership, cybersecurity rules that U.S. hasn't yet enacted.

- **Damages are lower in Korea (no multiplier yet):** U.S. treble damages = potential 3x award. Korea = actual damages (though 2x multiplier proposed for 2025). This means U.S. lawsuits are more lucrative, but harder to win.

**Classroom Discussion Questions:**

1. **If you were a franchisee, which would you prefer: U.S. 7-day cooling-off period or 3x damages after suing?** Why?

2. **Why do you think Korea doesn't have a registration system like California?** What are the tradeoffs?

3. **GS25 case (royalty increase): U.S. courts would allow this in most states if contract language permits. Korean court disallowed it based on "good faith." Which approach is fairer?**

4. **X Chicken case: FTC Korea fined the franchisor 800M won for earnings fraud. Fair punishment? Too light? Too harsh?**
