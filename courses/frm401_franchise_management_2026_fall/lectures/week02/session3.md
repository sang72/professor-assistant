## SECTION 2: FULL LECTURE SCRIPT

Welcome to the final session of Week 2. We have defined franchising. We have compared it to other growth models. Today, we examine two real cases: Krispy Kreme and Snip-Its. These cases show franchising in action.

Let me start with Krispy Kreme. This is an American donut company founded in 1937 by Vernon Rudolph in Winston-Salem, North Carolina. For decades, Krispy Kreme operated only in the Southeast. It was a regional brand. But in the 1990s, Krispy Kreme began to expand nationally and internationally. How? Through franchising.

Why did Krispy Kreme succeed as a franchise? First, the product is distinctive. Glazed donuts. They are famous. Customers recognize the brand. Second, the Service Delivery System is clear. Donuts are made fresh using specific recipes and equipment. The franchisor provides the recipes, the equipment, the supply chain. The franchisee operates the location. Third, customer demand is predictable.

But Krispy Kreme also faced challenges. Founder Vernon Rudolph was very particular about quality. He was reluctant to franchise widely because he feared loss of control. It took decades before Krispy Kreme embraced franchising fully.

[TIME: ~15 min]

---

Now let me tell you about Snip-Its. This is a children's hair salon started by entrepreneur Mandy Gatewood in Maryland in the 1990s. The concept is simple: a hair salon designed specifically for children. Colorful, playful, with entertainment while kids get haircuts.

Snip-Its franchises because the model is teachable. Training stylists to work with children is different. Snip-Its has developed techniques to make haircuts fun for kids. The interior design is consistent. The pricing is standardized. Franchisees buy into this system.

Why did Snip-Its franchise instead of opening corporate locations? Capital. The founder did not have capital to open 50 locations nationally. Franchising allowed growth without the capital burden.

[SHOW SLIDE 2: Snip-Its Model]

[TIME: ~30 min]

---

Let me extract lessons from these cases. First, clear Service Delivery Systems are critical. Both Krispy Kreme and Snip-Its have documented, teachable systems. Second, brand recognition matters. Krispy Kreme is famous for glazed donuts. Snip-Its is famous for kid-friendly haircuts. Third, franchisors must balance control and autonomy. They must enforce standards but allow franchisees some flexibility for local markets.

[ASK STUDENTS: What questions do you have about these cases? Why do you think Krispy Kreme was slow to franchise?]

[PAUSE — wait for student responses]

Good observations. Krispy Kreme was slow because the founder valued control. But this reluctance limited growth. Eventually, Krispy Kreme realized that franchising allowed scale without sacrificing quality, as long as the system was clear.

Thank you all. We have completed Week 2. Next week, we move to Chapter 2 and the Franchise Relationship Model. That framework will help us understand the deep dynamics between franchisors and franchisees. See you next week.
