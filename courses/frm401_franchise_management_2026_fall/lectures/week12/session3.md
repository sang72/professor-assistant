### Main Content (32 minutes)

**Segment 1: Dispute Resolution Mechanisms (8 minutes)**

**Level 1: Negotiation (Informal)**
- Franchisor and franchisee discuss directly
- Try to reach mutually acceptable solution
- No legal involvement
- Fastest, least expensive
- Success rate: 30-40% (many disputes can't be resolved this way)

**Level 2: Mediation**
- Neutral third party (mediator) facilitates discussion
- Mediator doesn't decide; guides parties toward agreement
- Both must agree to participate
- Korean law now requires attempt at mediation
- Cost: 5-10M won
- Success rate: 50-60%

**Level 3: Arbitration**
- Neutral arbitrator hears both sides
- Makes binding decision (arbitrator is judge)
- Faster than litigation
- Confidential (unlike court cases)
- Korean law allows arbitration; some franchise contracts require it
- Cost: 20-50M won
- Success rate: 100% (someone wins, someone loses)

**Level 4: Litigation (Court)**
- Formal lawsuit filed
- Courts apply franchise law and contract
- Lengthy process (1-3 years common)
- Public proceeding (transparent but reputational impact)
- High cost: 50M-500M+ won depending on damages claimed
- Result: Court judgment, enforceable

**Segment 2: Korean Dispute Resolution Process (8 minutes)**

**Step 1: Attempt Negotiation (Informal)**
- Franchisor and franchisee try to resolve directly
- If successful, done
- If not, move to formal process

**Step 2: Complaint to Fair Trade Commission (FTC-equivalent)**
- Korea has 공정거래위원회 (Fair Trade Commission - FTC)
- Franchisee can file complaint
- FTC investigates if franchisor violated franchise law
- No cost to franchisee
- FTC can order franchisor to:
  - Stop unfair practice
  - Compensate franchisee
  - Modify contract

**Step 3: Mediation (Now Required by Law)**
- If FTC investigation suggests violation, mediation required
- FTC-approved mediators facilitate
- Goal: Reach settlement
- If successful, case resolved
- If not, proceed to arbitration/litigation

**Step 4: Arbitration (Preferred in Korea)**
- Franchisee or franchisor can request arbitration
- Korea has franchise arbitration system
- Arbitrator reviews evidence, makes binding decision
- Faster than court (3-6 months typical)
- Cost shared by both parties

**Step 5: Litigation (Last Resort)**
- Either party can file lawsuit
- District court hears case
- Appeals possible to higher court
- Lengthy (1-3 years)
- Based on franchise law + contract + general law

**Segment 3: Real Cases - Dispute Resolution in Action (8 minutes)**

**Case 1: Korean Coffee Franchise Royalty Dispute (2023)**

**Facts:**
- Franchisor raised royalty from 5% to 6% unilaterally
- Franchisee refused to pay additional royalty
- Franchisee claimed contract violation

**Dispute Resolution Process:**
1. Negotiation failed (franchisor refused change)
2. Franchisee filed FTC complaint
3. FTC investigated, found royalty increase violated good faith
4. Mediation attempted, franchisor refused to settle
5. Arbitration filed
6. **Arbitrator ruled:** Royalty increase invalid; franchisor must refund excess
7. Franchisor appealed to court
8. **Court upheld:** Arbitrator decision upheld

**Timeline:** 18 months total
**Cost to Franchisee:** ~30M won (arbitration + legal fees)
**Outcome:** Franchisee won; franchisor refunded excess royalties to all franchisees network-wide

**Lesson:** Even if negotiation fails, legal system provides remedy.

**Case 2: U.S. Franchise Misrepresentation Lawsuit (2024)**

**Facts:**
- Fast-casual restaurant franchisor claimed franchisees earn "$100K+ profit annually"
- Reality: 30% of franchisees lost money in first 3 years
- Franchisee class action filed

**Dispute Resolution Process:**
1. Negotiation (franchisor rejected claims)
2. Class certification approved (multiple franchisees)
3. Discovery (evidence gathering)
4. Settlement reached: Franchisor agreed to:
   - Refund initial franchise fees for 200 franchisees ($7M)
   - Correct earnings disclosure in FDD
   - Pay plaintiff attorneys' fees ($1M)

**Timeline:** 2 years
**Outcome:** Franchisees recovered damages; franchisor forced to change practices

**Segment 4: Legal Framework Application to Franchising (8 minutes)**

**When to Sue (Franchisee Perspective):**

You should consider legal action if:
1. **Clear contract violation** (franchisor broke written agreement)
2. **Fraud/misrepresentation** (franchisor lied about earnings, support, etc.)
3. **Unfair termination** (terminated without good cause)
4. **Inadequate support** (franchisor failed to provide promised training, marketing, operations support)
5. **Royalty overcharge** (franchisor collected unauthorized fees)

**When NOT to Sue:**
1. Market changed (location became less viable) - not franchisor's fault
2. You didn't follow system - franchisee fault
3. Operational mistakes - your responsibility
4. Minor disputes (<5M won) - legal costs exceed recovery

### Examples & Case Studies (13 minutes)

**Case Study: Korean Chicken Franchise Legal Battle (2024-2025)**

**Timeline:**

**Month 1:** Franchisees notice declining profits (rent inflation)
**Month 2:** Franchisees ask franchisor for support/royalty reduction
**Month 3:** Franchisor refuses, claims franchisees need to improve efficiency
**Month 4:** Franchisees file FTC complaint alleging "inadequate support"
**Month 5-6:** FTC investigates; franchisor provides limited documentation of support
**Month 7:** Mediation attempted; franchisor refuses major concessions
**Month 8:** Franchisees file class arbitration for damages
**Month 10-12:** Arbitration hearing; both sides present evidence
**Month 13:** Arbitrator rules: Franchisor provided adequate support per contract; franchisees' struggles due to market conditions

**Outcome:** Franchisor won; franchisees pay their legal costs (~30M won)

**Lesson:** Even when franchisees believe they have a valid claim, outcome depends on evidence and contract terms. Legal battle is expensive and risky.

## SECTION 4: KEY NOTES

- **Prevention is best**: Good contracts and good faith relationships prevent most disputes
- **Escalation is expensive**: Legal costs can be 50-100M+ won
- **Settlement often best outcome**: Both parties lose in litigation
- **Korean system is improving**: FTC role, mediation requirement, arbitration option make access to justice better
- **Documentation critical**: Evidence determines outcomes; keep records of all franchisor-franchisee communications
