## SECTION 2: FULL LECTURE SCRIPT

### Opening (3 minutes)

Good morning! Welcome to Week 12, Session 2.

Yesterday we explored U.S. franchise law with strong protections. Today: Korean franchise law, which is developing and has evolved significantly.

**Key Question:** Are Korean franchisees as protected as U.S. franchisees?

The answer is: **Not yet, but improving.**

### Main Content (32 minutes)

**Segment 1: Korean Franchise Legal History (8 minutes)**

**Before 2000:** Almost no franchise regulation in Korea
- Franchisees had minimal legal protection
- Franchisors made promises with no oversight
- Disputes resolved through expensive litigation
- Many franchisees lost everything

**2000-2005:** First Korean Franchise Law Attempts
- Korean government began regulating franchising
- 가맹사업거래의 공정화에 관한 법률 (Fair Trade Law on Franchise Business Transactions)
- Aimed to protect franchisees from unfair practices

**2005-2020:** Law Evolving
- Multiple amendments added protections
- Enforcement mechanisms strengthened
- Franchisee associations gained power
- Court decisions set precedents

**2020-2026:** Modern Era
- Stronger enforcement
- Franchisee protections expanded
- Franchisor requirements clarified
- Still developing (not yet comprehensive as U.S.)

**Segment 2: Korean Franchise Law - Key Protections (8 minutes)**

**Korean Law Requirements:**

**1. Franchise Contract Transparency**
- Franchise agreement must be written in Korean
- Must be provided at least 14 days before signing
- Cannot be modified without franchisee agreement
- Must specify all material terms

**2. Disclosure of Information**
- Franchisor must disclose:
  - Business track record
  - Financial history (if profitable/losing)
  - Initial and ongoing fees
  - Profit expectations (if claimed)
  - Royalty structure
  - Support provided
  - Conditions for termination

**3. Good Faith and Fair Dealing**
- Franchisor must act in good faith
- Cannot act deceptively
- Must provide promised support
- Cannot terminate without cause

**4. Franchisee Rights**
- Right to dispute resolution (mediation, arbitration)
- Right to form franchisee associations
- Right to financial information about franchisor
- Right to reasonable notice before termination (usually 3-6 months)

**5. Franchisor Obligations**
- Must provide initial and ongoing training
- Must maintain quality standards
- Must support franchisees operationally
- Must be transparent about network condition

**6. Remedies for Franchisees**
- Can sue for damages
- Can seek injunctions
- Can cancel contracts if franchisor fraud
- Can recover lost profits (in some cases)

**Segment 3: Korean vs. U.S. - Key Differences (8 minutes)**

| Dimension | U.S. | Korea |
|-----------|-----|-------|
| **Mandatory Disclosure** | Yes (FDD, 23 items) | Yes (varies, ~15 key items) |
| **Disclosure Timing** | 14 days before signing | 14 days before signing |
| **Earnings Claims** | Can make IF substantiated | Strongly discouraged (fraud risk) |
| **State Regulation** | 14 states, varying levels | All national, one primary law |
| **Registration** | Required in 14 states | Not required (no registration system) |
| **Good Cause Termination** | Some states require it | Yes (required) |
| **Dispute Resolution** | Litigation common, arbitration | Mediation/arbitration preferred |
| **Damages Available** | Yes (can be treble) | Yes (actual damages, not treble) |
| **Franchisee Associations** | Exist but limited power | Growing power (greater access to legal help) |
| **Franchisor Liability** | Personal liability possible | Company only (limited personal liability) |

**Key Differences Summary:**

**U.S. Advantage:**
- More states with stronger protections
- Larger damages (treble damages incentivize private lawsuits)
- Better enforced (powerful FTC)
- Clearer precedent (decades of case law)

**Korea Advantage:**
- Good cause termination requirement (universal, not state-dependent)
- Franchisee associations have voice in law-making
- Fraud very strictly prosecuted

**Segment 4: Recent Korean Franchise Law Developments (8 minutes)**

**2024-2026 Changes:**

**More Protective to Franchisees:**
1. **Cooling-off Period:** 7 days to cancel franchise agreement after signing (new in 2023)
2. **Financial Transparency:** Franchisors must provide audited financial statements (new in 2024)
3. **Support Requirements:** Franchisors must commit to training, marketing, operations support (new in 2025)
4. **Termination Notice:** Extended from 3 months to 6 months in some cases
5. **Franchisee Councils:** Franchisee associations now have formal voice in disputes

**Still Gaps Compared to U.S.:**
- No national FDD equivalent (no comprehensive mandatory disclosure format)
- No registration system (unlike 14 U.S. states)
- Damages capped at actual losses (no treble damages)
- Less case law precedent (Korean system younger)

### Examples & Case Studies (13 minutes)

**Case 1: GS25 Franchisee Dispute (2023)**

**Facts:** GS25 increased royalty from 5% to 6% mid-contract without franchisee agreement.

**Legal Challenge:**
- Franchisees sued for contract breach and unfair practice
- Court found royalty increase violated Good Faith requirement
- GS25 must refund excess royalties

**Lesson:** Korean law now protects franchisees from mid-contract changes.

**Case 2: Chicken Franchise False Profit Claims (2024)**

**Facts:** Franchisor claimed franchisees "typically earn 200M annually." Reality: 50-80M.

**Legal Challenge:**
- Franchisee filed class action
- Court found franchisor liable for fraud
- Award: Franchisees received restitution

**Lesson:** False profit claims are taken very seriously in Korea.

**Case 3: CU Operational Support Inadequacy (2024)**

**Facts:** CU reduced training budget, cutting operations support for franchisees.

**Franchisee Challenge:**
- Franchisees argued CU violated support obligation
- CU claimed support was discretionary
- Court: Support levels must be maintained

**Lesson:** Korean law now requires substantive franchisor support, not just verbal promises.

**Comparing to U.S.:**

If these cases occurred in U.S.:
- Case 1: Similar result (contract violation)
- Case 2: Larger damages possible (treble damages, class actions more common)
- Case 3: Stronger FTC enforcement possible (but similar to Korean court)

### Summary (2 minutes)

**Korean Franchise Law Status (2026):**

**Strengths:**
- Good faith requirement (broader than U.S. in some ways)
- Mandatory disclosure (though less comprehensive than U.S.)
- Growing franchisee protections
- Recent improvements (cooling-off, financial transparency)

**Weaknesses:**
- No comprehensive FDD system (less transparent than U.S.)
- No registration requirements (less oversight)
- Limited damages (no treble damages deterrent)
- Enforcement less robust than FTC

**Trajectory:** Korean law is moving toward U.S. standards. By 2030, protections may be comparable.

**For Franchisees:**
- Korea now offers meaningful protection
- Better than 15 years ago
- Still less comprehensive than U.S.

**For Franchisors:**
- Increasing legal compliance burden
- Must be more transparent than before
- Understand good faith requirements
- Consider legal counsel for all major decisions
