## Opening Case: 7-Eleven

The Southland Ice Company opened its first convenience store in 1927 and called it a Tote'm. In 1946 the company changed its store name to 7-Eleven to reflect its operating hours.

**International franchising timeline:**
- 1964: Entered franchising
- 1968: First U.S. area licensing agreement
- 1971: First international licensing agreement (with Mexico)
- **1991**: Purchased by its Japanese franchisee, the Ito-Yokado Company (IY Group)
- 2003: Almost 80% of 7-Eleven's 24,400 stores located OUTSIDE the United States

**Lesson**: Franchising is not limited to U.S. borders, and a franchisee can even grow large enough to purchase the franchisor.

---

## Why International Franchising Has Taken On New Meaning

**Before 1990**: U.S. franchise presence abroad meant a McDonald's in Tokyo or one next to the Spanish Steps in Rome.

**By 2003**: Thousands of U.S. franchises in food, retail, and services have moved into emerging markets around the globe.

**Reasons:**
1. U.S. franchise market (set to reach $1 trillion in 2003) may be nearing saturation for existing franchises
2. "Globalization" — opening of trade and international commerce throughout the 1990s
3. Franchising simply WORKS — a proven business model that has generated wealth in the United States

**Arthur Andersen Report (1995)**: Two-thirds of all U.S. franchisors who decided to expand abroad did so based on FIRST CONTACT from a prospective FOREIGN franchisee.

**Important caution**: Entering a foreign country requires significantly more time, capital, and other resources than setting up another domestic franchise.

## Franchising in a Foreign Country: Using the International Franchising Checklist

### The 9-Criterion Checklist (Table 11-1)

For each criterion, assess whether it is:
- **Prohibitive**: Significantly reduces chance of opening stores profitably
- **Restrictive**: Extends the time to open profitable outlets
- **Conducive**: Adds to the speed of opening new stores and/or profitability

---

### Criterion 1: Legal and Regulatory Environment

**Key laws to examine:**
- Monetary exchange regulations
- Employment laws (minimum wage, maternity/paternity leave)
- Intellectual property protection (patents, trademarks, copyrights)

**Assessments:**
- Prohibitive: Country forbids franchising or severely restricts currency exchange
- Restrictive: Allows franchising but has non-existent or unenforced IP laws
- Conducive: Allows franchising by law AND enforces IP laws to international standards

**Resources:**
- UK: www.patent.gov.uk
- USA: www.uspto.gov
- Check if country is a WTO member (all members must sign the TRIPS agreement)
- WTO website: www.wto.org
- U.S. Dept. of State Country Commercial Guides: www.export.gov

**Important caution**: WTO membership alone doesn't guarantee well-enforced IP laws.
- China and Mexico: WTO members with major grievances filed against them for IP piracy

---

### Criterion 2: Import Rules and Customs Procedures

**Key considerations:**
- Import duties on raw materials and supplies
- Complexity and consistency of customs procedures

**McDonald's in Russia**: Built food-processing plants to supply restaurants and ensure quality (circumvented import issues).

**Resources:**
- U.S. Dept. of Commerce, International Trade Administration: www.export.gov → "Tariffs and Taxes"
- Department of State Country Commercial Guides: www.export.gov

---

### Criterion 3: Corporate Tax Rates

**Key consideration**: Franchisee already paying royalties and advertising fees based on REVENUE (not profits), so a high foreign tax rate on top of that could be devastating.

**Rule of thumb:**
- Above 35%: Restrictive or prohibitive
- Below 35%: Conducive to franchising

**Resources:**
- Ernst & Young "Worldwide Corporate Tax Guide" (free download from www.ey.com)
- International Monetary Fund Country Information: www.imf.org

---

### Criterion 4: Cost and Availability of Labor

**Two extremes:**
1. High cost in developed countries (Japan, Germany): Labor limited, well-educated, high cost of living
2. Very cheap in developing countries: Low cost but may be less well-educated, training difficult and/or expensive

**Conducive environment**: Countries with sufficiently sophisticated labor at lower cost than U.S. (e.g., China, Latin America)

**Resources:**
- IMF Country Reports: www.imf.org
- United Nations Statistics Division (literacy rates, education levels, population segments): www.un.org/Depts/unsd/

---

### Criterion 5: Purchasing Power of the Population

**Key insight**: Low purchasing power need not be prohibitive.
- Fast food franchises have done well in developing countries (relatively high quality but inexpensive product)
- BUT if purchasing power is too low, the environment is prohibitive

**Assessment tool**: Purchasing Power Parity (PPP)
- Better than converting GNP per capita at exchange rates (which assumes a dollar buys the same in every country)
- PPP takes into account: inflation, price and wage controls, import tariffs

**Resource**: World Bank Year 2000 Gross National Income using PPP: www.worldbank.org/data/ → "International Comparison Program" → "PPP" data

---

### Criterion 6: Economic and Political Stability

**Greatest risks in franchising abroad**: Wars, coups d'état, nationalizations, runaway inflation, governments in default.

**Assessment scale:**
- Conducive: 50+ years of stability (North America, most of Western Europe, Australia, New Zealand)
- Restrictive: Experienced disruptive turmoil in the past 20 years
- Prohibitive: Currently in turmoil

**Important principle**: With greater risk comes greater potential reward.

**Resources:**
- International Country Risk Guide (PRS Group): www.prsgroup.com (comprehensive but expensive)
- CIA World Factbook: www.odci.gov
- Dept. of State Country Reports: www.export.gov
- Economist Intelligence Unit: www.eiu.com

---

### Criterion 7: Existing Franchises Operating in the Country

**Surest way to determine viability**: See which franchises are already doing business there.

**Assessment scale:**
- Prohibitive: No franchises at all
- Restrictive: Few franchises, or declining number
- Conducive: Growing number of franchises operating there

**Resources:**
- Dept. of State Country Reports
- International Franchise Association: www.franchise.org/international.asp → Franchisor Services
- World Franchising website (Top 50 international franchisors): www.franchiseintl.com

**Note**: Some correlation between U.S. tourist travel patterns and viability of U.S. franchises abroad.

**Caveats**: "We doubt that Jiffy Lube would work in India (too many small workshops by the roadside), or a lawn care franchise in Singapore (too few houses with lawns)."

---

### Criterion 8: Language Differences

**Surmountable hurdle.**

**Solutions:**
- Have all documents drawn up in duplicate (English + foreign language)
- Find bilingual franchisees whenever possible
- Hire local consultant, tax professional, and attorney
- Consider a **Master Franchisee** from the country in question

**Master Franchisee example: Cartridge World (Australian franchisor)**
- Sells large regional franchise rights to franchisees
- Master franchisee knows territory better than the franchisor → builds right number of outlets
- Minimizes franchisor's points of contact

---

### Criterion 9: Cultural Differences

**Can be a make or break factor.**

**Examination areas:**
1. Nature of your offering or menu (Example: Beef is not widely eaten in India for cultural reasons)
2. Physical service delivery system (Décor, signage may need adjustment)
3. Products and services offered

**High Street locations in Great Britain**: Often have severe signage restrictions.

**Foreign Corrupt Practices Act**: Companies doing business with U.S. Government must adhere to this anti-corruption law wherever they trade. In cultures where bribery is normal business practice, this can severely limit opportunities and provide competitors with a financial competitive advantage.

**Best way to get a sense of cultural differences**: GO THERE and see first-hand how comparable local businesses are set up.

## Getting Serious: After You Have Selected a Country

**Best resource for U.S. franchisors**: The U.S. Government.

**U.S. Commercial Service** (offices at U.S. embassies in 100+ countries):
For a relatively small fee, this service will:
- Conduct primary market research
- Locate potential partners (Master Franchisees and franchisees)
- Find proven local consultants, tax and legal professionals
- Conduct background checks
- Host networking events at the embassy

**Website**: www.export.gov → Country & Industry Market Research

## International Market Entry Strategies

### Spectrum of Market Entrance Strategies (Table 11-2)

| Strategy | Franchisor Involvement | Notes |
|----------|----------------------|-------|
| Single Unit Franchisees | LEAST | Most problematic; limited brand awareness; one unit = "beta site" with franchisee as risk bearer |
| Territory Franchisees | Less | Well-capitalized franchisee commits to extensive development; large up-front fee + incremental payments |
| Master Franchisee | Middle | Assigns rights AND responsibilities; master franchisee operates like a franchisor locally; receives % of royalties |
| Joint Venture | More | Explicit partnership; franchisor bears % of capitalization AND management; more capital-intensive |
| Company Store | MOST | "Grass roots" approach; franchisor opens company-owned stores; tests SDS in foreign market context |

### Key Considerations for Each Strategy:

**Single Unit**: Very little brand awareness in foreign country. Putting overhead in place for one franchised unit often doesn't make financial sense.

**Territory Franchise Sales**: Marketing often directed to bankers, lawyers, and accountants in the target nation. Multiple territory franchisees can be sold in one country to build critical mass (but can also magnify country entry and operations glitches).

**Master Franchisee**: Shares in your benefits AND responsibilities. Master franchisee may:
- Open outlets under own ownership, OR
- Sell outlets to other franchisees that they will then support
- Receives a percentage of royalty fees

**Joint Venture**: Franchisor becomes partner with franchisee → significant control of the operation → but much more capital-intensive.

**Company Store**: Similar to domestic strategy recommendation from Chapter 1:
- Open company-owned stores methodically to test SDS in foreign context
- Build upon initial markets after success
- Then begin selling franchises OR offer company-owned outlets for sale to franchisees

**Geographic note**: Many franchisors base their first international headquarters in **Great Britain** because of:
- Relatively close proximity to Eastern U.S.
- Similarity of language and culture
- Relative closeness to other parts of Europe, Asia, and the Middle East

## Conclusion

**Why international markets are luring U.S. franchisors:**
- Populations far exceeding U.S. totals
- More open borders than ever
- More ubiquitous communication of brands

**BUT**: There is no risk-free approach to entering a foreign country.

**The lure of up-front fees and support-free royalties is an illusion.** A failed franchise effort in a foreign country may:
- Have far-reaching negative brand implications
- Truncate future international expansion
- Result in litigation

**The correct approach**: Use the Franchise Relationship Model (FRM) to ensure disciplined due diligence and more accurate assessment of the risk-return scenarios.
