## SECTION 2: FULL LECTURE SCRIPT (English)

### Opening (3 minutes)

Good morning, everyone. Welcome to Week 7, Session 2. We've been exploring the Service Delivery System and how it drives brand consistency across franchises.

But here's a critical question: How do customers even know about your franchise? How does a Starbucks franchisee in Seoul compete for customers? How does a CU convince someone to choose them instead of GS25?

That's marketing. And in franchising, marketing is complicated. It requires coordination between the franchisor (who builds national brand) and franchisees (who drive local sales).

Today, we're exploring how marketing works in franchise networks—and why it's often a source of tension.

---

### Segment 1: National vs. Local Marketing Framework (10 minutes)

[SHOW SLIDE: "Two Types of Marketing in Franchising"]

In a franchise system, there are two types of marketing happening simultaneously:

**1. National/Brand Marketing** — Franchisor-led
- Goal: Build global or national brand awareness
- Examples: Starbucks "Taste the Difference," Subway's global campaigns
- Funded by: Marketing fund (typically 1-2% of franchisee revenue)
- Ownership: Franchisor controls messaging, positioning, creative

**2. Local/Activation Marketing** — Franchisee-led
- Goal: Drive traffic to individual locations
- Examples: A local Starbucks running a "Buy One Get One" promotion, local radio ads, neighborhood events
- Funded by: Franchisee's own capital
- Ownership: Franchisee controls tactics, target audience, timing

These two must work together. National marketing builds brand desire ("I want Starbucks"). Local marketing converts desire into traffic ("Let's go to this specific location").

**The Problem: Incentive Misalignment**

Here's where it gets tricky.

The franchisor wants to spend marketing dollars where they get maximum brand lift. Maybe that means a Super Bowl ad ($5M) that benefits ALL franchisees equally. All 15,000 Starbucks locations benefit from that brand impression.

But an individual franchisee in Busan might think: "I don't care about a Super Bowl ad in America. I want you to spend that money helping ME attract customers in Busan." That franchisee would prefer local marketing support.

[WRITE ON BOARD: Franchisor Goal = Global Brand Building | Franchisee Goal = Local Sales Growth]

This misalignment creates tension. Franchisees pay 1-2% of revenue to a national marketing fund and question: "Is this fund really benefiting my location?"

**The Data on Marketing Effectiveness:**

Research shows:
- National brand marketing is highly effective (Starbucks, McDonald's, CU brands are worth billions)
- Local marketing is also highly effective (a new location with smart local marketing succeeds faster)
- But the ROI of each dollar varies:
  - Franchisor marketing dollar: Builds brand equity (intangible, long-term value)
  - Franchisee marketing dollar: Drives immediate sales (tangible, short-term value)

A franchisee sees immediate return on local marketing (customer comes in next week). They see delayed return on national marketing (build brand perception over months/years).

### Segment 2: Marketing Fund Mechanics & ROI (14 minutes)

[SHOW SLIDE: "How Marketing Funds Work in Franchising"]

Let's understand the mechanics of franchise marketing funds.

**Typical Structure:**

Franchisees pay 1-2% of gross revenue to a "Marketing Fund" or "Advertising Fund." This is separate from royalties.

**Example: Starbucks (2025)**
- Franchisee monthly revenue: $50,000
- Marketing fund contribution: 1% × $50,000 = $500/month
- Annual per store: $6,000
- System-wide (15,000 locations): $90 million annually

**How the Fund is Used:**

Starbucks uses that $90M+ for:
1. **National brand campaigns** (TV, digital, social media)
2. **New product launches** (seasonal drinks, new food items)
3. **Media buying** (securing prime advertising time/space)
4. **Creative development** (advertising agency work, content creation)
5. **Marketing research** (understanding customer preferences)
6. **Digital marketing** (website, app, email campaigns)
7. **In-store materials** (signage, promotional posters)

**Critical Question: Is the Marketing Fund Fair?**

This is where franchisee tensions often emerge.

A franchisee in Busan (South Korea) pays $6,000/year to the Starbucks marketing fund. This money goes to:
- A Super Bowl commercial (American advertising)
- Brand research for the global market
- Digital campaigns that might not even translate well to Korean consumers
- Global brand building that benefits Starbucks corporate more than individual Busan locations

The franchisee asks: "How does a Super Bowl ad help MY location? I need help attracting Korean customers in MY neighborhood."

**Franchisor Response:**

The franchisor argues: "The Starbucks brand is worth billions. That brand makes YOUR location valuable. Customers want to visit Starbucks because of our national brand, which we build with this fund. If the brand wasn't strong, you wouldn't be profitable."

This is true. But it doesn't directly address the individual franchisee's concern.

**Real Data on Marketing Fund Effectiveness:**

A study of franchise systems found:
- Average franchisee ROI on marketing fund contributions: 3:1 (if they contribute $1, they get $3 in incremental business)
- But range varies: 1:1 (ineffective fund) to 10:1 (highly effective fund)
- Higher-touch systems (more local marketing support) show better ROI
- Underfunded systems (franchisor uses fund for overhead, not actual marketing) show poor ROI

**Korean Example: CU Marketing Fund**

CU franchisees contribute 1-1.5% to a marketing fund. CU uses this for:
- National brand campaigns ("CU, Your 24-Hour Friend")
- Product launches
- Point-of-sale materials
- Digital integration (app, mobile payments)

By market research, CU's marketing is moderately effective (ROI ~3-4:1). Franchisees see value, but often want more localized support (in-store training, local event sponsorships).

**The Tension Over Marketing Fees:**

Many franchisees argue: "Marketing fund fees are too high and benefits aren't transparent. We'd get better ROI investing in local marketing ourselves."

Many franchisors respond: "Without national brand building, your local marketing would be ineffective. You need the brand."

Both are partly correct. The challenge is finding the right balance.

### Segment 3: Brand Building vs. Local Activation (11 minutes)

[SHOW SLIDE: "Brand Building vs. Local Sales Activation"]

Let me distinguish these two marketing approaches because they're often confused.

**Brand Building (Franchisor-Led, National Level)**

Goal: Create or strengthen brand perception and desire

Examples:
- Apple's "Think Different" campaign
- Nike's "Just Do It"
- Starbucks' positioning as a "Third Place"

These campaigns don't directly say "Come to our store today." Instead, they build emotional associations with the brand.

**Why it matters:** Customers have to want the brand before they'll search for a location.

**ROI Timeline:** 6-12+ months for full effect

**Investment:** High ($10M-$500M+ annually for major brands)

**Local Sales Activation (Franchisee-Led, Location-Specific)**

Goal: Drive traffic to THIS location, THIS week

Examples:
- "Happy Hour: 20% off all drinks 3-5pm Thursday"
- "Buy one sandwich, get one 50% off"
- Local sponsorships (school event, community sports team)
- Loyalty program promotions

These are hyper-local, short-term campaigns.

**Why it matters:** Even if customers love the brand, they won't visit YOUR location unless it's convenient, relevant, or promoted.

**ROI Timeline:** Immediate (promotion runs, customers come this week)

**Investment:** Low to moderate ($500-$5,000 per franchisee per month)

**The Optimal Mix:**

Best franchises use BOTH:
- Strong national brand building (makes the brand desirable)
- Strong local activation (drives traffic to individual locations)

**Example: McDonald's Excellence in Both**

National: McDonald's spends $1B+ annually on brand building. The "McDonald's" logo and "Happy Meal" concept are known globally. Kids recognize the Golden Arches.

Local: Franchisees run constant local marketing (school partnerships, seasonal promotions, community events). Different countries have different campaigns (McSpicy in India, McBurger in Korea, etc.).

Result: Both brand desire (from national campaigns) and location traffic (from local activation).

**Counter-Example: Underfunded Local Activation**

Some franchises create excellent national brands but don't support local franchisee marketing enough.

Result: Strong brand desire, but franchisees struggle to convert that desire into traffic because they lack marketing resources.

[ASK STUDENTS: If you're a franchisee with limited marketing budget ($1,000/month), would you spend it on:
- Paying into national marketing fund?
- Local promotion and community event sponsorship?
- Why might your answer differ from the franchisor's preference?]

### Segment 4: Korean Cases: CU & Starbucks Marketing (8 minutes)

[SHOW SLIDE: "Korean Franchise Marketing Strategies"]

Let me compare two Korean franchising success stories to understand national vs. local marketing dynamics.

**Case 1: CU Convenience Store Marketing**

**National Brand Building:**
- Brand message: "CU, Your 24-Hour Friend" (accessible, always available)
- National campaigns: TV commercials, digital ads, celebrity endorsements
- New product launches: National promotions for seasonal items
- Annual marketing budget: ~200 billion won

**Franchisee Local Marketing:**
- In-store promotions (1 + 1 deals, limited-time offers)
- POS materials (posters, banners)
- Loyalty program (membership benefits)
- Event sponsorships (neighborhood initiatives)
- Franchisee marketing budget: Varies, but typically 1-3% of sales (~300-500K won/month for typical store)

**Result:** CU has become Korea's largest convenience chain (13,000+ locations) through balanced national brand building + franchisee activation.

**Challenge:** As market maturation sets in (2024-2026), franchisees question whether national marketing spend is delivering enough value when their sales are declining.

**Case 2: Starbucks Korea Marketing**

**National Brand Building:**
- Brand positioning: Premium, aspirational coffee experience ("My Starbucks Moment")
- Global marketing: Heavy investment in digital, mobile app, loyalty program
- Korean-specific campaigns: Seasonal drinks (yuzu honey, chestnut), partnerships with Korean brands
- Annual marketing budget: Global ~$1B+, Korea portion significant

**Franchisee Local Marketing:**
- Individual location promotions (less than CU franchisees, because Starbucks Korea is predominantly company-operated)
- Community event sponsorships
- Employee engagement (brand ambassadors)

**Result:** Starbucks Korea has strong brand positioning despite market saturation. Premium positioning justifies premium pricing.

**Learning:** Starbucks' heavy brand investment protects franchise profitability by justifying price premium over competitors.

**The Dynamic (2024-2026):**

Both CU and Starbucks are re-evaluating marketing spend:
- CU: Needs to shift from brand building to franchisee support (help franchisees profitably in saturated market)
- Starbucks: Continuing premium brand positioning to sustain pricing power

The question both face: As profitability pressures increase, how much should they invest in brand building vs. franchisee activation support?

[ASK STUDENTS: If CU reduced national marketing spend by 30% and redirected that money to franchisee support programs (training, technology, local marketing assistance), would that be good strategy? Why or why not?]

[PAUSE for discussion]

### Summary & Q&A (4 minutes)

Marketing in franchises is divided between:
- **National/Brand Marketing:** Franchisor-led, builds brand desire, ROI is long-term and intangible
- **Local/Activation Marketing:** Franchisee-led, drives traffic to individual locations, ROI is immediate

Most franchisees contribute 1-2% of revenue to marketing funds. The effectiveness varies (ROI ranges from 1:1 to 10:1).

Tension arises because franchisor goals (global brand building) don't always align with franchisee goals (local sales growth).

Successful franchises balance both: Strong national brand building that creates customer desire, plus adequate support for franchisee local marketing.

In Korea (2024-2026), mature markets create pressure to shift from brand building toward franchisee support and activation. This is a strategic challenge for franchisors.

Are there questions about franchise marketing or the national vs. local dynamic?

## SECTION 4: KEY NOTES & IMPROVISATION TIPS

### Core Concepts to Emphasize

1. **Two Types of Marketing Must Work Together** — Strong brand without traffic is useless. Traffic without brand is unsustainable. Both are needed.

2. **Marketing Funds Can Be Contentious** — Franchisees pay into funds and want transparency on ROI. Franchisors should be able to justify spending with data.

3. **Local Marketing Support is Often Inadequate** — Many franchisors expect franchisees to do local marketing on their own dime. But then franchisees feel abandoned.

4. **Market Maturity Changes Marketing Strategy** — In growth markets, brand building is priority. In mature markets, activation and franchisee support becomes priority.

### Common Student Misconceptions to Correct

1. **"Brand marketing helps all franchisees equally."**
   - Correct: Brand marketing raises all boats, but benefits vary by location, demographics, competition.

2. **"Franchisees should focus only on local marketing."**
   - Correct: Without strong national brand, local marketing alone is ineffective.

3. **"Marketing fund money is always wasted."**
   - Correct: Some funds are highly effective (3-4:1 ROI), others are ineffective (1:1 or lower). Transparency matters.

### Classroom Discussion Prompts

1. "If you're a CU franchisee, would you want CU to increase national marketing spend or use that money to support YOUR local marketing?"

2. "How would you measure ROI of a national marketing campaign? How could Starbucks prove that their $1B spend benefits individual franchisees?"

3. "What's the right split between national and local marketing investment for a mature market like Korean convenience stores?"

### Real-World Examples to Emphasize

- **McDonald's:** Masterful balance of national branding and local activation
- **CU (Korea):** Strong national brand, but franchisees questioning value in saturated market
- **Starbucks (Korea):** Heavy brand investment maintaining premium positioning
- **Underfunded franchises:** Good brand but poor local marketing support = franchisee struggles

### Improvisation Tips for Live Teaching

1. **If students seem confused about marketing fund ROI:** Show the math. $1M marketing fund invested = customer impressions created = percentage become customers = dollar value created. This makes it concrete.

2. **If discussion about fairness:** Acknowledge that perfect fairness is impossible. National marketing benefits national focus. Acknowledge franchisee concerns are valid.

3. **If time is short:** Focus on one case (McDonald's or CU) and use it to show both national and local marketing dynamics.

- Have CU marketing campaign data ready (2024-2025)
- Have Starbucks global marketing budget information
- Prepare a simple ROI calculation for marketing fund
- Have news about franchisee complaints on marketing fees ready

### Post-Class Follow-up

- Assign: "Analyze one franchise's national marketing campaign and one franchisee's local marketing. How are they aligned or misaligned?"
- This deepens understanding of the franchisor-franchisee marketing dynamic.
