### Main Content (32 minutes)

**Frequently Asked Questions:**

**Q1: "The four mechanisms seem similar. How are they different?"**

Answer: They operate at different levels.
- **Capital Growth** = how franchisor finances expansion
- **Risk Distribution** = how risk is allocated between partners
- **Systems Replication** = how operational excellence is duplicated
- **Brand Building** = how brand value is created

Think McDonald's:
- Capital growth = 10,000 franchisees' money builds 40,000 restaurants
- Risk distribution = franchisees bear location risk; McDonald's bears system risk
- Systems replication = same process everywhere ensures quality
- Brand building = global ubiquity makes McDonald's the default choice

**Q2: "How do you apply red flags to a case?"**

Answer: Use this framework:
1. Read the case
2. Identify the problem (sales declining? quality issues? franchisee complaints?)
3. Check against 6 red flags:
   - Emphasis on upfront fees? (Yes/No)
   - Oversaturation? (Yes/No)
   - Weak systems? (Yes/No)
   - High failure rate? (Yes/No)
   - Franchisor instability? (Yes/No)
   - Inconsistent rules? (Yes/No)
4. Count how many red flags present
5. More red flags = more likely to fail

**Q3: "SDS seems abstract. What's a concrete example of SDS enforcement?"**

Answer: CU convenience store SDS:
- **Standard:** Every item in store comes through CU distribution
- **Enforcement:** Franchisees can't order from local suppliers
- **Technology:** POS shows approved suppliers only
- **Monitoring:** Inventory system tracks all orders
- **Result:** Consistent product quality and pricing across 13,000 stores

**Q4: "What's the difference between location problem and franchisee problem?"**

Answer: Important distinction.

**Location problem:**
- Sales declining because foot traffic in area decreased
- New competitor opened nearby
- Demographics shifted
- Solution: Relocate or accept lower profitability

**Franchisee problem:**
- Sales declining despite good location
- Quality issues
- Poor customer service
- Solution: Coaching, training, replacement if needed

On exam, you must distinguish. If location is the problem, blame the franchisor (should have chosen better location). If franchisee is the problem, address with training/monitoring.

**Q5: "How do I decide if something should be standardized or localized?"**

Answer: Ask these questions:
1. Is it a food safety/health issue? → Standardize (no exceptions)
2. Does it affect brand identity/recognition? → Likely standardize
3. Can it vary without hurting customer experience? → Can localize
4. Do local laws require different approaches? → Must localize

Example: Starbucks espresso machine calibration (health/quality) = standardized globally. Starbucks menu items (customer preference) = localized.

**Q6: "What if a franchisee refuses to comply with SDS?"**

Answer: Intervention hierarchy:
- Level 1: Coaching (friendly call, suggest improvement)
- Level 2: Training (formal training program, attendance required)
- Level 3: Compliance notice (formal written warning, deadline to improve)
- Level 4: Financial penalty (reduce royalties temporarily)
- Level 5: Termination (last resort, for serious non-compliance)

Don't jump to termination. Try support first. Only terminate if franchisee refuses help.

**Q7: "How do you calculate franchisee profitability?"**

Answer: Basic formula:

Monthly Revenue
- Cost of Goods Sold (30%)
- Labor (30%)
- Rent (10-15%)
- Royalties (5%)
- Utilities (3%)
- Other operating costs
= Net Profit

Profitable franchise: Net profit > 5M won/month
Marginal franchise: Net profit 2-5M won/month
Unprofitable franchise: Net profit < 2M won/month

**Q8: "What's the difference between Dunkin' and Korean cafes in terms of SDS?"**

Answer:

**Dunkin' SDS:**
- Strong systems (dough fermentation, frying standards)
- Defensible processes (competitors can't easily replicate)
- Franchisee success rate high (80%+)

**Korean Cafes SDS:**
- Weak systems (coffee making is relatively simple)
- Easy to replicate (any shop can make coffee)
- Franchisee success rate low (50%+)

Result: Dunkin' franchises are sustainable; Korean cafe franchises were not.

**Q9: "Can a good location save a bad SDS?"**

Answer: Temporarily, yes. A great location attracts customers even if SDS is weak.

But long-term: Quality consistency builds loyalty. If customers get inconsistent experience, they'll switch to competitor.

Example: Cafe Bene had good locations initially but weak SDS. Eventually, oversaturation + weak SDS caused failure.

**Q10: "How should I approach case analysis on exam?"**

Answer: Use this structure:
1. **Problem Statement** (1 sentence): Identify the core issue
2. **Framework** (1 paragraph): Which concepts apply? (mechanisms, red flags, SDS components)
3. **Analysis** (2-3 paragraphs): Apply concepts to the case specifically
4. **Recommendation** (1-2 paragraphs): What should company do?
5. **Conclusion** (1 sentence): Summarize your recommendation

This structure shows you understand the concepts AND can apply them.
