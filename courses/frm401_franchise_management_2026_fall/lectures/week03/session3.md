### Opening (3 minutes)

Good morning. This is the final session of Week 3. We've learned the Franchise Relationship Model. We've analyzed how franchisors and franchisees create value together. Today, we examine a case that illustrates these concepts perfectly.

I want to tell you the story of two pizza franchise companies. Both were founded around the same time. Both grew through franchising. Both became major global brands. Yet their franchise models diverged dramatically. Their relationship with franchisees was fundamentally different. And those differences shaped the entire trajectory of their businesses.

This case will show you why strategic choices about the franchise model matter enormously.

### Segment 1: Pizza Hut — The Sit-Down Restaurant Model (10 minutes)

[SHOW SLIDE: "Pizza Hut: Sit-Down Innovation"]

**The Beginning (1958)**

Pizza Hut was founded in Wichita, Kansas in 1958 by two brothers, Dan and Frank Carney. Their innovation was simple: They took pizza (an Italian immigrant food) and made it a full dining experience for American families.

Their concept:
- Pizza (of course)
- Salad bar (fresh vegetables, create-your-own salads)
- Beverages (soft drinks, beer, wine)
- Sit-down dining (comfortable booths, family atmosphere)
- Casual, fun environment ("Fun for Families")

This was revolutionary in the 1950s. Pizza before Pizza Hut was usually:
- A slice shop in Italian neighborhoods
- Cheap, quick, standing-room-only
- Not family-oriented
- Not a "dining experience"

Pizza Hut redefined pizza as a **family dining experience.** The sit-down restaurant was central to this positioning.

**Pizza Hut's Business Model**

Pizza Hut's system was complex:

1. **Store Design**: The distinctive "Pizza Hut" building with red roofs became iconic. The interior was deliberately designed for family dining—booths, color scheme, décor, ambiance.

2. **Full Service**: Servers took orders. Drinks were served. Food was brought to tables. This was different from fast-food takeout.

3. **Menu Standardization**: The pizza recipe was standardized. But there was also a full menu: salad bar, appetizers, desserts, beverages.

4. **Operational Complexity**: Managing servers, kitchen coordination, table service, ambiance — this required operational sophistication.

5. **Real Estate Strategy**: Pizza Hut locations were usually in shopping centers or standalone buildings—places where families would dine, not just grab food.

**Pizza Hut's Franchise Strategy (1960s-1980s)**

Pizza Hut grew aggressively through franchising. But because the model was complex, Pizza Hut's relationship with franchisees was heavily controlled:

- **Strict Brand Control**: Pizza Hut controlled virtually everything. Store design couldn't be modified. Menu was set by corporate. Pricing had guidelines. Décor standards were rigid.

- **Detailed Operations Manual**: Pizza Hut had one of the first comprehensive franchise operations manuals. It specified how to manage salad bars, how to train servers, how to coordinate kitchen operations.

- **Extensive Training**: Franchisees received weeks of training on running a full-service restaurant.

- **Tight Monitoring**: Pizza Hut monitored franchisees closely. Quality standards were exacting. Mystery shoppers visited regularly.

- **Financial Model**: Pizza Hut charged high franchise fees (for the time) but moderate royalties. The initial investment was substantial—you were building a restaurant, not a quick-service counter.

**Why This Model Made Sense**

Pizza Hut's sit-down model required control. If franchisees:
- Modified the décor
- Changed the menu significantly
- Cut corners on server training
- Eliminated the salad bar
- Reduced portion sizes

...the entire value proposition collapsed. The family dining experience depended on consistency.

So tight franchisor control was necessary. The relationship was more "franchisor directs" than "franchisor and franchisee partner."

[WRITE ON BOARD: Pizza Hut = Complex System = Tight Control = Franchisee as Operator (not entrepreneur)]

This worked well in the 1960s-1980s when sit-down casual dining was growing. Pizza Hut became hugely successful. By the 1980s, Pizza Hut was the #1 pizza chain globally.

**The Vulnerability**

But the model had a vulnerability: it depended entirely on the sit-down dining concept remaining desirable.

By the 1980s-1990s, consumer preferences were shifting. People wanted:
- Speed and convenience
- Takeout and delivery (not sit-down dining)
- Eating at home (growing trend)
- Fast-casual options (Panera, Chipotle, etc.)

The sit-down pizza restaurant, once revolutionary, was becoming outdated.

### Segment 2: Domino's — The Delivery-Focused Model (10 minutes)

[SHOW SLIDE: "Domino's: Delivery Innovation"]

**The Beginning (1960)**

Domino's was founded in 1960 by Tom Monaghan in Ann Arbor, Michigan. The founding concept was dramatically different from Pizza Hut.

Domino's focused on one thing: **Fast delivery of hot pizza.**

The innovation:
- Pizza (just pizza, no salad bar)
- Speed (fast making, fast delivery)
- Home delivery (you don't leave your house)
- Consistent quality (pizza made the same way every time)
- Affordable pricing (not a premium dining experience)

Tom Monaghan's insight was that many people didn't want to go out to eat. They wanted pizza **brought to their home.** Quality pizza, delivered hot and fast.

**Domino's Business Model**

Domino's system was deliberately simple:

1. **Small, Fast Kitchen**: No dining room. No waiters. Just a kitchen focused on making pizza quickly.

2. **Single Focus**: Pizza only (initially). No salad bar, no appetizers, no complex menu. This reduced operational complexity.

3. **Speed-Optimized Processes**: Every process was optimized for speed. Order-taking, dough preparation, baking, boxing, delivery.

4. **Delivery as Competitive Advantage**: Domino's pioneered pizza delivery. The "30-minute guarantee" (or free pizza) became famous. This was innovative.

5. **Simple, Replicable SDS**: Because the model was simple, it was easy to teach. A Domino's franchisee didn't need weeks of training. The system was straightforward.

**Domino's Franchise Strategy (1960s-1990s)**

Domino's grew through franchising with a fundamentally different approach:

- **Franchisee Autonomy**: Because the core system was simple (make pizza, deliver pizza), franchisees had more flexibility. Local franchisees could make decisions about delivery areas, local pricing, local marketing.

- **Looser Brand Control**: The red and blue logo was fixed. The pizza recipe was standardized. But beyond that, franchisees had freedom.

- **Simpler Training**: Franchisees didn't need weeks of training. The model was learnable quickly.

- **Lower Entry Costs**: Because you didn't need a fancy dining room, a Domino's franchise cost less than a Pizza Hut franchise.

- **Lighter Monitoring**: Pizza Hut monitored heavily. Domino's monitored less intensively. As long as pizza quality and delivery speed met standards, franchisees had autonomy.

**Why This Model Made Sense**

Domino's simple model allowed looseness. If franchisees:
- Chose their own delivery areas
- Made some local menu adaptations
- Optimized their operations locally
- Took local marketing initiatives

...the core value proposition (fast pizza delivery) was maintained.

So lighter franchisor control worked. The relationship was more "partnership" than "direction."

[WRITE ON BOARD: Domino's = Simple System = Lighter Control = Franchisee as Entrepreneur (with autonomy)]

This model proved powerful. Domino's grew rapidly. Franchisees felt they had ownership, not just control.

**The Growth Advantage**

By the 1980s-1990s, consumer behavior shifted toward:
- Ordering food from home
- Eating at home
- Convenience and speed over dining experience
- Affordable, quality meals

Domino's model aligned perfectly with these trends.

Pizza Hut's model didn't. Sit-down dining was declining.

### Segment 3: The Divergence — Why Different Strategies Emerged (13 minutes)

[SHOW SLIDE: "Pizza Hut vs. Domino's: The Divergence"]

Let me analyze exactly why these two companies diverged so dramatically.

**Business Model Fundamentals**

The key insight: **Pizza Hut chose complexity. Domino's chose simplicity.**

Pizza Hut said: "Our competitive advantage is a complete family dining experience. We need to control everything to deliver consistency."

Domino's said: "Our competitive advantage is fast delivery. Everything else is secondary. Simplicity allows speed."

These choices cascaded through everything:

| Dimension | Pizza Hut | Domino's |
|-----------|-----------|----------|
| **Core Value** | Family dining experience | Fast pizza delivery |
| **System Complexity** | High (salad bar, dining, servers) | Low (kitchen, delivery) |
| **Franchisor Control** | Tight (everything specified) | Light (autonomy encouraged) |
| **Entry Cost** | High (restaurant build-out) | Low (simple kitchen) |
| **Franchisee Role** | Operator (follow directions) | Entrepreneur (make decisions) |
| **Geographic Expansion** | Slower (requires careful site selection) | Faster (works almost anywhere) |
| **Training Requirements** | Extensive (weeks) | Brief (days to weeks) |

**The Consequence: Market Evolution**

In the 1970s, when Pizza Hut was dominant, the sit-down restaurant model was popular. Pizza Hut thrived.

But consumer behavior changed. By the 1990s:
- Home delivery was dominant
- People were busier (less time for dining out)
- Convenience was valued highly
- Pizza was casual, not a "dining experience"

Domino's simple, delivery-focused model aligned with this shift. Pizza Hut's complex, sit-down model didn't.

**Domino's Market Dominance**

By 2000, Domino's was the dominant pizza franchise globally. Why?
1. Alignment with consumer trends (delivery, speed, convenience)
2. Lower franchisee entry costs (more people could afford to open)
3. Franchisee entrepreneurship (motivated, autonomous franchisees)
4. Rapid expansion capability (simple system was easy to scale)

**Pizza Hut's Adaptation**

Pizza Hut didn't disappear. But it had to adapt:
1. Added delivery (copying Domino's)
2. Simplified menu (reducing complexity)
3. Franchisee autonomy (loosening control)
4. Faster service (moving away from sit-down)
5. Diversified portfolio (adding other concepts)

But these adaptations came after Domino's had already captured significant market share. Pizza Hut was playing catch-up.

**The FRM Perspective**

Let's analyze this through the Franchise Relationship Model:

**Pizza Hut's FRM:**
- **Customer**: Family seeking upscale casual dining experience
- **Opportunity**: 1960s-1980s: High. Families wanted to dine out.
- **SDS**: Complex. Every element mattered.
- **Transaction**: Franchisor directs nearly everything
- **Financial**: High fees and royalties (premium positioning)
- **Monitoring**: Tight control necessary to maintain consistency

This FRM worked beautifully... for a while. When the opportunity component weakened (shift away from sit-down dining), the entire system became vulnerable.

**Domino's FRM:**
- **Customer**: Individual/family wanting fast pizza at home
- **Opportunity**: 1970s onward: Growing. People wanted convenience.
- **SDS**: Simple. Kitchen + delivery. Easy to teach.
- **Transaction**: Franchisor specifies core; franchisee has autonomy
- **Financial**: Moderate fees, reasonable royalties (mass-market positioning)
- **Monitoring**: Light-touch. Core metrics (quality, delivery time) monitored. Local decisions trusted.

This FRM aligned with market trends for decades. The simplicity meant it could adapt and evolve.

**The Strategic Lesson:**

This case teaches a critical lesson: **In franchising, simplicity is a strategic advantage.**

A simple system:
- Is easier to teach
- Requires lower investment
- Allows franchisee autonomy
- Is more adaptable
- Scales faster

A complex system:
- Requires tight control
- Has higher entry costs
- Limits franchisee entrepreneurship
- Is harder to adapt
- Scales slower

Now, sometimes you NEED complexity (like Starbucks, which maintains quality through detailed specifications). But you pay a cost in control requirements, franchisee satisfaction, and scalability.

Domino's and Pizza Hut illustrate this perfectly.

[ASK STUDENTS: If you were starting a new franchise today (2025), would you choose simplicity (Domino's model) or complexity (Pizza Hut model)? Why?]

[PAUSE for student responses]

Great thinking. The answer often depends on your competitive advantage. If it requires meticulous control (Starbucks coffee quality), you need complexity. If it's based on speed, convenience, and consistency at scale, simplicity is better.

### Segment 4: Korean Parallel — Nene Chicken vs. BBQ Chicken (9 minutes)

[SHOW SLIDE: "Korean Fried Chicken: Nene vs. BBQ"]

Korea has its own parallel to Pizza Hut vs. Domino's: Nene Chicken vs. BBQ Chicken.

**Nene Chicken: The Simple Model**

Nene Chicken (founded 1991) focused on one thing: fried chicken. Specifically:
- Korean-style fried chicken (marinated, fried, seasoned)
- Fast service
- Takeout and delivery
- Affordable pricing

The system is simple:
- Kitchen (frying operation)
- Order counter
- Delivery operations
- Simple menu (fried chicken in various seasonings)

Nene Chicken's franchise strategy:
- Lower entry costs
- Simple operations
- Franchisee autonomy (local decisions)
- Rapid expansion (simple system)

Result: By 2020, Nene Chicken had grown to over 3,000 locations globally. It dominated Korean fried chicken.

**BBQ Chicken: The Premium Model**

BBQ Chicken (founded 1999) took a different approach:
- Premium fried chicken (higher quality ingredients)
- Seated dining (not just takeout/delivery)
- Full menu (chicken + sides + beverages)
- Higher pricing

The system is more complex:
- Dining area (requires nice décor, ambiance)
- Kitchen (premium prep)
- Servers (table service)
- Multi-item menu

BBQ Chicken's franchise strategy:
- Higher entry costs
- Complex operations
- Tighter brand control
- Slower expansion

Result: By 2020, BBQ Chicken had grown to about 1,000 locations. It's successful but smaller than Nene.

**The Comparison**

| Factor | Nene Chicken | BBQ Chicken |
|--------|--------------|-------------|
| **Core Concept** | Simple fried chicken | Premium fried chicken dining |
| **System Complexity** | Low | High |
| **Franchisee Control** | Light | Tight |
| **Entry Cost** | Lower (~80-100M won) | Higher (~150-200M won) |
| **Expansion Speed** | Fast | Slower |
| **Franchisee Role** | Entrepreneur | Operator |
| **Global Locations** | 3,000+ | 1,000+ |
| **Market Dominance** | Clear leader | Premium niche |

Interestingly, both are successful. But Nene's simple model enabled faster growth.

**Why the Difference?**

Nene chose simplicity: Excellent fried chicken, delivered/taken out, affordable. The core was the chicken. Everything else was secondary.

BBQ Chicken chose premium positioning: Excellent chicken in a nice dining environment. Everything (décor, service, ambiance) mattered.

Nene's simplicity meant:
- Faster franchisee adoption
- Lower investment barriers
- More franchisee autonomy
- Rapid expansion
- Easier to maintain quality (focused on one thing: frying)

BBQ Chicken's complexity meant:
- Careful site selection (need good locations)
- Higher investment (nice décor)
- Tighter control (to maintain premium positioning)
- Slower expansion
- Harder to maintain quality (many moving parts)

**Market Implications (2025)**

In 2024-2025, Korean fried chicken market is competitive and maturing. New challengers:
- Delivery-only concepts (no dining room)
- Fast-casual stands
- Premium omakase-style chicken
- International chains (KFC, etc.)

Nene's simple model provides resilience. It can adapt quickly—add delivery enhancements, adjust menu, modify pricing. BBQ Chicken's premium model is more rigid. It's harder to adapt without losing premium positioning.

In 2024-2025, Nene is thriving. BBQ Chicken is stable but facing pressure.

This parallels Pizza Hut vs. Domino's: Simple, adaptable models outperform complex, rigid models in dynamic markets.

### Summary & Q&A (5 minutes)

This case has shown us how fundamental franchise design choices shape everything:

**Key lessons:**

1. **Business model drives franchise model.** If your competitive advantage requires tight control, you need a complex franchise model (Pizza Hut). If it's based on simplicity and speed, a simple model works better (Domino's).

2. **Simplicity is Scalable.** Simple, replicable systems scale faster and reach more franchisees.

3. **Complexity requires Control.** Complex systems demand tight franchisor control, higher franchisee investment, and more intensive monitoring.

4. **Market Evolution Favors Adaptation.** Markets change. Simple systems adapt faster. Complex systems are vulnerable when their core advantage becomes less relevant.

5. **Franchisee Entrepreneurship.** Simple models allow more franchisee autonomy and entrepreneurship. Franchisees feel more like owners. This motivates better execution.

6. **Korean Parallel (Nene vs. BBQ).** Korea's fried chicken market shows the same pattern. Nene's simplicity enabled dominance. BBQ's premium model succeeded but more slowly.

**The Fundamental Insight:**

When designing a franchise system, ask: What is our core competitive advantage? Then design the franchise model to protect and amplify that advantage. Nothing more, nothing less.

Pizza Hut tried to control everything. Domino's controlled only what mattered (pizza quality, delivery speed, brand standards). Domino's approach was more effective.
