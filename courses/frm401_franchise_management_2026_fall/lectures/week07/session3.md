## SECTION 2: FULL LECTURE SCRIPT (English)

### Opening (3 minutes)

Good morning, everyone. Welcome to Week 7, Session 3—our final lecture on the Service Delivery System.

We've explored how SDS creates brand consistency globally. But here's the paradox that faces every global franchisor: **How do you maintain consistent brand while respecting local culture and preferences?**

Starbucks serves lattes the same way in Seattle and Seoul. But they also sell a "Honey Yuzu" drink in Korea, a "Cherry Blossom" drink in Japan, a "Red Bean Frappuccino" in China. Are they compromising the brand, or adapting smartly?

Today, we explore the localization paradox, and how successful global franchises navigate it. We'll look at Korean franchises going global—and what they're learning.

---

### Segment 1: Core vs. Customizable Framework (10 minutes)

[SHOW SLIDE: "The Localization Framework"]

Every franchise system has two layers:

**1. Core SDS (Non-Negotiable)** — What makes the franchise THE franchise
- Brand identity (logos, colors, values)
- Essential product/service quality standards
- Critical operational procedures
- Customer experience fundamentals

**2. Customizable Content** — What can be adapted to local market
- Product mix (menu items, flavors, varieties)
- Marketing messages and campaigns
- Store design and aesthetic elements
- Staff service style (warmth, formality, etc.)
- Local supply chain and sourcing
- Pricing (can vary by market, but within franchisor guidelines)

[WRITE ON BOARD: Core = Brand Identity + Quality | Customizable = Product Mix + Marketing + Design]

This framework is critical. If a franchisor customizes too much of the core, the brand becomes unrecognizable. If it customizes nothing, the brand fails in markets with different preferences.

**Example: McDonald's Global Strategy**

**Core (Non-Negotiable):**
- McDonald's brand identity (golden arches, Ronald McDonald, quality beef/chicken standards)
- Operational systems (kitchen procedures, food safety, staff training)
- Customer service (efficient, friendly, consistent)
- Franchise model (franchisees operate locations, McDonald's maintains standards)

**Customizable:**
- Menu items: U.S. has beef, India has Chicken (no beef due to Hindu/Muslim preferences), Korea has Bulgogi Burger, etc.
- Flavors: U.S. has standard ketchup, Korea adds sweet potato fries and hotdog-style items
- Store design: Can reflect local architecture and interior design styles
- Marketing: Global "I'm Lovin' It" message adapted to local languages and cultural contexts
- Pricing: Each market has different price points based on local income levels and competition

Result: McDonald's is recognizable worldwide (core identity protected), but feels local in each market (customization respected).

**Counter-Example: Uniqlo's Over-Customization Problem (2010s)**

Uniqlo (Japanese casual apparel) tried to expand globally. In some markets, they customized TOO MUCH:
- Stores looked completely different by country (lost brand identity)
- Product quality standards loosened in some markets (damaged brand reputation)
- Pricing varied wildly (confusing customers)
- Brand positioning changed by market (confused global brand perception)

Result: Uniqlo's global expansion was weaker than it could have been. They've since returned to stronger core standardization.

**The Key:** Protect core, customize content. Let customers know it's still McDonald's/Uniqlo/Starbucks, but adapt to their preferences.

### Segment 2: Cultural Adaptation Strategies (14 minutes)

[SHOW SLIDE: "How Global Franchises Adapt to Culture"]

Let me detail specific adaptation strategies for different elements.

**1. Product Mix Adaptation**

Most sensitive to localization because food/beverage preferences are deeply cultural.

**Starbucks Global Product Strategy:**
- Core: Premium coffee quality, espresso standards, beloved signature drinks (Caramel Macchiato, Pumpkin Spice Latte)
- Customized: Seasonal and regional drinks
  - Korea: Honey Yuzu, Chestnut Milk Latte, Green Tea offerings
  - Japan: Cherry Blossom, Matcha, Red Bean products
  - China: Mid-Autumn Festival specials, Red Bean drinks
  - Middle East: Rose, Saffron offerings

Why this works: Core coffee quality is protected. But each market gets flavors that resonate locally.

**2. Pricing Strategy**

Can vary significantly by market while maintaining franchisor brand positioning.

**Starbucks Pricing (2025):**
- U.S.: Tall coffee ~$3.45
- Korea: Tall coffee ~5,500 won (~$4.25) — Higher due to higher Korean labor/real estate costs
- India: Tall coffee ~₹150 ($1.80) — Lower due to lower purchasing power
- Japan: Tall coffee ~¥500 (~$3.40) — Similar to U.S.

This variation is intentional. Franchisor wants profitability AND accessibility in each market. Franchisees in each market need economics to work.

**3. Store Design Adaptation**

Often reflects local architecture, climate, and space constraints.

**Example: Korean Convenience Stores in Different Environments**
- CU in Seoul CBD: Compact, multi-level, high-tech (expensive real estate)
- CU in Korean countryside: Larger, single-level (cheap real estate, customers drive in)
- CU in Tokyo: Narrower, vertical design (Japanese space constraints)

Core design elements consistent (brand colors, logo placement, essential layout). But adaptation addresses local realities.

**4. Staff Service Style**

Different cultures have different service expectations.

**Starbucks Service:**
- U.S.: Friendly, personal, "can I get your name?" communication
- Korea: Respectful, formal, anticipatory (staff predicts what you want)
- Japan: Extremely polite, understated, respectful personal space
- Middle East: Warm, hospitable, engagement-oriented

The core (attentive, quality-focused service) is consistent. But style adapts to cultural expectations.

**5. Marketing & Messaging**

Absolutely must adapt to local language, culture, and values.

**Example: Nene Chicken Korea vs. Global**
- Korea: Positioning as "casual, young, vibrant" — targets youth and families
- Vietnam: Positioning as "premium" — there, Korean brand is seen as premium
- Thailand: Positioning as "adventurous and trendy" — appeals to trend-followers
- Japan: Positioning as "authentic Korean experience"

Same chicken product, completely different positioning by market. Each resonates with local values.

[ASK STUDENTS: Why would Nene Chicken position differently in Vietnam (premium) vs. Korea (casual)? What does this tell you about global brand strategy?]

### Segment 3: Korean Franchises Going Global (11 minutes)

[SHOW SLIDE: "Korean Franchises' Global Expansion (2020-2026)"]

Let me examine Korean franchises attempting global expansion. This is particularly relevant for you.

**Case 1: Nene Chicken Global Expansion**

Nene Chicken is Korea's largest chicken franchise (1,700+ locations in Korea by 2025). In the 2010s-2020s, they expanded globally: Vietnam, Japan, Thailand, Philippines, Indonesia, Malaysia, U.S.

**Localization Strategy:**

**Core (Protected):**
- Spicy Korean fried chicken quality and seasoning (the identity)
- Operational standards (cooking temperature, quality control, training)
- Franchise business model

**Customized (Adapted):**
- Heat level: Adjusted by country (U.S. wants less spicy, Vietnam wants more)
- Side dishes: Rice in Southeast Asia, fries in U.S., local sides in Japan
- Store design: Modern in U.S., traditional Korean in Vietnam and Thailand, contemporary in Japan
- Pricing: Premium in Vietnam/Thailand, moderate in U.S., adjusted to local income levels
- Marketing: "Experience Korea" in Southeast Asia, "Trendy Chicken" in U.S., "Casual Premium" in Japan

**Results (2024-2026):**
- Vietnam: 150+ locations, thriving (positioned as premium Korean chicken)
- Thailand: 100+ locations, growing (positioned as adventurous trendy food)
- Japan: 30+ locations, struggling (competition intense, market saturated with chicken options)
- U.S.: 20+ locations, slow growth (competing against KFC, Popeyes, domestic brands)
- Philippines: 50+ locations, steady (growing middle class, brand appeal)

**Learning:** Nene succeeds in markets where Korean brand is premium/novelty (Vietnam, Thailand, Philippines). Nene struggles where competition is intense (Japan, U.S.). This suggests: localization strategy alone doesn't guarantee success. Market conditions matter.

**Case 2: Korean Cosmetics Brands Globally**

Korean beauty brands (Innisfree, Missha, VT, COSRX) are expanding globally, often through franchise or partnership models.

**Localization Strategy:**

**Core:**
- Korean beauty product quality and innovation
- Brand image as "Korean beauty expertise"

**Customized:**
- Product mix: K-beauty products for Asian markets, more "comfortable" ingredients for Western markets, natural ingredients emphasized in eco-conscious markets
- Store design: Modern, minimalist (Korean aesthetic) in fashion-forward markets; traditional cosmetics styling in others
- Pricing: Premium positioning everywhere (leveraging "Korean beauty" trend), but absolute prices adjusted by market
- Service: Consultative in Asia (matching Korean service style), self-serve in Western markets

**Results (2024-2026):**
- Southeast Asia (Vietnam, Thailand, Philippines): Booming (Korean beauty trend is strong)
- China: Mixed (market competitive but huge)
- Japan: Strong (beauty-conscious market, respects Korean products)
- Western markets (U.S., Europe): Growing but slower (Korean beauty is niche, not mainstream yet)

**Learning:** Korean cosmetics brands succeed globally by positioning as "Korean expertise" (core identity) while adapting to local beauty preferences (customization). Market maturity matters—booming in trend-conscious Southeast Asia, still emerging in conservative Western markets.

**Case 3: Korean Coffee Franchises Struggling Globally**

Many Korean coffee franchises (Hollys, Caffè Bene, Ediya, Compose) have tried international expansion with mixed results.

**Why They Struggle:**
- Core challenge: Coffee is global and commoditized. Korean coffee franchises don't have unique positioning (unlike noodle or fried chicken franchises)
- Coffee preferences are strong and local (Italian espresso culture, American coffee culture, etc.). Hard to localize when there's strong local preference
- Competition is intense (Starbucks, local chains dominate almost every market)

**Example Failure:** Caffè Bene (Korean coffee franchise) attempted U.S. expansion (2010s). They tried to maintain Korean aesthetic and service style, but:
- U.S. customers wanted Starbucks, not Korean coffee experience
- Brand positioning was unclear ("Korean coffee?" vs. "Quality coffee")
- Unit economics never worked
- By 2020, most U.S. locations closed

**Learning:** Some franchises are inherently harder to localize. Coffee is particularly challenging because preferences are strong and competition is intense. Nene succeeded partly because Korean fried chicken is distinctive. Korean coffee franchises failed partly because coffee is not distinctive as "Korean."

### Segment 4: Failure Cases & Over-Adaptation Risks (8 minutes)

[SHOW SLIDE: "When Localization Goes Too Far"]

Over-adaptation is a real risk. When franchisors customize too much, they lose the brand essence.

**Case: Pizza Hut in Japan Over-Customization**

Pizza Hut Japan (late 2000s) tried heavily customizing menu:
- Okonomiyaki pizza (Japanese savory pancake)
- Takoyaki pizza (octopus balls)
- Mayo Jaga pizza (mayonnaise potato)
- Teriyaki pizza

Result: Customers were confused. Was this Pizza Hut or a Japanese fusion restaurant? The brand became unrecognizable. Pizza Hut Japan eventually returned to core menu while adding seasonal Japanese items.

**Learning:** Customize content (menu items), not core (what is Pizza Hut?). A few Japanese-inspired pizzas = smart localization. A menu that doesn't look like Pizza Hut = identity loss.

**Case: Starbucks Korea Nearly Over-Customizing**

At one point, Starbucks Korea went too far with localization:
- Too many Korea-specific products
- Design that looked too local, less Starbucks-ish
- Marketing that emphasized "Korean brand" over "Starbucks"

Result: Starbucks brand recognition weakened in Korea. Customers said "This is a Korean coffee shop" instead of "This is Starbucks Korea."

Starbucks corrected course: Maintain stronger visual brand identity, keep core products prominent, use localization selectively.

**The Balance:**

The sweet spot is:
- **Core Protection:** 60-70% of brand identity, products, operations protected/standardized
- **Customization:** 30-40% adapted to local market
- **Clear Communication:** Customers should immediately recognize it's the same brand, but adapted

This varies by industry:
- Food franchises: More customization acceptable (30-40% customization)
- Premium/luxury brands: Less customization needed (15-25% customization)
- Tech/service franchises: Moderate customization (20-35%)

### Summary & Q&A (4 minutes)

Global franchising requires balancing standardization and localization.

**The Framework:**
- Core SDS (brand identity, quality, operations): Protect rigorously
- Customizable content (products, design, marketing): Adapt smartly

**Successful Global Franchises:**
- McDonald's: Core protected, menu customized
- Starbucks: Core protected, seasonal drinks customized
- Nene Chicken: Korean identity protected, heat/sides customized by market
- Korean beauty brands: Korean expertise protected, product mix customized

**Risks:**
- Over-adaptation: Lose brand identity
- Under-adaptation: Fail in local market
- Wrong balance: Confuse customers about what the brand is

**Korean Franchises' Lessons (2024-2026):**
- Korean chicken brands (Nene) succeeding because Korean chicken is distinctive
- Korean coffee brands struggling because coffee is commoditized
- Korean cosmetics brands thriving because Korean beauty is trendy
- Success depends on both localization strategy AND market conditions

The best global franchisors are strategic about what to protect and what to adapt.

## SECTION 4: KEY NOTES & IMPROVISATION TIPS

### Core Concepts to Emphasize

1. **Core vs. Customizable is Not Subjective** — The franchisor must define clearly what is protected and what can adapt. Ambiguity leads to inconsistency.

2. **Localization is Not Surrender** — Customizing a menu item is not the same as abandoning brand identity. Strategic customization strengthens global brands.

3. **Market Position Matters** — Korean chicken is premium/trendy in Vietnam. Korean coffee is commodity-like in U.S. Your distinctive advantage varies by market.

4. **Over-Customization is a Real Risk** — Pizza Hut Japan learned this the hard way. Too much customization = customers don't recognize the brand.

### Common Student Misconceptions to Correct

1. **"Global brands must be identical everywhere."**
   - Correct: Global brands protect core identity but adapt content. McDonald's same burger, different menu.

2. **"Localization means abandoning the brand."**
   - Correct: Localization is strategic adaptation of non-core elements. Core identity remains.

3. **"Korean franchises can succeed globally by being Korean."**
   - Correct: Korean advantage depends on market perception. Nene succeeds because Korean chicken is exotic in Vietnam. Korean coffee doesn't succeed because coffee is commoditized globally.

### Classroom Discussion Prompts

1. "If you were expanding a Korean cosmetics brand to the U.S., what would you protect (core) and what would you customize?"

2. "Why did Nene Chicken succeed in Vietnam but not Japan? Is it the localization strategy or the market?"

3. "Is there a Korean franchise that's going global? Research it. What are they doing right or wrong with localization?"

### Real-World Examples to Emphasize

- **McDonald's:** Master of core protection + content customization
- **Starbucks Korea:** Early over-customization, learned to balance
- **Nene Chicken:** Success in Vietnam, struggles in Japan and U.S.
- **Pizza Hut Japan:** Over-customization example (Okonomiyaki pizza)
- **Korean cosmetics brands:** Leveraging K-beauty trend globally

### Improvisation Tips for Live Teaching

1. **If students seem confused about what's core vs. customizable:** Use McDonald's burger (core product) vs. menu items (customizable content). The burger definition is protected. But what goes WITH the burger is flexible.

2. **If discussion becomes abstract:** Ask about specific franchises they know (CU, Starbucks, Nene). Make them analyze real examples.

3. **If time is short:** Focus on the framework (core vs. customizable) and use 1-2 examples (McDonald's, Nene). Everything else flows from understanding the framework.
