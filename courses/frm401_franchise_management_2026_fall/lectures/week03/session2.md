## SECTION 2: FULL LECTURE SCRIPT (English)

### Opening (3 minutes)

Good morning. Last session, we introduced the Franchise Relationship Model. We saw that franchising has six interconnected components.

Today, we zoom in on the most important relationship: franchisor and franchisee. These are not adversaries. They're not customer and vendor in a simple transaction. They're interdependent partners in a complex ecosystem.

The question today is: **How do they create value together? What can go wrong? How do we prevent conflict?**

This is perhaps the most critical question in franchising. Because if franchisor and franchisee don't align, the entire system fails.

---

### Segment 1: The Interdependence Principle (9 minutes)

[SHOW SLIDE: "Franchisor and Franchisee Are Interdependent"]

Let me state this clearly: **Franchisors and franchisees are completely dependent on each other.** 

The franchisor cannot succeed without franchisees. The franchisee cannot succeed without the franchisor. Neither party can "win" at the expense of the other without destroying the system.

**Why is the franchisor dependent on franchisees?**

A franchisor's entire business model rests on franchisees succeeding. The franchisor's revenue comes from:
- Initial franchise fees (one-time, but limited by the number of franchisees)
- Ongoing royalties (recurring, but only if franchisees generate revenue)
- Marketing fees (only if franchisees exist)

If franchisees fail or decline, the franchisor's revenue evaporates. Royalties disappear. The franchisee network shrinks.

**Example:** McDonald's has 40,000 franchises worldwide. If 5,000 franchises failed in a year, McDonald's would lose millions in annual royalty revenue. If those franchisees were replaced with new ones, McDonald's would still face brand damage.

A struggling McDonald's location damages the McDonald's brand for everyone. Customers who eat at a poor-quality McDonald's location become skeptical of all McDonald's locations. That damages the entire system.

So the franchisor has an enormous incentive to ensure franchisees succeed. This is not charity. This is enlightened self-interest.

**Why is the franchisee dependent on the franchisor?**

A franchisee invests capital (often 300,000-500,000 in the US, 50-100 million won in Korea). That investment is usually the franchisee's life savings. The franchisee assumes enormous personal risk.

That risk is only acceptable if the franchisor's system is genuinely powerful. The franchisee depends on:
- Brand recognition (so customers come to the location)
- Proven operations system (so the location can succeed)
- Training and support (so the franchisee can execute well)
- Continued innovation (so the brand stays relevant)
- Honest financial projections (so the franchisee knows the true opportunity)

Without the franchisor's system, the franchisee is just another entrepreneur starting from scratch. The franchisee's competitive advantage evaporates.

[WRITE ON BOARD: 
FRANCHISOR NEEDS FRANCHISEE = Revenue + Growth + Market Presence
FRANCHISEE NEEDS FRANCHISOR = Brand + System + Support + Competitive Advantage
]

This is the core of franchising: **mutual dependence creates mutual alignment.**

It's different from traditional employment. An employer doesn't depend on an employee. The employer is the boss. But in franchising, both parties are dependent on each other. This creates a partnership dynamic.

[ASK STUDENTS: If franchisor and franchisee are so dependent on each other, why do conflicts ever happen?]

[PAUSE for student responses]

Great question. Even with alignment, conflicts arise. Why? Because:
1. Information asymmetry (one party knows more than the other)
2. Different time horizons (franchisor wants long-term, franchisee wants short-term profit)
3. Different risk profiles (franchisor diversified across many locations, franchisee's entire net worth in one location)
4. Ambiguity in contracts (what does "reasonable support" really mean?)
5. Changing market conditions (what worked in 2020 doesn't work in 2025)

### Segment 2: How Both Parties Create Value (13 minutes)

[SHOW SLIDE: "The Value Creation Dynamic"]

Let me map out exactly how both parties create value.

**For the Franchisee, the Franchisor Creates Value By:**

**1. Brand Recognition**

A franchisee who opens a Starbucks location doesn't start from zero. Customers already know Starbucks. They trust it. They want it. This brand equity took Starbucks 30+ years and billions of dollars to build.

The franchisee gets instant access to that brand. That's enormous value.

How much is it worth? Studies suggest that brand recognition is worth 30-50% of a new franchise location's success. A Starbucks franchisee gets customers simply because of the Starbucks name. An independent coffee shop owner must build brand recognition from scratch.

**2. Proven System**

The franchisor provides a documented, tested, proven system. The franchisee doesn't invent how to make coffee. The Starbucks operations manual is thousands of pages. It specifies:
- Equipment (espresso machines, grinders, brewers)
- Recipes (exact coffee blends, ratios, temperatures)
- Procedures (how to pull espresso, steam milk, pour drinks)
- Customer service (greeting, order-taking, delivery)
- Store design (layout, décor, ambiance)
- Hours and staffing (when open, how many staff)
- Pricing (what to charge)
- And so on...

This system took Starbucks decades to develop through thousands of locations. The franchisee gets it immediately.

How much value? If a franchisee had to develop this system independently, it would take years and millions of dollars. The franchisor provides it day one.

**3. Training and Support**

When a franchisee opens a location, the franchisor provides:
- Pre-opening training (franchisee and staff)
- Site selection assistance (helping choose the best location)
- Build-out guidance (how to construct the store)
- Equipment selection and installation
- Ongoing training (continuous improvement)
- Marketing support (local and national)
- Technology platforms (POS systems, online ordering)
- Supplier relationships (volume purchasing power)
- Problem-solving (when issues arise)

This is ongoing value. It's not a one-time thing.

**4. Innovation and Adaptation**

The franchisor invests in research and development. Starbucks continuously:
- Tests new drinks (responding to consumer trends)
- Develops new food items
- Improves operations (faster service, better equipment)
- Adapts to technology (mobile ordering, payment systems)
- Stays ahead of competition

The franchisee benefits from all of this. The franchisee doesn't have to invest in R&D. The franchisor does it.

**For the Franchisor, the Franchisee Creates Value By:**

**1. Capital Investment**

Opening a Starbucks costs $400K-$700K. The franchisor doesn't pay this. The franchisee does. 

This is enormous value for the franchisor. If the franchisor had to finance all locations directly, they would need tens of billions of dollars. They couldn't expand. Instead, franchisees provide the capital.

**2. Local Execution**

The franchisee is on the ground. The franchisee:
- Hires local staff
- Manages daily operations
- Builds relationships with local customers
- Solves local problems
- Maintains the store
- Handles customer service

The franchisor can't do this from headquarters. The franchisee is the boots on the ground.

**3. Motivated Ownership**

A franchisee owns the location. The franchisee has skin in the game. If the location fails, the franchisee loses everything.

This creates enormous motivation. The franchisee will work 60-80 hours per week. The franchisee will problem-solve. The franchisee will innovate locally (while following the system).

A hired manager (corporate employee) doesn't have this motivation. A manager earns a salary regardless of location performance. A franchisee's entire wealth depends on location success.

[WRITE ON BOARD: FRANCHISEE AS OWNER > MANAGER AS EMPLOYEE (in terms of motivation)]

**4. Risk Absorption**

When a location struggles, the franchisee absorbs the risk. If a location in a declining neighborhood fails, the franchisee loses the investment. The franchisor has already collected the initial franchise fee and some royalties. The franchisor's risk is limited.

This allows the franchisor to expand into uncertain markets. The franchisor can try many locations. Some will fail. But the franchisor isn't destroyed by failures—franchisees are.

This is harsh, but it's how franchising scales rapidly.

**The Value Balance:**

For franchising to work, both parties must feel they're getting value.

Franchisee Perspective: "I get brand, system, training, support. In exchange, I pay franchise fees and royalties. Net value: Positive. I'm better off with the franchisor's system than starting independently."

Franchisor Perspective: "I get capital investment, local execution, risk absorption, and franchise growth. In exchange, I provide brand, system, training, support. Net value: Positive. I grow faster and with less capital than if I owned locations directly."

When both parties see positive value, the system works. When either party feels they're giving more than they're getting, conflict arises.

[ASK STUDENTS: In the Korean coffee franchise boom (2015-2020), why did many franchisees end up unhappy? Using the value framework, what happened?]

[PAUSE for student responses]

Good analysis. The franchisor was saying: "You get our brand and system." The franchisee was paying fees. But the market was saturating. The franchisee wasn't making money. The franchisee's value equation became negative. "I'm worse off with this franchise than I would be doing something else."

### Segment 3: Sources of Conflict & Prevention (13 minutes)

[SHOW SLIDE: "Seven Common Franchise Conflicts"]

In theory, franchisors and franchisees are aligned. In practice, conflicts happen. Let me outline the seven most common.

**Conflict 1: Royalty Disputes**

The franchisee pays royalties (typically 5-7% of revenue). But what counts as "revenue"? 

Does royalty include:
- Revenue from all products sold?
- Or only food/beverage?
- What about taxes?
- What about discounts?

A franchisee might intentionally underreport revenue to reduce royalties. Or a franchisor might demand royalties on revenue the franchisee disputes.

**Example:** A Subway franchisee reports $1 million in revenue. Subway demands 6% royalties = $60,000. The franchisee argues: "That includes $100K in catering orders where we lost money." Should royalties apply?

**Prevention:** Clear, specific royalty definitions in the franchise agreement. Regular audits. Dispute resolution processes.

**Conflict 2: Insufficient Support**

The franchisee expects training, marketing support, technology help, operational guidance. The franchisor provides minimal support.

**Example:** A franchisee opens a location and gets initial training. After that, nothing. No follow-up calls. No marketing materials. No technology updates. The franchisee struggles and feels abandoned.

**Prevention:** Detailed specifications of franchisor support in the franchise agreement. Regular communication. Clear escalation processes for issues.

**Conflict 3: Competitive Harm**

The franchisor opens a new franchised location nearby, stealing customers from an existing franchisee.

**Example:** Franchisee A opens a CU store at a subway station. Two years later, the franchisor opens a CU store at the same station's exit. Franchisee A's revenue drops 30%. Is this fair?

**Prevention:** Territorial protection clauses in franchise agreements. Requirements for franchisor to notify franchisees before opening nearby locations.

**Conflict 4: Forced Purchases**

The franchisor requires franchisees to purchase supplies exclusively from franchisor-approved suppliers, often at premium prices.

**Example:** A McDonald's franchisee must buy beef from approved suppliers. Prices are higher than local suppliers. But franchisee can't negotiate. Franchisee feels exploited.

**Prevention:** Transparency about pricing. Reasonable price comparisons. Opportunities for franchisees to propose alternative suppliers.

**Conflict 5: Quality Degradation**

The franchisee cuts corners to reduce costs. Uses cheaper ingredients. Reduces staff. Skips cleaning. Brand quality suffers.

**Example:** A Starbucks franchisee uses cheaper coffee beans (violating the franchise agreement) to improve margins. Coffee quality declines. Customers notice. Brand reputation suffers.

**Prevention:** Regular monitoring. Mystery shoppers. Financial audits. Enforcement (fines, warnings, termination if necessary).

**Conflict 6: Unfair Termination**

The franchisor terminates the franchise agreement unfairly, without cause or due process.

**Example:** A franchisee has operated successfully for 10 years. Then the franchisor decides: "We're going to own this location directly." The franchisor terminates the franchise and opens a corporate location.

**Prevention:** Legal protections in franchise agreements. Due process requirements. Severance provisions. Clear termination conditions.

**Conflict 7: Unrealistic Projections**

The franchisor projects that a franchisee can earn $100K annually. The franchisee invests $500K. But actual earnings are $30K annually. The franchisee was misled.

**Example:** A Korean convenience store franchisor projects high earnings. A franchisee invests 80 million won. But due to market saturation, earnings are far below projections. The franchisee loses money.

**Prevention:** Honest financial disclosure. References to existing franchisees. Clear disclaimers: "Earnings vary widely." Regulatory requirements (US has Item 19 in FDD; Korea has similar regulations).

**Prevention Mechanisms:**

1. **Clear Written Agreements** — The franchise agreement should specify everything. Support, royalties, territory, termination, dispute resolution.

2. **Regular Communication** — Frequent contact between franchisor and franchisee prevents misunderstandings.

3. **Alignment of Financial Incentives** — Structure the financial model so both parties prosper when the business succeeds.

4. **Monitoring and Compliance** — Regular store checks ensure quality and identify problems early.

5. **Dispute Resolution Processes** — Mediation before litigation. This preserves relationships and is faster/cheaper.

6. **Transparency** — The franchisor should be honest about challenges, market conditions, and opportunities.

7. **Franchisee Input** — Some franchises have franchisee councils that provide input on policies. This increases buy-in.

[ASK STUDENTS: Of these seven conflicts, which is most damaging to the franchise system? Why?]

[PAUSE for student responses]

I'd argue Conflict 7 (unrealistic projections) is most damaging. Here's why: When franchisees are misled about earnings potential, they invest based on false expectations. When reality doesn't match, they lose faith in the franchisor. They cut corners to protect profitability. They create conflict. The system deteriorates.

Honesty about opportunity is critical.

### Segment 4: Korean Examples — CU vs. GS25 (8 minutes)

[SHOW SLIDE: "Franchisor-Franchisee Dynamics in Korea"]

In Korea, the convenience store franchise industry has experienced both harmony and conflict. Let me compare CU and GS25.

**CU's Value Creation (Success Case)**

**For CU's franchisees, value creation:**
1. **Strong brand** — CU is the #1 convenience store chain. Brand recognition is high.
2. **Proven system** — Store design, POS, supply chain are standardized and efficient.
3. **Supplier power** — CU negotiates with suppliers in bulk, reducing franchisee costs.
4. **Market leadership** — Being part of the #1 network creates confidence.

**For CU (the franchisor), franchisees create value:**
1. **Capital** — 13,000 franchisees have invested ~1 trillion won total
2. **Execution** — Each franchisee manages their location 24/7
3. **Network effect** — 13,000 locations create brand presence
4. **Risk distribution** — If one location fails, CU is not destroyed

**Result:** For years, CU thrived. Franchisees were generally profitable. CU grew to #1 position.

**CU's Conflicts (Emerging Issues, 2020-2025)**

But in 2024-2025, tensions emerged:

1. **Profitability Decline** — Labor costs rising (minimum wage up 76% in 9 years). Franchisee margins compressing.

2. **Market Saturation** — 40,000+ convenience stores in Korea. Too much supply. Revenue per store declining.

3. **Competitive Location Opening** — Franchisees allege that CU opens competing locations too close together, cannibalizing existing franchisees' revenue.

4. **Insufficient Support** — Some franchisees feel that CU collects royalties but provides inadequate support for the challenging market.

5. **Unfair Lease Terms** — CU's control over location selection means franchisees often end up with expensive leases negotiated by CU, but bearing all the risk.

**Franchisee Reaction (2023-2025):**
- Formation of CU Franchisee Association
- Public protests demanding fairer terms
- Demands for territorial protection
- Demands for support during difficult market

**GS25's Different Approach**

GS25 (owned by GS Retail) took a different approach:

1. **More franchisee autonomy** — Franchisees have more control over location decisions
2. **Lower entry barriers** — Lower initial fees than CU
3. **Less standardization** — More flexibility in store design and product mix
4. **Closer franchisor-franchisee partnership** — More communication and input

**Result:** GS25 has strong franchisee loyalty. Franchisees feel heard.

**But trade-off:** GS25 has slightly less market presence than CU (9,000 vs. 13,000 locations). Less brand consistency.

**The Lesson:**

CU's value creation model worked extremely well when the market was growing. But when market growth stopped and costs rose, the model became strained. Franchisees' value equation flipped negative.

This led to conflict and demands for rebalancing.

GS25's more flexible model allowed better adaptation to changing conditions. Franchisees had more input. The system was more responsive.

**For 2025 forward:**
- CU must rebalance the value equation (reduce fees, provide more support, protect territories)
- Or risk losing franchisees to competitors
- The Korean government's new franchisee protection laws will force this rebalancing

This illustrates a fundamental principle: **Franchising relationships must continually be rebalanced as conditions change.** What worked in 2010 may not work in 2025.

### Summary & Q&A (4 minutes)

Franchisor and franchisee are interdependent partners. Both create tremendous value for the other.

**Key takeaways:**

1. **Interdependence is the foundation.** Neither party can succeed without the other.

2. **Value is mutual.** The franchisor provides brand, system, support, innovation. The franchisee provides capital, execution, risk absorption, local presence.

3. **Balance is critical.** When both parties feel they're getting value, the system works. When balance tips, conflict arises.

4. **Common conflicts** range from royalty disputes to insufficient support to competitive harm to unrealistic projections.

5. **Prevention requires** clear agreements, communication, monitoring, transparency, and mechanisms for input and dispute resolution.

6. **Korean example shows** that when market conditions change, franchisor-franchisee relationships must be rebalanced or conflict emerges.

## SECTION 4: KEY NOTES & IMPROVISATION TIPS

### Core Concepts to Emphasize

1. **Interdependence is Not Weakness** — Many students think franchisees are weak because they're dependent on franchisors. Correct: Both are dependent. This creates alignment.

2. **Value Balance is Dynamic** — The relationship only works if both parties perceive positive value. This balance can shift with market conditions. CU's model worked great in 2010. In 2025, it's stressed.

3. **Korean Example is Contemporary** — CU franchisee conflicts are happening NOW (2024-2025). This is not abstract history. Many students know CU. Use this immediacy.

4. **Prevention is Better Than Cure** — Clear agreements, communication, and monitoring prevent 80% of conflicts.

### Common Student Misconceptions to Correct

1. **"The franchisor has all the power."**
   - Correct: The franchisor is dependent on franchisees for revenue, execution, and growth. If franchisees fail, franchisor fails.

2. **"Franchisees just follow orders."**
   - Correct: Franchisees are entrepreneurs who manage locations, make local decisions, and have skin in the game. It's partnership, not subordination.

3. **"Once a franchisee is established, both parties can relax."**
   - Correct: Relationships require continuous communication, monitoring, and rebalancing as conditions change.

4. **"Franchisee conflicts are just about money."**
   - Correct: Conflicts often arise from ambiguity, poor communication, or changing market conditions. Money is just the manifestation.

### Classroom Discussion Prompts

1. "Between franchisors and franchisees, who has more power? Why?"

2. "In the CU case, who is more responsible for the 2024-2025 conflict — CU or franchisees?"

3. "If you were a CU franchisee in 2024, what would you demand from CU?"

4. "Do you think the Korean government's franchisee protection laws are helpful or will they hurt franchising?"

5. "Why did GS25's more flexible approach create better franchisee relationships than CU's standardized approach?"

### Real-World Examples to Emphasize

- **CU Success (2010-2020)**: Shows how value creation works when market is growing and both parties prospering
- **CU Conflict (2024-2025)**: Shows what happens when market matures and value equation tips negative
- **GS25 Alternative**: Shows that there are multiple ways to structure franchisor-franchisee relationships

### Improvisation Tips for Live Teaching

1. **If students are skeptical about "interdependence":** Show them numbers. CU has 13,000 franchises. If even 2,000 failed, CU loses billions in revenue. CU NEEDS franchisees. Not theoretical—real.

2. **If students want to debate CU's fairness:** Have them research current (2025) CU vs. franchisee disputes. Look up Korean news articles. Show them videos of franchisee protests. Make it real.

3. **If students want to learn about "power dynamics":** Ask them: "If CU is so dependent on franchisees, why don't franchisees have more power?" Answer: Because franchisees are dispersed and lack coordination. CU is centralized. This gives CU structural power, even though both are dependent.

4. **If time is short:** Focus on the value creation section and the Korean case. These are the most important.

- Have current (2025) Korean news about CU franchisee issues ready
- Prepare specific numbers: Labor cost inflation, store saturation, profit margin compression
- Have GS25 vs. CU comparison data
