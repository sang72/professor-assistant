## The SDS as the Core

The SDS is the core of a franchise system, just as the "kernel" is the core of a software program's written code.

In its most narrow sense, the SDS could be:
- The way a building is set up
- How a cooking area is designed
- How a mobile fleet is dispatched and routed
- How customer service representatives are trained

**TIP 4-1: Kinko's Service Delivery System**
- Primary target audience (PTA): College students
- Location: As close to a major college or university as possible
- Internal flow: Triage system at wide front counter — evaluates specific customer demand, then funnels:
  - DIY (do-it-yourself) → self-serve computers or copiers
  - DIFM (do-it-for-me) → job requirements handled by staff
- Key SDS elements: Location, flow of internal customer traffic, detailed understanding of customer demand

## Platform for SDS and Influence of Buyer Behavior

"Location, location, location" — but this holds to varying degrees based on type of customer being targeted.

### Three Types of Purchasing Behavior:

**1. Spontaneous Purchase Behavior**
- Definition: Time between deciding to make the purchase and the actual purchase are nearly simultaneous
- Examples: Morning coffee, gasoline, daily newspaper
- Real estate requirement: CRITICAL — even a well-known brand like Dunkin' Donuts in a Class B site can fail
- Key factor: Convenience is paramount

**2. Semi-Planned Purchase Behavior**
- Definition: Two stages — triggering event + time gap before purchase
- Examples: Oil change (triggered by mileage), dry cleaning (triggered by schedule)
- Real estate requirement: MOST DIFFICULT to preselect
  - Not as acute as spontaneous
  - But "thought time" relaxes requirement for immediately convenient location
  - Goal: Minimize real estate cost while optimizing location benefits
- Key risk: Prime real estate imposes high fixed overhead; poor site also proves fruitless due to lower revenues

**3. Planned Purchase Behavior**
- Definition: Long time between deciding and purchasing; considerable consideration
- Examples: Automobile, furniture, home appliances, engagement ring
- Real estate requirement: NOT critical
- Alternative variables matter more: price, quality, overall brand perception

**TIP 4-2: Pizza Hut Location Choice**
Pizza Hut originally on prime real estate for family dining → when shifting to delivery model:
- "Delivery-only" locations could not/should not be on expensive real estate
- Had to be relatively well placed regarding access roads
- Clearly understood distinction between consumer purchasing behavior and cost requirements of physical locations

## How to Select Real Estate

**Top three criteria (Ernst & Young study):**
1. Projected sales potential
2. Population
3. Geographic location

71% of respondents use statistical models to develop sales forecasts for sites.

### The Trade Area
- Geographic area within which approximately 70-80% of total customers will originate
- Defined in MINUTES traveled, not miles
- Affected by: natural boundaries (lakes, rivers), constructed obstacles (major highways), anchor businesses

### Four-Step Site Viability Assessment:
1. Identify the percentage of people in the trade area that are in the PTA
2. Determine the estimated market share expected as % of PTA
3. Estimate number of visits per year × expected market share = total customers per year
4. Estimate average ticket price (ATP) × total customers = estimated annual revenue

### Six Key Site Selection Variables:

1. **Identification of PTA density**: Primary, secondary, and tertiary target audiences
   - Example: Boston Chicken expanded from families (primary) to elderly individuals (secondary)
   - Daytime vs. nighttime populations matter significantly
     - McDonald's: wants high daytime population (lunch)
     - Blockbuster Video: requires large nighttime population (video rentals)

2. **Traffic volume**: Measured in pedestrians and vehicles on 12-hour and 24-hour basis; weekday vs. weekend data

3. **Traffic patterns**: Inbound and outbound sides of the street
   - Dunkin' Donuts: Far more successful on the WORK-BOUND side of the street
   - 7-Eleven convenience store: More successful on the HOMEBOUND side

4. **Site visibility**: Measured in feet AND seconds before potential customer is aware of site
   - Must consider actual speed of automobiles, not posted speed limit
   - Must consider all seasons (foliage in New England changes visibility dramatically)

5. **Zoning and permitting**: Governmental ordinances
   - May require: land use attorney, architect, surveyor, civil engineer, environmental and traffic specialist
   - Can add additional 5-10% to land cost

6. **Traffic flow (Ingress/Egress)**: Entrance to and exit from a site
   - Drive-through facilities add complexity
   - Managing both in-store customer and drive-through customer flow is difficult but important

### Complementary vs. Contradictory Neighbors:

**Complementary neighbors** (cluster together to INCREASE traffic):
- Wendy's always wants to be near McDonald's
- Automobile dealerships cluster together
- Massachusetts Turnpike service areas with multiple franchise food offerings
- Rule: Create mini-trade areas that draw more customers than each franchise could individually

**Contradictory neighbors** (close proximity HURTS business):
- Blockbuster Video next to another major video franchise
- Zero-sum game: "Star Wars at one store is the same as Star Wars at another"
- 10 video stores next to each other = no incremental value-add

## The X Factors of Site Selection

Often overlooked variables:

1. **Employee base**: Prime location for customers may be poor for potential employees
   - Upscale marketplace may have few people willing to work at franchise wages
   - Staffing becomes difficult

2. **Green Acre Syndrome**: Rich population + very few retail establishments
   - You think you're entering an underserved market
   - As trade area develops, competition moves in
   - Could end up in an OVERSERVED market unable to support high overhead
   - Check: State and local municipalities for future development data

3. **Current and future traffic patterns**: Future road changes
   - New road through trade area could alter traffic patterns dramatically
   - Current main intersection could become significantly less traveled
   - Must factor in future infrastructure development

## How to Value Real Estate

**Three common methods:**

**1. Cost Approach**: Most often used when buying property with an existing building
- Estimate current cost to construct replacement + estimated land value + entrepreneurial profit
- Useful for testing reasonableness of construction cost, not true value

**2. Sales Comparison Approach**: Compare with similar recently sold properties
- Assumes market is efficient and sets value based on supply and demand
- Variables: size of lot, square footage of structures, quality of infrastructure
- Limitation: Commercial real estate = large number of submarkets, low turnover, limited buyers/sellers

**3. Income Capitalization Approach (BEST METHOD)**:
- Question: How much money can I make operating my franchise on this property?
- Convert anticipated cash flows into property value using market-derived capitalization rate
- Most commonly used method
- Limitation: Only an estimate — capitalization rates are unstructured

**Example calculation:**
- Pro forma annual sales: $2.5M to $3.0M
- Acceptable range of occupancy costs: $125,000 to $180,000
- Market capitalization rate: 10%
- Appropriate real estate price: $1.25M to $1.8M ($125K ÷ 10% to $180K ÷ 10%)

**"Highest and best use"**: Property value equals what the highest bidder will pay while still making economically viable use of the real estate. Dynamic measure — changes over time as cities grow and zoning laws develop.

## Financing Alternatives

**Common forms of leasing agreements:**

**Fee Simple**: Standard lease — franchisee pays periodic fee to owner of land

**Land Lease**: Franchisee leases land from owner + secures mortgage from bank for building
- Problem: Bank has rights to building but NOT land; landowner can take title to building if franchisee goes bankrupt
- Solution: Subordinated land lease (landowner grants bank first claim on assets)

**Wraparound Mortgage**: Bank already holds debt on land + combines with new debt for building
- Works only if initial debt is less than 60%

**Build to Suit**: Owner constructs new building on property; total cost of land + building capitalized into a single lease

---

## Lease Agreement Considerations

**Five most important considerations:**

1. **Cap rate and rent structure**: Fixed vs. variable
   - Variable: Lower early on, but as franchise succeeds, could grow excessive — places ceiling on future profitability
   - Fixed: Higher early → longer time to breakeven
   - Hybrid: Minimum rent below market rate, then percentage of revenue above specified threshold

2. **Fitting-out costs (leasehold improvements)**: Who pays?
   - Usually you pay directly, or if lessor agrees to pay, it's included in lease payment
   - Trade-off: Shorter lease term = greater flexibility + higher cost (landlord wants compensation for risk)
   - Example: 10-year lease with annual renewal has higher cap rate than 10-year lease with renewal at end of 10 years

3. **Escalation clauses**: Landlord's ability to raise rent over lease term
   - 2% per year over 15 years can have significant impact on profits

4. **Single, double, or triple net leases**: Lessee pays for insurance, taxes, utilities respectively

5. **Eminent domain clauses**: Government forces closure and buys out business
   - Who has rights to the money from the buyout? Varies dramatically between leases.

## Capital Lease vs. Operating Lease

Capital lease is MORE expensive in absolute dollars (paying for entire useful life).

A lease should be CAPITALIZED if it satisfies any of these:
1. Ownership automatically transfers to lessee at end of lease term
2. Lease contains a bargain purchase option (can buy $1,000,000 property for $1)
3. Lease term is more than 75% of useful life of the asset
4. Present value of lease payments is greater than 90% of purchase price

**Risk**: While negotiating, franchisee may inadvertently commit to a capital lease without realizing it.

## Advantages of Real Estate Ownership

- Accumulate equity while business services the mortgage
- Two ways to build equity: paying down mortgage + property appreciation
- Downside floor: If business fails, can sell real estate (hopefully at appreciated rate)
- BUT: Restricts ability to grow quickly

---

## Lowering the Cost of Capital

Real estate can lower total cost of capital:
- Debt is cheaper to obtain than equity
- Real estate most often financed with mortgage (asset-based = more favorable interest rate)
- Show bank multiple alternative uses for property to lower perceived risk and interest rate

**Jiffy Lube example**: Franchisees given handbook from franchisor outlining 100 alternative uses for Jiffy Lube real estate — encouraged to share with bank during financing negotiations.

### Off-Balance-Sheet Ownership Structures:

**1. Sole proprietorship**: Simple to set up, no double taxation, but unlimited personal liability

**2. General partnership**: Simple, same tax implications, but unlimited and equal liability for all partners

**3. Limited partnership**: General partners fully liable; limited partners only liable for cash invested

**4. Real estate trust**: Shields identity of true owner; otherwise same as limited partnership

**5. Limited liability partnership**: Reduces risk for all partners — the S corporation of real estate

**6. Limited liability corporation (LLC)**: Most popular form today — all advantages of both C corp and S company without disadvantages

## Allocating Risk

**If franchisor owns real estate and leases to franchisee:**
- Franchisor has additional risk/return from real estate
- Makes money from BOTH royalty and lease payments
- If relationship goes badly: Franchisee stops paying royalty first, then lease
- Franchisor has more control — could more easily replace franchisee

**If franchisee owns real estate:**
- If relationship goes badly: Franchisee could change the sign and continue as independent
- Gives franchisee more leverage
