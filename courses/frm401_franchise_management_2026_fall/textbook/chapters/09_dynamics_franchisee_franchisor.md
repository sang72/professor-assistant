## Opening Context

**What separates great franchises from mediocre ones?**

The authors' belief: Beyond all the systems, spreadsheets, and technologies, the fundamental success factor for franchises is an understanding that the franchisor-franchisee alliance is an intimate business relationship.

**Sobering statistics:**
- Almost 50% of all franchisors are in a lawsuit at any given time
- 30% of franchisees are unhappy enough with the relationship to consider severing it

**Conflict is not totally unhealthy**: Questioning, probing, arguing, and even fighting can sometimes produce very healthy results.
- Example: Pizza Hut had dramatically contentious relations with franchisees in the late 1970s → revised license agreement → successful new launch of pizza-delivery program

**But when conflict reaches the courts**: Drains time, talent, and money; seldom results in a healthy relationship going forward.

## Four Components of Relational Dynamics

### 1. Wealth Creation

**Definition**: A view by each player about how the franchise system will help them become wealthy or at least will provide a market ROI.

**For some, wealth means:**
- An income stream or accumulation of assets
- Being in the industry they love
- The product or service they provide
- The people they serve
- Their role in the business
- Degree of control in decision making

**Survey finding**: Of more than 600 franchisees surveyed, ALL of them expected the venture to create some personal wealth beyond their management salary.

**Financial warning signs:**
Look at a franchisor's balance sheet for royalties payable:
- If accounts receivables are RISING and days receivable are INCREASING → franchisee conflict may be brewing
- When franchisees become pressed financially, the FIRST bill extended is the royalty payment

**Consequences of widespread underperformance:**
- Small number of outlets in trouble → closure or franchisor acquisition
- Large number of underperforming outlets → affects royalties or services
- Changes happen through renegotiation or litigation
- The market will not allow status quo with underperforming operating stores

### 2. Communication

**Core principle**: Partners who commit to as many as 20 years of working together must remain focused enough to protect the brand AND be flexible enough to react to constantly changing market forces.

**Essence of relational dynamics**: Making adjustments to the relationship in response to market demand that benefit BOTH parties.

**The role of field personnel**: The key to the informal communications system. When franchisees believe their field support contact is clearly and accurately communicating, the level of conflict remains healthy — even when the message is not one the franchisee wants to hear.

**Formal communication systems:**
- Written store reviews with clearly delineated expectations and performance feedback
- Advertising and promotional materials planned in advance and delivered to operating units
- Regional and national meetings for education and strategic planning with franchisee input
- Formalized business planning at franchisee level with custom tools
- Training aids (curriculum, videos, certification)

**"Relational exchange"**: Both franchisee and franchisor see their current income and future wealth as tied to the performance of their franchise partner.

**Fairness**: Subjectively assessed by the franchisee. If fairness is perceived to be absent in the distribution of revenue, the franchisee will assess alternatives to the current relationship or start behaving badly.

### 3. Value of Trademark or Brand

**The relational dynamics question**: NOT "Should I do this at all?" but rather "Now that I am a party to this franchise relationship, what is the value of continuing to be so?"

**The "franchise seven-year itch"**: 
- There is evidence that if franchisees aren't rewarded by the brand in the first 7 years of the relationship, then contemplation changes to action (leaving the system)

**If franchisees see the trademark as able to grow and sustain value**: They try to work through problems.

**If they see little value in the trademark at present or over time**: It's fairly reasonable to contemplate leaving the system.

**Key principle**: The amount of brand capital lost upon exit from the franchise relationship = the perceived exit costs. Higher exit costs = franchisee thinks it will be expensive to replace the value received from the franchisor.

### 4. Exit Costs

**Either party may make the decision to exit the franchise relationship.**

**Critical timing advice**: Assess exit costs while relationships are STRONG, before conflict arises.

**Purpose of knowing exit costs:**
1. Confirms understanding of the value of the franchise
2. Motivates you to manage and plan for conflict before it happens
3. Helps you assess the value of your firm and establish a sale price when you're ready to sell

**Exit options:**
1. Continue as an independent (different costs)
2. Sell the franchise (difficult if poor performance is underlying cause; sale price would be significantly depressed)

### Exit Cost Analysis (Table 9-1)

| Exit Cost Category | Relationship Category | Cost Analysis Question |
|-------------------|-----------------------|------------------------|
| Exiting will increase training cost | Business Format | How many days of training do you currently receive from the franchisor? |
| Exiting will require operating changes | Business Format | Will you have to replace special/proprietary tools, equipment, or software? |
| Company investment is unique to the relationship | Business Format | Some services from the franchisor are difficult or impossible to replace — how much will that increase delivery costs? |
| Exiting will be costly in lost customer goodwill | Business Format | If you change the name from the franchise name to an independent name, how much revenue will be lost? |
| Exiting will be costly in finding alternative suppliers | Expense | How will you buy supplies as an independent? You'll lose access to national/regional supply contracts. |
| Exiting will increase marketing budget | Expense | You'll have to advertise your new name and logo. How much will that increase your marketing budget? |
| Exiting will be costly in legal expense | Expense | A franchise license agreement is a contract that is not easily severed and almost always requires legal counsel. |
| Company has invested time and energy into relationship | Psychic | Most franchisees report they've built informal relationships in the franchise system that provide benefits. What costs or lost opportunities might result from severance? |
| Exiting will be costly in store conversion | Asset | Both external and internal signage must be changed. This is the single BIGGEST store conversion cost. |

## Case Study: Quick Lube Franchisee Company (QLFC)

*(Note: Names of people and companies are fictitious, but events are a true depiction.)*

### How to Read This Case:
Keep these four concepts in mind:
1. **Wealth Creation**: A change in control of the franchisor almost always causes concern. QLFC wonders how the multiple roles of "Big Oil" will cause an unfocused approach to wealth creation for franchisees.
2. **Communications**: When there is trauma in the franchisor, field support personnel must go into high gear. Instead, QLFC panics and quickly seeks refuge in the courts.
3. **Brand**: The franchisor is suddenly the franchisee's biggest SUPPLIER. Red flag: What brand will be promoted — Quick Lube or Big Oil?
4. **Exit Costs**: Brand confusion lowers exit costs. If the franchisee believes the franchisor is diluting the brand, losing that brand and operating under another name is not a big hurdle. A company with $30 million revenue suing a multi-billion-dollar oil company is a David vs. Goliath battle.

### Historical Background

**Super Lube**: Innovator of the quick-lube concept, founded March 1986 by Jeff Martin.

**Frank Herget**: One of four founding members of Martin's management team.

**The split**: Herget believed the future was in operating service centers; Martin was passionately committed to franchising service centers as fast as possible.

**Resolution**: Herget swapped his Super Lube founder's stock (valued at $64,000, originally purchased for $13,000) for a company-owned service center in northern California → QLFC founded.

|------|-----|-----|-----|-----|-----|-----|-----|-----|-----|-----|

| Sales ($M) | .5 | 1.6 | 2.1 | 3.8 | 8.5 | 15.5 | 19 | 27 | 28 | 30 |

**Long-term goal**: Build QLFC into a big chain of Super Lube service centers with a public stock offering or merger.

### Financial Performance (FY 1998 Budget)

**Annual figures:**
- Total Sales: $31,215,866
- Total COGS: $7,047,624
- Variable Expenses: $10,419,876
- Fixed Expenses: $4,485,890
- Real Estate Cost: $4,375,713
- Store Operating Income: $4,886,763
- Overhead: $3,181,214
- Operating Income: $1,705,549
- Net Income: $955,009

*(Note: Royalties to franchisor = 7% of gross sales)*

**Key team member**: Mark Roberts — Super Lube's former CFO, joined QLFC in 1993, brought financial sophistication.

### Growth Financing

**Challenge**: Average cost of land acquisition and construction rose to $750,000 per service center (from ~$350,000 ten years earlier).

**Funding sources:**
1. Off-balance-sheet real estate partnerships
2. Oregon bank: ~$4 million
3. Texas bank: ~$3 million
4. **Key deal**: Huston Oil: $6.5 million of subordinated debt
   - Terms: 8% interest-only for five years, then straight-line amortization in years 6-10
   - Condition: QLFC contractually committed to purchasing Huston products

### Super Lube's Problems

Despite bridge financing of $10 million and a successful IPO, Super Lube's growth continued to outpace its ability to finance it.

By mid-1990s: Super Lube was in technical default to its debt holders.

**Huston deal**: Acquired 80% of Super Lube in a debt-restructuring scheme.

**Meanwhile**: Franchisees were growing increasingly discontented as Super Lube's financial problems mounted.

### The Huston Era

**Huston's first moves**: Replaced virtually the entire management team at Super Lube.

**New CEO**: Previously a long-term employee of a K-Mart subsidiary.

**Hard-line position**: Insisted that Huston motor oil would be an important part of the franchise system.

**First national convention after takeover**: A DISASTER. Franchisees already frustrated; dismayed by franchisor's focus on motor oil sales instead of service center-level profitability.

### QLFC's Response

Herget decided to make a thorough analysis of the historical relationship between QLFC and Super Lube. After 3 months of research and documentation:

**The Summit Meeting** (at Super Lube's offices):
- 3-hour monologue by Herget
- Super Lube's response: "Treat the past as sunk cost; trust that future performance will be better"
- Regarding Huston conflict of interest (as both supplier and franchisor): "Huston bailed Super Lube out of a mess and franchisees should be grateful, not combative"

**QLFC Board decision**: Select a law firm and pursue litigation against Huston.

**Three months after the summit**: A suit against Huston was filed.

**Huston's response**: Denied charges and filed a counter suit.

**Three months into document search and depositions**: Huston requested a meeting.

**Herget's immediate action**: Called a board meeting and prepared to make a recommendation for QLFC's strategic plan.
