### Main Content

#### Segment 1: Unit Economics Fundamentals (15 minutes)

**What is Unit Economics?**

Unit economics is the analysis of profit contribution from a single unit of activity. For franchises, the units can be:
- **Per customer transaction** (e.g., one coffee sale)
- **Per product** (e.g., one fried chicken order)
- **Per location** (e.g., one franchisee's monthly operation)

Let me use **per customer transaction** as the example.

**The Coffee Example:**

A franchisee sells one coffee (medium Americano):

**Sales:** 5,000 won

**Costs for this one coffee:**
- Coffee beans and milk: 1,000 won (fixed component of COGS)
- Cup and napkins: 300 won
- Labor to prepare coffee: 500 won (allocated from total labor cost)
- **Total Variable Costs:** 1,800 won

**Contribution:** 5,000 - 1,800 = **3,200 won per transaction**

This 3,200 won is called **Contribution Margin**. It's the amount left over from this one sale that contributes to:
- Paying fixed costs (rent, equipment depreciation, utilities, insurance)
- Paying franchisor royalty
- Generating profit

**Fixed vs. Variable Costs Explained:**

**Variable costs** change with volume:
- COGS (coffee beans, milk, cups): If you sell 100 more coffees, COGS goes up proportionally
- Labor: If you need to serve 100 more customers, you need more labor hours
- Delivery costs: If you make 100 more deliveries, delivery costs increase

**Fixed costs** stay the same regardless of volume:
- Rent: Whether you sell 100 or 1,000 coffees this month, rent is still 8M won
- Equipment: Whether it's used 4 hours or 16 hours daily, depreciation is same
- Insurance: Doesn't change based on sales volume
- Utilities: Roughly fixed (slight variable component with more customers)

**Why This Distinction Matters:**

When you have a high contribution margin, you need less volume to break even. When you have a low contribution margin, you need very high volume to break even.

**Contribution Margin Ratio:**

Contribution Margin ÷ Sales = Contribution Margin Ratio

In the coffee example: 3,200 ÷ 5,000 = **64% contribution margin ratio**

This means 64% of every sale is available to cover fixed costs and profit.

**Comparing Two Franchises:**

Let's compare coffee and fast-food chicken on a per-transaction basis:

**COFFEE (Medium Americano)**
- Sales: 5,000 won
- Variable costs: 1,800 won (COGS 1,000 + cup 300 + labor 500)
- Contribution: 3,200 won
- Contribution margin ratio: 64%

**FRIED CHICKEN (Small box, 2 pieces)**
- Sales: 8,000 won
- Variable costs: 3,200 won (COGS 2,000 + packaging 400 + labor 800)
- Contribution: 4,800 won
- Contribution margin ratio: 60%

**Analysis:**

Chicken has higher absolute contribution (4,800 vs. 3,200), but lower contribution ratio (60% vs. 64%). 

Which is better? **Chicken.** Here's why: Even though contribution ratio is slightly lower, the absolute contribution is much higher. To generate 1M won in contribution:
- Coffee needs: 1M ÷ 3,200 = 312.5 customer transactions
- Chicken needs: 1M ÷ 4,800 = 208 customer transactions

Chicken customers are worth more individually. You need fewer transactions to reach the same contribution.

**Now Add Fixed Costs:**

A location has fixed costs:
- Rent: 7M/month
- Equipment depreciation: 1M/month
- Insurance: 0.5M/month
- Utilities: 1.5M/month
- **Total Fixed Costs: 10M/month**

**For Coffee to break even:**
- Need contribution that covers 10M in fixed costs
- 10M ÷ 3,200 per transaction = 3,125 transactions per month
- At average 100 transactions per day: Need ~31 days of full operation
- ✓ Achievable

**For Chicken to break even:**
- Need contribution that covers 10M in fixed costs
- 10M ÷ 4,800 per transaction = 2,083 transactions per month
- At average 70 transactions per day: Need ~30 days of operation
- ✓ Achievable

Both break even with reasonable volume. Coffee needs slightly higher volume.

#### Segment 2: Break-Even Analysis & Contribution Margin Deep Dive (15 minutes)

**Moving from per-transaction to per-location:**

Let's build unit economics for an entire chicken franchise location.

**Monthly Unit Economics (Single Location):**

| Metric | Amount | Per Customer | % of Sales |
|--------|--------|--------------|-----------|
| **REVENUE** | | | |
| Transactions/month | 2,500 | 1.0 | — |
| Average transaction value | 8,000 | 8,000 | — |
| **Total Sales** | **20M** | **8,000** | **100%** |
| | | | |
| **VARIABLE COSTS** | | | |
| COGS | -6M | -2,400 | -30% |
| Labor (direct customer service) | -3M | -1,200 | -15% |
| Packaging | -1M | -400 | -5% |
| **Total Variable Costs** | **-10M** | **-4,000** | **-50%** |
| | | | |
| **CONTRIBUTION MARGIN** | **10M** | **4,000** | **50%** |

Now we pay fixed costs:

| Metric | Amount |
|--------|--------|
| Contribution margin | 10M |
| **FIXED COSTS** | |
| Rent | -4M |
| Equipment/depreciation | -1M |
| Utilities | -1.5M |
| Insurance | -0.5M |
| Management salary (owner) | -2M |
| **Total Fixed Costs** | **-9M** |
| | |
| **NET PROFIT** | **1M** |
| Net Profit Margin | 5% |

This location has 1M monthly profit on 20M sales. Not great (5% margin is thin), but viable.

**Key Insight: Contribution Margin Ratio of 50%**

A 50% contribution margin ratio means:
- First 50% of sales covers variable costs
- Second 50% of sales covers fixed costs and profit

At 20M sales with 50% CMR:
- 10M covers variable costs
- 10M available for fixed costs + profit
- Fixed costs are 9M
- Profit is 1M

**What Happens If Volume Drops to 15M (25% decline)?**

| Metric | Before | After |
|--------|--------|-------|
| Sales | 20M | 15M |
| Variable costs (50%) | -10M | -7.5M |
| Contribution margin | 10M | **7.5M** |
| Fixed costs | -9M | -9M (SAME) |
| Net profit | 1M | **-1.5M** (LOSS!) |

**This is critical.** A 25% drop in sales turns profit into loss. Why? Because fixed costs don't decrease. Your rent is still 4M even if you sell 25% less.

This is why **location and volume matter so much**. A franchisee whose location drops from 2,500 to 1,875 customers/month goes from 1M profit to -1.5M loss. That's an unsustainable 2.5M swing.

**Break-Even Volume Calculation:**

Break-even is when Net Profit = 0. 

Formula: **Fixed Costs ÷ Contribution Margin Ratio = Break-Even Sales**

In our example:
- Fixed costs: 9M
- CMR: 50%
- Break-even sales: 9M ÷ 0.50 = **18M/month**

Below 18M sales, location loses money. Above 18M sales, location makes profit.

At 18M sales:
- Contribution: 9M (= 50% of 18M)
- Fixed costs: -9M
- Profit: 0

**What's Our Safety Margin?**

Our actual sales: 20M
Our break-even: 18M
Our safety margin: 2M (or 10% of sales)

This means sales can drop by 10% before we hit break-even. Margins tighter than 10% are risky.

**Korean Chicken Franchise Analysis Using Break-Even:**

Remember from Week 9 the chicken franchisee who went from 10.8M profit (2020) to -0.75M loss (2024)?

Let's look at her break-even point.

**2020 Unit Economics:**

| Metric | Amount | % |
|--------|--------|---|
| Sales | 70M | 100% |
| Variable costs | -35M | 50% |
| Contribution | 35M | 50% |
| Fixed costs | -24.2M | 34.6% |
| Net profit | 10.8M | 15.4% |

**Break-even = 24.2M ÷ 0.50 = 48.4M/month**

She had 70M sales, so she was 45% above break-even. Safe margin.

**2024 Unit Economics:**

| Metric | Amount | % |
|--------|--------|---|
| Sales | 75M | 100% |
| Variable costs (30% COGS + 35% labor) | -48.75M | 65% |
| Contribution | 26.25M | 35% |
| Fixed costs | -27M | 36% |
| Net profit | -0.75M | -1% |

**Break-even = 27M ÷ 0.35 = 77.1M/month**

She now needs 77.1M in sales just to break even. But she's only generating 75M. She's underwater.

The contribution margin ratio collapsed from 50% to 35%. Here's why:
- In 2020: COGS 30% + Labor 30% = 60% variable costs = 40% contribution

You might think rent is a fixed cost (doesn't change with volume), but in Korea, rent is semi-fixed. It increases during renegotiation. In 2020, rent was 10% of sales. In 2024, it was 18%.

This rent increase (from 7M to 13.5M) is an additional 6.5M cost. That alone explains most of the profit decline.

#### Segment 3: Cost Structure Sensitivity Analysis (12 minutes)

**Understanding Sensitivity: How Do Changes in Costs Affect Profit?**

A key question franchisees face: **If one cost increases by 5%, how much does my profit change?**

The answer depends on the size of that cost and your contribution margin.

**Scenario: Labor Costs Increase 10%**

**Current (2024):**
- Labor: 26.25M (35% of sales)
- If increased 10%: 26.25M × 1.10 = 28.875M
- Additional cost: 2.625M
- Profit changes from -0.75M to -3.375M
- **Impact: -2.625M**

At -3.375M loss, franchisee is hemorrhaging money.

**Why is the impact so large?**

Because profit is already thin (-0.75M). When you add 2.625M in costs, you swing from near break-even to significant loss.

**Scenario: Sales Increase 10% (With No Cost Changes)**

**Current (2024):**
- Sales: 75M
- If increased 10%: 75M × 1.10 = 82.5M
- Variable costs increase 10%: 48.75M × 1.10 = 53.625M
- Contribution: 82.5M - 53.625M = 28.875M
- Fixed costs: -27M (same)
- Profit: 28.875M - 27M = 1.875M
- **Impact: +2.625M**

Interesting! A 10% sales increase generates the same 2.625M additional profit that a 10% labor cost increase eliminates.

This reveals **leverage**: Small changes in volume have outsized impact on profit when margins are tight.

**Three Profitability Scenarios for a Chicken Franchisee (2024-2025):**

**PESSIMISTIC (Sales Down 10%, Labor Costs Up 10%):**
- Sales: 67.5M (down from 75M)
- Variable costs: 53.625M (up from 48.75M due to labor increase)
- Contribution: 13.875M
- Fixed costs: -27M
- Profit: **-13.125M/month** (SEVERE LOSS)
- This franchisee is bankrupt

**REALISTIC (Sales Flat, Costs Increase 5%):**
- Sales: 75M
- Variable costs: 48.75M × 1.05 = 51.1875M
- Contribution: 23.8125M
- Fixed costs: -27M
- Profit: **-3.1875M/month** (LOSS)
- Franchisee needs to make major changes (relocate, cut labor, negotiate rent)

**OPTIMISTIC (Sales Up 10%, No Cost Increase):**
- Sales: 82.5M
- Variable costs: 53.625M
- Contribution: 28.875M
- Fixed costs: -27M
- Profit: **+1.875M/month** (BREAK-EVEN MOVING TO PROFIT)
- Franchisee survives

**The Franchisee's Dilemma:**

Given these scenarios, what should the franchisee do?

1. **Reduce costs:**
   - Negotiate rent reduction (lower fixed costs)
   - Improve labor efficiency (reduce labor %)
   - Buy ingredients from cheaper supplier (reduce COGS %)

2. **Increase sales:**
   - Local marketing (increase customer visits)
   - Delivery platform (expand addressable market)
   - Menu innovation (increase average transaction value)

3. **Exit:**
   - Sell location
   - Rebrand as independent
   - Close and recover equipment value

Most franchisees try #1 and #2 first. If those fail, #3 becomes necessary.

**Real Option for This Franchisee: Delivery Pivot**

Modern chicken franchises are discovering that delivery can help. Here's why:

**Original Model: Walk-in customers only**
- Rent must be prime location (expensive): 18% of sales
- Labor: 35% (staff doing counter service)
- Sales: 75M

**Delivery Model: Walk-in + delivery**
- Rent can be back-street location (cheaper): 8% of sales
- Labor: Different mix (more kitchen, less front-of-house): 28%
- Sales: 100M (due to larger addressable market)

**New Unit Economics:**

| Metric | Original | Delivery Model |
|--------|----------|----------------|
| Sales | 75M | 100M |
| COGS | -22.5M (30%) | -30M (30%) |
| Labor | -26.25M (35%) | -28M (28%) |
| Rent | -13.5M (18%) | -8M (8%) |
| Royalty | -4.5M (6%) | -6M (6%) |
| Other | -6M (8%) | -10M (10%) |
| **Profit** | **-0.75M** | **18M** |
| **Profit %** | **-1%** | **18%** |

This franchisee could move to a cheaper location, add delivery, and go from -0.75M loss to 18M profit. That's a 18.75M swing.

This is exactly what smart Korean chicken franchisees are doing in 2024-2025. They're moving out of expensive Gangnam/Myeongdong locations into cheaper areas and relying on delivery.

### Summary and Korean Franchise Crisis Context (5 minutes)

**What We Learned Today:**

1. **Unit economics** breaks down profit by contribution (what's left after variable costs)
2. **Contribution margin ratio** = (Sales - Variable Costs) ÷ Sales
3. **Break-even** = Fixed Costs ÷ Contribution Margin Ratio
4. **Sensitivity:** Small changes in cost or volume have outsized impact on thin-margin businesses
5. **The Korean chicken crisis** is fundamentally a contribution margin problem
   - Variable costs increased (chicken prices, labor)
   - Fixed costs increased (rent inflation)
   - CMR collapsed from 42% to 35%
   - Franchisees went from profitable to unprofitable

**Why This Matters:**

Understanding unit economics helps you:
- Identify which costs are killing profitability
- Calculate what sales volume you need
- Understand why some franchises fail while others thrive
- Make strategic choices (relocate, reduce labor, add delivery)

### Vocabulary Emphasis

- **Unit economics:** Per-unit profit analysis
- **Variable costs:** Costs that change with volume
- **Fixed costs:** Costs that stay same regardless of volume
- **Contribution margin:** Sales minus variable costs
- **Contribution margin ratio:** Contribution as % of sales
- **Break-even:** Sales level where profit = zero
- **Safety margin:** How much below break-even you can go
- **Sensitivity:** How much profit changes when costs/volume change
- **Semi-fixed costs:** Costs that are partially fixed (rent can increase during renegotiation)
