## Recent Key Trends in U.S. Franchising

### Overview of Federal and State Franchise Regulation

**Federal level**: Federal Trade Commission (FTC) adopted Trade Regulation Rule 436 in 1979.
- Specifies the MINIMUM amount of disclosure that must be made to a prospective franchisee

**State level**: Over a dozen states have adopted their own rules and regulations.
- Known as "registration states"
- Include most of the nation's largest commercial marketplaces: California, New York, Illinois
- Generally follow a more detailed disclosure format: The Uniform Franchise Offering Circular (UFOC)

**The UFOC:**
- Originally developed by Midwest Securities Commissioners Association in 1975
- Monitoring and revisions now under authority of North American Securities Administrators Association (NASAA)
- New version adopted April 1993; approved by FTC December 1993
- Required to be written in "plain English"

## A Brief Review of the Evolution of Franchise Regulation

**Timeline:**
- 1970: California adopted the first Franchise Investment Law
- 1978: FTC adopted its trade regulation rule (effective October 21, 1979)
- Following states now regulate franchise offers:
  - **Full registration required** (pre-approval before offering/selling): California, Illinois, Indiana, Maryland, Minnesota, New York, North Dakota, Rhode Island, South Dakota, Virginia, Washington
  - **Hawaii**: Filing required + delivery of offering circular
  - **Michigan and Wisconsin**: File Notice of Intent
  - **Oregon**: Pre-sale disclosure only
  - **Texas**: File notice of exemption

## The FTC Rule

**Governing authority**: FTC's power to regulate unfair and deceptive trade practices.

**Two types of offerings regulated:**
1. Package and product franchises
2. Business opportunity ventures

**For the franchise definition, THREE characteristics are required:**
1. Franchisee sells goods/services that meet franchisor's quality standards under the franchisor's trademark
2. Franchisor exercises significant assistance in the franchisee's method of operation
3. Franchisee is required to make a minimum payment of $500 or more to franchisor

**Business Opportunity Ventures — THREE characteristics:**
1. Franchisee sells goods/services supplied by the franchisor or affiliate
2. Franchisor assists franchisee in securing accounts, locations, or vending machines
3. Franchisee is required to make a payment of $500 or more

**FTC Rule EXEMPTIONS:**
1. Fractional franchises
2. Leased department arrangements
3. Purely verbal agreements

**FTC Rule EXCLUSIONS:**
1. Employer/employee relationships and general business partners
2. Membership in retailer-owned cooperatives
3. Certification and testing services
4. Single trademark licenses

### The 20 Required Disclosure Items

1. Identifying information about the franchisor
2. Business experience of the franchisor's directors and key executives
3. The franchisor's business experience
4. Litigation history
5. Bankruptcy history
6. Description of the franchise
7. Money required to be paid to obtain or commence the franchise operation
8. Continuing expenses to the franchisee
9. Persons with whom the franchisee is required to do business
10. Realty, personalty, services that the franchisee must purchase
11. Consideration paid by third parties to the franchisor as a result of franchisee purchases
12. Franchisor assistance in financing
13. Restrictions on the franchisee's conduct of business
14. Required personal participation by the franchisee
15. Termination, cancellation, and renewal of the franchise
16. Statistical information about the number of franchises and their rate of termination
17. Franchisor's right to select or approve a site
18. Training programs
19. Celebrity involvement
20. Financial information about the franchisor

**Timing requirements**: Disclosure document must be given to prospective franchisee at the EARLIER of:
1. First personal meeting between prospective franchisee and franchisor
2. Ten business days prior to execution of a contract
3. Ten business days before payment of money relating to the franchise relationship

**The 10-day and 5-day disclosure periods may run concurrently.**

## State Franchise Laws

**The FTC Rule creates a minimum U.S. federal standard** — states may provide additional protection.

### Four Major Types of State Definitions of a Franchise:

**A. Majority State Definition** (California, Illinois, Indiana, Maryland, Michigan, North Dakota, Oregon, Rhode Island, Wisconsin):
1. Franchisee is granted the right to engage in business under a marketing plan prescribed in substantial part by a franchisor
2. Operation substantially associated with the franchisor's trademark
3. Franchisee is required to pay a fee

**B. Minority State Definition** (Hawaii, Minnesota, South Dakota, Washington):
1. Franchisee is granted the right to engage in business using the franchisor's trade name or related characteristics
2. Franchisor and franchisee have a common interest in the marketing of goods or services
3. Franchisee pays a fee

**C. New York Definition** (unique):
1. Franchisor is paid a fee by the franchisee
2. EITHER: essentially associated with the franchisor's trademark OR franchisee operates under a marketing plan prescribed in substantial part by the franchisor

**D. Virginia Definition**:
1. Franchisee is granted the right to engage in business at retail under a marketing plan prescribed in substantial part by a franchisor
2. Franchisee's business is substantially associated with the franchisor's trademark
3. (Note: Virginia does NOT have a "fee" element in its definition)

## Guidelines for Determining What Is a Franchise

**Common reasons companies avoid the franchise definition:**
a. Overseas franchisors uncomfortable with disclosure requirements
b. Mid-sized companies feeling their industry would react adversely to franchise controls
c. Company preferring not to disclose aspects of its past
d. Small company concerned about perceived costs
e. Belief that becoming a franchisor increases chance of being sued *(this is a myth)*
f. Other specific fears about franchising

**The "three-legged stool"**: Three elements of a franchise definition — companies try to sacrifice ONE leg to avoid compliance.
- In a brand-driven environment, most won't sacrifice the trademark license leg
- In a cash-flow-focused environment, most won't waive the franchise fee
- The THIRD leg (significant assistance/control) is where most creative structuring attempts focus

**Court's approach**: Examines the "totality of the relationship" with emphasis on REALITY and PRACTICE rather than the written word.

### FTC Analysis — Three Key Components:

**1. Trademark**: Franchisee is given the right to distribute goods or services under the franchisor's trademark or service mark.

**2. Required Payment**: Franchisee is required to pay at least $500 to the franchisor as a condition of obtaining the franchise or commencing operations. Payments made at any time prior to, or within 6 months after commencing operations, are aggregated.

**3. Significant Control and Assistance**: Must be "significant" — relates to the degree to which the franchisee is DEPENDENT on the franchisor's superior business expertise.

**Types of Control or Assistance:**

| Type of Control/Assistance |
|---------------------------|
| Site approval |
| Site design/appearance requirements |
| Dictating hours of operation |
| Personnel advice |
| Site selection assistance |
| Furnishing detailed operations manual |
| Required participation in promotional campaigns |
| Restrictions on customers |
| Restrictions on sales area or location |
| Formal sales, repair, or business training |
| Establishing accounting systems |
| Furnishing management, marketing, or production techniques |
| Accounting practices |
| Personnel policies/practices |

**Strategic questions before proceeding:**
- Is the relationship short-term or long-term?
- Are we prepared to sacrifice brand awareness in a brand-driven competitive environment?
- Are we prepared to deliver the level and quality of training expected in the franchisor-franchisee relationship?
- How interdependent do we really need or want to be?
- To what extent will training, support, marketing, and other key functions truly be uniform and centralized?
- If we operate in the gray area without a UFOC, how comfortable are we with the possibility of a regulatory investigation and system-wide rescission offer?

## Preparing the Disclosure Document

**Most franchisors elect to adopt the UFOC format** because many registration states do not accept the FTC Rule format.

**When FTC Rule format might offer advantages** (if limiting to non-registration states):
- Requires less information than UFOC in several areas
- Three-year phase-in period for use of audited financials (vs. UFOC requirement from onset)
- Allows financially weak franchisor to phase in — UFOC requires audited financials from start

**Warning for early-stage franchisors**: State franchise examiners may impose costly escrow and bonding procedures or require personal guarantees for financially weak franchisors. Some may be denied registration entirely.

## The Mechanics of the Registration Process

**Filing package components:**
- Offering circular
- Franchise agreement
- Supplemental agreements
- Financial statements
- Franchise roster
- Mandated cover pages
- Acknowledgment of receipt
- State-specific forms (corporation verification statements, salesperson disclosure forms, consent to service of process)

**Initial filing fees**: $250 to $750
**Renewal filing fees**: Usually $100 to $250

**Process:**
1. Counsel "custom tailors" the UFOC to meet special requirements of the particular state
2. Documents filed with state franchise administrator
3. Specific franchise examiner (usually an attorney) is assigned
4. Examiner reviews offering materials
5. At least one "comment letter" requesting changes or additional information
6. Timeline: As quickly as 6 weeks, as slowly as 6 months

**Ongoing reporting requirements vary by state:**
- Maryland: Only state requiring QUARTERLY reports
- Annual filing of renewal application or annual report: All registration states
- Advertising materials must be approved in advance by all registration states EXCEPT Virginia and Hawaii
- Salesperson disclosure forms required by all registration states EXCEPT Virginia
