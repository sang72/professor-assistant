## SECTION 2: FULL LECTURE SCRIPT

Welcome to the final session of Week 3. We have introduced the Franchise Relationship Model. We have explored how franchisors and franchisees create value together. Today, we examine a case that brings all of this to life: Pizza Hut versus Domino's.

Both are pizza franchises. Both operate worldwide. Yet their franchise models evolved differently. Understanding why teaches us about the relationship between franchisor and franchisee.

[SHOW SLIDE 1: Pizza Hut History]

Pizza Hut started in 1958 in Wichita, Kansas. The concept was simple: sit-down pizza restaurants. Family-friendly atmosphere. Salad bar. Beverages. The restaurant experience was central.

Pizza Hut grew by franchising. By the 1970s, Pizza Hut was a major franchise operation. The franchisor controlled much about the system: menu, pricing, restaurant design. Franchisees operated locations but had limited flexibility.

[TIME: ~10 min]

---

[SHOW SLIDE 2: Domino's History]

Domino's also started around the same time, in 1960 in Michigan. The founder, Tom Monaghan, focused on one thing: fast pizza delivery. No dine-in. No salad bar. Just pizza, made quickly, delivered to your door.

Domino's also grew by franchising. But the franchise model was different. Domino's gave franchisees more autonomy in local decisions. The core was delivery speed and pizza quality, but franchisees had freedom within that.

[TIME: ~20 min]

---

[SHOW SLIDE 3: The Key Difference]

Here is the crucial difference. Pizza Hut's model relied on dining experience. You had to control many variables: ambiance, service, beverages. This required tight franchisor control.

Domino's model was simpler. Delivery. Pizza quality. Speed. This could be franchised with less control. Franchisees could make local adaptations.

As delivery became dominant in pizza consumption, Domino's model proved superior. Pizza Hut was burdened by the complexity of sit-down restaurants. Domino's was lean and focused on delivery.

[WRITE ON BOARD: Pizza Hut = Complex System = Tight Control | Domino's = Simple System = Franchisee Autonomy]

[TIME: ~30 min]

---

[SHOW SLIDE 4: Lessons for FRM]

What does this case teach us about the Franchise Relationship Model? First, the simpler the Service Delivery System, the easier it is to franchise. Second, franchisors must balance control and autonomy. Too much control stifles innovation. Too little control damages the brand. Third, the franchisor's business model choice shapes the franchise relationship. Pizza Hut chose a complex model. Domino's chose simplicity. These choices had consequences.

[TIME: ~40 min]

---

[ASK STUDENTS: If you were Pizza Hut in the 1990s, how would you have responded to Domino's success? What changes would you make?]

[PAUSE — wait for student responses]

Good thinking. Pizza Hut eventually adapted, adding delivery and streamlining menus. But they had to overcome years of system complexity. Domino's started simple and stayed simple.

This case shows that the franchisor-franchisee relationship is not static. It evolves based on market conditions, customer preferences, and competitive dynamics.
